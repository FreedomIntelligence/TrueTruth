## English Abstract

Hypertension is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease. Salt substitutes may reduce sodium intake while maintaining palatability, but comparative effects across formulations remain uncertain.

We conducted a systematic review and frequentist random-effects network meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials in adults comparing salt substitutes with regular salt, other substitutes or no intervention. Databases (PubMed, Embase, CENTRAL, CNKI, Wanfang), WHO-ICTRP and ClinicalTrials.gov were searched from inception to Oct 3, 2025 (PROSPERO CRD42023451859). We assessed the risk of bias using a modified Cochrane tool and conducted a random-effects network meta-analysis, with evidence certainty evaluated through the GRADE approach.

We included 34 randomised controlled trials involving 37,063 participants across 15 countries (17 from China, 17 from other countries; mean age 62.3 years). Our results indicate that moderate-potassium and low-sodium salt substitutes (25–40% KCl, 60–79% NaCl) probably reduce all-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, non-fatal cardiovascular events and systolic blood pressure (SBP) compared to regular salt, with reductions of 7–17 deaths per 1000 individuals and 4.39–4.64 mmHg for SBP, based on moderate to high certainty evidence. Mortality and cardiovascular benefits are predominantly driven by one large Chinese trial (SSaSS, n = 20,995); excluding this trial eliminated statistical significance for all-cause mortality. Among non-Chinese studies, none contributed mortality data. Substitutes with higher potassium or very low sodium showed similar blood pressure reductions but provided less certain evidence regarding mortality and events. No substitute increased adverse events or withdrawals, and acceptability was comparable to regular salt.

Salt substitutes, particularly moderate-potassium and low-sodium formulations, represent a promising sodium reduction strategy. However, current evidence for mortality and cardiovascular event benefits is dominated by one large Chinese trial and has very limited generalisability beyond Chinese populations with high discretionary salt use. These products appear acceptable and safe in people without renal impairment, but clinicians should rule out kidney disease and hyperkalaemia risk before recommending them, and large trials in non-Chinese populations are needed.

## 背景 / Background

Increased systolic blood pressure (SBP), a modifiable cause of cardiovascular disease (CVD), accounts for 16.0% of deaths globally with hypertension consuming up to 8.5% of gross domestic product (GDP) in industrialized nations [1–3]. Dietary intake of sodium chloride (NaCl) is a key factor in the onset of hypertension [4, 5], prompting recommendations for salt reduction to reduce CVD risk and mortality [6]. However, challenges arise in reducing population-level salt intake due to individual preferences and palatability issues, as well as broader supply-level factors in food production, distribution, and marketing [7].

Salt substitutes alter salts’ composition while preserving its flavour, and represent a promising intervention for reducing sodium intake [8]. Salt substitutes are products that replace NaCl with potassium chloride (KCl) or other constituents such as magnesium salts or chitosan [9]. Recent systematic reviews have established the effectiveness of salt substitutes in reducing blood pressure and cardiovascular events [10]. However, variability in salt substitute formulations may lead to variations in their efficacy and acceptability [11]. The comparative effectiveness of different substitute types and compositions remains unclear, limiting evidence-based selection.

We therefore conducted this first-ever systematic review and network meta-analysis, applying Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation (GRADE) and minimal important difference (MID) thresholds, to compare the effects of widely available salt substitutes on blood pressure, non-fatal cardiovascular events, and mortality, and to assess acceptability and safety.

## 方法 / Methods

We prepared this manuscript in accordance with the PRISMA-NMA guidelines [12], and adhered to a registered protocol (PROSPERO–CRD 42023451859; Additional file 1: Text S1). Pairs of reviewers independently performed the study selection, data extraction and risk-of-bias assessments with discrepancies resolved through discussion or with adjudication from a third author, if required.

We searched PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials, China National Knowledge Infrastructure, Wanfang database, the World Health Organization’s International Clinical Trials Registry Platform and ClinicalTrials.gov from inception to October 3, 2025. The search strategy (Additional file 1: Table S1) employed a combination of controlled vocabulary and keywords pertaining to salt substitutes, blood pressure, cardiovascular diseases and randomised controlled trials (RCTs). We also reviewed reference lists of included studies and relevant reviews.

We included RCTs that enrolled adults (≥ 18 years) comparing salt substitutes (substituting a minimum of 3% of NaCl with KCl or other constituents), with either regular salt (≥ 97% NaCl), other salt substitutes or no intervention. We excluded trials involving combined interventions (e.g. salt substitutes and salt restriction). Important outcomes included all-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, non-fatal cardiovascular events and systolic and diastolic blood pressure (DBP). Other outcomes included withdrawals due to adverse events (AEs), acceptance, adverse events related to intervention, withdrawals due to any reason, serum potassium, serum sodium, urinary potassium and urinary sodium.

We used Rayyan [13], a web-based platform to assist with study selection. We screened titles and abstracts using selection criteria detailed in Additional file 1: Table S2, then evaluated potentially eligible full-texts. We extracted study characteristics, demographic information, description of interventions and comparators and outcomes (Additional file 1: Table S3). For crossover RCTs, we included only the first period. We assessed the risk of bias using a modified Cochrane 1.0 instrument [14]. For cluster-RCTs, we followed the Cochrane handbook (Additional file 1: Text S2) [15].

Given the complexity of salt substitute formulations and uncertainty about which compositional features drive health outcomes, we classified salt substitutes using three strategies (Table 1): (1) by the presence of specific components comprising at least 3% of the salt mixture, assuming that variations in composition may lead to different health outcomes [16]; (2) by KCl content, categorized as high (50 to 65%), moderate (25 to 40%) and low (5 to 20%) potassium salt substitutes, assuming that potassium levels affect health outcomes [11]; and (3) by NaCl content, categorized as reduced (80 to 97%), low (60 to 79%), very low (40 to 59%) and extremely low (2 to 39%) sodium salt substitutes, assuming that sodium levels affect health outcomes [17].

We computed continuous variables as the mean difference (MD) using change scores when reported by at least 50% of studies, and otherwise we used the final (endpoint) values [18]. For dichotomous outcomes, we calculated risk ratios (RRs) along with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We used available Cochrane guidance to impute missing values (Additional file 1: Text S3) [19], and to extract graphically-represented data when necessary.

Assuming similar heterogeneity across all comparisons and considering the correlations induced by multi-arm studies, we conducted random-effects arm-based network meta-analyses using a frequentist weighted least squares methodology [20]. For direct comparisons, we executed DerSimonian-Laird random-effects pairwise meta-analyses. We evaluated statistical heterogeneity within each pairwise and network meta-analysis comparison using I2 and Q [21]. We calculated indirect estimates from the network through node splitting and back calculation methods (i.e. subtracting direct from network estimates). For every enclosed loop within the network, we examined local inconsistency using both back calculation and separate assessment of indirect and direct evidence [22]. We evaluated the transitivity assumption by comparing the distributions of key population and intervention study characteristics among studies grouped by comparison [23]. Where at least ten studies were available, we assessed small-study effects and publication bias using funnel plots [24].

We conducted network meta-regression analyses to explore the impact of mean age, proportion of females, mean body mass index (BMI), baseline SBP and baseline DBP (Additional file 1: Text S4). We used the Instrument to assess the Credibility of Effect Modification Analyses (ICEMAN) for assessing the credibility of effect modification analyses [25]. We also conducted sensitivity and additional analyses including exclusion of trials conducted in Chinese populations, trials in hypertensive patients and subgroup analyses by intervention duration for blood pressure outcomes.

For each network meta-analysis, we evaluated the certainty of evidence based on estimates of the minimally important difference (MID) threshold for direct, indirect and network comparisons [26, 27]. We initially assigned high certainty to direct comparisons, rating down for risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness and publication bias. For indirect evidence, we rated certainty based on the lowest certainty among direct comparisons in the most dominant first order loop, further rating down for intransitivity [28, 29]. In our evaluation of network evidence, we began with the certainty derived from the dominant direct and/or indirect comparisons and further considered incoherence or imprecision [30]. We used the MID thresholds for mortality, non-fatal cardiovascular events and blood pressure, based on estimates used in previous dietary guidelines and systematic reviews [31–33]. For fatal outcomes, the threshold was 5 events per 1000; for non-fatal outcomes, the threshold was 10 events over 5 years [31, 32]; while for SBP and DBP (mean of follow-up 10.5 months), the thresholds were 3.0 and 2.0 mmHg, respectively [34]. For other continuous outcomes (serum and urinary electrolytes), we specified the MID as 0.3 times the baseline standard deviation (SD) [35]; for other dichotomous outcomes, we specified the MID as documented in Additional file 1: Table S4. We rated down for imprecision if the 95% confidence intervals (CIs) of pooled estimates crossed our MID thresholds [36, 37]. For dichotomous outcomes, we calculated anticipated absolute effects by applying the summary RRs or ORs to baseline risks (Additional file 1: Text S3 outlines the formulas) [38]. We defined intermediate baseline risk as a 5–10% 5-year cardiovascular event risk and high baseline risk as a 20–30% 5-year cardiovascular event risk [32, 39]. The baseline risks were ascertained using control group data from a meta-analysis conducted by the Cholesterol Treatment Trialists’ Collaboration (Additional file 1: Text S5) [39].

There was no funding source for this study.

For all-cause mortality, both formulations (i.e., moderate-potassium and low-sodium) reduced deaths by seven per 1000 followed, among individuals at intermediate cardiovascular risk over 5 years, based on moderate certainty evidence. For those at high cardiovascular risk, we found 17 fewer deaths per 1,000 over 5 years, based on high certainty evidence.

For cardiovascular mortality, both substitutes reduced deaths by five per 1000 at intermediate risk and by 16 per 1000 at high risk over a 5-year period, with both findings supported by moderate certainty evidence.

The mortality benefits of moderate-potassium and low-sodium salt substitutes were primarily supported by a large Chinese RCT (n = 20,995 participants) [61]. After excluding this study, while the significant all-cause mortality benefit was eliminated (still showing reductions of 4 and 10 deaths/1000 for those at intermediate and high cardiovascular risk), cardiovascular mortality remained significantly reduced, with 9 and 29 fewer deaths per 1000 in intermediate and high risk populations, respectively, over 5 years. Additionally, the certainty in effects for high-potassium and very low-sodium (e.g. 50% NaCl + 50% KCl) salt substitutes were less pronounced with only very low to low certainty evidence for mortality outcomes. No significant difference was found between the substitutes.

Ten trials with 26,505 participants reported the risk of non-fatal cardiovascular events, including angina (n = 2) [41, 62], stroke (n = 1) [63], major adverse cardiovascular events (n = 1) [61] and unspecified or unclear events (n = 6) [45, 48, 51, 65, 69, 73]. Nine of ten trials were included in the analysis while one was excluded because there were no events. Moderate-potassium and low-sodium (9 trials; 26,048 participants) substitutes were probably beneficial for intermediate risk individuals and might be beneficial for high cardiovascular risk individuals compared to regular salt, based on low to moderate certainty evidence over 5 years. However, these effects were also influenced by the same large Chinese RCT [61], and excluding it made the benefits insignificant (while low-sodium substitutes still showing reductions of 24 and 80 deaths/1000 in intermediate and high risk populations, respectively; Additional file 6: Table S1). The certainty in effects for high-potassium, low-potassium, reduced-sodium and very low-sodium salt substitutes were less pronounced and very low to low (Table 4). No significant difference was found between the substitutes. After excluding six studies with unspecified cardiovascular events, the results did not change.

Twenty-nine trials with 27,631 participants reported blood pressure, of which 28 trials encompassing 27,621 participants with available data were included in the analysis. High-potassium (e.g. 50% NaCl + 50% KCl) and moderate-potassium (e.g. 62.5% NaCl + 25% KCl) salt substitutes probably reduced SBP compared to regular salt, based on moderate certainty evidence. Reduced-sodium (e.g. 87% NaCl + 6.5% KCl) and very low-sodium (e.g. 50% NaCl + 50% KCl) salt substitutes reduced both SBP and DBP compared to regular salt, based on moderate to high certainty evidence. Low-potassium (e.g. 87% NaCl + 6.5% KCl), low-sodium salt substitutes and extremely low-sodium (e.g. 33% NaCl + 67% KCl) salt substitutes showed potential effects on blood pressure reduction, though these findings were supported by low to very low certainty evidence, and salt substitutes with lower NaCl content did not demonstrate enhanced effects, possibly due to limited sample size resulting in less precise measurement.

The results of meta-regression indicated that salt substitutes may have a larger effect on both SBP and DBP in older patients, and a larger effect on SBP in those with higher baseline SBP. The credibility of these interaction effects assessed using ICEMAN subgroup criteria was judged as low (Additional files 4 and 5). One trial that provided conclusions only (no explicit data) aligned with the current findings (Additional file 6: Table S2) [42]. No significant difference was found in the comparisons between salt substitutes on SBP. Intervention duration did not significantly affect blood pressure reduction (Additional file 6: Fig. S1).

Among ten trials comprising 7060 participants, a total of 117 AEs related to intervention were reported, averaging 2 per 100 person-years of follow-up. However, the network meta-analysis only included five trials with 3336 participants and 117 AEs, excluding five trials due to zero-cells. With moderate certainty evidence, high-potassium, moderate-potassium, reduced-sodium, low-sodium and very low-sodium salt substitutes demonstrated trivial to no difference in AEs compared to regular salt. The evidence regarding AEs for low-potassium and extremely low-sodium salt substitutes was less conclusive and rated as low certainty. No significant difference was found between substitutes. Qualitative descriptions of AEs across the trials corroborated these findings, indicating that the salt substitutes did not significantly increase the risk of AEs, even among elderly individuals with chronic kidney disease or those taking potassium-sparing medications.

Ten trials involving 3311 participants documented a total of 10 withdrawals due to AEs, with an incidence rate of 3 per 1000 person-years of follow-up. However, a quantitative analysis on this outcome was not conducted due to zero-cells across most trials. Further examination of these reported cases suggested that the AEs leading to the withdrawals were likely unrelated to the salt substitutes.

Six trials, involving 1881 participants, reported data on acceptance by conducting surveys on acceptability of salt substitutes and recording withdrawals due to non-acceptance. No evidence suggested a poorer acceptance of the salt substitutes compared to regular salt.

Twenty-four trials comprising 26,152 participants reported 4636 withdrawals, of which 19 trials comprising 25,584 participants were included in the analysis. Compared to regular salt, moderate-potassium salt substitutes probably have trivial or no withdrawal risk over 5 years based on moderate certainty evidence, while the certainty in effects for other substitutes were less pronounced and only very low to low.

Regarding electrolyte balance, very low to moderate certainty evidence suggested that increased KCl content, coupled with correspondingly reduced NaCl levels, may lead to elevated urinary potassium excretion and decreased urinary sodium excretion.

## 讨论 / Discussion

This systematic review and network meta-analysis of 34 RCTs involving 37,063 participants from 15 countries provides a comprehensive evaluation of salt substitutes with varied compositions. In patients primarily with hypertension and without kidney disease at intermediate to high cardiovascular risk, our NMA of salt substitutes suggests that K-salts, composed of varying proportions of NaCl and KCl, demonstrate important reductions in all-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, non-fatal cardiovascular events and SBP. While recent mechanistic study suggests that potassium enrichment may contribute more substantially to blood pressure lowering effects than sodium reduction [74], our NMA of salt substitutes with different KCl and NaCl content did not detect significant differences in blood pressure reduction between formulations, and further investigation is required to clarify these mechanisms across diverse populations and settings. Compared to regular salt, moderate-potassium and low-sodium salt substitutes (i.e. 25 to 40% KCl and 60 to 79% NaCl) demonstrate the most compelling evidence, with probable reductions in all-cause mortality (7 to 17 fewer deaths/1000 followed at intermediate and high cardiovascular risk, respectively) and cardiovascular mortality (5 to 16 fewer deaths/1000 followed at intermediate and high cardiovascular risk, respectively) over 5 years, based on moderate to high certainty evidence. These substitutes may also reduce the risk of non-fatal cardiovascular events and decrease SBP, albeit with low to moderate certainty evidence. Notably, these substitutes appear to maintain favourable safety and acceptability profiles, with no significant increase in AEs or palatability concerns. Salt substitutes with higher or lower KCl and NaCl content (e.g. 50% NaCl + 50% KCl) demonstrate significant and important effects on blood pressure, but the certainty of evidence for mortality and cardiovascular events is very low to low due to limited study population size. Although higher potassium concentrations in salt substitutes may theoretically confer greater benefits [11], our analysis shows that while these formulations demonstrate larger point estimates in SBP reduction, the difference between various concentrations based on our MID estimates is not important.

Multiple previous reviews concluded that the blood pressure-lowering effects of salt substitutes are generalizable across diverse populations and geographic regions [8, 75, 76], with mortality benefits similarly extrapolated to global settings [8]. With a larger evidence base, our findings reveal limitations overlooked by previous reviews and confirm Greenwood et al.’s cautious interpretation that current evidence [10], dominated by a single large Chinese trial (SSaSS, n = 20,995 participants), has very limited generalizability beyond populations following Chinese dietary patterns characterized by high discretionary salt use and home food preparation. Our sensitivity analysis reinforces this concern: excluding the SSaSS trial eliminated the statistically significant all-cause mortality benefit, demonstrating that pooled mortality effects are driven predominantly by this single geographic and cultural context. Beyond confirming this geographic limitation with a larger dataset (34 vs. 16 trials), our NMA provides three novel contributions: first, formulation-specific guidance through head-to-head comparisons revealing that moderate-potassium substitutes have more robust mortality evidence than high-potassium substitutes; second, systematic application of minimal important difference thresholds demonstrating that moderate-potassium formulations exceed clinically meaningful thresholds; and third, transparent GRADE certainty ratings for each formulation, enabling evidence-based selection of specific salt substitute types rather than treating them as a homogeneous intervention class.

The concentration of evidence in Chinese populations, who tend to prepare food at home more often than many Western populations, have higher sodium intake levels, and consume less processed or ultra-processed foods [77–79], may limit the global generalizability of our findings. Although point estimates continued to show reductions in mortality after excluding the SSaSS trial (4 and 24 fewer deaths/1000 in intermediate risk populations for all-cause mortality and non-fatal cardiovascular events, respectively), the loss of statistical significance underscores the need for large-scale trials in other populations.

Further research is needed to establish effectiveness and safety in diverse populations with varied food systems [80], including non-Chinese populations where the proportion of food prepared outside the home is higher [78, 79], as well as Black populations that are more likely to have sodium sensitivity [81, 82]. The uncertainty surrounding the effectiveness and safety of other compositions warrants further investigation. Caution should be exercised when considering increased potassium fortification of the food supply, as this may pose a risk of hyperkalaemia and sudden cardiac death among those with impaired renal function and other risk factors [83].

When considering the implementation of salt substitutes, it is crucial to account for palatability from a geographical and cultural perspective related dietary sodium intake. The acceptability of reduced-sodium products together with products with increased potassium may vary across populations, particularly between China and Western countries, potentially influencing the effectiveness of low sodium substitutes [77, 84, 85]. Moreover, while salt substitutes did not appear to significantly increase the risk of AEs or withdrawals due to AEs compared to regular salt in the overall population, safety concerns persist for patients with chronic or undiagnosed kidney disease or renal impairment. In addition, if patients are on potassium sparing diuretics, they may be more susceptible to hyperkalaemia and at risk of sudden death due to impaired potassium excretion [86]. Still further, patients with type 1 or 2 diabetes may also be at risk of hyperkalaemia [87–89]. Hyperkalaemia can cause cardiac arrhythmias and, in severe cases, sudden cardiac death, with evidence suggesting its association with cardiac events and cardiovascular mortality across all levels of kidney function, even in individuals without prior kidney problems [90, 91]. Clinicians should rule out kidney problems before suggesting salt substitutes and future studies should exclude or possibly prioritize the evaluation of safety and efficacy in some of these vulnerable populations (e.g. diabetes). Although our study did not find significant concerns regarding the acceptability of salt substitutes, evidence suggests that some individuals may not tolerate the taste of foods with high potassium content, potentially leading to reduced adherence to low-sodium salt substitutes [92, 93]. There is however evidence that some patients can increase that tolerance and acceptability of salt substitutes [94].

With respect to study strengths, this is the first systematic review with network meta-analysis to report on the cardiovascular health outcomes of salt substitutes with varied compositions and different KCl and NaCl content, expanding upon prior meta-analyses which relied entirely on pairwise analyses only [75, 76]. Our network meta-analysis has allowed for a comparative effectiveness analysis of 7 salt substitutes (combining different potassium and sodium content classifications), determining that moderate-potassium and low-sodium salt substitutes (i.e. 25 to 40% KCl and 60 to 79% NaCl) has the best available evidence for patient important mortality and major cardiovascular events. Compared to prior reviews, we also assessed acceptability, safety, evidence certainty using GRADE methods, and we determined the comparative effectiveness of available salt substitutes using MID thresholds, all of which are important factors to optimally support clinical decision-making and guideline development.

Our study is not without limitations. The predominance of Chinese participants may limit the generalizability of the findings to other populations. Additionally, variations in taste perception and adherence across different salt substitute formulations might have influenced the results. Our analysis could not fully account for total dietary sodium and potassium intake beyond the substitutes, and the inconsistent relationship between reduced NaCl content in salt substitutes and urinary sodium excretion suggests that incomplete replacement of regular salt or additional dietary sodium sources may have confounded the results. The safety assessment in special populations, such as those with chronic kidney disease or on potassium-sparing medications, was insufficient, necessitating targeted future investigation. Moreover, consistent with a recent systematic review [10], the long-term effectiveness and safety of salt substitutes remains to be established. The validation of urinary sodium and potassium excretion as markers of adherence and effectiveness also requires further research. Lastly, the current categorization of potassium salt dosages was simplified for the purpose of the network meta-analysis; future studies could explore more refined network models using original dosage data, while cautioning against overfitting.

## 结论 / Conclusion

This systematic review and network meta-analysis provides evidence that salt substitutes, particularly moderate-potassium and low-sodium formulations, probably reduce all-cause and cardiovascular mortality, as well as SBP, making them, in addition to WHO suggestions such as choosing minimally processed low sodium food products [95], a promising sodium reduction strategy with the caveat that individuals without kidney impairment at intermediate and high cardiovascular risk need more investigation. However, the evidence supporting these mortality benefits is derived predominantly from a single large Chinese trial (SSaSS, n = 20,995). Sensitivity analysis excluding this trial eliminated statistical significance for all-cause mortality, demonstrating very limited generalizability beyond Chinese populations with high discretionary salt use and home food preparation. Among non-Chinese trials, none examined mortality outcomes. The applicability to other populations, including other Asian populations with different dietary patterns or higher processed food consumption, remains highly uncertain. These substitutes demonstrated favourable safety and acceptability profiles in patients without renal impairment. Clinicians should first rule out underlying kidney problems and hyperkalaemia risk before recommending salt substitutes.
