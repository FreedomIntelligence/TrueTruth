## English Abstract

Dietary sodium reduction affects blood pressure (BP), the renin‐angiotensin‐aldosterone system (RAAS) and natriuretic peptides. Whether baseline levels of these hormones impact BP response to sodium reduction remains unknown. Aldosterone dysregulation relates to higher cardiovascular risk, but the relation to sodium reduction has not been uncovered.

We conducted a randomized clinical trial of 72 patients with hypertension, who were randomised 2:1 to four weeks of dietary sodium reduction or a control group. 24‐h BP and 24‐h urine collection were performed and blood samples (renin, aldosterone, pro‐ANP, BNP) were drawn before and after. Antihypertensive medication was continued throughout the study.

We found a significant increase in plasma levels of renin and aldosterone in the low‐salt group compared to the control group. We performed a post‐hoc separation of the low‐salt group based on the change in aldosterone after sodium reduction into aldosterone responders and non‐responders, and found significantly higher baseline aldosterone levels in the non‐responders (p = 0.01). Systolic 24‐h BP did not change in the aldosterone non‐responders, but decreased 9 mmHg among the aldosterone responders (p = 0.01 for difference). Pro‐ANP and BNP decreased in the aldosterone responders, however, increases were found in the non‐responders. There was a tendency towards a larger decrease in body weight in the aldosterone responders (p = 0.054).

In conclusion, sodium reduction revealed a group of aldosterone non‐responders with impaired RAAS activation, higher aldosterone levels and no BP response. This group showed opposite responses in natriuretic peptides and less decrease of body weight than aldosterone responders.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension has become a global public health issue and the reasons are several; increasing body weight, smoking, less physical activity, and higher dietary sodium intake are amongst the most important modifiable risk factors [1, 2]. Reducing sodium intake is included in the 2024 guidelines on treating hypertension from the European Society of Hypertension (ESH) and a maximum daily salt (sodium chloride, NaCl) intake of 5 grams is recommended [2].

Dietary sodium affects numerous hormone axes, influences vascular function, changes body water content and alters the immune system [3, 4, 5]. It is well established that sodium reduction activates the renin‐angiotensin‐aldosterone system (RAAS) and causes changes in the vasopressin‐aquaporin‐2 (AQP2)‐pathway [5, 6, 7]. Aldosterone dysregulation refers to aldosterone levels inappropriately high relative to sodium intake and is often evaluated using the aldosterone‐to‐renin‐ratio (ARR) [8]. Previously, elevated aldosterone or ARR levels were only studied in relation to diagnosis of primary hyperaldosteronism. Recent years, the understanding has changed into considering aldosterone levels on a spectrum, and it is shown that high aldosterone levels are more common in the population than previously believed [9]. Studies imply that aldosterone dysregulation is a risk factor for developing hypertension and cardiovascular disease even at levels far from primary hyperaldosteronism [10, 11]. How people with dysregulated aldosterone respond to sodium reduction has not yet been investigated, and only few studies have investigated how dietary sodium alters ARR [12, 13, 14].

High copeptin levels (reflecting the level of vasopressin) have shown to increase the risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke and mortality [15, 16, 17]. Studies indicate that patients with resistant hypertension have higher levels of copeptin [18]. However, it is unclear how copeptin relates to blood pressure (BP) response after sodium reduction. The same goes for the levels of natriuretic peptides ANP and BNP in hypertensive patients, and how these hormone systems work together after a sodium reduction is not entirely clear [19, 20].

In a previous paper, we showed that dietary advice to patients with essential hypertension leads to relevant sodium reduction and significantly lower BP [21]. In this paper, we aim to elucidate the physiological responses to this dietary sodium reduction, performed in a population of medically treated patients with hypertension. In particular, we aimed to explore the aldosterone response to sodium reduction and how this affects BP.

We hypothesized that sodium reduction would lead to activation of RAAS, and this activation would differ according to baseline aldosterone levels, and was related to BP response. We also hypothesized that ARR would be affected. Additionally, we hypothesized that baseline copeptin levels were associated to BP change after sodium reduction.

## 方法 / Methods

A detailed description of study design, methods and participants is presented elsewhere [21]. In short, we conducted a non‐blinded, randomized controlled trial of 72 participants with HTN at the University Clinic in Nephrology and Hypertension, Gødstrup Hospital and Aarhus University, Denmark. Participants were randomized 2:1 to four weeks of self‐performed dietary sodium reduction or a control group continuing their normal diet. Before and after the intervention, measurements of body weight, 24‐h BP and 24‐h urine collection were conducted and blood samples were drawn after 30 min rest on an examination couch. All blood samples were collected before noon and in supine position. Usual hypertensive medication was continued unaltered.

Eligible participants were ≥ 18 years, diagnosed with essential hypertension, with eGFR > 15 mL/min/m2, urinary albumin‐to‐creatinine ratio ≤ 500 mg/g and BMI ≤ 35 kg/m2. Exclusion criteria were diabetes mellitus, clinically relevant heart or liver disease, cancer other than non‐melanoma skin cancer, secondary hypertension, use of more than three types of BP medication, use of immunosuppressant medication, pregnancy or lactation.

BP was measured for 24‐h using a cuff‐based apparatus (Mobil‐O‐Graph PWA, I.E.M., Stolberg, Germany). At the screening visit office BP was measured in both arms, and if difference in BP was more than 10 mmHg, the arm with the highest BP was chosen for upcoming measurements. If measurements were identical the non‐dominant arm was chosen. Measurements were made every 20th minute throughout day and night.

Plasma BNP was analyzed by the Department of Clinical Biochemistry, Gødstrup Hospital as routine procedure. Remaining urine and blood analyses were conducted at the University Clinic in Nephrology and Hypertension. Renin and aldosterone were analyzed using immune‐radiometric assay kits (DIAsource ImmunoAssays S.A., Leuvain‐la‐Neuve, Belgium). The lowest detectable concentration of renin was 4.36 pg/ml and of aldosterone 26.7 pg/ml. AQP2 was measured by radioimmunoassay as previously described [6]. Copeptin (a part of the molecule pre‐pro‐vasopressin) and pro‐ANP was measured with Time‐Resolved Amplified Cryptate Emission (TRACE)‐technology using BRAHMS KRYPTOR Compact Plus (ThermoFisher Scientific B·R·A·H·M·S GmbH, Hennigsdorf, Germany). For analysis of plasma and urinary osmolality freeze point depression with an Advanced Instruments A2O osmometer (Advanced Instruments, Norwood, MA, USA) was used. Urinary cyclic guanosine monophosphate (cGMP) was measured using competitive enzyme immunoassay (R&D systems, Minneapolis, Minnesota, USA).

For the power calculation, we considered a change in systolic BP of 5 mmHg to be significant and expected a standard deviation of 8 mmHg. With a two‐sided α‐level of 0.05 and power of 80% we needed 21 participants in each group. A 2:1 ratio was chosen to ensure power even if adherence to low sodium diet was poor. As our power calculation was based on systolic BP changes the results presented in this paper should be considered exploratory.

All statistical analyses were performed using STATA version 19.5 (StataCorp LLC, Texas, USA). Data were visually inspected for normality. In case of non‐normality, Wilcoxon ranksum test or Spearman's Rho were performed. In case of normality, student's t‐test either with equal or unequal variances was applied where appropriate. Multiple linear regression models were made with that is change in systolic BP as dependent variable and adjusted for baseline systolic BP and change in sodium excretion. These regression models only included data form the intervention group. P‐values < 0.05 were considered statistically significant. All outcome measures in this manuscript were prespecified, except that we for the investigation of the role of aldosterone performed a post‐hoc separation of the intervention group based on the observed increase in plasma aldosterone. We chose to separate them into the two thirds with the highest increase and the one third with the lowest increase, thus creating the arbitrary cut‐off between an aldosterone responders or aldosterone non‐responder of 15 pg/ml. We created our own cut‐off for two reasons; our intervention was not directly comparable to any other studies on sodium reduction, and no other studies had performed such separation in medicated hypertensive patients.

Approval was given by The Central Denmark Region Committees on Health Research Ethics (Journal number 1‐10‐72‐241‐21) and the study was registered at clinicaltrials.gov (NCT05165823). All participants signed informed consent prior to any study related activity and participants could withdraw their consent at any time. The study was conducted according to the Declaration of Helsinki 2013.

Study data was kept in REDCap (Research Electronic Data Capture) hosted at Aarhus University [22]. The REDCap randomization module was used and participants were randomized in blocks of 12. Data was extracted in anonymized form for data analysis.

## 讨论 / Discussion

Our main finding was the observed variation in physiologic responses to dietary sodium reduction in treated patients with essential hypertension. Based on the change in aldosterone they were divided into two groups. One group responded as expected after sodium reduction; RAAS was activated, ANP and BNP decreased, body weight was reduced and BP decreased. The other group, the aldosterone non‐responders, reacted opposite; besides the lack of RAAS‐activation they reacted with increases in natriuretic peptides, less loss of body weight, and no BP response.

The observed increases in both renin and aldosterone following sodium reduction are in accordance with our hypothesis and existing literature [3, 23, 24]. The additional separation of the intervention group into aldosterone responders and aldosterone non‐responders showed, however, that RAAS activation differed noticeably in the study population even with comparable reductions in urinary sodium excretion. The aldosterone responders react as expected as written above, and there was a clear correlation between increase in aldosterone and decrease in BP. We interpret this as the loss of body water and the changes in natriuretic peptides probably increase RAAS activity, and thus the increase in aldosterone reflects an intact and healthy hormonal system.

The aldosterone non‐responder group was defined by absent or low increase in plasma aldosterone, but were also characterized by higher baseline aldosterone levels, less renin activation, and no BP response as well as opposite changes in natriuretic peptides compared to the aldosterone responders and less decrease in body weight. This suggests an impaired volume response after sodium reduction in the aldosterone non‐responders, thus supporting the concept of “aldosterone dysfunction” or “aldosterone dysregulation” in these participants [9]. Our data suggest that patients on this spectrum with insufficiently suppressed aldosterone relative to sodium intake seem to have no effect on BP of lowering sodium intake. Interestingly, renin levels at baseline in the group of aldosterone non‐responders were not more suppressed than in the group of aldosterone responders, nor was ARR higher. This contrasts the findings by Hundemer et al. concluding that suppressed renin levels could serve as a measure of aldosterone dysregulation [25]. However, the phenotype of aldosterone dysregulation in their study differed from the ones in our study in three important aspects: 1) Some of the participants were normotensives, 2) Medication was discontinued, and 3) The aldosterone dysregulated group experienced the largest decrease in BP after changes in dietary sodium. That the aldosterone non‐responders (representing patients with aldosterone dysregulation) experience no change in BP nor loss of body weight in response to sodium reduction, were to some extend surprising findings, as previous studies of sodium reduction in patients with primary aldosteronism show large BP reductions as well as loss of body weight [26]. A reason for this difference might be that the hormonal dysregulation in aldosterone dysregulation goes beyond aldosterone and involves natriuretic peptides, renin and perhaps even more hormones. Hence, participants with aldosterone dysregulation might react differently to sodium reduction than patients with primary aldosteronism.

High aldosterone levels in combination with high sodium intake has previously been shown to cause endothelial dysfunction, increase arterial stiffness, cause inflammation and cardiac hypertrophy [27]. Thus, identifying these patients could be of great clinical interest, as studies show that these negative effects could be counteracted by a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists [28]. The participants with aldosterone dysregulation in our study were well‐treated on a maximum of three antihypertensive drugs, thus not fulfilling criteria for resistant hypertension as is commonly associated with high aldosterone levels.

Other studies too have observed changes in both renin and aldosterone when sodium intake is altered, however, whether ARR changes has only been investigated in relation to primary aldosteronism and results are inconsistent [12, 13, 14]. Our results show a tendency towards a decrease in ARR after sodium reduction, which is in line with the results of Baudrand et al. and Williams et al., though our findings were not statistically significant [12, 13]. Previous studies have demonstrated an association between high ARR and increased cardiovascular risk [11]. If sodium reduction can in fact improve ARR, it is conceivable that this may partly explain the link between sodium intake and cardiovascular health, independently of BP. Interestingly, there was no difference in baseline ARR nor difference in the change in ARR between aldosterone responders and non‐responders as would perhaps be expected. These data could be affected by the participants’ antihypertensive drugs or that our sample size is too small to show this variation. Aldosterone dysregulation is a new research topic and whether ARR or aldosterone levels alone (or perhaps renin levels as previously mentioned) should be used to identify patients with this condition has not yet been established, but could depend on whether the patient receives antihypertensive medication or not, as these are known to alter plasma levels of renin and aldosterone [29]. Our study suggests that in patients using antihypertensive drugs aldosterone might be the preferred parameter. Interestingly, we did not find significant differences in neither baseline aldosterone or changes in aldosterone between patients receiving ACE‐inhibitors or AT2‐receptor antagonists and patients not receiving them, though we did find a tendency towards lower aldosterone levels in patients receiving AT2‐receptor antagonists. This could suggest an element of “aldosterone escape” in some patients on RAAS‐inhibitors, specifically either aldosterone production without the involvement of ACE (i.e. via ACTH, ANP, acetylcholine or inflammatory processes), or angiotensin I accumulation causing ACE‐inhibitors to be insufficient [30, 31].

We found decreases in urine and plasma osmolality after sodium reduction. This is consistent with the measured reduction in AQP2/creatinine excretion ratio. Plasma levels of copeptin also decreased, though not significantly compared to the control group. In a crossover study, Tasevska et al. found, consistent with our results, that high salt intake increases plasma copeptin compared to levels at low salt intake [32]. We did not find the hypothesized relation between copeptin levels at baseline and BP response to sodium reduction. To our knowledge, no other studies have examined this, and thus further studies are needed to elaborate this possible connection.

Suppression of the vasopressin‐ AQP2‐pathway could be one of the driving factors of the loss of body weight in the low‐salt group [33]. When salt intake is reduced, plasma osmolality decreases, causing suppression of vasopressin and less expression of AQP2 leading to increased urinary water excretion and loss of body water and weight [34, 35]. We believe, that the lacking correlations in our data between copeptin, osmolality and body weight are due to the complex hormonal mechanisms (i.e. RAAS and natriuretic peptides) acting to maintain body water homeostasis, resulting in a non‐linear relationship between copeptin and those parameters.

We hypothesized that sodium reduction would lead to decreases in pro‐ANP and BNP [36]. However, the changes only occurred when the intervention group was separated according to aldosterone response, which shows that the two groups differ in their ability to volume regulate. The aldosterone responders reacted as expected with decreases in both natriuretic peptides, urinary cGMP (an indicator of renal ANP activity) and body weight [37, 38]. On the contrary, the non‐responders showed no ability to regulate natriuretic hormone levels according to sodium intake, urinary cGMP did not change and body weight hardly decreased. ANP is known to suppress the release of aldosterone, and the lack of ANP response could contribute to the missing aldosterone response. More studies on the interconnection of these hormone systems are needed to elaborate this dysregulation. It is important to note that these results are based on small groups and even though the p‐values are very low, the statistical uncertainty remains present.

Another noteworthy finding was that they key determinant of BP reduction was neither baseline sodium excretion nor the magnitude of change, but rather the absolute level of sodium excretion at follow‐up. This confirms that recommendations of a specific upper limit to sodium intake is relevant.

The largest limitation to our study is the limited sample size—especially with the additional separation of the intervention group. This reduces the statistical power as well as the generalizability of our findings. Urinary sodium excretion was only measured at baseline and follow‐up. Thus, we cannot be sure participants complied during the entire study period and how this might have affected outcomes.

All outcomes presented in this paper were secondary endpoints which increases the risk of type 2 errors. Interpretation of baseline plasma renin and aldosterone levels are complicated by the fact that patients continued their medications, however discontinuing them would have moved the study further away from the clinical every day, we wanted to investigate.

The separation of the intervention group into aldosterone responders and non‐responders was not pre‐defined and performing such separation results in two non‐randomized groups. This introduces selection bias and these results should be seen as observational comparisons of how decreased sodium intake affects the two groups differently. Our results thus mainly describe associations and not necessarily causality. The two groups were comparable in most baseline characteristics, though more aldosterone non‐responders received diuretics (68.8 % vs 31.2 %) This might interfere significantly with results as diuretics lead to lower blood volume and decrease in BP which activates RAAS and natriuretic peptides. Diuretics might also alter total body water and thus result in less decrease in body weight after sodium reduction in those receiving diuretics. Thus, use of medication could be a confounding factor.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In conclusion, this post hoc analysis suggests that a group of patients with hypertension present with higher plasma aldosterone levels, blunted aldosterone response and reduced BP response to sodium reduction. This was partly mediated by an impaired volume response due to lack of decline in natriuretic peptide levels. These findings support the notion of “aldosterone dysfunction” as an important clinical target, but also suggests that inappropriately high aldosterone might not cover the entire physiological dysregulation in these patients. More and larger studies investigating aldosterone dysregulation in relation to sodium reduction are needed before clinical application.
