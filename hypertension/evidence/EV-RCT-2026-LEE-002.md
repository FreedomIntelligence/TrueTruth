## English Abstract

This study aimed to evaluate the safety, including blood pressure changes, and medication compliance associated with a generic prolonged-release formulation of mirabegron (Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg) in Korean adults with overactive bladder (OAB) through a large-scale, real-world, multicenter, retrospective observational study.

Patients with OAB who were prescribed Selebeta® PR once daily for at least 3 months between July 2020 and July 2021 from 95 medical institutions in Korea were included. The primary endpoint was the proportion of patients with an increase in systolic blood pressure (SBP) of ≥10 mmHg after 3 months of treatment. Secondary endpoints included additional thresholds of blood pressure elevation, changes in SBP and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) and pulse rate over time, improvement in OAB symptom, incidence of adverse events, and medication compliance.

A total of 2,091 patients were enrolled in the study. After 3 months of treatment, 8.8% of the patients had an SBP increase of ≥10 mmHg; 3.7%, ≥15 mmHg; and 2.3%, ≥20 mmHg. DBP increased by ≥5 mmHg in 16.8% and by ≥10 mmHg in 6.7% of patients. OAB symptoms also improved significantly in the subgroup with available OAB symptom score data. The overall incidence of adverse events was 2.1%, mostly mild, and mean medication coverage was 73.6%.

This study demonstrated that Selebeta® PR is an effective and well-tolerated treatment for adults with OAB, with no clinically meaningful increase in blood pressure and a low incidence of adverse events.

## 背景 / Background

Overactive bladder (OAB) is a prevalent urological condition characterized by urinary urgency with or without urgency incontinence and is typically accompanied by frequency and nocturia [1]. The condition affects approximately 12.2% of the adult population in Korea, which is comparable to the 11.8% prevalence reported in European countries and Canada [23]. Lower urinary tract symptoms, including OAB, significantly affects patients' quality of life across multiple domains, including social activities and interpersonal relationships, with studies suggesting that its detrimental effects exceed those of diabetes mellitus [45].

Current pharmacological management of OAB primarily involves antimuscarinic agents and β3-adrenergic receptor agonists [6]. While antimuscarinics have long been the mainstay treatment, they are limited by anticholinergic side effects, including dry mouth, constipation, and blurred vision, which often lead to treatment discontinuation [7]. The efficacy of mirabegron, the first approved β3-adrenergic receptor agonist, is comparable to that of antimuscarinic agents, with fewer adverse anticholinergic effects, suggesting that it is an important therapeutic alternative [8].

Despite its favorable tolerability profile, mirabegron provides warnings regarding potential blood pressure elevation. However, discrepancies exist between controlled clinical trial data and real-world experience. Postmarketing surveillance in Japan revealed that among 1,138 patients, including 111 with hypertension and 173 with elevated blood pressure at baseline, fewer than three adverse drug reactions related to blood pressure elevation were reported [9]. This finding suggests that real-world experience in noninterventional settings may differ significantly from that in controlled clinical trials, particularly in primary care environments. In line with these observations, recent Korean real-world studies have further reaffirmed the safety and therapeutic effectiveness of generic mirabegron formulations in routine clinical practice [10].

This observational study aimed to retrospectively evaluate blood pressure changes and other safety parameters in OAB patients treated with Selebeta® PR (a prolonged-release generic formulation of mirabegron 50 mg, equivalent to Betmiga® [Astellas Pharma Inc.], manufactured by Chong Kun Dang Pharmaceutical Corp.) for at least 3 months in routine clinical practice. Additionally, the study sought to assess medication compliance and identify factors potentially influencing blood pressure changes and adverse events in these patients. These insights are crucial for establishing a comprehensive safety profile that reflects real-world clinical practice.

## 方法 / Methods

This noninterventional, multicenter, retrospective observational study (post-marketing observational of patient study) was conducted across 95 medical institutions in South Korea between July 2020 and July 2021. The study protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board (approval number: P01-202112-21-015, version 1.2, November 17, 2021) and was performed in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. The written informed consent was obtained from all patients.

Eligible participants were adults (≥19 years) who had received 50 mg mirabegron prolonged-release tablets (Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg) continuously for at least 3 months for the treatment of OAB symptoms. Participants were required to have blood pressure measurements available before and after treatment initiation. Pretreatment blood pressure measurements were collected within 30 days before treatment initiation, while post-treatment measurements were collected during the treatment period or within 14 days after treatment completion.

Exclusion criteria included hypersensitivity to the active or other ingredients of mirabegron, uncontrolled severe hypertension (systolic blood pressure [SBP] ≥180 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure [DBP] ≥110 mmHg) at the most recent prescription date, serious cardiovascular disease. Serious cardiovascular disease was defined as conditions in which mirabegron might be considered unsafe in routine clinical practice, including recent myocardial infarction, unstable angina, decompensated heart failure, clinically significant arrhythmias requiring treatment, or other unstable cardiovascular conditions. All eligible patients were prescribed Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg once daily, in accordance with standard clinical practice.

Patient data were retrospectively collected from medical records using electronic case report forms. The collected information included demographic characteristics (age, sex, smoking and drinking history), medical history of OAB and other conditions, details of Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg administration, concomitant medications, physical measurements, vital signs, and adverse events. Blood pressure values were obtained from routine outpatient records and included measurements performed prior to treatment initiation (within 30 days before the first prescription) and during the treatment period; if no in-treatment data were available, measurements taken within 14 days after treatment completion were used. Reported time intervals (1, 2, and 3 months) represent categorization of naturally occurring clinical measurements based on actual visit dates, rather than protocol-defined follow-up assessments. The overactive bladder symptom score (OABSS) was assessed, with total scores categorized as mild (≤5 points), moderate (6–11 points), or severe (≥12 points).

Patients were classified into three analysis sets for statistical evaluation. The safety analysis set included all eligible patients who had received mirabegron for at least 3 consecutive months. The primary efficacy set comprised patients from the safety population whose blood pressure measurements were available before and after treatment and was used to evaluate the primary endpoint. The secondary efficacy set included those with complete OABSS data at both time points and was used for secondary efficacy analyses related to symptom improvement.

The primary endpoint was the proportion of patients with an increase in SBP ≥10 mmHg after 3 months of treatment compared with baseline. Secondary endpoints included the proportion of patients with SBP increases ≥15 mmHg and ≥20 mmHg, DBP increases ≥5 mmHg and ≥10 mmHg, changes in SBP/DBP and pulse rate at 3 months, and changes in OABSS after 3 months of treatment.

Additional analyses were conducted to examine blood pressure changes according to hypertension status, baseline blood pressure levels, and concomitant medications (α-blockers, antimuscarinic agents, and antihypertensive drugs). Safety assessments included the incidence of adverse events and their relationship to mirabegron. Medication compliance was calculated as the proportion of days covered (PDC) by dividing the total number of prescription days by the observation period and multiplying by 100%.

The sample size was calculated on the basis of the primary endpoint. Referencing a previous study comparing mirabegron and placebo, we assumed that the proportion of patients with SBP increases ≥10 mmHg would be 13.5% in the mirabegron group compared with 11.3% in the placebo group [11]. With an alpha of 0.05 and a power of 0.90, a target enrollment of 2,520 patients was determined (including 100 patients to account for potential data errors).

Statistical analyses were performed using SAS® version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc.). Patients were categorized into a safety analysis set (all patients who received mirabegron for at least 3 months) and an efficacy analysis set (patients in the safety set with available OABSS data before and after treatment). For continuous variables, paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests were used to compare pre- and post-treatment values on the basis of normality of distribution. For categorical variables, chi-square tests or Fisher’s exact tests were performed. McNemar’s test was used to compare blood pressure changes according to baseline blood pressure categories. Logistic regression analysis was conducted to assess factors influencing blood pressure changes, particularly concerning concomitant medications. A complete-case analysis was used for logistic regression, such that only patients with available data for each covariate were included in the respective model. Descriptive statistics included means, standard deviations, medians, minimums, and maximums for continuous variables and frequencies and percentages for categorical variables. All the statistical tests were two-sided, with a significance level of 0.05.

## 结果 / Results

A total of 2,091 patients were included in this study, of whom 2,090 were included in the safety analysis set. Among these patients, 1,609 were included in the primary efficacy set, and 187 were included in the secondary efficacy set. An overview of the patient distribution is presented in Fig. 1.

The mean age was 67.5±12.4 years, and 1,487 patients (71.1%) were male. Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics are summarized in Table 1. Among the participants, 479 (22.9%) had a history of prior pharmacological treatment for OAB. The most frequently prescribed agent was mirabegron (n=365, 76.2%), followed by solifenacin (n=65, 13.6%), imidafenacin (n=30, 6.3%), and propiverine (n=16, 3.3%). Hypertension and diabetes mellitus were present in 22.8% and 7.3% of the patients, respectively. The mean treatment duration was 159.1±96.6 days. Within the safety analysis set, 436 patients (20.9%) discontinued the study drug. The most commonly reported reasons for discontinuation were physician decision (17.7%), patient request (1.4%), adverse events (0.3%), and perceived lack of efficacy (0.1%).

Of the 899 patients (55.9%) who completed blood pressure measurements at the 3-month follow-up, 8.8% of the patients (142/1,609) experienced an increase in the SBP of ≥10 mmHg from baseline. Secondary thresholds were even lower: 3.7% of patients had an increase of ≥15 mmHg, and 2.3% had an increase of ≥20 mmHg. DBP increases were observed in 16.8% of patients for ≥5 mmHg and in 6.7% for ≥10 mmHg. These proportions of patients with clinically significant blood pressure elevations are summarized in Fig. 2. The mean SBP significantly decreased by 1.48 mmHg, from 128.29±14.34 mmHg at baseline to 126.81±13.91 mmHg (p<0.001). The mean DBP also showed a statistically significant reduction of 0.80 mmHg, decreasing from 79.10±9.47 mmHg at baseline to 78.30±9.38 mmHg at 3 months (p=0.015). The mean changes in SBP and DBP from baseline to 3 months are presented in Fig. 3. To identify factors associated with an increase in the SBP ≥10 mmHg, a logistic regression analysis was performed. Among the various clinical and medication-related factors, only age was significantly associated with increased SBP. The odds ratio was 1.031 (95% CI 1.014–1.048, p<0.001), indicating a modest but statistically significant increase in risk with advancing age (Table 2). Other variables, including sex; smoking status; comorbidities (e.g., diabetes mellitus and benign prostatic hyperplasia); and concomitant use ofα-blockers, antimuscarinics, or antihypertensive agents, were not significantly associated. To support the interpretation of these findings, detailed counts and proportions of SBP elevations for each clinical variable included in the regression model have been summarized in the Supplementary Table 1.

Changes in OAB symptom scores were assessed in a subset of 187 patients. After 3 months, the total OABSS score significantly improved from 5.63±2.47 at baseline to 3.47±2.41 (p<0.001), indicating clinically meaningful improvement in OAB symptoms, including urgency, frequency, and nocturia. Medication compliance, as measured by the PDC, was 73.6%±30.1%. No significant difference was observed in the incidence of adverse events between patients whose compliance was less than 80% and those whose compliance was 80% or greater (p>0.05).

In the safety analysis set, 43 adverse events (2.1%) were reported. Among these events, 41 (2.0%) were mild in severity, and 2 (0.1%) were moderate. Two events did not occur at the time of data collection. Thirteen events (0.6%) were considered related to the study drug. The most common management approach was continuation of treatment without dose adjustment (1.7%), while treatment discontinuation occurred in 7 patients (0.3%). The most frequently reported events were gastrointestinal disorders (1.1%), followed by infections and infestations (0.2%), nervous system disorders (0.2%), skin and subcutaneous tissue disorders (0.2%), and vascular disorders (0.2%).

## 讨论 / Discussion

This large-scale, real-world, multicenter observational study evaluated the safety, clinical utility, and medication compliance of Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg, a generic formulation of Betmiga®, in 2,090 patients with OAB across 95 centers in Korea. Among the 1,609 patients who received Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg for at least 3 months and completed the primary efficacy assessment, 8.8% experienced an SBP increase of ≥10 mmHg compared with baseline, which was lower than the 11.3% previously reported, although the difference was not statistically significant [11]. However, the proportion of patients with SBP increases ≥15 mmHg was lower in this study, whereas the proportions of patients with DBP increases ≥5 mmHg and ≥10 mmHg were relatively high. Among 187 patients with available pre- and post-treatment OABSS data, the total score decreased after 3 months of treatment, and the proportions of patients reporting urgency incontinence and increased voiding frequency also decreased, indicating a potential trend toward symptom improvement among patients with available OABSS data. Although some previous studies have raised concerns regarding the cardiovascular safety of β3-adrenergic receptor agonists [12], the present findings suggest that Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg may provide symptom relief in the subset of patients with available OABSS data and can be used with an acceptable safety margin in terms of blood pressure elevation.

The mean age of the patients in this study was 67.5 years, indicating a predominantly elderly population. Given the rapid aging of the Korean population, evaluating the safety of pharmacological treatments in older adults and those with comorbidities is of increasing clinical importance. Unlike randomized controlled trials, which typically involve more selective populations [13], this study included a substantial proportion of patients with comorbid conditions such as hypertension and diabetes mellitus. Notably, in a subgroup of 197 patients with hypertension, a significant reduction in SBP was observed, providing important evidence supporting the safe use of Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg in older adults in Korea. Additionally, approximately 40% of the patients in the study were taking concomitant medications, and no significant increases in blood pressure were noted even when Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg was used in combination with α-blockers, antimuscarinic agents, or antihypertensive drugs. These findings suggest that Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg maintains a favorable safety profile even in real-world polypharmacy settings, which is a key clinical advantage for elderly patients who often require multiple medications.

Initial concerns regarding the potential of mirabegron to induce hypertension originated from a 12-month randomized controlled trial conducted by Chapple et al. [12], in which the SBP increased by an average of 0.2 mmHg and the incidence of hypertension-related adverse events was slightly greater in the mirabegron group (11.0%) than in the tolterodine group (10.6%). However, subsequent studies have consistently reported that mirabegron does not cause clinically significant increases in blood pressure. The PILLAR study, a phase IV, placebo-controlled trial in patients aged ≥65 years, revealed no significant differences in blood pressure changes between the mirabegron and placebo groups. In fact, self-measured SBP decreased by an average of 2.95 mmHg in the group receiving 50 mg mirabegron. The number of hypertension-related adverse events was also comparable, with five cases in the mirabegron group and two in the placebo group [11]. Notably, more than 50% of the patients in the PILLAR study had preexisting hypertension, and the composition of the elderly population was similar to that in our study. Although randomized controlled trials such as the PILLAR study and real-world retrospective analyses differ fundamentally in their study design and therefore cannot be directly compared, the PILLAR data provide valuable contextual information that helps interpret and contextualize the safety outcomes observed in our cohort. In this context, the current findings—demonstrating no clinically meaningful changes in blood pressure and a low incidence of hypertension-related adverse events—provide real-world evidence that complements and reinforces the safety profile established in previous randomized controlled trials.

Previous studies, including multiple randomized controlled trials, have reported significant improvement in OAB symptoms and a very low incidence of adverse events with mirabegron monotherapy [141516]. In contrast, antimuscarinic agents—another class of OAB medications with a different mechanism of action—are frequently associated with adverse effects such as xerostomia and constipation, which have been cited as major reasons for poor long-term compliance [161718]. Korean data have similarly reported low persistence and adherence with antimuscarinic therapy, highlighting a limitation in the long-term management of OAB [19]. In the present study, the overall incidence of adverse events with Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg was only 2.1%, with gastrointestinal disorders being the most commonly reported. Most events were mild in severity. One serious adverse event (0.1%) was reported in a patient diagnosed with polydipsia, leading to discontinuation of the medication. This low rate of adverse events may support the use of mirabegron as a long-term therapy and contribute to improved compliance, particularly compared with antimuscarinic agents. In fact, treatment discontinuation due to adverse events occurred in only 0.3% of patients. Furthermore, this study provides meaningful data on medication compliance. Using a PDC-based analysis, real-world compliance over at least 3 months was quantified, offering valuable insights for future updates to clinical practice guidelines or reimbursement policies in Korea.

This study was conducted as a multicenter, retrospective observational study to reflect real-world clinical practice; however, several limitations should be acknowledged. First, owing to the noninterventional nature of the study, a causal relationship between mirabegron administration and changes in blood pressure could not be established, and potential confounding factors such as comorbidities or concomitant medications could not be fully controlled. Second, because blood pressure values were extracted from routine clinical records rather than obtained under standardized measurement conditions, potential variability related to measurement techniques, timing, and clinical settings may exist, and some missing data may have been included. Moreover, as this study relied on multicenter retrospective real-world data collected in routine practice, blood pressure measurement devices and procedures could not be fully standardized across participating institutions, introducing possible heterogeneity in measurement environments that should be considered when interpreting the results. Third, the observation period was limited to 3 months, which may not be sufficient to assess long-term cardiovascular safety, particularly in older patients or those with underlying cardiovascular conditions. Fourth, only 8.9% of the total study population had complete OABSS data available for symptom improvement analysis, limiting the generalizability of efficacy findings to the broader cohort. Fifth, key lifestyle variables such as smoking and alcohol consumption had high rates of missing data, necessitating complete-case analysis in the regression models; therefore, the associations observed for these variables should be interpreted with caution. Sixth, the number of patients using antihypertensive medication was extremely small, which resulted in unstable regression estimates due to quasi-complete separation. Accordingly, the odds ratio for antihypertensive use should be considered uninterpretable. Seventh, medication compliance was assessed on the basis of the PDC, an indirect measure derived from prescription records, which may not fully reflect actual medication-taking behavior. Finally, among patients who discontinued treatment, a substantial proportion were categorized as having stopped treatment on the basis of “physician decision,” but the specific clinical reasons were not consistently documented, making it difficult to determine whether discontinuation was due to adverse effects or other considerations. These limitations should be accounted for when the findings are interpreted and compared with results from long-term randomized controlled trials.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In this large-scale, real-world, multicenter observational study, Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg was generally well tolerated and not associated with clinically meaningful increases in blood pressure. Nevertheless, a subset of patients-particularly older individuals-experienced blood pressure elevations, underscoring the need for monitoring in clinical practice. Improvements in OAB symptoms were observed, but efficacy findings were limited by incomplete data. Overall, Selebeta® PR Tab. 50 mg appears to be a reasonable treatment option for adults with OAB, including elderly patients and those with comorbidities, provided that blood pressure is carefully monitored. in routine practice, particularly in aging populations with complex comorbidities.
