## English Abstract

Hypertension is a well-established risk factor for myocardial ischemia, which may often remain asymptomatic. This study aims to evaluate the effects of diltiazem on blood pressure and myocardial ischemia in patients with grade I–II primary hypertension. Between January 2018 and April 2022, 160 patients diagnosed with mild to moderate primary hypertension were enrolled. Patients were assigned to 1 of 4 treatment groups: benazepril monotherapy group (benazepril 10 mg/day, n = 44); irbesartan monotherapy group (irbesartan 150 mg/day, n = 45); diltiazem monotherapy group (diltiazem hydrochloride sustained-release tablets 90 mg/day, n = 36); and combination therapy group (diltiazem hydrochloride sustained-release tablets 90 mg/day plus either benazepril 10 mg/day or irbesartan 150 mg/day, n = 35). Systolic and diastolic blood pressure were recorded at baseline and post-treatment. Myocardial ischemia was assessed using 24-hour Holter electrocardiogram monitoring, and the total ischemia burden (TIB) was calculated as the cumulative duration of ST-segment depression. All treatment groups demonstrated statistically significant reductions in both systolic and diastolic blood pressure following intervention (P < .05). The combination therapy group exhibited a significantly greater reduction in diastolic blood pressure compared to the diltiazem monotherapy group (P < .05). TIB significantly decreased in the diltiazem monotherapy, benazepril monotherapy, and combination therapy groups (P < .05), whereas the reduction observed in the irbesartan monotherapy group was not statistically significant. Additionally, the benazepril monotherapy group demonstrated a significantly greater reduction in TIB compared to irbesartan (P < .05). Diltiazem effectively reduced both systolic and diastolic blood pressure and demonstrated anti-ischemic properties. Combination therapy with diltiazem and other antihypertensive agents, particularly benazepril, may offer additional therapeutic benefits. These findings support the potential role of diltiazem in the management of patients with mild to moderate hypertension and subclinical myocardial ischemia.

## 背景 / Background

Coronary spastic angina is characterized by transient myocardial ischemia resulting from coronary artery spasms, often occurring in the absence of significant atherosclerotic disease. This condition is associated not only with episodic angina but also with an elevated risk of myocardial infarction and malignant ventricular arrhythmias, which may lead to sudden cardiac death.[1–5]

Diltiazem, a calcium channel blocker, has demonstrated efficacy in dilating epicardial coronary arteries and preventing coronary artery vasospasm. These pharmacologic effects contribute to improved coronary microcirculation and enhanced exercise tolerance. Diltiazem has also been associated with a reduction in angina frequency and a broad range of cardiovascular risk factors, while maintaining a favorable safety profile.[6]

In the present study, a cohort of 160 patients diagnosed with primary hypertension were enrolled to evaluate the effects of diltiazem on blood pressure regulation and myocardial ischemia in this population.

## 方法 / Methods

Patients diagnosed with primary hypertension who attended follow-up visits at Zhangzhou Hospital, affiliated with Fujian Medical University, between January 2018 and April 2022, were enrolled in this study.

Inclusion criteria comprised patients aged between 18 and 80 years, irrespective of sex, who were using fewer than 2 antihypertensive medications and maintained blood pressure levels below 160/95 mm Hg (1 mm Hg = 0.133 kPa). Blood pressure measurements were obtained in accordance with the 2005 Chinese Guidelines for the Management of Hypertension. A diagnosis of hypertension was confirmed when systolic blood pressure (SBP) was ≥140 mm Hg and/or diastolic blood pressure (DBP) was ≥90 mm Hg, based on 3 separate days, each satisfying the defined diagnostic thresholds.[7]

Patients were excluded from the study based on the following conditions:

(1) Blood pressure ≥ 180/110 mm Hg, or persistent blood pressure ≥ 160/95 mm Hg despite the use of 3 antihypertensive agents.

(2) Diagnosis of atrial fibrillation or other severe arrhythmia confirmed by a 12-lead conventional electrocardiogram (ECG); presence of secondary ST-T segment abnormalities attributable to complete bundle branch block or pre-excitation syndrome observed on 24-hour ambulatory ECG (Holter monitoring); or ST-T changes attributed to electrolyte imbalances or digitalis use.

(3) Documented dysfunction of the thyroid, liver, kidney, or pancreas.

(4) Presence of chronic wasting conditions or malignancies.

(5) History of myocardial infarction or cerebrovascular events within the preceding 6 months.

(6) Abnormal laboratory findings, including alanine aminotransferase or aspartate aminotransferase levels > 150 U/L (3 times the upper limit of normal), or creatinine > 350 μmol/L).

Demographic and clinical baseline data were systematically collected for all participants. These included age, sex, height, weight, smoking history (defined as consumption of more than 5 cigarettes per day for a duration exceeding 5 consecutive years), alcohol consumption history (defined as intake of at least 50 g of liquor per day for over 5 consecutive years), family history of hypertension, and presence of chronic comorbidities.

Blood pressure measurements were performed by a single trained examiner to ensure consistency, with 3 readings obtained at each time point and averaged for comparison. All patients underwent cardiac color Doppler ultrasonography and 24-hour ambulatory electrocardiogram (Holter ECG) monitoring.

Initial treatment was selected based on clinical indications, using a 1st-line antihypertensive agent. A total of 160 patients who met the inclusion criteria and completed the required assessments were enrolled. Patients were assigned to 1 of 4 treatment groups: benazepril monotherapy group (benazepril 10 mg/day, n = 44); irbesartan monotherapy group (irbesartan 150 mg/day, n = 45); diltiazem monotherapy group (diltiazem hydrochloride sustained-release tablets 90 mg/day, n = 36); and combination therapy group (diltiazem hydrochloride sustained-release tablets 90 mg/day plus either benazepril 10 mg/day or irbesartan 150 mg/day, n = 35).

The dosage selection for this study was based on 2023 Guideline for the Management of Hypertension in China[8] and approved drug labeling information.

For diltiazem sustained-release tablets, the dose of 90 mg/day was selected as the starting dose. According to the guideline, the recommended daily dose of diltiazem sustained-release tablets ranges from 90 to 360 mg, administered once or twice daily.

For benazepril, the dose of 10 mg/day represents the standard initial dose recommended for patients not receiving diuretics. The guideline recommends a daily dose of 5–40 mg. The drug labeling for benazepril hydrochloride tablets (brand name: Luotingxin, Beijing Novartis Pharmaceuticals Co., Ltd.) specifies that, for patients not taking diuretics, the recommended initial dose is 10 mg orally once daily.

For irbesartan, the dose of 150 mg/day is the recommended initial and maintenance dose. The guideline recommends 150 to 300 mg once daily. The prescribing information for irbesartan tablets (brand name: Aprovel, Guangzhou Tonghui Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd.) states that, the recommended initial and maintenance dose is 150 mg once daily and 150 mg once daily provides better 24-hour blood pressure control than 75 mg.

In cases where blood pressure remained ≥160/95 mm Hg after 2 weeks of treatment, additional antihypertensive drugs were introduced. Diuretics were the preferred second-line agents, while β-adrenergic receptor blockers were prescribed for patients with inadequate heart rate control. The therapeutic goal was to achieve and maintain blood pressure levels at or below 140/90 mm Hg (SBP/DBP).

All patients underwent follow-up evaluations at 1-year post-treatment initiation. Follow-up assessments included complete blood, lipid profile, hepatic and renal function tests, fibrinogen levels, cardiac color ultrasonography, and repeat 24-hour Holter ECG monitoring.

All participants adhered to a light dietary regimen for 3 consecutive days prior to sample collection, during which the use of vasoactive medications was suspended. After the dietary adjustment period, venous blood samples were collected after a 12-hour overnight fast. These blood samples were utilized to perform routine hematological analysis, lipid profiling, hepatic and renal function evaluation, and fibrinogen measurement.

All biochemical analyses were performed using the HITACHI 7170A automatic biochemical analyzer in the clinical biochemistry laboratory of the study institution, ensuring consistency and reliability of results.

Following enrollment, all participants underwent Holter ECG monitoring using the DIM DMS300-4A device in conjunction with Cardioscan Holter ECG heart rate variability analysis software. Continuous ECG recordings were obtained via CM7, CM5, and MAVF leads. All recordings were reviewed by trained personnel to identify ST-segment changes and arrhythmias, with careful exclusion of artifacts to ensure diagnostic accuracy.

Automated software was used to quantify heart rate variability parameters and to detect supraventricular and ventricular arrhythmias. Myocardial ischemia was diagnosed based on the 3 “1” criteria: ST-segment depression of ≥1 mm from the isoelectric baseline; duration of ST-segment depression exceeding 1 minute; and an interval of more than 1 minute between 2 ischemic episodes.

Total ischemia burden (TIB) was defined as the product of the magnitude of ST-segment depression and the cumulative duration of both symptomatic and asymptomatic myocardial ischemic episodes recorded during the 24-hour monitoring period.

In this study, ST-segment episode plots were scanned at a uniform magnification using an EPON scanner. TIB was calculated using the Image-Pro Plus 6.0, (IPP 6.0) image analysis software. Following scanning, the 24-hour ST-segment episode plots were extracted using the Meitu application. Image calibration was performed using word processing software. The calibration parameters were defined as follows: 1 hour on the Holter ECG corresponded to 1 cm along the horizontal axis and 0.1 mV corresponded to 0.25 cm along the vertical axis. All images were standardized according to these calibration settings (Fig. 1A).

(A) ST episode graph after calibration, scale: 1 hour = 1 cm, 0.1 mv = 0.25 cm; (B) ST-segment depression curves generated by IPP 6.0 software, with automated calculation of the area under the curve. IPP 6.0 = Image-Pro Plus 6.0.

ST-segment depression curves on the calibrated images were plotted using IPP 6.0 software. The area under each curve was measured and interpreted as the extent of myocardial ischemia. The cumulative area of ST-segment depression over the 24-hour monitoring period was defined as the TIB (Fig. 1B).

Participant data were compiled into a structured database using Microsoft Office Excel 2021. Statistical analyses were conducted using the Chinese version of the Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS), version 28.0.

For continuous variables conforming to a normal distribution, comparisons of pre- and post-treatment values within the same group were performed using paired t tests. Repeated measures analysis of variance was applied to assess differences across multiple time points between groups, while 1-way analysis of variance was used for baseline comparisons among groups.

For variables exhibiting a non-normal distribution, data are expressed as median with interquartile ranges. Paired-samples nonparametric tests were employed to assess within-group changes. Given the non-normal distribution of TIB, a log10 transformation was applied prior to statistical testing, and the results were reported as mean ± standard deviation.

Linear correlation analysis was used to evaluate the relationship between 2 continuous variables. Multivariate stepwise regression analysis was conducted to identify factors independently associated with the outcomes. A P value < .05 was considered statistically significant. To assess clinical significance, Cohen d-effect sizes were calculated for between-group comparisons. Effect size interpretation: d ≤ 0.2 indicates a small effect, 0.2 < d ≤ 0.8 a medium effect, and d > 0.8 a large effect.

## 讨论 / Discussion

In this study, 160 patients with mild to moderate primary hypertension were treated with diltiazem, benazepril, irbesartan, or a combination of these agents. Diltiazem was associated with significant reductions in blood pressure and also demonstrated a notable anti-myocardial ischemia effect, as reflected by reductions in TIB.

These findings are consistent with previous studies. Hu et al reported that diltiazem significantly reduced TIB in a cohort of 62 patients with a history of myocardial infarction, as measured by 24-hour Holter ECG monitoring.[9] The present findings are consistent with these observations, showing a measurable decrease in TIB following diltiazem treatment.

The anti-ischemic properties of diltiazem may be mediated through several pathophysiological mechanisms. Myocardial ischemia disrupts cellular energy metabolism, leading to dysfunction of membrane-bound sodium and calcium pumps. This dysfunction promotes extracellular calcium influx and mitochondrial calcium accumulation, resulting in intracellular calcium overload. The subsequent increase in mitochondrial energy demand to export excess calcium further accelerates ATP consumption and exacerbates energy depletion. As a calcium channel blocker, diltiazem alleviates calcium influx, thereby preventing overload, reducing ATP degradation, and facilitating the removal of intracellular calcium. These actions help maintain mitochondrial function and protect myocardial structure and contractility.

Additionally, diltiazem has been shown to inhibit matrix metalloproteinase activity, thereby reducing calcium overload and limiting myocardial fibrosis.[10] By suppressing the activity of P38 mitogen-activated protein kinase and c-Jun N-terminal kinase, diltiazem inhibits the synthesis of collagen types I and III, contributing to the reversal of ventricular remodeling and further enhancing its anti- ischemic properties.[11]

In a study by Ishida et al, coronary artery spasm was induced in a murine model through intravenous administration of ergotamine. Mice treated with diltiazem hydrochloride exhibited significant inhibition of ST-segment elevation and PQ interval prolongation on surface ECG, electrophysiological changes typically associated with coronary vasospasm.

Studies have indicated that increased activity of phospholipase C contributes to the pathogenesis of coronary artery spasm, potentially through enhanced phosphorylation of the Cav1.2 calcium channel and upregulation of protein kinase C activity. Diltiazem hydrochloride has been shown to inhibit both Cav1.2 phosphorylation and protein kinase C activation, suggesting that its anti-myocardial ischemia effect may, in part, be mediated through modulation of these signaling pathways.[12]

Coronary spastic angina may trigger episodes of angina pectoris even in the absence of atherosclerosis and is associated with an increased risk of myocardial infarction, malignant ventricular arrhythmias, and sudden cardiac death.[1–5] Diltiazem has been shown to induce vasodilation of epicardial coronary arteries and prevent epicardial coronary spasm. Additionally, it improves coronary microcirculation, enhances exercise tolerance, reduces the frequency of angina episodes, mitigates various cardiovascular risks, and maintains a favorable safety profile.

In a clinical study conducted by Peng et al, diltiazem was found to significantly enhance exercise tolerance in individuals with stable coronary artery diseases and hypertension when compared with angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs), angiotensin receptor blockers, and β-blockers.[6] Furthermore, diltiazem has demonstrated efficacy in improving coronary artery slow flow and non-reflow phenomena, further supporting its expanding role in the management of coronary artery disease and arrhythmias.[1]

Evidence also supports the use of diltiazem in reducing the frequency of angina attacks, maintaining sinus rhythm, and enhancing exercise tolerance among individuals with atrial fibrillation.[13,14]

Coronary microcirculatory function is modulated by multiple physiological factors, including heart rate, systemic blood pressure, myocardial contractility, and myocardial mass. In the context of hypertension, these regulatory mechanisms may become impaired, contributing to coronary microvascular dysfunction. According to the 2013 European Society of Cardiology guidelines, non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, such as diltiazem, and β-blockers are recommended as first-line pharmacologic interventions for the management of coronary heart disease.

Diltiazem has demonstrated efficacy in reducing early morning surges in blood pressure and heart rate, as well as in lowering the incidence of cardiovascular events. Its mechanism of action involves selective inhibition of calcium influx through L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle, particularly within the microvasculature. This leads to vasodilation and improved myocardial perfusion. These vasodilatory and antihypertensive effects appear to be less pronounced in agents targeting the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS).

In a comparative study conducted by Kook et al, both nebivolol and diltiazem were effective in reducing coronary artery spasm; however, diltiazem demonstrated superior efficacy.[15] These findings suggest that diltiazem may offer cardioprotective benefits in individuals with hypertension.

Additional preclinical data from Dawei et al demonstrated that diltiazem significantly diminished cellular damage induced by calcium overload following myocardial injury, thereby providing a protective effect on injured myocardial tissue.[16] In the present study, patients with preexisting myocardial ischemia demonstrated significant improvement in myocardial ischemia after treatment with diltiazem, benazepril, irbesartan, and combination therapy. The therapeutic efficacy was statistically significant and comparable among all treatment groups.

In contrast, among patients without preexisting myocardial ischemia, administration of diltiazem, benazepril, and combination therapy was associated with prevention of new-onset myocardial ischemia. However, patients receiving irbesartan exhibited a progression in total TIB compared to baseline values. These findings indicate that diltiazem, benazepril, and their combination may delay the progression of myocardial ischemia and exert cardioprotective effects, whereas irbesartan did not demonstrate a preventive benefit in this context. It should be noted that the sample size in this study was relatively limited, warranting the need for further validation in larger clinical populations.

In the present study, treatment with diltiazem, benazepril, irbesartan, and combination therapy were all associated with reductions in total TIB. The magnitude of TIB reduction observed in the diltiazem group was comparable to that in the benazepril and combination therapy groups, with no statistically significant differences detected among these 3 groups. However, benazepril demonstrated significantly greater efficacy than irbesartan in reducing the total TIB.

These findings are consistent with data from a meta-analysis conducted by Vark et al, which evaluated the impact of RAAS inhibitors on mortality in individuals with hypertension.[17] The findings indicated that RAAS inhibitors were associated with a 5% reduction in all-cause mortality and a 7% reduction in cardiovascular mortality compared to control treatments. Subgroup analysis revealed that ACEIs independently reduced all-cause mortality by 10%, whereas angiotensin receptor blockers did not demonstrate a statistically significant reduction. The difference in all-cause mortality between the 2 was statistically significant. These findings suggest that ACEIs may provide superior survival advantage within the RAAS inhibitor class, consistent with the findings of the present study. However, the subgroup of patients receiving irbesartan in this study comprised only 45 participants, and further investigation with a larger sample size is warranted to validate these observations.

No serious adverse events were observed during the study period. Common adverse reactions of diltiazem include bradycardia, atrioventricular conduction block, and edema. Since this study excluded patients with baseline heart rate <60 bpm or conduction abnormalities, tolerability was favorable. A limitation is that the relatively small sample size and 1-year follow-up duration were insufficient to assess long-term safety. Clinical use still requires monitoring of heart rate and cardiac rhythm.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Diltiazem demonstrated effective antihypertensive properties and sustained anti-myocardial ischemia benefits. In patients with asymptomatic myocardial ischemia and hypertension, diltiazem significantly delayed disease progression. Among patients with preexisting myocardial ischemia, diltiazem contributed to notable improvements, with efficacy comparable to that of benazepril.

These findings underscore the potential clinical utility of diltiazem in the management of mild to moderate hypertension complicated by myocardial ischemia. However, due to the relatively small sample size and limited follow-up duration, additional large-scale, multicenter randomized controlled trials are warranted to validate these findings and assess long-term cardiovascular outcomes. Future research should also examine the effects of diltiazem on health-related quality of life, exercise tolerance, and biochemical markers of myocardial injury across diverse hypertensive populations. Additionally, the development of optimal combination regimens and individualized treatment strategies involving diltiazem may further enhance its application in comprehensive cardiovascular risk management.
