## English Abstract

Hypertension (HTN), a chronic inflammatory condition, is a major cardiovascular risk factor. Dysregulation of inflammatory cytokines and brain-gut peptides contributes to its progression. This study aimed to evaluate the effects of a Tai Chi rehabilitation program on blood pressure, inflammatory cytokines [interleukin-6 (IL-6), interleukin-10 (IL-10)], and brain-gut peptides [neurotensin (NT), gastrin (GAS)] in patients with HTN, hypothesizing that Tai Chi would improve these parameters compared with Fitness exercise or no intervention.

A randomized controlled trial enrolled 105 patients with HTN and coronary heart disease. Participants were randomized into the Tai Chi group (n = 35), Fitness group (n = 35), or Blank group (n = 35). The intervention lasted 24 weeks (3 sessions per week, 60 min per session, 40–60% heart rate reserve). Blood pressure and serum levels of IL-6, IL-10, NT, and GAS were measured before and after the intervention using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). Statistical analysis was performed with SPSS version 27, applying paired t-tests, analysis of variance (ANOVA), and Pearson correlation.

Ninety participants completed the study (Tai Chi group: 31, Fitness group: 28, Blank group: 31). The Tai Chi group showed significant reductions in systolic blood pressure (SBP) and IL-6 (p < 0.001), along with increases in IL-10, NT, and GAS (p < 0.001) compared to baseline. Compared with the Fitness group, the Tai Chi group achieved greater increases in NT and GAS (p < 0.05). Compared with the Blank group, the Tai Chi group demonstrated superior reductions in SBP and IL-6 (p < 0.05). Reductions in IL-6 were negatively correlated with increases in NT and GAS (p < 0.001).

Tai Chi rehabilitation lowered systolic blood pressure, reduced inflammation, and improved brain-gut peptides, supporting its role as a complementary therapy for hypertension. The modest sample size, 14.3% dropout rate, and recruitment from two hospitals may limit generalizability, and larger multi-center studies with longer follow-up are needed.

## 背景 / Background

In recent years, Tai Chi, as a traditional Chinese martial art, has gained widespread attention for its potential benefits in the rehabilitation of chronic diseases [1, 2]. Hypertension (HTN) is a major global public health issue with a complex pathogenesis involving inflammatory responses and neuroendocrine dysregulation [3, 4]. Studies have shown that inflammatory factors like interleukin-6 (IL-6) play a significant role in the development and progression of HTN, while anti-inflammatory factors such as interleukin-10 (IL-10) have protective effects [5, 6]. Additionally, brain-gut axis (e.g., neuropeptide Y (NT), gastrin (GAS), etc.), as key components of the brain-gut axis, are involved in regulating cardiovascular function, and their abnormal levels are closely associated with HTN [7, 8].

IL-6, as a pro-inflammatory cytokine, can activate the NF-κB signaling pathway, leading to impaired intestinal barrier function and affecting the regulation of the autonomic nervous system, thereby inhibiting the secretion of NT and GAS [9–12]. In contrast, IL-10, as an anti-inflammatory factor, can improve neuro-immune regulation, promote the recovery of brain-gut axis function, and consequently increase the levels of NT and GAS [13–15]. Previous studies have shown that chronic inflammatory states can lead to reduced levels of NT and GAS [16, 17], while IL-10 may enhance vagal nerve activity and restore brain-gut axis function by regulating the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway [18–21].

Numerous studies have explored the effects of Tai Chi on patients with HTN. A randomized controlled trial (RCT) found that Tai Chi training significantly reduced blood pressure levels in elderly patients with HTN while improving their quality of life [22, 23]. Another study indicated that Tai Chi, as a low-to-moderate-intensity aerobic exercise, has beneficial regulatory effects on elderly patients with HTN [23, 24]. However, research on how Tai Chi affects inflammatory factors and neuro-gastrointestinal system hormone levels in patients with HTN remains limited.

This study conducted an RCT in patients with primary HTN to systematically observe the effects of a 24-week Tai Chi rehabilitation program on blood pressure, inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, IL-10), and brain-gut peptides (NT, GAS), in direct comparison with a Fitness rehabilitation program and a Blank control. The aim was to determine whether Tai Chi could significantly improve blood pressure and related biological indicators, to explore the correlations between inflammatory cytokines and brain-gut peptides, and to elucidate potential mechanisms underlying the antihypertensive effects of Tai Chi. By comparing different exercise modalities, this study seeks to provide evidence on regulating inflammatory cytokines and brain-gut peptide levels for better blood pressure control, thereby offering a promising non-pharmacological intervention strategy for the comprehensive management of HTN.

## 方法 / Methods

This RCT adopted a 2 × 3 mixed design with two measurement time points (pre- and post-intervention) and three intervention arms (Tai Chi, Fitness, Blank). Due to the obvious differences between interventions, participants and intervention instructors were not blinded; however, statistical analysts remained blinded to group allocation: Tai Chi group, Fitness group, and Blank group. Participants were randomly assigned to the three groups in a 1:1:1 ratio. The intervention lasted 24 weeks, with three 60-minute sessions per week.

The primary endpoint was the change in systolic blood pressure (SBP), in line with the primary intervention target recommended by the 2018 Chinese Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of HTN. Secondary endpoints included: (i) inflammatory factors IL-6 (pro-inflammatory) and IL-10 (anti-inflammatory); (ii) brain-gut peptides NT and GAS. The exploratory endpoint was the correlation between inflammatory factors and NT, GAS. Endpoint selection was based on clinical relevance (SBP directly reflects blood pressure control) and biological plausibility (inflammatory factors and NT, GAS are involved in the pathophysiology of HTN.

The experimental design includes two time points (pre-intervention and post-intervention) to assess changes in blood pressure levels (systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP)), inflammatory factors (IL-6, IL-10), and GAS and NT among the groups. Data were analyzed using SPSS statistical software. The study flowchart is shown in Fig. 1.

Study Flowchart

This study complied with the Declaration of Helsinki and was approved by the Academic and Ethics Committee of the School of Physical Education and Sport, Beijing Normal University (approval date: 22 June 2022; approval number: 2022011 H). It was registered with the International Traditional Medicine Clinical Trial Registry (registration number: ITMCTR2024000813; registration date: 2024/12/13). All participants provided informed consent after being informed of the study purpose, procedures, potential benefits, and risks, and were free to withdraw at any time without penalty.

All indicators were quantitatively analyzed using the ELISA method. All kits were purchased from R&D Systems, USA, including IL-6 (Catalog No. D6050, detection range 3.12–300 pg/mL, sensitivity 0.70 pg/mL), IL-10 (Catalog No. D1000B, detection range 3.9–250 pg/mL, sensitivity 1.0 pg/mL), NT (Catalog No. EHL-NT-1, detection range 5-200 pg/mL, sensitivity 2.5 pg/mL), and GAS (Catalog No. EHL-GAS-2, detection range 10–500 pg/mL, sensitivity 5.0 pg/mL). After thawing, plasma samples were appropriately diluted according to the kit instructions, and 50 µL of standards, samples, or negative controls were added to a 96-well plate, followed by incubation at 37 °C for 1–2 h. After washing 4–5 times, 100 µL of enzyme-labeled secondary antibody was added and incubated for another 30–60 min. Subsequently, 100 µL of TMB substrate solution was added, and the reaction was allowed to proceed in the dark for 10–15 min before being terminated with 50 µL of stop solution. The optical density (OD value) was measured at 450 nm using a Bio-Rad iMark microplate reader. Sample concentrations were calculated using a four-parameter nonlinear regression fit, expressed in pg/mL, and the coefficient of variation (CV%) was calculated to ensure intra-assay CV% < 10% and inter-assay CV% < 15%, guaranteeing data accuracy and reproducibility. All ELISA measurements were carried out in triplicate.

All data were analyzed using SPSS 26.0 software. Normally distributed continuous data were expressed as mean ± standard deviation (Mean ± SD), intergroup comparisons were performed using one-way analysis of variance, while intragroup comparisons before and after the intervention were performed using paired-sample t-tests. For non-normally distributed data, the median (P25, P75) was used, and intergroup comparisons were performed using the Kruskal-Wallis H test, while intragroup comparisons were performed using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test. Categorical variables were analyzed using the chi-square (χ²) test, and the odds ratio with its 95% confidence interval was calculated. Cohen’s d-effect size was also calculated to assess the magnitude of differences in intergroup and intragroup comparisons. To control for type I error due to multiple comparisons, the Bonferroni correction was applied to between-group analyses of secondary endpoints, with the significance level set at α = 0.05. All statistical tests were two-sided, with a significance level set at p < 0.05.

Baseline IL-6 levels showed no significant difference among the three groups (Table 3; Fig. 2C). After the intervention, IL-6 decreased from 124.94 ± 14.01 to 118.21 ± 14.67 in the Tai Chi group (p < 0.001) and from 122.03 ± 13.27 to 120.11 ± 12.76 in the Fitness group (p < 0.05), but increased from 124.11 ± 10.93 to 126.68 ± 12.90 in the Blank group (p < 0.01). Between-group analysis showed greater IL-6 reduction in the Tai Chi group than in the Blank group (p < 0.05), with no significant difference between the Tai Chi and Fitness groups.

Baseline NT and GAS levels were similar among groups (Table 3; Fig. 2D and E). After the intervention, NT increased from 14.43 ± 1.43 to 77.27 ± 17.01 in the Tai Chi group (p < 0.001) and from 13.30 ± 1.77 to 61.79 ± 10.30 in the Fitness group (p < 0.01), with no significant change in the Blank group (p > 0.05). Post-intervention NT levels were higher in the Tai Chi group than in the Fitness and Blank groups (both p < 0.001). Similarly, GAS increased from 50.24 ± 6.60 to 55.66 ± 6.84 in the Tai Chi group (p < 0.001) but showed no significant change in the Fitness or Blank groups (both p > 0.05). Post-intervention GAS levels were higher in the Tai Chi group than in the Fitness group (p < 0.05) and the Blank group (p < 0.001).

Pearson correlation analysis was conducted to examine the relationships between changes in IL-6 and IL-10 levels and changes in NT and GAS levels (Table 4). Changes in IL-6 levels were negatively correlated with changes in NT (r = -0.296, p < 0.001) and GAS (r = -0.396, p < 0.001). Changes in IL-10 levels were positively correlated with NT (r = 0.374, p < 0.001) and GAS (r = 0.334, p = 0.001).

## 讨论 / Discussion

This (RCT demonstrated that a 24-week Tai Chi rehabilitation program reduced SBP in patients with hypertension, accompanied by favorable changes in inflammatory cytokines and gut-brain axis peptides. Compared with the blank control group, the Tai Chi group showed lower IL-6 and higher IL-10, NT, and GAS levels; compared with the calisthenics group, Tai Chi was associated with greater improvements in SBP, IL-10, NT, and GAS, while the reduction in IL-6 was comparable. These findings suggest that Tai Chi may facilitate blood pressure control, at least in part, through improvements in inflammatory balance and gut–brain signaling.

Our results are consistent with previous evidence showing that mind–body exercise can reduce blood pressure and modulate inflammatory pathways [27–29]. Notably, this study is the first to simultaneously observe the coordinated trends of decreased IL-6, increased IL-10, and elevated NT/GAS in hypertensive patients, suggesting that blood pressure reduction may be linked with concurrent changes in inflammatory and neuroendocrine markers. IL-6 is a key pro-inflammatory factor in hypertension progression [30, 31], whereas IL-10 suppresses inflammation and protects endothelial function [32, 33]. NT is known to exert vasodilatory and autonomic regulatory effects [34], while GAS elevation may reflect improved gastrointestinal function. The parallel changes in these markers provide new clinical evidence for a potential “blood pressure reduction–inflammatory balance–gut-brain peptide” pathway.

Although these associations are biologically plausible, the underlying mechanisms remain speculative. This study did not directly measure autonomic nervous activity or endothelial function; therefore, explanations involving vagal activation or gut–brain axis signaling should be regarded as hypotheses. Future research with larger samples, longer follow-up, and more precise mechanistic assessments (e.g., LC-MS/MS validation of NT) is needed to further clarify the biological pathways underlying Tai Chi rehabilitation.

To comprehensively assess the health effects of Tai Chi, we conducted a PubMed literature review (2008–2024) using the keywords “Tai Chi,” “Hypertension,” “Inflammation,” “GAS and NT,” and “Randomized Controlled Trial.” Eleven key studies were identified, highlighting Tai Chi’s clinical value in blood pressure management and inflammatory regulation (Table S2 and Fig. 3) [35–41]. Evidence consistently shows that Tai Chi lowers both SBP and DBP more effectively than health education, no intervention, or other exercise forms, with stronger effects in younger patients (< 50 years) [42, 43]. Practice of the 24-form or Yang-style for ≥ 12 weeks appears optimal, and compared with aerobic exercise, Tai Chi achieves greater 12-month SBP reductions and more stable nighttime blood pressure [23]. Long-term training also improves lipid metabolism and vascular function [44], reinforcing its cardiovascular benefits. These findings, together with our present results, suggest that Tai Chi provides a safe, feasible, and sustainable adjunct to conventional therapy in hypertension. By improving blood pressure and inflammatory profiles, Tai Chi rehabilitation may contribute to long-term cardiovascular health promotion and individualized hypertension management.

To examine Tai Chi’s distinct effects, we searched PubMed and Google Scholar (2008–2024) using the terms “Tai Chi,” “Aerobic Exercise,” “GAS and NT,” and “Inflammation.” Representative studies are summarized in Table S3 [45, 46] and Fig. 4. In inflammation control, Tai Chi generally shows stronger and more consistent effects than aerobic exercise. Both Tai Chi and yoga can reduce oxidative stress and pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., TNF-α, IL-6), but Tai Chi demonstrates greater anti-inflammatory benefits [47]. Tai Chi has also been linked to increased anti-inflammatory gut microbiota and improved gut barrier function [48], whereas evidence for aerobic exercise is less consistent. In brain-gut axis regulation, Tai Chi significantly elevates GAS and NT levels and improves gastrointestinal function, while aerobic exercise shows minimal effects [48]. Additional studies suggest Tai Chi may reduce cortisol and improve gastrointestinal peptides and metabolic profiles [49]. These findings, together with our RCT, indicate that Tai Chi may offer broader regulatory effects on inflammation and gut–brain signaling compared with other aerobic exercises.

In our trial, 24 weeks of Tai Chi reduced SBP and IL-6 while increasing IL-10, NT, and GAS, supporting its dual role in blood pressure control and biological marker modulation. Although the marked NT increase observed here is notable, it should be interpreted with caution given pre-analytical variability and assay limitations [50, 51]. Future studies should apply standardized sampling protocols and advanced assays to validate these findings.

No serious adverse events were observed, and adherence was good (Table S4), suggesting that the Tai Chi rehabilitation program is safe and feasible for patients with hypertension. This study has several limitations. The sample size was relatively small (n = 90), and participants were recruited from only two hospitals in Shandong Province, consisting mainly of middle-aged and older patients with primary hypertension, which limits generalizability to younger individuals, refractory hypertension, or those with multi-organ comorbidities. The intervention lasted 24 weeks, without long-term follow-up, so the durability of blood pressure reduction and the preventive potential for cardiovascular events (e.g., stroke, myocardial infarction) remain unclear. The findings were also based on data from only two centers, lacking multi-center validation to ensure broader applicability. In addition, Tai Chi should be considered an adjunct to, rather than a substitute for, pharmacological treatment. Finally, the dropout rate was 14.3%, which, although within expectations, may have introduced attrition bias. Future studies should be conducted with larger sample sizes, across multiple centers, and with longer follow-up to confirm and extend these findings. Incorporating mechanistic endpoints such as endothelial function and autonomic regulation, as well as direct comparisons with other forms of exercise, will help to clarify the unique benefits and optimal application of Tai Chi in hypertension management.

## 结论 / Conclusion

The results of this study indicate that the primary benefit of the Tai Chi rehabilitation program lies in its significant reduction of SBP in patients with HTN. As secondary endpoints, Tai Chi was found to decrease IL-6 levels while increasing IL-10, NT, and GAS levels, with these differences remaining statistically robust after multiple comparison corrections. These findings suggest that Tai Chi may help improve blood pressure control partly through modulating inflammatory balance and brain-gut axis function, although the independent clinical significance of these effects requires further verification. Overall, Tai Chi appears to support blood pressure management while contributing to anti-inflammatory regulation and enhancing neuro-gastrointestinal hormone levels. These findings support Tai Chi as a promising non-pharmacological approach for HTN management and provide a theoretical foundation for further mechanistic studies.
