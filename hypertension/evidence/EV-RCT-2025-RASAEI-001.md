## English Abstract

This study aimed to evaluate the effects of the Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet on blood pressure (BP) indices and urinary metabolites in overweight and obese children over an 8-week period.

In this triple-blind randomized controlled trial, 60 children (8–12 years) were assigned to a DASH or control group for 8-week. Urinary sodium (UNa), potassium (UK), creatinine, UNa-UK ratio, systolic BP (SBP), diastolic BP (DBP), mean arterial pressure (MAP), mid-BP (MBP), mean proportional arterial pressure (MPAP), and pulse pressure were measured before and after the intervention. Twenty-four-hour UNa excretion and salt intake were estimated using the INTERSALT equation. Outcomes were analyzed using analysis of variance on an intention-to-treat basis.

The mean age and body mass index of participants were 11.8 ± 0.8 years (69% girls) and 25.9 ± 3.52 kg/m2, respectively. Fifty-eight children completed the study (29 per group). After 8-week, the DASH group showed significantly lower UNa (110 vs. 123 mmol/L), UNa-UK ratio (1.84 vs. 1.99 mmol/L), SBP (97.7 vs. 105 mmHg), DBP (68.9 vs. 64.6 mmHg), MBP (88.1 vs. 81.2 mmHg), MAP (81.6 vs. 75.6 mmHg), and MPAP (92.3 vs. 84.7 mmHg) compared with controls (P for all < 0.05). The mean change percent in UNa, UNa-UK ratio, SBP, MBP, MAP, and MPAP was significantly greater in the control group than in the DASH group (P for all < 0.05). Salt intake increased in the control group, but decreased in the DASH group (8.32 vs. −2.88 gr/day; P-value = 0.001).

The DASH diet improved BP indices and urinary metabolites in overweight and obese children, suggesting its potential as a non-pharmacological intervention for hypertension management in pediatric populations.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension (HTN), a multifactorial and progressive cardiovascular disorder [1], is a leading modifiable risk factor for early mortality, affecting over 25% of adults worldwide [234]. It exhibits a direct and continuous correlation with adverse health outcomes, highlighting its significant public health burden [5]. Among the well-established risk factors for HTN, excessive sodium (Na) intake is a critical contributor [67]. Current dietary guidelines recommend limiting Na intake to less than 2,000 mg/day while increasing K intake to over 3,500 mg/day to reduce the risk of HTN and cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) [89].

The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet emphasizes the consumption of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy, fish, and nuts intake [310]. This dietary pattern is specifically designed to improve cardiovascular health and has also been associated with a reduced risk of type 2 diabetes (T2D), certain cancers, metabolic syndrome (MetS), and other chronic conditions [111213].

Extensive research supports the role of the DASH diet in managing HTN and reducing CVD risk across populations. Recent evidence underscores its effectiveness, particularly in lowering systolic blood pressure (SBP) and improving endothelial function in adolescents with elevated blood pressure (BP) [14]. For instance, studies show BP reduction and HTN prevention in Chinese youth [15], while a clinical trial linked the DASH diet to reduced adiposity but not mean arterial pressure (MAP) [16]. An inverse association between DASH adherence and SBP was observed in adolescents aged 14–18, though not in other age groups or for diastolic BP (DBP) [17]. However, findings remain inconsistent; some studies found no association with CVD risk, particularly in Brazilian adolescents [18], and while the DASH diet improved insulin sensitivity in overweight and obese youth, reductions in CVD risk factors were not consistently observed [19].

Despite evidence supporting the DASH diet benefits, its effects on BP indices and urinary metabolites in overweight and obese children remain unclear, with inconsistent findings. The underlying mechanisms and potential variations by age and metabolic status are not well understood. This study addresses these gaps through an 8-week triple-blind randomized controlled trial (TB-RCT) to evaluate the DASH diet impact on BP indices and urinary metabolites, providing robust evidence for its role in HTN management in this population.

## 方法 / Methods

This TB-RCT was conducted from January to March 2023, adhering to the ethical principles of the 1975 Declaration of Helsinki. Ethical approval was granted by the Ethics Committee of Dezful University of Medical Sciences (IR.DUMS.REC.1402.003), and the trial was registered in the Iranian Registry of Clinical Trials (IRCT20240623062229N1). The study complies with the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials guidelines.

The primary outcomes were alterations in urinary Na (UNa), UK, creatinine (UCr), UNa-UK ratio, SBP, DBP, MAP, mid-BP (MBP), mean proportional arterial pressure (MPAP) and pulse pressure (PP).

The study was conducted in Dezful, where 95 elementary schools were divided into six districts. One school per district was randomly selected, and 10 students from each school were systematically chosen. A total of 60 children (aged 8–12 years, body mass index (BMI) > 85th percentile) were enrolled and randomly assigned to the DASH diet (n = 30) or control group (n = 30) (enrolled participants were children with overweight and obesity, defined as BMI > 85th percentile for age and sex). Written consent was obtained from parents, and the 8-week intervention focused on the DASH diet [2021]. Exclusion criteria encompassed a history of CVDs or renal diseases, hyperactivity disorder, thyroid or hormonal disorders, T2D, sleep apnea, adherence to specific dietary regimens, or the use of medications such as those for weight loss or HTN. Non-compliant participants were excluded.

The study maintained a triple-blind design, with researchers, participants, and the statistical team masked to group assignments. A healthcare provider explained the diet protocol, and anthropometric measurements were taken by an independent researcher. Data were recorded using random codes and analyzed without knowledge of group allocations.

Body weight (BW) was recorded to the nearest 100 g using a Seca scale, with participants dressed in lightweight clothing. Height measurements were taken while participants stood barefoot against a wall, ensuring proper alignment of the head, shoulders, pelvis, and heels. BMI was derived by dividing BW (kg) by the square of height (m2) (kg/m2).

BP measurements were conducted between 8:00 AM and 12:00 PM by trained staff using a manual sphygmomanometer (HONSUN HS-20A; Honsun, Nantong, China), calibrated by the Institute of Standards and Industrial Research of Iran. The cuff was placed on the right upper arm at heart level. The cuff was rapidly inflated to 30 mmHg above the point where the radial pulse ceased, ensuring accurate SBP detection, even in hypertensive individuals. SBP and DBP were recorded at the appearance (phase I) and disappearance (phase V) of Korotkoff sounds, respectively, while deflating the cuff at a rate of 2–3 mmHg per second. Participants rested seated for 15 minutes before measurements. BP was measured twice, with a 3–5 minute interval between readings, and the average of the two measurements was used as the final value.

Participants provided second-void urine samples between 7:00 AM and 9:00 AM after fasting overnight for 10–12 hours, at baseline and the end of week 8. These samples were aliquoted, frozen, and sent to a central laboratory for analysis. UNa and UK levels were measured using an electrolyte analyzer (Jokoh EX-CA Electrolyte Analyzer; JOKOH, Kawasaki, Japan), with intra and inter assay coefficients of variation (CVs) of 2.3%, 2.6%, 3.7%, and 3.9%, respectively. UCr concentrations were analyzed in urine samples diluted 1:20 with distilled water using Jaffe reaction and commercial kits (Delta Darman Part Co., Tehran, Iran) on an autoanalyzer (Hitachi 917 Chemistry Analyzer; Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan) at a wavelength of 500 nm. The autoanalyzer was calibrated with a Cr calibrator (Delta Darman Part Co.), and results were multiplied by 20. The intra and inter assay CVs for UCr were 5% and 6%, respectively.

The DASH diet used in this intervention was adapted from its original adult version to meet the unique nutritional needs of children (based on age and caloric needs). In this study, the diet was designed to provide macronutrient distributions of 53–58% carbohydrates, 15–18% protein, and 26–30% fat. The dietary plan included recommendations for 4–6 servings of whole grains, 3–5 servings of fruits, vegetables and lean protein, 2–3 servings of low-fat dairy, fats and oils, 3–4 servings of nuts and seeds and no more than 3 servings of sweets per day. Additionally, daily Na intake was restricted to less than 2,300 mg [2223]. In comparison to other dietary regimens, the DASH diet is particularly rich in key micronutrients, including Ca, K, and Mg [17]. The energy requirements for each participant were calculated individually using the formulas outlined in the American Dietetic Association Pediatric Weight Management Guidelines, tailored specifically for maintaining BW [24]. It is important to note that calorie intake for children in the DASH group remained unchanged throughout the study period. To ensure compliance, weekly phone calls were made to parents to assess their adherence of child to the diet and resolve any issues. Children were given food diaries, and parents were instructed on how to precisely record all food and beverage intake, including portion sizes.

## 结果 / Results

A total of 60 children (69% girls), aged 8–12 years, were randomly assigned to the DASH diet or control group based on the 85th BMI percentile. Two participants (one from each group) were lost to follow-up, leaving 58 children for analysis. The mean age of participants was 11.8 ± 0.82 years. Height distribution varied across age groups, with mean (± SD) height-for-age z-scores (HAZ) of −0.37 ± 1.38 in the DASH group and −0.04 ± 1.05 in controls (P = 0.300), ranging from −3.50 (moderate stunting) to + 2.81 (tall stature). Notably, 12% of participants met criteria for stunting (HAZ < −2) while 15% exceeded + 1 HAZ. Analysis of treatment effect modification by growth status revealed no significant interaction between HAZ categories and intervention group for either SBP (P-interaction = 0.079) or DBP (P-interaction = 0.401). The consistent effect sizes across HAZ categories suggest the DASH diet benefits children regardless of growth status. Fig. 1 outlines the recruitment process and study follow-up. Table 1 demonstrates no significant differences in demographic, anthropometric, or clinical characteristics between the DASH diet and control groups. Table 2 presents BP indices, urinary metabolites in the DASH and control groups after the 8-week follow-up, showing significantly lower values in the DASH group for UNa (110 vs. 123 mmol/L), UNa-UK ratio (1.84 vs. 1.99 mmol/L), SBP (97.7 vs. 105 mmHg), DBP (68.9 vs. 64.6 mmHg), MBP (88.1 vs. 81.2 mmHg), MAP (81.6 vs. 75.6 mmHg), and MPAP (92.3 vs. 84.7 mmHg) compared to the control group (P for all < 0.05). ## 讨论 / Discussion

This study examined the effects of the DASH diet on BP and urinary metabolites in children with overweight and obesity. After an 8-week intervention, the DASH group showed significantly lower BP indices, including SBP, DBP, and MAP, compared to the control group. Additionally, UNa and the UNa-UK ratio were significantly reduced in the DASH group, while the control group exhibited increases in these markers, reflecting higher salt intake. These findings suggest that the DASH diet may play a beneficial role in BP regulation and Na homeostasis in children, supporting its potential as a dietary strategy for HTN prevention and management.

The mechanisms underlying the effects of DASH diet on BP and urinary metabolites remain incompletely understood. While some studies have reported protective effects of the DASH diet on BP [141529], others have found neutral findings [18193031]. In this study, the DASH diet significantly improved BP indices and urinary metabolites, consistent with previous findings in pediatric population [293233]. For instance, a 6-month DASH-focused behavioral intervention in adolescents with elevated BP was more effective than routine care in reducing SBP and improving endothelial function [14]. Similarly, a study in Chinese children and adolescents found that greater adherence to a protective dietary pattern, aligned with DASH principles, was associated with lower odds of elevated BP and PP [1534]. A cross-sectional study among Iranian schoolchildren also linked higher DASH adherence to lower BP [29]. Furthermore, systematic reviews and randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have highlighted the benefits of DASH diet in improving BP and addressing overweight and obesity in adolescents [3233]. Notably, adherence to the DASH diet has been associated with lower UNa-UK ratios, a marker linked to reduced CVDs risk [353637]. Diets rich in nuts, seeds, fruits, and fish have also been shown to lower the UNa-UK ratio [38]. Also, higher adherence to a Mediterranean diet correlates with better nutrient intake, including reduced Na and increased K [39]. The Shandong Ministry of Health Action on Salt and Hypertension recently reported a non-linear increase in HTN risk with higher UNa-UK ratios and a linear association with elevated SBP, DBP, MAP, and MBP [4041]. Additionally, a higher dietary Na-K ratio has been linked to increased CVDs risk over 6.3 year and 10.6 year periods [42]. However, some studies have found no significant association between DASH adherence and BP in children and adolescents [43], while others reported no link between the DASH diet and arterial HTN [1930]. A longitudinal study also found that DASH adherence was associated with some components of MetS but not BP over a 3.6 year follow-up [31]. These discrepancies may stem from methodological differences, unmeasured confounding factors, variations in study design, and individual heterogeneity in genetic predisposition, lifestyle, and gut microbiota composition [44454647].

The DASH diet may reduce HTN risk through multiple physiological and metabolic pathways. It interacts with the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system, enhancing angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibition and promoting natriuretic and diuretic effects [48]. Reducing saturated fat intake may also lower BP by increasing serum ACE levels [49]. The emphasis of DASH diet on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy provides K, Mg, Ca, and fiber, all of which are associated with BP reduction [50]. K, crucial for cell function via the Na-K ATPase pump, improves BP by promoting natriuresis, enhancing endothelial vasodilation, and improving baroreflex sensitivity [1051]. K regulates BP and promotes Na excretion, while Ca counteracts hypertensive effects of Na [52]. Adequate Ca stabilizes smooth muscle cell membranes, and increased Ca intake may reduce plasma renin activity [10]. Mg, found in whole grains and nuts, regulates BP by blocking Ca channels and enhancing nitric oxide and prostaglandin E production [1053]. However, supplements of K, Mg, and fiber were less effective than the DASH diet in reducing BP, suggesting that other dietary components play a key role [54]. The DASH diet also provides vitamins, phytochemicals, and antioxidants like polyphenols and flavonoids, which reduce oxidative stress, inflammation, and subclinical cardiac injury, contributing to HTN improvements [5556]. The reduction in inflammation, potentially mediated by increased dietary fiber intake, altering gut microbiota, suppressing inflammatory cytokines, and stimulating short-chain fatty acid production alongside the anti-inflammatory effects of higher fruit, vegetable, and magnesium intake [575859]. Additionally, fruits and vegetables rich in inorganic nitrate improve endothelial function, reduce arterial stiffness, and decrease platelet aggregation by converting nitrate to nitric oxide [60]. Collectively, these mechanisms highlight the multifaceted impact of DASH diet on hormonal, vascular, and cardiac function, supporting its role in HTN management [61].

The strengths of this study include its rigorous TB-RCT design, which enhances the reliability and validity of the findings. This is the first study to examine the effects of the DASH diet on BP indices and urinary metabolites in overweight and obese children within a TB-RCT framework, providing novel insights. However, several limitations should be noted. First, the sample of study was geographically restricted, potentially limiting generalizability to other populations. Second, the 8-week intervention period may have been insufficient to capture long-term effects, warranting extended follow-up studies. Third, reliance on self-reported dietary data and telephone-based monitoring in young participants may have introduced biases, affecting the precision of the results. Fourth, another limitation of our study is the absence of formal assessment of pubertal stage (e.g., Tanner staging), which could influence BP measurements. While we attempted to control for this by selecting participants within a narrow age range, the potential confounding effects of early pubertal development cannot be entirely ruled out. Fifth, while our study captured an unusually wide HAZ range, the small subgroups at growth extremes limit precise effect estimation. Future trials should stratify recruitment by growth status to enable robust subgroup analyses. Sixth, physical activity data were not collected and thus could not be adjusted for in the analyses. Given that physical activity significantly affects pediatric BP and may differ by sex and age, this omission represents a potential confounding factor. However, randomization likely mitigated some of this bias, as no significant differences were observed in key baseline variables between groups. Seventh, the relatively small sample size resulted in a post-hoc power of 72.2% (α = 0.05, effect size = 0.66) for detecting the observed differences in DBP, which is below the conventional 80% threshold. While this suggests some risk of type II error, the effect size was clinically significant and aligns with findings from previous similar studies [28]. Therefore, larger, multi-center RCTs with diverse pediatric populations are needed to confirm and extend these findings.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In conclusion, the DASH diet significantly improved SBP, DBP, MAP, MBP, MPAP, UNa, and UNa-UK ratios in children with overweight and obesity over 8-week, suggesting its potential as an effective intervention for HTN management. However, further research with longer follow-up periods, larger and more diverse samples, and improved methodological rigor is needed to confirm and expand upon these findings.
