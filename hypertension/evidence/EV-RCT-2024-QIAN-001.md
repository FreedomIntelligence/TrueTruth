## English Abstract

A systematic literature review and network meta-analysis was conducted on azilsartan medoxomil (AZL-M) versus other antihypertensive drugs’ efficacy in hypertensive patients.

The search utilized English platforms, from January 2000 until December 2023, resulting in 10,380 articles being screened. Screening criteria included hypertension (mild or moderate); first-line treatment and washout periods; studies (monotherapy) with AZL-M, angiotensin type II receptor blockers (ARBs), angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs), angiotensin receptor neprilysin inhibitor (ARNIs), beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers (CCBs), and diuretics, either as intervention or comparator; and antihypertension efficacy as an outcome measure. Study design was randomized clinical trials. Efficacy variables included absolute office systolic and diastolic blood pressure (BP) reductions. A total of 21 publications provided adequate data for analysis, of which 20 studies reported both systolic and diastolic BP and one study reported only the diastolic BP.

In 21 studies on systolic BP, against the common comparator placebo, the differences in systolic BP were significantly in favor of AZL-M, amlodipine, candesartan, irbesartan, nebivolol, nifedipine, olmesartan, sacubitril valsartan, telmisartan, and valsartan. The surface under the cumulative ranking curve (SUCRA) ranking shows that AZL-M 80 mg had the highest ranking, with a possibility of 93% being the best in all other included treatments. In 20 studies on diastolic BP, against the common comparator placebo, the differences in diastolic BP were significantly in favor of AZL-M, amlodipine, bisoprolol, nebivolol, olmesartan, sacubitril valsartan, telmisartan, and valsartan. The SUCRA ranking shows that AZL-M 80 mg had the highest ranking, with a possibility of 90% being the best in all other included treatments.

AZL-M at 40 mg and 80 mg shows favorable efficacy compared to other anti-hypertensives, and the 80 mg dosage seemed to be the most efficacious of all the included treatments in reducing both office systolic and diastolic BP in patients with mild-to-moderate hypertension.

## 背景 / Background

High blood pressure (BP), or hypertension, is a major risk factor and leading cause of death related to cardiovascular disease (CVD) and stroke [1, 2]. Approximately 40% of the adult population across the globe is affected by hypertension [2]. Nearly 9.4 million deaths occur worldwide every year because of hypertension [3]. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), hypertension affects an estimated 1.28 billion adults worldwide, with a higher prevalence in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) [4, 5].

The prevalence of hypertension in China has increased significantly over the last 30 years [6, 7]; this increase is mostly attributable to increased life expectancy in the general population, along with adult lifestyle changes. Hypertension impacts 25% of Chinese adults overall and up to 40% of Chinese adults aged ≥ 45 years [8–10]. In 2015, the prevalence in China’s adult population was 27.9%, with 245 million people having the condition. In 2018, the prevalence of apparent adult hypertension in China Chronic Disease and Risk Factor Surveillance (CCDRFS) was 27.5% (95% CI 26.6–28.4%) [11]. However, recent published data describe that the rates of hypertension awareness, treatment, and control in China are 51.6%, 45.8%, and 16.8%, respectively, still at a low level [12]. As cardiovascular disease prevention and treatment have advanced rapidly in recent years [13], the Chinese government and public health agencies are implementing policies and guidelines to address the growing prevalence of hypertension and enhance hypertension management [14]. China’s current guidelines (2018) recommend a BP control target of < 140/90 mmHg (< 130/80 mmHg for patients with diabetes or renal disease). The US updated guidelines define stage 1 hypertension as 130–139/80–89 mmHg and recommend pharmacologic treatment for high-risk patients. If these guidelines are adopted in China, 55% of adults aged 45–75 will fall in the hypertensive category, increasing the number of untreated patients [15].

According to Chinese hypertension guidelines, the pharmacologic treatments recommended for high-risk patients include the following classes of antihypertensive drugs: calcium channel blockers, angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs), angiotensin type II receptor blockers (ARBs), diuretics, and beta-blockers [15]. ARBs and ACE inhibitors, which are widely used across the globe for the treatment of hypertension owing to their well-established safety profiles, are better tolerated than other antihypertensive drugs [16–18].

The pursuit of a more potent BP-lowering effect often carries an increased risk of adverse events. However, this paradigm has shifted following technologic advances leading to the discovery of a unique structurally and chemically even newer agent, azilsartan medoxomil (AZL-M).

In clinical studies, this compound showed strong BP-lowering efficacy with good tolerability and a good adverse event profile. This new drug has been compared with several but not all available older agents. We therefore performed the present network meta-analysis to obtain an overview of antihypertensive drugs in patients with mild-to-moderate hypertension, focusing on the efficacy of AZL-M.

Two researchers independently screened the title and abstract to identify potentially relevant studies. The extracted data included study characteristics, patient characteristics, interventions, comparators, outcomes, and other relevant results. We also searched the references in the identified articles for possible inclusion. A third investigator verified the extracted data. Discrepancies were handled using a consensus procedure. The quality of the extracted studies was evaluated and classified into four categories: high, moderate, low, and extremely low. The quality of the evidence was appraised. Cochrane’s risk of bias assessment tool was used to assess the risk of bias at the study level [21].

The effects of various antihypertensives on the changes in office systolic and diastolic BP were estimated for all individual studies. The network meta-analysis combined both direct and indirect treatment comparisons. Pairwise meta-analyses of all interventions were performed to evaluate the possible statistical heterogeneity of studies within each comparison for systolic BP and diastolic BP. Antihypertensives were chosen as comparators.

The network meta-analysis was conducted using a Bayesian random-effects model. Various antihypertensives were ranked by their effects relative to baseline. The hierarchy between various antihypertensives was estimated by the ranking probabilities of the frequency table results. This was estimated using the Surface Under the Cumulative RAnking (SUCRA) curves [22].

The Cochrane Collaboration's risk of bias assessment tool was used to assess risk of bias [21]. The network meta-analysis (including the heterogeneity and inconsistency test) was conducted using the GeMTC package-based R Shiny app using Bayesian methods [23, 24].

Ethical approval was not required because this research is based on previously conducted published studies and does not contain any new studies with human participants or animals performed by any of the authors.

We identified 10,380 records by searching the databases. Most of the identified articles were from PubMed database (n = 10,372, 99.9%). A few articles were identified through bibliographic search (n = 8, 0.1%). After screening abstracts, 918 (8.8%) articles were assessed for eligibility. From these assessed articles, 730 (80%) were removed because of inappropriate intervention, outcomes not of interest, or incomplete data. Finally, 188 studies were included at the data extraction and analysis stage. Of these, the 49 RCTs that form the network were included. Few studies did not have sufficient data. ## 讨论 / Discussion

The findings of our meta-analysis were that AZL-M was superior to the other antihypertensives in lowering both office systolic and diastolic BP up to ≤ 12 weeks treatment period. The mean difference in office systolic and diastolic BP compared with placebo was − 11.94/− 6.15 mmHg and − 12.98/− 7.47 mmHg for AZL-M 40 mg and 80 mg, respectively (Table S3a and Table S3b). The SUCRA plot (Fig. 6a and Table S4a) shows that AZL-M 80 mg had a 93% probability of being the best drug for systolic BP reduction followed by amlodipine 10 mg (91%) and sacubitril valsartan 200 mg (88%), and AZL-M 80 mg had a 90% probability of being the best drug for diastolic BP reduction followed by sacubitril valsartan 200 mg (84%) and amlodipine 10 mg (83%) (Fig. 6b and Table S4b).

The mean difference in office systolic and diastolic BP was in favor of AZL-M 40 mg compared with olmesartan 40 mg (− 2.38/− 0.56 mmHg) and sacubitril valsartan 100 mg (− 2.81/− 0.56 mmHg), respectively. In addition, the mean difference in office systolic and diastolic BP favored AZL-M 80 mg compared with amlodipine 10 mg (− 0.25/− 0.49 mmHg) and sacubitril valsartan 200 mg (− 1.01/− 0.68 mmHg), respectively (Table S3a and Table S3b). Such differences are appreciable in the control of hypertension, especially as it has been substantiated in various articles and hypertension guidelines that individuals with hypertension have higher lifetime risks for CVD and experience the onset of CVD morbidity 5 years earlier than individuals with normal BP. Among middle-aged adults, every 20 mmHg increase in systolic BP or 10 mmHg elevation of diastolic BP is associated with a doubling in the rate of death resulting from stroke, ischemic heart disease, and other vascular causes [15, 45].

Our findings are in keeping with the results of previously published studies based on randomized comparison in which systolic or diastolic DBP reductions up to − 24.2/− 11.5 mmHg respectively have been reported for AZL-M at compliance rates of > 60% [2]. These and other studies suggest that AZL-M offers advantages over other antihypertensive medications, particularly in terms of BP reduction [46–48]. In an analysis of five different studies comparing AZL-M to other antihypertensives for the treatment of hypertension, a meta-analytical approach can provide insights into its relative efficacy. This meta-analysis indicated that azilsartan has a more favorable efficacy profile than ARBs in reducing systolic and diastolic BP. Another study compared AZL-M with ACEIs and found that AZL-M was more effective in achieving target BP values, with a similar safety profile to ACEIs. Additional research comparing AZL-M with olmesartan showed that AZL-M may have superior antihypertensive efficacy [49, 50]. A comparison between AZL-M and valsartan also suggested that AZL-M could be more effective in managing primary hypertension [47]. Subsequent data have shown that the long-term experience (56 weeks) of AZL-M alone or with either of two of the most commonly used diuretic agents (chlorthalidone and thiazide diuretic hydrochlorothiazide) as part of a treat-to-target strategy in patients with essential hypertension provided further evidence of sustained antihypertensive efficacy with good safety and tolerability [15, 51].

A key mechanism in understanding the superior BP-lowering effect of AZL-M is that it binds to the angiotensin II type 1 (AT1) receptor more persistently for up to 24 h and is not affected by drug elution. With the unique oxadiazole ring replacing the traditional tetrazole ring structure, AZL-M is more durably attached to the AT1 receptor and induces stronger inverse agonism. Henceforth, it dissociates more slowly, and the rate of dissociation is not dependent on blood concentration, so it can exert a longer lasting, earlier, and more powerful antihypertensive effect [52, 53]. Additional studies have also recorded excellent target organ(s) protection [54–56]. In patients with mild-to-moderate renal insufficiency, the dose of the drug does not need to be adjusted [57]. The long-term safety of the drug, known to be well tolerated, has been established in numerous publications [58].

Most importantly, safety is a primary issue in using antihypertensive drugs. In the Ongoing Telmisartan Alone and in Combination with Ramipril Global Endpoint Trial (ONTARGET; NCT00153101) and the International Verapamil SR Trandolapril Study (INVEST; NCT00133692), an increase in the death rate from cardiovascular causes was observed among patients with known coronary heart disease if the systolic BP was < 120 mmHg during the time the patient was receiving therapy with ACEIs or ARBs (in ONTARGET) or CCBs or beta-blockers (in INVEST). Thus, any adverse effect appears to be more closely related to the achieved BP than the class of drug used. The concern about potential overtreatment is reflected in the guidelines published by the European Society of Hypertension, which state that physicians should avoid lowering BP excessively (i.e., to values < 120/70 mmHg) in persons with underlying cardiovascular disease.

Our study has to be interpreted within the context of its limitations. In this study, we have taken several steps to minimize the implication of bias in selecting the literature. Recognizing that bias can influence the outcomes of a literature review, we established clear inclusion and exclusion criteria to guide our selection process. We utilized multiple sources to ensure a comprehensive and balanced review of the existing literature. To further reduce bias, we consulted with experts in the field and conducted a peer review of our literature selection process. Our methodology is transparently documented, allowing for replication and scrutiny by other researchers. We also addressed conflicting evidence by considering a range of perspectives and discussing differing viewpoints. Despite these extensive efforts, we acknowledge that some bias might still be present. Future research should continue to explore this topic and address any remaining gaps.

Furthermore, despite the heterogeneity in the data, steps were taken to ensure that the studies included in the meta-analysis were sufficiently similar in terms of patient populations, settings, and other variables that could influence the results. This is done to maintain the validity of the indirect comparisons being made. Also, in a meta-analysis for hypertension treatment, the inclusion of diuretic data would be preferred as diuretics are a common first-line treatment option. They are especially important for their proven efficacy in reducing the risk of cardiovascular disease and managing BP levels. Unfortunately, within the randomized controlled trials published over the period from January 2000 to December 2023, there were no eligible diuretics data (recent publications focus on combination rather than standalone dosing); hence, the absence of such data may limit the comprehensiveness of the analysis. Additionally, the SUCRA ranking for amlodipine 5 mg was relatively low, possibly because of the lack of statistically significant differences in two-by-two comparisons and the scarcity of eligible RCTs post-2000, particularly for monotherapy of amlodipine. Moreover, recent publications on amlodipine 5 mg often focus on fixed-dose combination (FDC) or single-pill combination (SPC) rather than standalone usage, potentially skewing the available data. Finally, the meta-analysis did not include an assessment of ambulatory BP or an evaluation of the safety profiles of the interventions. Further research is highly recommended, particularly to explore direct comparisons between AZL-M and other drugs as well as, most importantly, to investigate the reduction of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), target organ protection, renal failure, or death.

## 结论 / Conclusion

High blood pressure, or hypertension, is a major risk factor and a leading cause of death related to cardiovascular disease (CVD) and stroke. In 2015, the prevalence in China’s adult population was 27.9% with 245 million people with the conditionThe pursuit of a more potent blood pressure (BP)-lowering effect often carries an increased risk of adverse events. However, this paradigm has shifted following the discovery of a unique structurally and chemically even newer agent, azilsartan medoxomil (AZL-M). This systematic review and network meta-analysis evaluated antihypertensive drugs in patients with mild-to-moderate hypertension, focusing on the efficacy of AZL-MAgainst the common comparator placebo, the differences in systolic BP were significantly in favor of AZL-M, amlodipine, candesartan, irbesartan, nebivolol, nifedipine, olmesartan, sacubitril valsartan, telmisartan, and valsartan, while the differences in diastolic BP were significantly in favor of AZL-M, amlodipine, bisoprolol, nebivolol, olmesartan, sacubitril valsartan, telmisartan, and valsartanThe findings of our meta-analysis were highly in favor of AZL-M over the other antihypertensives in lowering both office systolic and diastolic blood pressures. The SUCRA ranking shows that AZL-M 80 mg had the highest probability of being the best drug for both systolic and diastolic blood pressure reduction

Our study showed that AZL-M at 40 mg and 80 mg had favorable efficacy compared to other anti-hypertensives, and the 80 mg dosage seemed to be most efficacious of all the included treatments in reducing both office systolic and diastolic BP in patients with mild-to-moderate hypertension. AZL-M, with increasing availability/accessibility, will surely further improve BP control in conjunction with good tolerability and safety profile.
