## English Abstract

Acute myocardial infarction (AMI) patients typically present with a constellation of one or more risk factors including hypertension, dyslipidemia, obesity, or diabetes. Neurohormonal modulation has been a mainstay pharmacotherapy in patients; however, patient compliance is a major obstacle towards clinical efficacy due to side effects and lifelong drug regimens. FDA approval of ultrasound renal denervation (uRDN) for the treatment of resistant hypertension raises the possibility of uRDN therapy for additional disease states involving sympathetic overactivity. In the current pilot study, we sought to explore if prior uRDN has cardioprotective effects against AMI in a comorbid‐laden minipig model. Göttingen minipigs (female) were subject to mineralocorticoid excess and a Western high‐fat, high‐salt diet to induce hypertension, obesity, and hyperlipidemia. Minipigs were randomized to bilateral uRDN (n = 5) treatment or sham‐RDN (n = 5) after 4 weeks. After 6 weeks of hypertension and Western high‐fat, high‐salt diet, animals were subjected to a 75‐min left anterior descending coronary artery occlusion followed by 2 weeks of reperfusion. Markers of renal nerve viability, ischemic injury, and cardiac structure and function were assessed. In the uRDN treatment group, there was reduced renal norepinephrine content, improved survival, and reduced myocardial infarct area and calcification compared to sham‐RDN treatment. Preservation of the myocardial performance Tei index indicated preserved systolic and diastolic function 2 weeks post AMI. The beneficial effects of uRDN were independent of any reductions in blood pressure. Our pilot study provides new preliminary evidence regarding the efficacy of uRDN in a preclinical large animal model of AMI that features clinically relevant comorbidities.

## 背景 / Background

The current guidelines for coronary artery revascularization of acute myocardial infarction (AMI) are percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) and subsequent dual antiplatelet therapy [1]. While PCI is the single most impactful treatment strategy to reduce mortality, it is often the case that ischemia–reperfusion injury leads to scar formation, subsequent adverse cardiac remodeling, and ultimately heart failure. The efficacy of PCI is further complicated by the increasing prevalence of comorbidities observed in patients with coronary artery disease, including hypertension and hypercholesterolemia [2]. Renal denervation (RDN) has recently emerged as an effective treatment for resistant hypertension in the clinic and holds great promise to attenuate the severity and progression of cardiovascular diseases [3]. RDN procedures are extremely safe, have essentially no side effects, consistently reduce blood pressure, and appear to be highly durable [4]. Evidence suggests that the beneficial effects of RDN therapy to reduce blood pressure may actually increase over time [5]. Furthermore, RDN therapy avoids the issue of patient noncompliance that frequently occurs with most antihypertensive medications [6].

The renal sympathetic nervous system is critically involved in the pathophysiology of acute myocardial infarction (AMI) and heart failure (HF) [7]. We and others have previously reported that radiofrequency (RF‐RDN) exerts cardioprotective effects in AMI and both cardioprotective and vasculoprotective actions in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction [8, 9, 10, 11]. We previously reported that RDN therapy attenuates myocardial infarct size in spontaneously hypertensive rats via several mechanisms that extend beyond blood pressure reduction [8]. We found that bilateral RF‐RDN administered prior to coronary ligation and reperfusion significantly attenuated myocardial oxidative stress and pro‐apoptotic signaling pathways while increasing cytoprotective eNOS‐mediated nitric oxide production in the myocardium [8]. RF‐RDN at 28 days prior to AMI reduced myocardial infarct size by 40% at 24 h post‐reperfusion and significantly improved left ventricular ejection fraction at 7 days post‐reperfusion [8].

More recently, Wang et al. [12] reported that RDN attenuates myocardial inflammation and myocardial infarct size in a porcine model of myocardial ischemia–reperfusion injury. In this study, bilateral RF‐RDN was performed following 90 min of balloon coronary artery occlusion. At 30 days following reperfusion, there was a modest reduction in myocardial infarct size and no benefit in terms of LV function measured with echocardiography and cardiac magnetic resonance. Post‐ischemic myocardial sympathetic activation and inflammation were reduced following RF‐RDN.

The present pilot study evaluated the effects of early ultrasound RDN (uRDN) therapy to treat AMI in a clinically relevant model of hypertension, obesity, and hyperlipidemia. The study is a preliminary investigation into the use of uRDN as a pretreatment for patients at high risk for AMI.

## 方法 / Methods

We and others have previously reported that RDN exerts cardioprotective and vasculoprotective actions in heart failure [7]. In the current pilot study, the utility of using a HFHS diet along with DOCA in the Göttingen mini‐swine has been described previously [13]. In brief, 10‐month‐old intact female Göttingen minipigs (12–15 kg) were acquired (Marshall BioResources, Rose, New York) and assigned to 2 groups: Sham‐RDN or uRDN (n = 5 each, total = 10). Animals were implanted with telemetry for systemic blood pressure and ECG monitoring throughout the 10‐week protocol (Figure 1A). Furthermore, all animals were fed a high fat, high salt diet (see below) to induce dyslipidemia and were administered subcutaneous deoxycorticosterone (DOCA) pellets to elicit a hypertensive phenotype. uRDN was performed 2 weeks prior to myocardial ischemia–reperfusion injury. All animal experiments were performed in accordance with the NIH Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals, the Public Health Service Policy on the Humane Care and Use of Laboratory Animals, and the Animal Welfare Act. Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee approval was obtained from the Louisiana State University Health Sciences Center–New Orleans before initiation of these experimental studies. Outcomes to be measured are designated in (Figure 1A) and further detailed methods and description of statistical analysis can be found in the Data S1.

## 结果 / Results

The combination of HFHS diet and DOCA leads to > 20 mmHg increases in arterial blood pressure and 2‐ to 4‐fold increases in circulating cholesterol levels (Figure 1B,C). Our results are very similar to previously reported observations [13]. We did not observe any effect of uRDN on either systolic or diastolic blood pressure throughout the 4‐week follow‐up between the study groups (Figure 1B). These data confirmed the manifestation of a severe hypertensive state and pathological dyslipidemia in all animals, more closely mimicking the clinical history of comorbidities seen in the patient population with increased risk for AMI.

While there are no clinically defined markers of successful renal sympathetic denervation, we performed tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) staining as a measure of nerve viability and overall activity. Herein Figure 1D,E, we observe a significant reduction in renal artery TH staining in a uRDN‐treated animal. Kidney cortex norepinephrine content was measured as an additional biomarker of sympathetic nerve overactivity. Overall, we observed a 45% reduction in total norepinephrine following uRDN predominantly in a unilateral fashion (Figure 1F). Herein, we confirmed uRDN reduces renal sympathetic nerve viability and activity.

Measurement of cardiac troponin I (cTnI) was used to quantify initial ischemia–reperfusion injury. While the uRDN‐treated animals exhibited a trending reduction in cTnI in this small pilot study cohort (i.e., n = 4–5 animals per group), there was no significant difference between groups (Figure 1G). Despite no change in cTnI, there was increased mortality within the sham‐RDN group in comparison to uRDN (Figure 1H). There was no significant difference in left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) measured by echocardiography (Figure 1I). However, the myocardial performance “Tei” index was significantly elevated in sham‐RDN animals providing an early measure of cardiac dysfunction (Figure 1J), even with the limited number of biological replicates in the control group. In using myocardial H&E staining to investigate infarct size and other structural abnormalities, we observed significant differences in epicardial infarct area (Figure 1K) and endocardial dystrophic calcification in the ischemic zone in the sham‐RDN compared to uRDN animals (Figure 1L).

## 讨论 / Discussion

Hypertension remains the most widespread and predominant cardiovascular disease condition often leading to increased risk of stroke, kidney disease, myocardial infarction, heart failure, and death [14]. Effective treatment of hypertension is associated with reductions in cardiovascular morbidity and mortality, yet effective blood pressure control remains elusive and represents a major unmet need in cardiovascular disease [14]. Percutaneous, catheter‐based renal neuromodulation devices using either radiofrequency or ultrasound energy have emerged as a novel approach to treat patients in whom lifestyle modifications and anti‐hypertensive agents do not adequately control blood pressure [15]. The US Food and Drug Administration recently approved 2 renal denervation systems including an ultrasound‐based uRDN device (i.e., Recor Paradise) and the multipolar radiofrequency (RF) RDN catheter (Medtronic Spyral) with approvals in November 2023 [15]. The uRDN system emits ultrasound energy through the main renal arteries effectively destroying the surrounding renal nerves and significantly reducing blood pressure and thereby avoiding the need to introduce the catheter into renal artery side branches [16]. The water‐filled cooling balloon of the Paradise uRDN catheter achieves a circumferential ring of ablation at a depth of 1–6 mm outward from the vessel lumen [16]. The uRDN system involves a 50–60 min procedure time with a very low complication rate (i.e., 1%) and results in blood pressure reductions of approximately 6.3 mmHg in untreated hypertension, and reductions of approximately 4.5 mmHg in resistant hypertension [15, 16, 17]. The use of anti‐hypertensive medications was significantly reduced following uRDN [17] and the efficacy of uRDN appears to improve over time. To date, there have been 4 randomized, sham‐controlled clinical trials of uRDN that include: RADIANCE‐HTN, SOLO, RADIANCE‐HTN TRIO, and RADIANCE II; all met the primary endpoint of a greater reduction in daytime ambulatory systolic blood pressure at 2 months post‐uRDN [3, 16, 17, 18]. Thus, uRDN represents a novel approach to effectively treat resistant hypertension and potentially attenuate the incidence of stroke, AMI, and heart failure. In the present study, we sought to evaluate the potential effectiveness of uRDN prior to coronary artery occlusion and reperfusion in a clinically relevant swine model of severe hypertension and hyperlipidemia. This preliminary pilot study was designed to explore another application for this catheter‐based neuromodulation device and attenuate myocardial reperfusion injury and halt the progression from AMI to heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF).

Activation of the sympathetic nervous system is a primary compensatory response to myocardial ischemia–reperfusion injury that results from a multitude of afferent signals including baroreceptor reflex, altered peripheral organ perfusion, and an inability to maintain cardiac output and mean arterial pressure. Chronic overactivation of the sympathetic nervous system exacerbates cardiac remodeling and alters multiple systemic neurohormonal signaling cascades which originate in other critical organs like the kidneys [19]. Many therapeutic strategies to limit infarct size and therefore preserve cardiac structure and function have failed to translate from the bench to bedside [20]. Clinical efforts have been focused on early reperfusion and neurohormonal modulation through pharmacotherapies to mitigate the manifestation and progression of inevitable heart failure in AMI patients. Despite proven efficacy, untoward side effects and lifelong drug regimens lead to staggering low compliance [21]. Many of these neurohormonal drug regimens are also used to combat hypertension. Patients with hypertension and several other modifiable risk factors including obesity and dyslipidemia are at a greater risk of suffering an AMI [22]. Novel therapeutic strategies which target sympathetic overactivation without the inherent limitations of approved pharmacotherapies and may be used to attenuate other comorbidities in high‐risk patients warrant exploration.

We and others have performed preclinical investigations confirming the blood pressure lowering effects of RDN [8, 9, 23]. More intriguingly, we have demonstrated in preclinical small and large animal models robust efficacy in the context of AMI and heart failure (HF), independent of the blood pressure lowering actions of RDN therapy [9, 10]. We have previously demonstrated that radiofrequency (RF) RDN attenuates oxidative stress, G protein‐coupled receptor kinase 2 signaling, increases cardiac nitric oxide, and ameliorates myocardial ischemia–reperfusion injury in spontaneously hypertensive rats [8]. Our group has also reported that delayed RF‐RDN therapy following the onset of heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) in spontaneously hypertensive rats attenuated renal neprilysin activity, increased circulating natriuretic peptide levels, and significantly improved left ventricular and vascular function [9]. We observed similar results in a porcine model of HFrEF utilizing normotensive swine subjected to myocardial infarction and delayed treatment with the EnligHTN RF‐RDN catheter resulting in blockade of renin‐angiotensin pathway signaling and cardiac fibrosis resulting in significant improvements in LVEF [10]. We have also demonstrated that RF‐RDN therapy following the onset of HFrEF was synergistic with cardiosphere‐derived stem cells resulting in robust reductions in cardiac fibrosis and significant improvements in left ventricular function in both normotensive and hypertensive rats [11]. These findings are corroborated by independent laboratories demonstrating antiarrhythmic effects of RDN independent of blood pressure lowering [24]. Given the association of hypertension with major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) (i.e., AMI), we investigated the cardioprotective efficacy of ultrasound renal denervation (uRDN) performed prior to AMI in a preclinical hypertensive and dyslipidemic swine model.

Despite the small sample size in the present study, we did observe beneficial effects and some trends for benefit. We found that pretreatment with the Recor Paradise uRDN catheter system reduced the extent of myocardial infarct size at 2 weeks following AMI in swine subjected to hypertension and dyslipidemia. Furthermore, we also observed strong trends in terms of cardiac troponin I, reduced mortality following AMI, and improved left ventricular performance. This preliminary pilot study provides novel insights regarding the potential effectiveness for the use of RDN in patients with increased risk of AMI. Given these positive preliminary data, it would be highly valuable to perform additional studies to potentially confirm the beneficial effect (s) of uRDN on initial injury to AMI in the presence of hypertension and dyslipidemia in a larger cohort of animals. Future preclinical studies are required to provide further evidence of benefit in utilizing uRDN in the context of AMI.
