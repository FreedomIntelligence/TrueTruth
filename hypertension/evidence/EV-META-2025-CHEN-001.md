## English Abstract

To evaluate maternal–fetal outcomes and therapeutic efficacy in Takayasu arteritis (TA)-complicated pregnancies through integrated retrospective analysis and meta-analytic synthesis.

A dual-design study was conducted: (1) retrospective analysis of 20 pregnancies (17 patients) at West China Second Hospital (2012–2024), stratifying TA phases (acute/prolonged/stable); (2) systematic review with random-effects meta-analysis of 16 studies (568 pregnancies globally). Clinical data encompassed maternal–fetal profiles, TA-specific variables, laboratory metrics (hematologic/coagulation parameters), and therapies (glucocorticoids /immunosuppressants /antihypertensives). Outcomes were compared against normative standards using t-tests, Wilcoxon, chi-square, and meta-regression.

Among 20 pregnancies (median maternal age 28.5 years), 50% had at least one obstetric complication, with arterial stenosis (80%) and hypertension (40%) predominant. Meta-analysis revealed 42.6% adverse outcomes: gestational hypertension (26.1%), fetal growth restriction (17.7%), and preterm delivery (13.6%). Hematological analysis (n = 20) showed elevated WBC, PCT, TT, fibrinogen, urinary protein, and ALT (all P < 0.05), alongside reduced PT, albumin, and bilirubin (P < 0.05). Regarding the analysis results of inflammatory indicators, CRP (prepartum) (95%CI = 0.969–1.034, OR = 1.001), CRP (postpartum) (95%CI = 0.920–1.217, OR = 1.058), and ESR (95%CI = 0.952–1.101, OR = 1.024) showed no statistically significant association with pregnancy outcomes. Neither pre-pregnancy nor gestational glucocorticoids (prednisone vs methylprednisolone) or immunosuppressants significantly reduced complications (all RR 95% CI crossed 1; P > 0.05). Antihypertensive therapy showed no correlation with preeclampsia (P > 0.05).

TA significantly elevates maternal–fetal risks, driving hypertension, growth restriction, and preterm birth via vasculopathic-inflammatory pathways. Postpartum hypercoagulability (↑fibrinogen, ↓prothrombin time) necessitates multidisciplinary coagulation monitoring and mandatory thromboprophylaxis.

## 背景 / Background

Takayasu Arteritis (TA) is a chronic granulomatous vasculitis predominantly affecting the aorta and its primary branches. The disease exhibits a marked female predilection, with concentration at reproductive age (20–30 years), and demonstrates higher prevalence among Asian populations, particularly those of Eastern descent [1]. Pathological features involve transmural inflammation of arterial walls, leading to progressive luminal stenosis, vascular occlusion, and aneurysm formation, which collectively elevate cardiovascular morbidity and mortality risks [2]. In accordance with the 2021 American College of Rheumatology/Vasculitis Foundation (ACR/VF) guidelines for Takayasu arteritis (TA) management, disease progression was stratified into three phases: acute phase (active inflammation, 0–3 years post-diagnosis), prolonged phase (remission, 3–9 years), and stable phase (chronic scarring, 9–12 years) [3]. While precise etiology remains elusive, current evidence implicates complex interactions among genetic susceptibility (particularly HLA associations), environmental triggers, and autoimmune dysregulation [4].

Pregnancy complicated by TA constitutes a high-risk clinical scenario with significant maternal–fetal complication s [5]. Maternal complications arise predominantly from impaired organ perfusion secondary to vascular stenosis, manifesting as accelerated hypertension, decompensated heart failure, and cerebrovascular events [6]. Fetal sequelae include intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), preterm delivery, and stillbirth, with miscarriage rates exceeding population baselines by 3.5-fold [7]. A comparative cohort studies demonstrated significantly elevated risks of gestational hypertension, preeclampsia, and placental insufficiency [8]. Pregnancy-induced hemodynamic adaptations leading to increased circulating blood volume and cardiac output, frequently exacerbate pre-existing cardiovascular abnormalities in TA patients [9].

Existing management guidelines derive predominantly from small retrospective series, with limited characterization of therapeutic outcomes [10]. Critical knowledge gaps persist regarding: 1) Differential effects of glucocorticoid regimens (dexamethasone sodium phosphate vs. prednisone vs. pulse methylprednisolone) on maternal disease activity and fetal outcomes; 2) Safety profiles of immunosuppressive agents (cyclophosphamide, azathioprine, tacrolimus, sirolimus, mycophenolate mofetil) during organogenesis and lactation; 3) Efficacy of adjuvant therapies including antihypertensives, antiplatelet agents, and statins in mitigating vascular complications [11]

This study aims to conduct a comprehensive evaluation of maternal–fetal outcomes in TA-complicated pregnancies through a observational study, systematic literature review, and meta-analytic synthesis.

## 方法 / Methods

This retrospective study analyzed 17 patients with Takayasu arteritis (TA)-complicated pregnancies treated at the Department of Obstetrics and Gynecology, West China Second Hospital, Sichuan University, between January 2012 and October 2024. The study included 20 pregnancies occurring after TA diagnosis, with longitudinal data spanning from initial symptom onset to obstetric outcomes. All subjects met the 1990 American College of Rheumatology (ACR) classification criteria for TA, which requires fulfillment of ≥ 3 diagnostic parameters: (1) disease onset < 40 years; (2) limb claudication; (3) diminished brachial artery pulse; (4) inter-limb systolic blood pressure difference > 10 mmHg; (5) subclavian/aortic bruits; (6) angiographic confirmation of aortic/primary branch stenosis/occlusion, excluding atherosclerotic or fibromuscular etiologies [12].

Azathioprine remains an important conventional immunosuppressant in the treatment of vasculitides, including ANCA-associated vasculitis (AAV), eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA), and Takayasu's arteritis (TAK). In AAV, while rituximab is significantly superior to azathioprine for relapse prevention, particularly in patients with relapsing disease, azathioprine serves as an alternative option when rituximab is inaccessible or contraindicated [13]. For EGPA, azathioprine demonstrates comparable overall remission rates to methotrexate; although methotrexate has a faster onset and superior steroid-sparing effect, azathioprine may offer better control against asthma/ENT relapses [14]. In TAK, azathioprine and methotrexate show similar efficacy in remission induction, relapse prevention, and radiographic progression, yet the azathioprine group is associated with a higher rate of subsequent vascular interventions [15], suggesting it may be a less preferred first-line option for patients at risk of vascular complications. Thus, azathioprine retains its clinical value across different vasculitides, though it is often outperformed by biologic agents or methotrexate in key outcomes such as relapse prevention, steroid dose reduction, and avoidance of vascular procedures. Clinical decision-making should be individualized based on the specific vasculitis type, relapse risk, and patient tolerance.

Obstetric complications were defined as the occurrence of the following issues during pregnancy or delivery: (1) hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP), including preeclampsia or eclampsia [3]; (2) pregnancy loss (spontaneous abortion); (3) preterm birth (delivery occurring prior to 34 completed weeks of gestation); (4) medically indicated pregnancy termination; (5) fetal growth restriction (estimated fetal weight < 10th percentile for gestational age); or (6) intrauterine fetal death.

Preeclampsia was diagnosed according to American College of Obstetrics and Gynecology (ACOG) criteria [16], with newly-onset hypertension (systolic blood pressure ≥ 140 mmHg and/or diastolic ≥ 90 mmHg) after 20 weeks of gestation, accompanied by either proteinuria (≥ 300 mg/24-h urine collection or protein/creatinine ratio ≥ 0.3) or end-organ dysfunction. Eclampsia was defined as unexplained generalized tonic–clonic seizures in patients with preeclampsia.

Maternal complications encompassed the following: (1) cesarean section-associated morbidity (postpartum hemorrhage [blood loss ≥ 1000 mL], hematoma formation, or peripartum hysterectomy); (2) newly-onset or exacerbated arterial hypertension necessitating ≥ 1 additional antihypertensive agent; (3) venous thromboembolism (VTE), including deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism; (4) de novo aneurysm formation or progression of preexisting aneurysms; (5) cerebrovascular events (ischemic/hemorrhagic stroke or transient ischemic attack). These complications were considered potentially attributable to physiological adaptations of pregnancy, exacerbation of preexisting conditions, or pregnancy-specific pathological processes.

Given the importance of assessing disease activity in Takayasu arteritis (TA) for analyzing pregnancy outcomes, we prioritized selecting clinically common and readily accessible inflammatory markers—C-reactive protein (CRP, including two key time points: prepartum and postpartum) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)—as surrogate indicators to indirectly reflect disease activity. This choice not only aligns with routine clinical monitoring practices for TA but also accounts for the practical constraint of marker availability in retrospective studies.

However, due to the inherent retrospective nature of the study (a design that relies on the completeness of historical clinical records rather than prospective standardized data collection) and incomplete information in some patients’ past medical archives, we encountered unavoidable limitations: On one hand, most patients did not undergo systematic pre-pregnancy standardized disease activity scoring for TA (such as the internationally recognized ITAS2010 criteria, which requires comprehensive evaluation based on symptoms, signs, and multi-dimensional indicators and demands high completeness of medical records). On the other hand, even for CRP and ESR—relatively basic inflammatory markers—our team made every effort to trace relevant data by meticulously reviewing each included patient’s inpatient medical records, outpatient follow-up notes, and laboratory test reports one by one. Despite these efforts, we only managed to collect valid test results (including prepartum CRP, postpartum CRP, and ESR data) from a small subset of patients.

Although these limited data still provided a foundation for exploring the association between inflammatory markers and pregnancy outcomes, the objective limitation of insufficient data coverage may have impacted the statistical power of the results to some extent. We hereby transparently disclose this research limitation: this not only shows our respect for data authenticity but also reflects our earnest efforts to mine valid information as much as possible and ensure research rigor under the constraints of existing conditions.

Continuous variables were reported as median (IQR) and assessed for normality using the one-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov test. Normally distributed variables (p > 0.05) were analyzed with a one-sample t-test, while non-normally distributed variables (p < 0.05) were analyzed using the one-sample Wilcoxon signed-rank and Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests. Collected data were compared with the reference values The Report on the Health Status of Chinese Residents (2023) [17] was utilized as the population standard in one-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov or Wilcoxon signed-rank analyses. Statistical significance was defined as a two-tailed p < 0.05. Categorical variables are presented as count (percent) and are compared between groups using Fisher's exact test.

In cases of limited sample size (expected cell counts < 5 in contingency tables), Fisher’s exact test was employed for categorical comparisons. All statistical analyses were performed using IBM SPSS Statistics software, version 29.0.1.0.

According to the search strategy outlined in the research protocol (Fig. 1), a search was conducted in PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, and ClinicalTrials.gov databases. The search was cut off on September 2024. The literature screening process was strictly carried out in accordance with the standards of the PRISMA statement. All retrieved and manually added literature was initially screened independently by three members based on titles, abstracts, and keywords. The literature that passed the initial screening would then be independently screened by two members based on the full text. Any inconsistent opinions that emerged during the screening process would be discussed by all members. Duplicate records were identified and removed using EndNote X9 software, followed by automated literature management. Data extraction and statistical analyses were conducted using Stata 15.0.

Eligible participants were pregnant women diagnosed with Takayasu’s arteritis (TA) according to the 1990 American College of Rheumatology (ACR) criteria or Ishikawa classification criteria. Eligible study designs included observational studies and case–control studies. Included studies were required to report maternal age, gravidity, parity, pre-pregnancy illness of TA duration, and at least one of the following pregnancy outcomes: preterm birth (< 34 gestational weeks), low birth weight (< 2500 g), gestational hypertension (blood pressure ≥ 140/90 mmHg), preeclampsia, fetal growth restriction (estimated fetal weight < 10th percentile for gestational age), cesarean delivery rate, neonatal asphyxia (1-min Apgar score ≤ 7), or stillbirth. Only English-language publications with complete datasets were included. Studies were excluded for duplicate publication, insufficient data, inconsistent study design, or ambiguous diagnostic criteria.

Two investigators independently extracted the following data: 1. Study characteristics: first author, publication year, study location, and design. 2. Participant characteristics: sample size, maternal age, gravidity, parity and smoking history. 3. TA-related parameters: diagnostic criteria, vascular subtypes, disease duration, and treatment regimens. 4. Surgical details: Cesarean section rate and anesthesia methods. 5. Pregnancy outcomes: Preterm birth, low birth weight, gestational hypertension, preeclampsia, FGR, neonatal asphyxia, stillbirth, and postoperative complications (e.g., hemorrhage). 6. Comorbidities and medications: chronic hypertension, diabetes, and relevant pharmacotherapies.

Effect sizes and follow-up data were recorded for all outcomes. For studies lacking explicit effect sizes, estimates were derived from available data. Discrepancies between investigators were resolved through cross-verification. Authors with incomplete data were contacted for clarification.

Dichotomous outcomes (e.g., preterm birth, low birth weight) were analyzed using event rates (ERs). Continuous outcomes (e.g., neonatal birth weight) were summarized as means or medians with 95% confidence intervals (CIs).

Meta-analyses employed random-effects models to account for interstudy heterogeneity, with results visualized via forest plots. Heterogeneity was quantified using the I2 statistic (I2 > 50% indicating substantial heterogeneity). To address potential heterogeneity in maternal–fetal outcomes relative to the timing of Takayasu arteritis (TA) diagnosis, we conducted a subgroup analysis stratifying pregnancies into two groups: those occurring before TA diagnosis (pre-TA) and those occurring after TA diagnosis (post-TA). Outcomes of interest included adverse pregnancy outcomes, gestational hypertension, preeclampsia, and fetal growth restriction (FGR). Pooled event rates (ERs) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were calculated using random-effects models. Between-group differences were assessed using meta-regression or subgroup interaction tests where applicable. Heterogeneity was quantified using the I2 statistic.

Sensitivity analyses assessed result stability by sequentially excluding individual studies or those with high risk of bias.

Publication bias was evaluated via funnel plots and quantified using Egger’s or Begg’s tests. Asymmetry in funnel plots prompted discussion of potential causes (e.g., small-study effects). Adjusted analyses were conducted if significant bias was detected.

All statistical analyses were performed using Stata 15.0 (StataCorp). Results for primary outcomes were presented in three-line tables.

Association of therapeutic interventions with pregnancy outcomes in Takayasu arteritis

It is noteworthy that this study has key data gaps in the assessment of disease activity and comparative analysis of treatment regimens, which impose certain limitations on the interpretation of factors influencing outcomes. Firstly, due to the inherent limitations of the retrospective study design, the included patients lacked standardized disease activity records (e.g., ITAS2010 scores) from pre-pregnancy to the peripartum period, making it impossible to clarify the association between disease activity status (active/inactive) and maternal–fetal outcomes—an association that is crucial for risk stratification in TA-complicated pregnancies. Secondly, treatment regimen data showed that all patients using DMARDs were concurrently treated with glucocorticoids, and there was no 'DMARDs-only treatment subgroup'. Additionally, the sample size of DMARDs users was only 13, which not only prevented a direct efficacy comparison between DMARDs and glucocorticoids but also made it difficult to rule out the confounding effect of combined medication on outcomes. ## 讨论 / Discussion

Takayasu arteritis (TA) complicating pregnancy presents a complex clinical scenario, and our study findings underscore the need for nuanced interpretation of therapeutic interventions. Notably, Table 4 reveals no significant differences in obstetric outcomes between prednisone and methylprednisolone regimens (both pre-pregnancy and gestational), nor between immunosuppressant use or antihypertensive therapy groups. This lack of statistical significance likely stems from the small sample size (e.g., n = 17 for glucocorticoid comparisons), compounded by the low incidence of TA and the retrospective study design. Heterogeneity in disease activity phases (acute, prolonged, stable) across patients may also obscure treatment effects, as inflammatory burden and vascular involvement severity could overshadow regimen-specific impacts. Additionally, standardized glucocorticoid dosing and close monitoring of disease activity in our cohort might have mitigated inter-regimen variability, highlighting the need for larger studies to detect subtle treatment differences.

Our single-center analysis first demonstrates that arterial stenosis (80%) and hypertension (40%) were predominant maternal manifestations, with 50% of pregnancies experiencing ≥ 1 obstetric complication. A systematic review of our single-center cohort identified diabetes as the primary comorbidity unrelated to TA, affecting 25% of pregnancies (n = 5/20; Table 1). Notably, in this small subgroup, no significant correlation was observed between diabetes and adverse maternal–fetal outcomes (e.g., preterm birth, FGR, preeclampsia) (P > 0.05). Given the lack of statistical significance and the limited sample size of this subgroup, we classified diabetes as a baseline characteristic of the cohort rather than conducting a separate stratified analysis in the main text. This finding aligns with the broader pathophysiological framework of TA-complicated pregnancies, where vascular inflammation and consequent placental insufficiency are the dominant drivers of adverse outcomes—potentially overshadowing the incremental risk of diabetes in this specific population. However, it is important to acknowledge that the small sample size of diabetic patients limits the generalizability of this observation; larger multicenter studies with stratified analyses of comorbidities (including diabetes) are needed to clarify their independent impact on TA-associated pregnancy outcomes. Hematological parameters (Tables 2 and 3) revealed significant elevations in WBC, PCT, TT, Fg, urinary protein, and ALT, alongside reductions in PT, albumin, and total bilirubin, indicating a systemic inflammatory and hypercoagulable state. This aligns with the pathophysiology of TA, where vascular inflammation and endothelial dysfunction exacerbate placental ischemia and thrombotic risk, contributing to fetal growth restriction (FGR) and preterm birth. The observed hyperfibrinogenemia and altered coagulation markers directly link to the heightened postpartum thromboembolic risk noted in our cohort.

Subsequently, the meta-analysis integrating 16 studies (n = 568 pregnancies) corroborates these findings, reporting a 42.6% incidence of adverse outcomes, primarily gestational hypertension (26.1%), FGR (17.7%), and preterm delivery (13.6%). The consistency between our single-center data and global meta-analysis highlights the generalizability of TA-related maternal–fetal risks, despite geographic and ethnic variations. Both datasets emphasize that active disease during pregnancy correlates with poorer outcomes, reinforcing the importance of preconception disease remission.

Overlapping themes across analyses include the central role of vascular inflammation in driving placental insufficiency and the limitations of current therapeutic strategies. While glucocorticoids and immunosuppressants are standard of care, their efficacy in modifying pregnancy outcomes remains unproven in our study, likely due to the aforementioned sample constraints. The meta-analysis further underscores heterogeneity in study designs and disease activity assessments, which may influence outcome reporting.

This study bears inherent limitations. The retrospective design and small sample size (20 pregnancies) restrict statistical power, particularly for subgroup analyses, as it restricts statistical power for subgroup analyses (e.g., comparisons of glucocorticoid regimens or immunosuppressant efficacy) and limits the ability to draw definitive conclusions about therapeutic interventions. This constraint is inherently linked to the low prevalence of Takayasu arteritis (TA) — a rare disease with an estimated incidence of 0.4–2.6 cases per 100,000 individuals annually [4] — particularly among reproductive-aged women, which makes large-scale single-center enrollment challenging.

To partially mitigate this limitation, we integrated a global meta-analysis encompassing 16 studies and 568 pregnancies, which provided a broader perspective on TA-associated maternal–fetal risks (e.g., 42.6% adverse outcome rate, predominantly gestational hypertension, fetal growth restriction, and preterm birth). This synthesis enhanced the generalizability of our findings, as it captured geographic and ethnic diversity (e.g., data from France, Brazil, Turkey, India, and China) that complemented our single-center, Asian-only cohort. Notably, the meta-analysis corroborated our key single-center observations — including the high prevalence of arterial stenosis (80% in our cohort vs. consistent vascular involvement across meta-analysis studies) and postpartum hypercoagulability (elevated fibrinogen, reduced prothrombin time) — reinforcing the validity of our conclusions despite the small single-center sample. Furthermore, as the largest maternal and children's specialty hospital and a referral center for critical obstetric care in Western China, our institution manages a relatively large cohort of pregnant patients with Takayasu's arteritis, which may help mitigate the typical limitations of a single-center study by enhancing the generalizability of our findings to this specific regional population.

Nevertheless, we recognize that expanding the sample size remains critical to address unresolved questions, such as the differential efficacy of biologic agents (e.g., TNF-α inhibitors) in TA-complicated pregnancies or the independent impact of comorbidities like diabetes on outcomes. Looking forward, future research should prioritize multicenter registries to accumulate sufficient sample sizes for robust analyses. Incorporating advanced biomarkers (e.g., VEGF, cell-free DNA) and placental perfusion imaging (e.g., MRI) could refine risk stratification and mechanistic understanding. Prospective studies evaluating biologic agents (e.g., TNF-α inhibitors) in TA-complicated pregnancies are urgently needed to optimize therapeutic algorithms. Concurrently, basic science investigations into the immunogenetic and inflammatory pathways underlying TA-placental interactions may inform targeted preventive strategies, ultimately improving maternal–fetal outcomes in this high-risk population.

This study systematically assessed the effect of timing of Takayasu arteritis (TA) diagnosis on pregnancy outcomes using subgroup analyses. The results showed that the incidence of adverse pregnancy outcomes in patients who were pregnant before TA diagnosis was significantly higher than that in patients who were pregnant after TA diagnosis. This difference may be related to disease activity, timing of therapeutic intervention, and intensity of clinical monitoring.

Preeclampsia and fetal growth restriction (FGR) did not differ significantly between groups.This may due to: ① some patients in the post-TA group had their first clinical manifestations during pregnancy, and the degree of vascular lesions overlapped with those in the pre-TA group; ② The limited sample size may reduce the statistical power of the test; ③ The differences between the two groups were partially bridged by similar symptomatic treatment (antihypertensive drugs, anticoagulation, etc.).

In addition, post-TA patients usually received systematic management by a multidisciplinary team after diagnosis, including collaborative follow-up of rheumatology and individualized immunosuppressive therapy, which may effectively control the disease progression and improve pregnancy outcomes. The study of Isobe, M [33] also emphasized that disease stability and standardized treatment before pregnancy are the key to improve the prognosis of TA pregnant women. In the future, more prospective studies are needed to further clarify the independent effect of diagnostic timing on TA pregnancy outcomes on the basis of standardized disease activity assessment.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Pregnancies complicated by Takayasu arteritis (TA) impose significant maternal–fetal risks, with observed adverse outcomes including gestational hypertension, fetal growth restriction (FGR), preterm birth, and heightened thrombotic complications, which collectively elevate maternal cardiovascular morbidity and fetal-neonatal mortality/morbidity. These findings underscore the urgency of recognizing TA as a high-risk condition requiring specialized perinatal management. Optimal care necessitates preconception counseling to assess disease activity, vigilant antenatal monitoring of vascular and coagulation parameters, and tailored pharmacotherapy balancing disease control with fetal safety. This study highlights the need for multidisciplinary collaboration to mitigate vascular inflammation-driven complications, while identifying critical knowledge gaps in therapeutic optimization that warrant further translational research.
