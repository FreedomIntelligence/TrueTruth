## English Abstract

Telemedicine has strong potential to improve hypertension management, yet its uptake into routine care remains limited. Implementation strategies (IS) enhance evidence‐based intervention (EBI) use. Identifying IS used in pre‐implementation trials and understanding whether these trials are explanatory or pragmatic can inform the expedited translation of EBI. Despite the availability of tools to evaluate trial design and report IS, clinical researchers often face challenges in systematically describing IS from pre‐implementation studies.

Using the Pragmatic Implementation Strategy Reporting tool (PISRT), we describe a step‐by‐step approach to retrospectively report IS from 13 telemedicine trials of hypertension management identified from a systematic review. For each trial, we also mapped nine trial design domains along efficacy to effectiveness spectrum using the PRagmatic Explanatory Continuum Indicator Summary–2 (PRECIS–2) tool. Three clinical researchers new to implementation science underwent short training on implementation science methods including the Expert Recommendation for Implementation Change (ERIC) project, supervised by a fourth clinical researcher with working implementation science knowledge. The clinical researchers clarified important implementation science concepts, terminologies, PRECIS–2 domains and data harmonizing approaches across trials guided by published literature, lectures and group discussions during pilot coding sessions. After individual data extraction, the clinical researchers met to select and operationalize IS and trial design domains along the efficacy to effectiveness spectrum using consensus achieved through group discussions. We partnered with two implementation science experts to refine our approach and elaborated each ERIC IS by providing relevant examples in implementing IS in telemedicine management of hypertension intervention. We also defined intervention components for each trial to help distinguish EBI from IS.

We describe a practical manual for systematically reporting IS and attributes of trial designs from efficacy and effectiveness telemedicine trials for hypertension management. We provide a step‐by‐step approach to support clinical researchers in reporting IS and assessing the readiness of trials evaluating an EBI for real‐world use. Future work should evaluate the effectiveness of our methods, including implementation science experts evaluating any discordance with our IS reporting.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension control continues to be a significant challenge, with recent data showing a declining trend in blood pressure (BP) control among US adults [1]. Telemedicine has emerged as a promising strategy for improving hypertension control [2, 3, 4], with growing evidence indicating it can be more effective than traditional clinic‐based care in achieving BP control. Yet, the uptake of telemedicine hypertension management in clinical practice has been slower than anticipated. Factors such as billing and reimbursement, access to technology, lack of integration into existing workflows, data privacy concerns, fear of losing in‐person interaction, and limited digital literacy among patients and providers continue to hinder its widespread implementation in routine care [4, 5, 6, 7].

Recognizing that the uptake of evidence‐based interventions (EBIs) into routine practice is an active and context‐dependent process, implementation science has emerged as a distinct discipline, defined as ‘the scientific study of methods to promote the systematic uptake of research findings and other EBIs into routine practice' [8, 9]. Implementation science aims to expedite the integration of EBIs like telemedicine into routine clinical practice. Implementation strategies (IS) are essential methods to facilitate EBIs adoption into real‐world settings [9, 10, 11], and are traditionally identified and evaluated following efficacy (explanatory) and effectiveness (pragmatic) studies.

Identifying and reporting IS used in efficacy and effectiveness research is one way to shorten the time for EBI use [12]. However, while IS are frequently used in efficacy and effectiveness studies, they are often underreported [13]. Inconsistent or incomplete reporting hinders IS use [14, 15, 16, 17, 18]. Implementation researchers have called on clinical researchers to report IS earlier during efficacy and effectiveness trials, both prospectively during trial design, and retrospectively after the trial results are published, including summarizing IS used in studies identified from systematic reviews using tools such as the Pragmatic Implementation Strategy Reporting Tool (PISRT) [12]. The PISRT combines several core implementation research frameworks and taxonomies. It pulls IS names, definitions and ancillary information from the Expert Recommendations for Implementing Change (ERIC) project [11]. To operationalize IS, the PISRT combines guidance from Proctor et al.'s Implementation Strategy Specification [10] and Presseau et al.'s Action, Actor, Context, Target, Time framework [19]. The PISRT encourages researchers to operationalize each strategy by reporting: action (describe the IS), actor (who delivers the strategy), context (setting where action is performed), dose (frequency and intensity), action targets (conceptual models of implementation that mechanistically explain how IS effect implementation outcomes and whom the strategy is targeted at), temporality (timing of the strategy in relation with other strategies or other elements of the intervention or implementation phase), implementation outcomes (implementation outcomes targeted by the strategy) and justification (empirical, theoretical or pragmatic justifications for the choice of IS) [10, 19]. To our knowledge, although the PISRT has been cited > 60 times, it has been used to report IS only a few times and primarily used by implementation researchers [13, 20]. This limited use suggests that the tool is underutilized by clinical researchers and clinicians, the intended audience, hindering the efforts to accelerate research‐to‐practice translation.

The PRagmatic Explanatory Continuum Indicator Summary‐2 (PRECIS‐2) tool assesses how close a clinical trial design evaluating an EBI is to real‐world practice [21]. This information helps determine the likelihood of EBI adoption in the real world [21, 22]. The PRECIS‐2 tool maps nine trial design domains along a spectrum from very explanatory to very pragmatic (scores 1−5, respectively), including: eligibility (who is eligible), recruitment (how are participants recruited), setting (where is the trial conducted), organization (what experience and resources are needed to deliver the intervention), flexibility in intervention delivery (how much flexibility is there to deliver the intervention), flexibility in adherence (how important is it to monitor the adherence to the intervention), follow‐up (how closely are participants followed‐up), primary outcome (how relevant is the outcome to participants) and primary analysis (is intention‐to‐treat analysis used) [21]. Given that pragmatic trial findings are more generalizable than explanatory trials [21], understanding where the design of a trial evaluating an EBI falls along the explanatory to pragmatic spectrum is essential for assessing the readiness of EBI use in routine practice. Unfortunately, labelling trials as ‘explanatory or pragmatic without formal assessment’ [23, 24], and ‘insufficient and inconsistent reporting of IS and their operationalization’ [17, 18, 25] are both common, resulting in a barrier in EBI translation. Therefore, providing practical guidance that integrates trial design information with IS used in pre‐implementation studies can enhance the consistency of their reporting.

In this methodological manuscript, we describe the processes for identifying and reporting IS used in explanatory and pragmatic trials of telemedicine management of hypertension. Specifically, we detailed the process of (1) systematically reporting and operationalizing IS from efficacy and effectiveness trials using the PISRT, (2) mapping the nine trial design domains for each trial using the PRECIS‐2 tool, (3) assessing agreement between clinical researchers and implementation science experts in reporting and operationalization of IS and (4) evaluating agreement in the PRECIS‐2 scores and IS operationalization between trial conduct (assessed by trial PI, if available) versus reported (based on our data extraction). An additional aim was to provide practical guidance to clinical researchers and practitioners undertaking similar efforts.

## 方法 / Methods

A quantitative content analysis approach informed our study design [26]. To select an IS and its operationalization or to assign a PRECIS score, we utilized a group consensus‐building approach frequently used in civic activities, which involves thoughtful deliberations and incorporates diverse perspectives to aid in decision‐making with the aim of achieving agreement among the group [27]. We used such deliberations after extracting trial design characteristics and IS data from 13 previously identified US‐based telemedicine trials for hypertension management from a systematic review [28]. All eligible trials from the systematic review were included, regardless of whether the telemedicine intervention was clinically superior to clinic‐based care. We registered the study protocol with PROSPERO (registration number: CRD42022372552). A detailed account of the methodological approach is available in Additional File S1. Broadly, our processes can be summarized into the following three phases (Figure 1).

Training, pilot testing and data extraction.

The principal investigator (Y.P.) is an early‐career clinical researcher with a growing focus on implementation science. The remaining three coders (S.W., A.E., V.S.) had no prior experience in implementation research. Coder's training covered efficacy versus effectiveness trials, PRECIS‐2 tool and important implementation science concepts, such as frameworks, outcomes, ERIC strategies and the PISRT model. Training included lectures and reviewing important papers (Additional Files S2−S3).

Each coder independently extracted data from two trials and later discussed the process iteratively in five group meetings, which helped us create and refine the coding manuals and tools (Additional Files S4−S12). The coders demonstrated the ability to follow the steps outlined in the coding manuals before proceeding with data extraction.

Each coder independently extracted PRECIS‐2 and IS data from each trial following the coding manuals and tools before meeting in a group (Figure 1). Each coder discussed their data, and the group reached a final agreement via consensus after attaining approval from each coder [27]. Reaching a consensus through such deliberations rather than achieving a majority or quantitative agreement is a rigorous approach that fosters diversity in perspectives [17].

We embedded several templates within data extraction tools. These include tables that reminded the coders what contents were relevant for each data element and the agreed‐upon data after coding a prior trial to facilitate consistent coding across trials (e.g., Additional Files S7 and S10). We used multiple approaches to address homonymy (multiple meanings for the same IS), synonymy (different terms suggesting same or overlapping IS), and instability (unpredictable changes in the foregoing) for IS within and across trials (Figure 1, Table 1).

We invited two implementation science experts (C.O., B.N.R.) to evaluate our IS coding manual, tools, processes and IS and its operationalized data. After the initial review, they suggested clarifying two items before full review: (1) differentiate EBI from IS when coding, and (2) code only explicitly communicated IS and not implied ones. We subsequently elaborated the core components of telemedicine intervention for each trial (Additional File S11). Two clinical researchers (Y.P. and R.A.) then revisited the data, considering only those explicitly communicated IS [13].

Both clinical and implementation science researchers thought that it would be helpful to clarify all 73 ERIC IS in the context of our EBI. Accordingly, the implementation science experts first provided hypothetical examples of all 73 ERIC IS related to the telemedicine intervention, which was later modified by the study PI. The lead author of the ERIC project then reviewed and provided clarifications of the 73 IS examples. We presented this as the ‘Understand It’ column in the PISRT with ‘Ancillary Material’ providing clues indicating a given IS and tips to distinguish one IS from other similar IS (Additional File S12). The presented IS in the ‘Understand It’ column represents one possible example.

At the time of manuscript submission, the clinical research team was in the final phase of data extraction. The implementation science experts' review and written input on IS data coded by clinical researchers has not yet been conducted and will occur in the next phase of the study. To provide directions for future research, the PI of a trial [29], who is a clinical and implementation science researcher (H.B.B.), will share their agreement with the coded IS and the PRECIS‐2 data.

For data presentation and analysis, we will provide a narrative description of the most and the least frequently used IS, patterns of implementation outcomes (e.g., adoption, fidelity, sustainability, acceptability, feasibility, penetration, cost) reported across the 13 trials, and calculate the mean number of IS reported per trial. To assess agreement in IS data between clinical researchers and implementation research experts, we will show the agreement rate and discuss any disagreements. We will use a similar approach when investigating consistency between trial conduct and reporting for one trial to explore the adequacy of trial reporting. Additionally, we will examine IS and domains based on their importance and feasibility [30], and calculate the correlation with BP reduction identified from the meta‐analysis [28].

We will use descriptive statistics to summarize mean PRECIS‐2 scores and plot the mean score for each domain on the PRECIS‐2 spider plot [21]. We will examine the correlation of the PRECIS‐2 score (both per trial and per design domain across trials) with BP reduction. Using the overall score for each trial, we will further categorize trials as explanatory (score 2.5 or lower), intermediate (score between 2.5 and 3.5) and pragmatic (score 3.5 or higher) [31].

## 讨论 / Discussion

Uncontrolled BP is a significant public health problem [1]. Despite the overwhelming evidence in support of telemedicine for hypertension management [28], its adoption has been slow. Our study will contribute to the understanding of relevant IS in the use of telemedicine and the readiness of published trials designed to inform its implementation in routine practice. In the process, we created a set of practical tools and step‐by‐step guidance for clinical researchers to report IS and trial design (Table 1). Specifically, this study provides practical guidance to (1) systematically report and operationalize IS from pre‐implementation studies; (2) map trials along the explanatory‐pragmatic continuum, which is highly relevant for implementation; (3) provide approaches to harmonize data extraction across studies identified from a systematic review; (4) assess agreement of IS coded by clinical and implementation researchers; and (5) evaluate agreement between trial conduct and its reporting.

Implementation researchers have previously developed the PISRT to identify IS from pre‐implementation studies [12]. However, to our knowledge, the tool has not been widely used by clinicians and clinical researchers. One possible explanation is the inherent complexity of the concepts and language of implementation science, which can limit clinical researchers' use of such a tool. Similarly, the PRECIS‐2 tool systematically examines where trial designs lie along the explanatory to pragmatic spectrum [21]. An EBI evaluated in a pragmatic trial design is more likely to be adopted. We believe that the practical guide we developed will help researchers and implementers identify EBIs with high implementation potential and strategies to support their uptake. Furthermore, understanding agreement between trial conduct versus reporting will inform optimal approaches to report IS and trial design by clinical researchers in efficacy and effectiveness studies.

In our experience, understanding IS by non‐implementation researchers is an iterative process. For example, even after the coding started, the clinical research team reviewed additional literature about IS (facilitation), and the differences between various IS (assess for readiness and identify barriers and facilitators vs. conduct local needs assessment; and conduct educational meetings vs. conduct ongoing training vs. provide ongoing consultation). As such, the definition and ancillary material about IS in the PISRT were helpful but insufficient. We believe that the ‘Understand It’ column in the modified PISRT including its ancillary material would be particularly helpful and a major contribution to the literature (Additional File S12).

We describe several highlights of our experience (Table 1). It took more time, practice and training to use the PISRT than the PRECIS‐2 tool. Piloting helped the team standardize reporting language and clarify several items, such as the distinction between IS dosing (e.g., engaging patients a total of four times with phone calls with each call lasting approximately 10 min) versus temporality (e.g., calls at 1 week and 1, 2 and 4 months). Session recording allowed us to revisit for clarification. When selecting a potential IS, a common question was whether it was an EBI versus an IS. We revisited our coding after delineating the EBI components. For example, providing a BP monitor to patients was initially selected under the IS ‘changing physical structure and equipment’, yet it was later considered part of EBI after EBI components were established. To enhance workflow, we recommend gathering IS operationalization details immediately after identifying IS, rather than returning to them later. We also advise minimizing the time gap between individual coding and group or follow‐up meetings to ensure that the discussion points remain fresh in mind. We found that not all coders consistently identified the same set of IS. To reduce the likelihood of missing IS, we suggest having at least three coders.

Only one‐third of self‐labelled pragmatic trials are truly such, making it particularly challenging to identify how ready the EBIs are for use in routine practice [23]. The PRECIS‐2 tool is valuable for characterizing trials across the explanatory‐pragmatic continuum [23, 32, 33]. However, rating the PRECIS‐2 tool's domains can be challenging [32]. Often, multiple elements are part of the score for one PRECIS‐2 domain, and how important each element is often arbitrary. For example, for recruitment, there could be mail invitations and financial incentives that would lower the PRECIS‐2 score, but recruiting patients from scheduled clinic visits would increase the score. Assigning a precise score is difficult when multiple recruitment strategies were used in a trial. Furthermore, comparing scores between trials that report elaborate information and trials with limited information is challenging. Thus, when multiple elements are at play, using a priori specification (if possible) can be helpful. Typically, IS are used to enhance EBIs that have demonstrated positive clinical effects. However, the net clinical result is the effectiveness of the combination of EBI and the IS supporting the EBI [11]. For this study, we included trials from the systematic review that did not demonstrate clinical benefit [28]. We believe that identifying IS from negative studies is important.

Readers should be cognizant of several shortcomings of our study. We could only code for what was communicated in the published trials. Despite our detailed process, we could have still missed coding data. Some coders had limited clinical research experience, and perhaps more experienced members could bring additional insights into discussions. However, we believe that accomplishing a complex task rigorously, even with less experienced team members, requires a comprehensive approach, which in turn can be insightful for people with a range of experience. A pooled assessment of IS operationalization can provide evidence on the association between conceptual action targets and implementation outcomes [30]. Nevertheless, less explicit reporting of the implementation outcomes in the pre‐implementation studies remains a barrier. The ideal approach would be verifying the IS and PRECIS‐2 scores from the PI of each study, but this is untenable for 13 studies published between 1992 and 2019. In addition, these investigators may not have expertise in the PRECIS‐2 tool or implementation research. Therefore, we invited implementation research experts to provide face validity for our IS‐related work. Ultimately, the proposed approach is best viewed as a work in progress. Future work should evaluate the effectiveness of our method and the alignment of our data with implementation science experts using rigorous qualitative and quantitative methods.

In conclusion, this study contributes to advancing the implementation of telemedicine‐based hypertension management by identifying key IS and trial design attributes. If supported by future research, the methods and manuals developed through this work can further the field by informing the selection of IS and gauging the fitness of EBI use in routine care. Such guidance can help implementers identify translation‐ready interventions and implementation approaches earlier in the process, ultimately advancing the implementation of EBIs.
