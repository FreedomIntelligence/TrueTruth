## English Abstract

The optimal preoperative management of angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs) and angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) remains debated. This meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) assessed withholding vs. continuing ACEIs/ARBs on intraoperative hypotension and postoperative outcomes.

PubMed, Web of Science, and China Biology Medicine were comprehensively searched to identify RCTs comparing preoperative withholding ACEIs/ARBs with a continuing therapy in adult patients. Two authors independently extracted and pooled data using a random-effects model. The primary outcome was the incidence of intraoperative hypotension. Secondary outcomes included duration of intraoperative hypotension, vasopressor use, postoperative hypotension and hypertension, major adverse cardiovascular events, acute kidney injury, duration of mechanical ventilation, length of stay in ICU and hospital, and 30-d mortality. Evidence was assessed using the Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development and Evaluation approach.

Fourteen RCTs with 4063 patients were included. Withholding ACEIs/ARBs before noncardiac surgery was associated with a reduced incidence of intraoperative hypotension (36.4% vs. 48.4%; risk ratio = 0.73, 95% confidence interval = 0.60–0.88, p = 0.001, I2 = 63%; a very low certainty of evidence); it was associated with a shorter duration of intraoperative hypotension (mean difference = −5.41 min), reduced vasopressor use (37.5% vs. 51.2%), and increased postoperative hypertension (27.8% vs. 20.2%), without affecting other outcomes (low or very low certainty of evidence).

While withholding ACEIs/ARBs may reduce intraoperative hypotension, the very low certainty of evidence underscores the need for future research to confirm clinical implications.

## 背景 / Background

Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs) and angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) are widely recommended as first-line therapies for hypertension, and approximately 30% to 40% of surgical patients are on long-term treatment with these medications [1–3]. Preoperative management of ACEIs/ARBs has been a topic of debate, as withholding these medications before surgery may reduce the risk of intraoperative hypotension and adverse outcomes [4,5]; however, recent studies have suggested that continuing ACEIs/ARBs did not significantly increase the risk of major postoperative complications [6–8]. As a result, whether to withhold or continue ACEIs/ARBs before surgery remains controversial.

In 2014, the American College of Cardiology and the American Heart Association recommended continuing ACEIs/ARBs before surgery [9]. However, the updated 2024 guidelines suggested withholding ACEIs/ARBs before high-risk noncardiac surgery for patients with well-controlled blood pressure to limit hypotension, while continuing therapy for patients with heart failure [10]. There are similar recommendations on the 2022 European Society of Cardiology guidelines [11]. In contrast, Canadian and Japanese guidelines recommended withholding ACEIs/ARBs before surgery [12,13]. All these recommendations were based on limited evidence.

Previous meta-analyses have suggested that continuing ACEIs/ARBs was associated with an increased rate of hypotension but did not affect postoperative mortality [14,15]. However, these analyses combined data from randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and retrospective observational studies, which compromised the validity of the conclusions. To address this gap, we conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis based solely on RCTs, including the most recent Stop-or-Not and SPACE trials [7,8]. Our goal was to determine the effects of withholding vs. continuing ACEIs/ARBs on intraoperative hypotension, vasopressor use, postoperative complications, and healthcare resource utilization. Moreover, the certainty of evidence was assessed using the Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development and Evaluation (GRADE) approach.

## 方法 / Methods

This meta-analysis was prospectively registered in the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO). The review methods were established prior to the start of this study. There were no significant deviations from the protocol. The study followed the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 statement (Supplement 1) [16]. This work was reported in line with the Assessing the methodological quality of systematic reviews (AMSTAR) guidelines (Supplement 2) [17].

Two reviewers independently searched the PubMed, Web of Science, and China Biology Medicine (CBM) from inception to August 31, 2024. The full search strategy is presented in Table S1. There were no language or other restrictions. All search results were imported into EndNote X9 for management.

We included studies based on the following criteria: (1) RCT only, (2) adult patients undergoing surgery, (3) comparison of preoperative withholding with continuing ACEIs/ARBs, and (4) reported outcomes related to hypotension, vasopressor use, major adverse events, or mortality.

Exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) duplicate publications, (2) observational studies, reviews, editorials, letters, or case reports, (3) animal or irrelevant studies, and (4) studies not addressing preoperative withholding ACEIs/ARBs. Any discrepancies were resolved through re-evaluation and group discussion.

Two reviewers independently extracted relevant data, including study details (first author, year of publication, and region), patient demographics (age, surgery type, and anesthesia method), ACEI/ARB type, timeframe for follow-up, and main outcomes. Discrepancies were resolved through re-checking the published data and group discussion.

The primary outcome was the incidence of intraoperative hypotension, with the thresholds for hypotension predefined in each included study (Table S2). Secondary outcomes included duration of intraoperative hypotension, perioperative use of vasopressors, postoperative hypotension and hypertension, major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), acute kidney injury (AKI), duration of mechanical ventilation, length of intensive care unit (ICU) stay, length of hospital stay, and in-hospital or 30-d mortality.

Two reviewers assessed the quality of included studies using the Cochrane evaluation system [18,19]. Risk of bias was evaluated in the following domains: random sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of patients and personnel, blinding of outcome assessors, incomplete outcome data, selective reporting, and other biases. Each domain was rated as ‘low’, ‘high’, or ‘unclear’ risk of bias. The overall risk of bias for each study was classified as ‘low’ (low risk in all domains), ‘high’ (high risk in at least one domain), or ‘unclear’ (unclear risk in at least one domain without any high-risk domains). For the main outcomes, the certainty of evidence was assessed using the GRADE approach [20,21], considering factors such as study design, risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and other considerations. Two reviewers independently rated evidence certainty as ‘high’, ‘moderate’, ‘low’, or ‘very low’. Any disagreements were resolved through consensus among all reviewers.

Data were analyzed using the RevMan (v 5.3, Cochrane Collaboration, Copenhagen, Denmark). Risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence interval (CI) was presented for dichotomous data, and mean difference (MD) with 95% CI for continuous data. Data were pooled using a random-effects model due to anticipated clinical heterogeneity. Heterogeneity was evaluated using the I2 test, with I2 > 75% suggesting significant heterogeneity [18,22]. Prespecified subgroup analyses were conducted based on allocation concealment and anesthesia methods. Heterogeneity between subgroups was evaluated using the I2 statistic and Cochran Q test. Publication bias was analyzed with the Begg’s rank correlation test and Egger’s linear regression using the STATA (v 17.0, Stata Corp, College Station, TX) [23], and the results are presented as bias plots. The significance level was set as a P value < 0.05. No multiple comparison corrections were planned; thus, the secondary outcomes were interpreted as exploratory.

## 结果 / Results

Our initial search yielded 8221 publications (857 from PubMed, 5523 from Web of Science, and 1841 from CBM) (Figure 1). After excluding 958 duplicates, 7263 studies were screened by title and abstract. Of these, 47 full-text articles were reviewed. Finally, 14 RCTs involving 4063 patients were included in this meta-analysis [4–8,24–32].

Eight studies totalling 3105 patients undergoing noncardiac surgery reported intraoperative hypotension events (Table 2) [4–8,24,29,32]. The incidence of hypotension was 36.4% (567/1556) in the ACEIs/ARBs withholding group, which was significantly lower than 48.4% (750/1549) in the continuing group (RR = 0.73; 95% CI, 0.60 to 0.88; p = 0.001, I2 = 63%) (Figure 3A). No significant publication bias was detected in the Begg’s test (p = 0.805; Figure 3B) or Egger’s test (p = 0.493; Figure 3C). Subgroup analyses based on allocation concealment and anesthesia methods showed no significant differences between groups (Figures S1 and S2).

Withholding ACEIs/ARBs before surgery was associated with a shorter duration of intraoperative hypotension (MD = −5.41 min; 95% CI, −9.28 to −1.54 min) (Figure S3) [5–7], and the need for vasopressors (37.5% vs. 51.2%; RR = 0.70; 95% CI, 0.58 to 0.84) (Figure S4) [4–7,24,28,29,31,32]. There was no significant difference in postoperative hypotension (RR = 0.65; 95% CI, 0.42 to 1.02) (Figure S5) [5,6,24,25], whereas withholding ACEIs/ARBs was associated with an increased rate of postoperative hypertension (RR = 1.49; 95% CI, 1.09 to 2.03) (Figure S6) [5,6,8,24,27].

The two groups were comparable in terms of MACE (RR = 0.60; 95% CI, 0.15 to 2.30) and AKI (RR = 0.97; 95% CI, 0.79 to 1.20). In-hospital or 30-d mortality was 0.7% in the ACEIs/ARBs withholding group and 1.3% in the continuing group (RR = 0.71; 95% CI, 0.35 to 1.42). There were no differences in the duration of mechanical ventilation, length of ICU stay, or length of hospital stay.

The GRADE certainty of evidence for intraoperative hypotension was rated as very low (Table 3). For secondary outcomes, the certainty of evidence was low for postoperative hypertension, MACE, AKI, and in-hospital or 30-d mortality, and very low for duration of intraoperative hypotension, perioperative use of vasopressors, postoperative hypotension, duration of mechanical ventilation, and length of ICU and hospital stay.

## 讨论 / Discussion

In this meta-analysis, we synthesized data from 14 RCTs involving 4063 patients to evaluate the management of ACEIs/ARBs on intraoperative hypotension and postoperative outcomes. Our primary finding is that withholding ACEIs/ARBs resulted in a lower incidence of intraoperative hypotension for patients undergoing noncardiac surgery (36.4% vs. 48.4%; a 27% reduction in relative risk). However, the GRADE assessment rated the certainty of evidence as very low, highlighting the need for caution in interpreting these results.

Among secondary outcomes, withholding ACEIs/ARBs was associated with a shorter duration of intraoperative hypotension, reduced vasopressor use, and an increased rate of postoperative hypertension. These findings suggest that withholding ACEIs/ARBs may limit intraoperative hypotension and its associated risks. No significant differences were observed in other critical outcomes, including postoperative hypotension, MACE, AKI, mortality, or duration of mechanical ventilation and hospital stay. Again, the low to very low certainty of evidence for these outcomes underscores the need for further robust studies.

Dr. Hollmann and colleagues conducted a meta-analysis to suggest that continuation of ACEIs/ARBs did not affect postoperative major complications or mortality but was associated with an increased rate of hypotension [14]. However, the mixed literature (including 5 RCTs and 4 cohort studies) with a limited sample size precluded definitive clinical interpretation. A most recent meta-analysis by Dr. Ahmed and colleagues also included both RCTs and retrospective observational studies to show that withholding ACEIs/ARBs before noncardiac surgery was associated with reduced intraoperative hypotension and AKI with nonsignificant effects on MACE or mortality [15]. In contrast, our meta-analysis showed that withholding ACEIs/ARBs led to a similar reduction in intraoperative hypotension but had no effect on AKI. The renoprotective benefit in Ahmed et al.’s study was driven predominantly by observational data. Furthermore, there were several unmeasured confounders, such as concomitant use of diuretic, additional blood pressure medications, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors, and intraoperative fluid administration. These factors may modify hemodynamic stability and influence AKI risk. The low certainty of evidence for AKI in our GRADE assessment highlights the need for further studies.

Unlike previous meta-analyses that included observational studies, our analysis exclusively synthesized RCTs, thereby minimizing confounding. Notably, we incorporated the most recent evidence from the Stop-or-Not and SPACE trials [7,8]. Intraoperative hypotension was prespecified as the primary outcome, and we also investigated its duration and the related outcomes (need for vasopressors and postoperative hypotension and hypertension). Furthermore, we determined the certainty of evidence for the outcomes using the GRADE approach.

Intraoperative hypotension is a well-documented risk factor for major adverse outcomes following surgery [33–35]. While our results indicate that continuing ACEIs/ARBs was related to a higher rate of intraoperative hypotension, we found no association between ACEI/ARB management and severe postoperative complications such as MACE or AKI. The 5.4-minute reduction in hypotension duration may explain the lack of mortality difference, as brief hypotension is less likely to induce end-organ damage. Meanwhile, the recent trials also supported no association between the administration strategy of ACEIs/ARBs and major postoperative complications [7,8]. In the POISE-3 factorial trial, patients undergoing noncardiac surgery were randomized to receive a hypotension-avoidance strategy (intraoperative mean arterial pressure target ≥ 80 mmHg and ACEIs/ARBs withheld before and for 2 days after surgery) or a hypertension-avoidance strategy (intraoperative mean arterial pressure target ≥ 60 mmHg and ACEIs/ARBs continued before and after surgery) [36]. Although the hypotension-avoidance group experienced less clinically significant hypotension, the incidence of major postoperative vascular complications was similar between groups. These findings are consistent with our results that perioperative ACEIs/ARBs management had no impact on postoperative major complications or mortality.

Several limitations should also be considered. First, the variability in hypotension definitions (e.g. systolic blood pressure < 80–90 mmHg) and anesthesia protocols across studies limits the generalizability of our results. Future trials should standardize these definitions and protocols. Second, significant heterogeneity was observed for several outcomes, reflecting differences in patient baseline characteristics and surgical interventions. Third, the primary outcome was based solely on noncardiac surgical patients. The impact of ACEIs/ARBs management on cardiac surgery outcomes warrants separate investigation. Finally, the majority of the evidence originated from Western countries, and further studies are needed to address this clinical question in diverse healthcare settings worldwide.

In conclusion, this systematic review and meta-analysis provides the most recent evidence suggesting that withholding ACEIs/ARBs before noncardiac surgery was associated with a reduced incidence of intraoperative hypotension, but the very low certainty of evidence highlights the need for future research.
