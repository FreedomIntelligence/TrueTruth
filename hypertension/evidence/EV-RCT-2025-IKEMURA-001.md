## English Abstract

Clinical trials typically report average health status outcomes by treatment at single points in time, as opposed to participants’ trajectories (or journeys) over time. While ISCHEMIA demonstrated better mean health status at discrete times with an invasive treatment among those with baseline angina, the patterns of individual participants’ angina over time are unknown.

To identify patterns of individual participants’ angina over time after invasive or conservative management strategies for chronic coronary disease.

In this secondary analysis of the ISCHEMIA trial, which enrolled participants with chronic coronary disease and moderate to severe ischemia from July 2012 to January 2018, we used ordinal latent trajectory analysis to assess angina frequency over a two-year period, separately for participants assigned to the initial invasive and initial conservative arms. Angina frequency was defined using the Seattle Angina Questionnaire Angina Frequency (SAQ-AF) score, re-categorized as daily/weekly (0–60 points), monthly (61–99 points), and no angina (100 points). Participants without baseline angina were excluded.

Among 2977 participants with baseline angina, 1505 (50.6%) were randomized to initial invasive and 1472 (49.4%) to initial conservative management; baseline characteristics were well balanced between groups. Six distinct patterns of angina trajectories were identified in each arm and were qualitatively similar: 1) rapid resolution, 2) gradual resolution, 3) early improvement with persistent infrequent angina, 4) severe angina with improvement, 5) modest angina with minimal change, and 6) severe angina without improvement. In the invasive group, the most common patterns included rapid resolution (27.1%) and early improvement with persistent infrequent angina (32.1%), while the conservative group more often showed modest angina with minimal change (42.1%) and fewer cases of rapid resolution (12.8%) or early improvement with persistent infrequent angina (10.2%).

Patients with chronic coronary disease and angina experienced diverse symptom trajectories, ranging from rapid resolution to severe or persistent angina. A greater proportion of conservatively managed patients experienced unfavorable angina patterns over two years compared with those treated invasively. When health status is monitored over time, such patterns may help identify patients with persistent symptoms who could benefit from additional therapy.

Ordinal latent trajectory analysis was used to evaluate angina frequency over two years in this secondary analysis of the ISCHEMIA trial. Six common angina trajectories were identified in 2,977 patients with baseline angina, ranging from rapid resolution to severe or persistent symptoms. These patterns were qualitatively similar between initial invasive and conservative management strategies, although persistent angina was more frequent in conservatively managed patients than in those treated invasively. This study provides the first comprehensive characterization of angina trajectories in a randomized trial, offering valuable insights for patient counseling on expected symptom progression over time.

## 背景 / Background

The primary treatment goals for chronic coronary disease (CCD) are improving patients’ survival and health status, including their symptoms, function, and quality of life.(1) Multiple trials testing different CCD management strategies have failed to demonstrate better survival with initial revascularization,(2,3) but have consistently shown health status benefits.(4,5) Thus, current guidelines emphasize the health status benefits of treatment decisions and encourage shared decision-making.(1,6)

Clinical trials typically summarize health status outcomes within treatment groups by comparing mean scores at discrete points in time.(7,8) However, this approach averages the experiences of all patients and does not reflect potential variation in individual patients’ health status trajectories over time. For example, two patients may both report no angina at one year, but one may have rapidly become angina free, while the other endured persistent symptoms for months before becoming asymptomatic. Latent class modeling is a statistical method that seeks to identify common patterns among individuals’ longitudinal trajectories.(9) For patients with CCD, understanding these patterns may help clinicians anticipate the likely course of angina following invasive or conservative treatment strategies, recognize patients’ likely trajectory as they follow their health status over time, and to leverage these insights to discuss additional treatment options with their patients. To date, no prior CCD strategy trial has applied latent class models to characterize health status recovery over time.

To address this need, we leveraged data from the International Study of Comparative Health Effectiveness with Medical and Invasive Approaches (ISCHEMIA) trial —a large, international study with detailed, longitudinal health status assessments—to examine participants’ patterns of angina following initial invasive or initial conservative strategies. These were not hypothesis-driven analyses and were intended to describe naturally occurring patterns of symptom recovery after CCD treatment.

## 方法 / Methods

The design and results of the ISCHEMIA trial have been published previously.(3,4) Briefly, ISCHEMIA was a randomized, unblinded trial of 5,179 participants with CCD and moderate or severe ischemia on non-invasive stress testing. Participants were randomized 1:1 to an initial invasive (guideline-directed medical therapy [GDMT] with angiography and revascularization, if feasible) or initial conservative strategy (GDMT only, with angiography and revascularization reserved for failure of medical therapy). The study protocol was approved by the New York University Grossman School of Medicine (the coordinating study site) and by the institutional review boards and ethics committees at each participating site. All participants provided written informed consent.

To characterize trajectories of angina in ISCHEMIA, we included participants with valid baseline health status data and at least one health status assessment during the first two years of follow-up, congruent with the primary ISCHEMIA health status analyses.(4) We excluded participants whose baseline SAQ Angina Frequency score was 100 points (e.g., no angina, N=1635, 35.4%) because the results of ISCHEMIA demonstrated that the principal health status benefits of an initial invasive strategy were among those with baseline angina and for whom the insights from these analyses would be most relevant. While the development of angina in patients with silent ischemia could be important, the focus of this study was on participants with angina to focus on those with a compelling need for symptom relief. In designing these analyses, it was anticipated that the patterns of angina over time would differ between those managed conservatively and those undergoing angiography and revascularization. Accordingly, an a prior decision was made to model each randomized arm independently to enable different patterns of recovery to be estimated within each management strategy.

Angina-related health status was measured with the 7-item Seattle Angina Questionnaire (SAQ).(8) The SAQ quantifies CCD-related health status over the prior 4 weeks in three domains: Angina Frequency, Physical Limitation, and Quality of Life. Scores range from 0 to 100, with higher scores signifying less angina, better physical functioning, and quality of life.(8,10) For this study, we focused on the SAQ Angina Frequency (SAQ-AF) score, since angina is the cardinal manifestation of CCD. This SAQ domain also has a clinically intuitive interpretation based on prior studies validating categories of SAQ-AF scores with daily angina diaries, where scores ≤60 represent daily/weekly angina, scores of 61–99 reflect monthly angina and scores of 100 represent no angina over the prior month.(11) The SAQ has been shown to be highly valid, reliable, sensitive to changes in clinical status,(12–14) as well as being predictive of death, acute coronary syndromes, and healthcare costs.(15,16) In ISCHEMIA, the SAQ was assessed before randomization, at 1.5, 3, and 6 months, and then every 6 months thereafter until trial completion. For these analyses, we focused on health status assessments during the first 24 months of follow-up because this time period represented a clinically relevant time frame to inform medical decision-making(17), and because there was little missing health status compared with later assessments (primarily due to censoring at the end of the trial).

Latent trajectory modeling seeks to determine whether a small number of underlying patterns can summarize longitudinal data from multiple individuals.(9) We fit ordinal latent trajectory models to the three SAQ-AF score categories: daily/weekly, monthly, and no angina. We chose to model categories of angina frequency since they are highly interpretable and because SAQ-AF scores in ISCHEMIA were non-normally distributed (semi-continuous, skewed, ceiling effects).

The method of latent trajectory modeling employed used growth mixture models (GMMs), as implemented in the R package ‘lcmm’.(18) GMMs are similar to standard mixed models of longitudinal data but allow individuals’ trajectories to be expressed as a weighted combination of a small number of latent (i.e., not directly observed) trajectory patterns. Commonly, these patterns are referred to as latent “classes” or subgroups, with weights interpreted as probabilities of belonging to each class. These classes represent typical courses of angina recovery, and each patient was assigned to the class for which their estimated membership probability was highest. The separability of the classes is quantified by a relative entropy statistic ranging from 0 to 1, where 1 denotes perfect separability. Relative entropy is not a measure of model fit, but rather reflects the degree to which patients’ trajectories fall into clearly separate groups. Higher entropy values thus increase the confidence that each patient’s particular trajectory is uniquely represented by one of the latent patterns.

As with mixed models, GMMs include a regression specification for the effect of time that is linear in the coefficients. Given that participants’ trajectories in ISCHEMIA were highly nonlinear (most commonly, rapid improvement followed by a plateau), we used restricted cubic splines, with knots at 1.5 and 6 months, for the time effect. In GMMs, the coefficients of the time effect define a particular mean trajectory, which varies by class. The classes are not known in advance but are discovered through model fitting. Fitting a GMM requires pre-specifying the number of classes. Multiple models are thus fit and compared, varying the number of classes. In exploratory analyses such as these, there is no “correct” model, but models are compared based on such factors as fit, complexity, interpretability, and external validity. We selected our final models based on Bayesian Information Criteria, which quantifies relative model fit, correcting for model complexity, and also considered the clinical interpretability of the classes.

As with mixed models, GMMs can include patient-level random effects, which allow for variation in individuals’ trajectories around their class means but can be difficult to fit and optimize, particularly in the presence of random effects. A special case of GMMs, commonly known as latent class growth analysis (LCGA), omits the random effects and assumes that each patient’s trajectory is, apart from random noise, exactly expressible by the latent trajectory patterns. A consequence of this is that LCGA tends to identify more classes than GMMs with random effects, which could enhance interpretability. Both LCGA models and random-intercept GMMs were fit and compared (as well as GMMs with random intercepts and slopes, but these could not be fit). Model fit statistics are presented in Supplemental Table 1. For both treatment groups, for both types of models, six classes were found to be optimal by BIC. Furthermore, in both groups, the optimal LCGA model had a slightly better fit than the optimal random-intercept GMM, and the class trajectories and memberships were very similar. Based on model parsimony and fit, the six-class LCGA models were selected as the final models. Model entropy was 0.75 for conservative patients and 0.71 for invasive patients. To facilitate description and interpretation, we classified patients according to the class for which they had the highest probability of belonging. Average posterior probabilities were calculated for each class and are reported in Supplemental Table 2. These values exceed 0.7 across all classes, suggesting adequate classification certainty. In addition, we calculated the odds of correct classification for each class, which denotes the ability of the model to correctly assign patients to the class, versus random chance, and mismatch probabilities, which denote how accurately the model estimates the overall class probabilities; these statistics are included in Supplemental Table 3. To visually assess the heterogeneity and distinctiveness of individual trajectories, we generated spaghetti plots of raw SAQ-AF scores over time stratified by latent class assignment.

The primary results are represented as plots of the estimated trajectories for each class, along with the number of patients categorized in each class. Since we fit ordinal models of angina frequency categories, the trajectories are represented as probabilities of having daily/weekly, monthly, or no angina as a function of time. Crude rates of angina frequency categories by follow-up assessment within the assigned latent classes are included in Supplemental Figures 1 and 2. To further enhance the interpretability, we also calculated mean SAQ-AF scores at each follow-up assessment within each class. Finally, we descriptively summarized patient demographics, baseline characteristics, and interventions during the study period by class for each treatment group.

Among the 2,977 patients in our cohort, 2,300 (77.3%) completed the two-year follow-up assessment, 66 (2.2%) died prior to two years, 225 (7.6%) failed to complete their two-year assessment, and 386 (13.0%) were censored prior to two years due to termination of the ISCHEMIA trial. Additionally, 774 (26.0%) missed one or more follow-up assessments (535 missed exactly one; 293 missed two or more). For the primary analysis, we assumed all missing SAQ-AF scores were missing at random, conditional on the latent classes. As a sensitivity analysis, we imputed missing scores using multiple imputation by chained equations, as implemented in the R package ‘mice’.(19) The imputation model included randomized treatment groups and all available SAQ-AF scores through 24 months. Using this model, we generated four “complete” randomly imputed data sets and repeated the latent class analyses separately on each. In each case, the resulting latent classes were nearly identical to those found in the primary analysis, with class membership counts varying only slightly. Therefore, we report here the primary analyses on unimputed data; results for the imputed data sets can be found in Supplemental Figures 3 and 4. Analyses were performed with SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC) and R Statistical Software version 4.3.3 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). The R code used to specify the latent class model, along with sample data and model output, is provided in the Supplemental Appendix to enhance reproducibility.

## 结果 / Results

Of the 5179 participants randomized in the ISCHEMIA trial, we excluded those from five sites (481 patients) whose SAQ data was not valid due to improper form completion, and 51 in the initial invasive arm and 30 in the initial conservative arm who were missing either a baseline or all follow-up health status assessments over 24 months. There were no significant differences in baseline characteristics between included and excluded participants.(4) Lastly, we excluded 1635 participants whose SAQ-angina frequency score was 100 points at baseline, indicating no angina over the prior 4 weeks, resulting in a final analytic cohort of 2977 participants; 1505 (50.6%) randomized to an invasive strategy and 1472 (49.4%) randomized to an initial conservative strategy. Both groups had well-balanced baseline characteristics (Supplemental Table 4).

Supplemental Figure 5 describes the individual participants’ SAQ-AF trajectories over two years, highlighting the heterogeneity across participants. Figures 1 and 2 depict the distinct angina trajectory classes over time for the initial invasive and initial conservative groups, respectively. The six latent patterns of angina trajectories were qualitatively similar for both treatment groups and were interpreted as:

Class 1: “Rapid resolution of angina” - Participants with primarily monthly angina at enrollment experienced rapid resolution after 1.5 months. Class 2: “Gradual resolution of angina” - Participants with primarily monthly angina at enrollment who experienced gradual resolution over 12 months. Class 3: “Early improvement with persistent infrequent angina” – This group primarily had monthly angina at enrollment and experienced early resolution over three months, with about 20–40% having monthly angina over time. Class 4: “Severe angina with substantial improvement” – Most participants in this group had daily/weekly angina at enrollment and experienced gradual resolution over 2 years. Class 5: “Modest angina with minimal change” – Most participants had monthly angina at enrollment and experienced little improvement over the next 2 years. Class 6: “Severe angina without change” - Most participants had daily/weekly angina at enrollment without much improvement over time.

Supplemental Figures 6 and 7 describe the individual participants’ SAQ-AF trajectories within each identified class. Furthermore, Supplemental Figure 8 describes the mean SAQ-AF score within each identified class at each time point.

While the identified classes in the initial invasive and initial conservative groups were qualitatively similar, differences in the distribution across classes differed by treatment assignment. For participants assigned to an initial invasive strategy, the most common class was Class 3 (early improvement with persistent infrequent angina, N=483, 32.1%;), whereas class 5 (modest baseline angina with minimal change) was most common in those treated conservatively (N=620, 42.1%). Notably, in the invasive strategy group, 27.1% of patients were assigned to Class 1 (rapid resolution), and 3.7% to Class 6 (severe angina without change). In contrast, in the conservative strategy group, 12.8% were assigned to Class 1, and 6.2% to Class 6.

Individual participants were well separated into these identified classes within the initial invasive and initial conservative groups based on their calculated probabilities (Supplemental Table 2). Tables 1 and 2 summarize the characteristics of participants across the identified classes of angina over time.

Within the initial invasive group, the mean age was similar across the identified classes, whereas Class 6 (severe angina without changes) had a greater percentage of female participants than the other classes (35% in Class 6 vs. 20% to 31% in other classes, Table 1). The mean body mass index (BMI), the proportion of those with prior myocardial infarction, and the number of diseased vessels were similar across the identified classes, but the proportion of patients with severe ischemia was lowest in Class 6. In addition, Classes 4, 5, and 6 had a substantial number of participants in the invasive arm who did not undergo revascularization within six months of enrollment (28%, 28%, and 38% in Classes 4, 5, and 6, respectively), including a substantial proportion without any significant coronary stenoses (10%, 11%, and 20% in Classes 4, 5, and 6, respectively). Participants in these three classes frequently had more antianginal medications at 24 months than baseline (21%, 24%, and 48% in Classes 4, 5, and 6, respectively).

In the initial conservative group, as in the invasive group, the mean age was similar across classes, whereas classes 4 (severe angina with substantial improvement) and 6 (severe angina without change) had a higher proportion of women, higher mean BMI, and a history of myocardial infarction than other classes (Table 2). Classes 1 (rapid resolution of angina) and 6 had more participants who smoked at baseline, many of whom continued smoking throughout 24 months, as compared with the other classes. Notably, the degree of ischemia and the number of diseased vessels by coronary computed tomography angiography were similar across all classes (Table 2). Regarding the treatment after randomization, all classes, except class 1, had increased use of antianginal medications at 24 months compared with baseline. Furthermore, one-quarter of participants in class 4 underwent revascularization within six months of enrollment.

## 讨论 / Discussion

Although multiple clinical practice guidelines for CCD emphasize the importance of shared decision-making, there has been little information to share with patients about how their symptoms may change over time with initial invasive or initial conservative management. This is because most prior studies have reported population-level mean benefits of invasive management on patients’ health status at discrete points in time rather than the patterns of individual patients’ angina over time. Therefore, in the largest trial comparing management strategies for patients with CCD and moderate to severe myocardial ischemia among initially symptomatic patients, we investigated angina trajectories over time to more completely describe patients’ journeys following an initial invasive or initial conservative treatment strategy. We identified six distinct angina trajectories within both groups. These six angina patterns were qualitatively similar with both treatment strategies, although the probabilities of following these trajectories differed between strategies. Specifically, a greater proportion of conservatively managed patients experienced persistent angina over time, as compared with those treated invasively. These findings provide a detailed, patient-centered view of how angina symptoms may evolve over time in patients with chronic coronary disease and highlight that those with persistent angina may warrant further evaluation or treatment.

The ISCHEMIA trial, the largest international trial of CCD management strategies, collected frequent, serial health status outcomes to create a unique opportunity to explore the longitudinal trajectories of angina over time.(3,4) While a previous observational study described several distinct health status trajectories among patients undergoing angiography for CCD (20), it was limited by fewer health status assessments and treatment selection by clinicians rather than randomization. This prior study identified four unique trajectories that differed from those found in this analysis. This discrepancy may be partly due to the inclusion of patients without baseline angina and the single analysis that combined patients with and without revascularization, which could have obscured the observed trajectories of each management strategy. Additionally, it modeled multiple SAQ domains as continuous variables (20), which may be more difficult to interpret given that a patient may be in one trajectory pattern for one SAQ domain (e.g., angina frequency) and a different one for another (e.g., physical limitations). In the present study, we focused on SAQ-AF as the cardinal manifestation of CCD and categorized it into universally understood thresholds of daily/weekly, monthly, and no angina to support clinical interpretability.

The present study identified six unique trajectories of angina within both the initial conservative and initial invasive groups. Classes 1 and 2 were characterized by angina resolution that varied by the rapidity with which angina was eliminated. While the initial conservative group had fewer participants in Class 1 than the invasive group, the total proportion of participants with angina resolution (e.g., the sum of Classes 1 and 2) was similar (35.6% and 36.0%, respectively). On the other hand, the initial conservative group had slightly more participants in Classes 5 and 6, characterized as more frequent angina with minimal change, than the initial invasive group. Collectively, these data highlight not only a more favorable angina trajectory with initial invasive management than initial conservative management but also the variability of treatment benefits for both management strategies.

These analyses also highlight that coronary revascularization does not immediately relieve symptoms in all patients and underscore the importance of serial health status measurement in routine care so that treatment can be titrated to improve symptom control iteratively over time. If recommendations to integrate formal health status assessments, such as the SAQ, in routine clinical care, (21) clinicians may be able to identify which trajectory a patient is following, particularly among those with more severe (daily/weekly) angina. Should such patients fail to improve by 3 months, then they are more likely to follow Class 6 (severe angina without changes) than Class 4 (severe angina with substantial improvement). In this example, a patient following trajectory Class 6 could be considered for additional intensification or evaluation for alternative diagnoses, such as microvascular dysfunction or vasospastic angina (22).

The descriptions of participant characteristics across trajectory groups found that women managed conservatively were more likely to be in groups with worse angina patterns, a finding that aligns with prior studies showing women with CCD to have worse health status than men.(23) Additionally, the proportion of patients with less optimal angina patterns (Classes 4–6) who did not undergo revascularization in the invasive arm for lack of significant coronary stenoses further suggests that microvascular dysfunction may be a potential underlying mechanism rather than overt macrovascular disease.(24) It is also noteworthy that participants who continued smoking were more likely to belong to classes with less favorable health status trajectories, as noted previously in patients undergoing PCI,(25) underscoring the importance of optimal secondary prevention. Importantly, the number of diseased vessels and the magnitude of ischemia were similar across classes. While it is not surprising that patients in trajectory groups with worse angina patterns generally had lower SAQ scores at baseline, reflecting their initial symptom burden, future research could validate these findings and explore predictive tools to estimate an individual patient’s likely angina pattern. These findings thus provide a foundation for future work to examine modifiable predictors of trajectory class membership, assess whether targeted interventions can shift patients toward more favorable recovery patterns, and support efforts to enhance shared decision-making and patient communication about symptom relief over time.

Our findings should be interpreted in the context of several potential limitations. First, as is customary in latent class modeling, we assigned each participant to the class to which they had the highest posterior probability. While this approach inevitably involves some classification uncertainty, overall separability was acceptable: all average posterior probabilities exceeded 70% (Supplemental Table 2). To further evaluate distinctiveness, we examined cross-class probabilities. For instance, 27.4% of patients assigned to Class 1 had a non-zero probability of being classified as Class 3, whereas only 3.2% of those in Class 3 had any notable probability of assignment to Class 1. This asymmetry supports that the trajectories are meaningfully distinct, despite some expected overlap, and not merely products of random variation. Second, some SAQ-AF data were missing, primarily due to trial termination before full follow-up could be completed. To assess the potential impact of missingness on our findings, we performed a sensitivity analysis using multiple imputation by chained equations. Across four imputed datasets, the resulting latent classes remained qualitatively consistent with those from the primary analysis, with only minor differences in class membership (Supplemental Figures 3 and 4). These results support the robustness of our main findings despite the presence of incomplete data. Third, although qualitatively similar trajectory patterns were observed across treatment groups, comparisons between the invasive and conservative strategies were descriptive only and not based on a unified model. Therefore, interpretations should be limited to within-group inferences. Fourth, the study focused on 24 months of health status and did not examine long-term health status outcomes. However, given the stability of health status over time in ISCHEMIA, analyzing longer-term health status is unlikely to provide different insights than those observed at 24 months, a clinically relevant outcome.(4) Finally, our findings are only applicable to patients who met the inclusion criteria of the trial and do not extend to patients with acute coronary syndromes, reduced left ventricular ejection fraction, left main coronary artery disease, or refractory angina despite GDMT, who were excluded from ISCHEMIA.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Among ISCHEMIA trial participants with baseline angina, six unique trajectories of angina recovery were observed for both initial invasive and initial conservative strategies, ranging from rapid resolution to severe, persistent angina. A greater proportion of conservatively managed patients experienced persistent angina over time as compared with those treated invasively. These data highlight variations in longitudinal trajectories of angina in patients with CCD, and serial health status measurement would be warranted to optimize their care over time.
