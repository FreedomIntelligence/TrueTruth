## English Abstract

Primary hypertension (PH) affects over one billion individuals globally, yet less than 30% achieve controlled blood pressure (BP) with medication. Many patients require a combination of multiple medications to reach targets, but adverse effects and financial burdens undermine adherence. Additionally, prehypertension affects 25%–50% of adults, increasing the risk of cardiovascular complications. Early detection and management of prehypertension are crucial for delaying the need for pharmacological interventions. In recent years, clinical guidelines have increasingly emphasised non-pharmacological interventions for PH management. However, the diversity of non-pharmacological therapies and the inconsistencies in efficacy challenge clinical decision-making. This study aims to use network meta-analysis (NMA) to synthesise existing evidence on non-pharmacological interventions for PH, offering updated clinical insights and evidence-based support to optimise treatment strategies. It will also provide recommendations for integrating these interventions into community-based chronic disease management.

To identify potentially relevant randomised controlled trials, a reverse search strategy will be employed to ascertain all non-pharmacological interventions for PH. A well-constructed search strategy will be applied across nine academic databases (Web of Science, Embase, PubMed, PsycINFO, CENTRAL, AMED, CNKI, WF and VIP database) and three clinical trial registries (WHO ICTRP, ClinicalTrials.gov and ChiCTR) for studies conducted between 1 January 2014 and 1 August 2024. Two investigators will independently extract information from eligible articles and document reasons for exclusions. The primary outcomes will encompass changes in systolic and diastolic BP. Pairwise and Bayesian NMA will be conducted using ‘meta’ and ‘GeMTC’ package (R 4.4.1). Risk of bias will be assessed using the Risk of Bias 2 tool, and the quality of evidence will be evaluated according to the Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development and Evaluation approach.

As this review involves secondary analysis of previously published data, ethical approval is not required. The results will be published in peer-reviewed journals.

## 背景 / Background

Primary hypertension (PH) is a significant global health concern and has become the leading contributor to cardiovascular disease and mortality worldwide.1 Epidemiological studies estimate that more than one billion people are affected by PH globally,2 with the majority (approximately two-thirds) residing in low-income and middle-income countries.3 4 Effective management of hypertension remains a challenge due to both the complexity of treatment and the high costs associated with pharmaceutical interventions. Notably, more than half of hypertensive patients require two or more medications to achieve target blood pressure (BP) levels.5 However, polypharmacy is often associated with side effects, financial burdens and poor adherence to treatment regimens.6 This, in turn, results in suboptimal BP control, with fewer than 30% of patients worldwide achieving recommended targets.3 Alarmingly, prehypertension, defined as the systolic BP between 120 and 139 mm Hg and diastolic pressure between 80 and 89 mm Hg, affects 25%–50% of adults, placing them at high risk for developing hypertension and cardiovascular diseases.79

Given these challenges, the need for effective, accessible and cost-efficient alternatives to pharmaceutical treatments has gained increasing attention. Non-pharmacological interventions (NPIs), which have been acknowledged by the International Society of Hypertension and the American Heart Association as beneficial for individuals with prehypertension and mild PH,10 11 offer a promising approach. These interventions, including lifestyle modifications, exercise, yoga, dietary changes and others, are not only less invasive but also potentially reduce the risk of polypharmacy-related complications.12 Despite these advantages, their clinical implementation remains limited for several reasons. First, the comparative efficacy of these interventions has not been well established due to the limited number of high-quality studies available.13 As a result, their recommendation level in major clinical guidelines remains relatively low.10 11 14 15 Second, the deployment of these interventions is often hindered by a lack of trained healthcare professionals, especially in low-resource settings where the burden of PH is greatest.1618

While previous meta-analyses have assessed the efficacy of individual non-pharmacological interventions,1922 they fall short in providing a comprehensive comparison of multiple interventions. Moreover, existing network meta-analyses (NMAs) have not adequately included complementary, alternative or traditional therapies widely used in regions such as Africa and China, where large populations rely on these treatments.11 Therefore, there is a critical gap in the literature regarding the relative effectiveness of different non-pharmacological strategies for PH management. This gap has implications for both clinical practice and public health policy, as it limits the development of evidence-based recommendations and feasible service delivery models.

In response to these limitations, we propose to conduct an NMA to integrate existing evidence and compare the efficacy of various non-pharmacological interventions for managing PH. NMA allows for the simultaneous comparison of multiple interventions, even when direct comparisons are lacking, thereby providing a more robust and comprehensive evaluation of their relative effectiveness.23 24 Through this approach, we aim to generate high-quality evidence that can inform clinical decision-making and guide the development of more accessible and effective hypertension management strategies.

(1) To systematically integrate research on non-pharmacological interventions for PH over the past decade using NMA, providing comprehensive aggregated evidence to improve PH management. (2) To generate robust evidence for developing an optimised framework for incorporating non-pharmacological interventions into community-based chronic disease management.

## 方法 / Methods

The registration process for this systematic review protocol has been finalised on PROSPERO (CRD42023451073). This protocol will adhere to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) Protocol guidelines to ensure transparency and completeness in reporting25 (see online supplemental appendix 1). The methodological framework for conducting the review will be based on established practices, including the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions and the Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) framework for evidence quality assessment. The NMA will follow PRISMA-NMA guidelines,26 with the anticipated start date in October 2024 and the end date in December 2024.

The diagnostic criteria for PH follow the 2018 Guidelines for the Management of Arterial Hypertension, jointly published by the European Society of Cardiology and the European Society of Hypertension, which define PH as a systolic BP above 140 mm Hg and/or a diastolic BP above 90 mm Hg.27

Types of participants: adult patients with a systolic BP greater than 140 mm Hg and/or a diastolic BP greater than 90 mm Hg, measured multiple times using methods such as home BP monitoring, office or clinic BP measurement, or a 24 hour ambulatory BP monitoring, as well as those who have been previously diagnosed with PH and are currently on antihypertensive medication.

Types of interventions: this study conducted a reverse search first (online supplemental appendix 2). The interventions were not restricted so as to search as much as possible. After obtaining the studies, the frequency of the keywords and text words was sorted, and the intervention items that appeared less frequently or were irrelevant were eliminated. We then classified all interventions and extracted keywords to determine the non-pharmacological interventions that need to be included. The top 10 interventions were exercise, relaxation, acupuncture, diet, yoga, lifestyle, meditation, breathing, tai chi and stress reduction. Therefore, we included studies incorporating, but not limited to, these 10 interventions.

Types of control group: the control group consisted of either other NPIs or usual care. The usual care was defined as participants maintaining their usual lifestyle without making any changes during the intervention period.

Types of outcomes: the primary outcome indicator will be the change in systolic and diastolic BP. Adverse events related to non-pharmacological interventions will also be collected, such as poor BP control, nutritional imbalances, exercise-related injuries, local discomfort from acupuncture, infections, dizziness, emotional reactions and interactions with other medications.

Types of studies: we will comprehensively review and include randomised controlled trials (RCTs) of non-pharmacological interventions specifically targeting PH. The language will be restricted to English and Chinese.

Non-RCTs, including prospective cohort studies, cross-sectional studies, case-control studies, case reports, editorials, letters, conference abstracts, animal studies, reviews and meta-analyses.

Studies with insufficient information or unsuccessful attempts to contact the authors for missing data.

Studies with a sample size of fewer than 10 participants in either the intervention or control groups.

A comprehensive search will be conducted across three authoritative Chinese databases (CNKI, WF and VIP database) and six English medical databases (Web of Science, Embase, PubMed, PsycINFO, CENTRAL and AMED) to identify relevant studies published between 1 January 2014 and 1 August 2024. To avoid potential publication bias, supplementary data will be sourced from major clinical registries, including the WHO ICTRP, ClinicalTrials.gov and ChiCTR, to cover grey area studies. In addition, a manual search of references from included studies and similar reviews will be conducted to identify any further relevant studies.

The unique search strategies for each database are outlined in online supplemental appendix 2. The searches will be based on the following keywords: (1) disease-related terms, such as hypertension, elevated BP and high BP; (2) non-pharmacological interventions, including, but not limited to, exercise, relaxation, acupuncture, diet, yoga, lifestyle, meditation, breathing, tai chi and stress reduction and (3) study types, limited to RCTs.

Duplicate studies will be excluded using EndNote X9 reference management software.

Two reviewers (TX and QL), trained in systematic review methodology, will screen the titles, keywords and abstracts for relevance. Irrelevant studies will be excluded from further review.

Full texts of the remaining studies will be reviewed to confirm their eligibility based on the inclusion criteria. Any disagreements will be resolved through arbitration by a third reviewer (FL or TG).

Studies excluded after full-text review will have reasons for exclusion documented in online supplemental appendix 3.

Two independent investigators (TX and QL) will extract data using a predesigned data extraction form, which will include: (1) study identification (publication year, country and authors); (2) general study information (sample size, study design and the number of centres); (3) participant characteristics (age, sex, medication use and BP grading); (4) intervention and control details; (5) primary outcomes (eg, a 24 hour ambulatory BP or immediate BP) and (6) adverse events. After extracting the data, if there are too many interventions, we will categorise the interventions according to commonalities, and the groups that may have been formed are shown in online supplemental appendix 4. In cases of missing data, the authors of the studies will be contacted. Additionally, if study authors are unresponsive after two contact attempts over a 2 week period, those studies will be excluded. Figure 1 outlines the full study selection process.

Two assessors (XZ and YS) will independently evaluate the quality of the included RCTs using the Risk of Bias 2 (ROB2) tool developed by the Cochrane Collaboration.28 This tool assesses five key domains: the randomisation process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, outcome measurement and selection of reported results. Each study will be rated as ‘low risk,’ ‘high risk’ or ‘some concerns’ of bias. Any disagreements will be resolved by a third reviewer (FL or TG) to ensure consistency in the final assessment. All evaluation results will be comprehensively documented in the data extraction form, detailing the rationale for each assessment and the resolution of any disputes, thus ensuring the transparency and reproducibility of the study.

Pairwise meta-analysis will be conducted using the ‘meta’ package in R, initially comparing studies with similar interventions and outcome measures. Results will be reported as OR for dichotomous data and mean differences (MD) with 95% CI for continuous variables. Statistical significance will be defined as p<0.05. Heterogeneity across pairwise comparisons will be assessed using the I2-value and p value. If I2 is below 50% or p>0.1, a fixed-effect model using the Mantel–Haenszel procedure will be employed for data pooling. Otherwise, the Der Simonian–Laird procedure will be adopted to apply a random-effects model for the meta-analysis.29

The Bayesian NMA will be performed using the ‘GeMTC’ software package in R (V. 4.4.1), based on the Markov Chain Monte Carlo method.30 Stata V. 18.0 will be used to generate network plots of intervention comparisons. The relative ranking of non-pharmacological interventions will be calculated using the surface under the cumulative ranking curve and presented graphically.31 Node-split modelling will assess network consistency between direct and indirect comparisons, with statistical significance set at p<0.05. Based on these results, either a consistency or inconsistency model will be selected. Convergence will be evaluated using the Potential Scale Reduction Factor (PSRF), where values close to 1 indicate successful convergence.32

In accordance with the Cochrane Handbook 5.4 guidelines, heterogeneity will be considered significant if the I² statistic is 50% or greater, combined with a p value < 0.05.33 When substantial heterogeneity is detected, subgroup analyses will be conducted based on intervention types (eg, exercise, yoga, acupuncture), age, the severity of PH and different BP measurement methods. Additionally, sensitivity analyses will be performed to verify the accuracy of the study results and the consistency of the inferences.

To identify potential publication bias, the comparison-adjusted funnel plot and the netfunnel command (Stata 18.0) will be employed when more than 10 trials are included.

The quality of evidence will be assessed using the GRADE system.34 The ROB2 tool will evaluate the risk of bias, focusing on randomisation procedures, adherence to intended antihypertensive interventions, handling of missing BP data and the accuracy of BP measurement techniques. Heterogeneity will be assessed using the I² statistic, with evidence downgraded if I² exceeds 50% without an adequate explanation, which could suggest variability in the effectiveness of interventions across different hypertension populations. Indirectness will be considered if the study populations, interventions or outcomes significantly differ from those relevant to clinical hypertension management, such as differing thresholds for BP control. Imprecision will be assessed by examining whether the confidence intervals around effect estimates for BP reduction are wide or cross clinically significant thresholds for hypertension treatment goals. Publication bias will be evaluated using funnel plots and statistical tests, downgrading evidence if there is an indication of selective reporting or publication bias in studies of non-pharmacological interventions for hypertension.

This protocol does not involve patient or public participation in its development.
