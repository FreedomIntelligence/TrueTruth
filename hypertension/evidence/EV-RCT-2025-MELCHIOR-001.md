## English Abstract

Catheter-based renal denervation of the sympathetic nerves (RDN) is still not a generally accepted treatment option for patients with resistant hypertension. Data on long-term outcomes, efficacy and safety beyond three years are scarce. We aimed to report the clinical outcomes after RDN in a cohort of 19 patients with resistant hypertension, followed for up to 11 years.

We reviewed patient records from all consecutive patients who underwent RDN between January 2012 and October 2013. All recorded measurements of office blood pressure (BP) listed in the patient file, antihypertensive medications, cardiovascular events, and renal function between 2012 and 2023 were collected. For each year of follow-up (FU), the median office BP was calculated based on all available recordings. The primary endpoint was the change in systolic and diastolic office BP from baseline (prior RDN) to the final year of FU. A secondary endpoint was the change in office BP from baseline to the mean office BP across the entire FU period.

A total of 19 consecutive patients underwent RDN treatment, 10 men and 9 women with a median age of 63 years (range 35–74 years). The median patient FU was nine years (interquartile range (IQR) 8–10 years). The median systolic BP decreased from 181 mmHg (IQR 28.5 mmHg) to 142 mmHg (IQR 23.5 mmHg), with a median reduction of 41 mmHg (IQR 25 mmHg) (p < 0.001). Similarly, the median diastolic BP decreased from 97 mmHg (IQR 13.5 mmHg) to 77 mmHg (IQR 8.5 mmHg), with a median reduction of 21 mmHg (IQR 9.5) (p < 0.001). The changes in BP showed large interindividual variation and the BP reduction appeared to be progressive over time. The antihypertensive medication 10 years after RDN did not differ significantly from the treatment given at baseline. The overall median reduction in total Daily Defined Dose (DDD) was -0.68 and not statistically significant (p = 0.45).

In the present cohort of patients with resistant hypertension who underwent RDN 10 years previously, a sustained and progressive reduction in office BP was observed throughout the follow-up period, despite minimal changes in antihypertensive treatment.

## 背景 / Background

Despite the FDA approval of catheter-based renal sympathetic denervation (RDN) for the treatment of patients with resistant hypertension in 2023 and its Class IIb recommendation in the ESC guidelines (2024), RDN is still not a widely accepted treatment option for patients with difficult-to-treat hypertension, often referred to as resistant hypertension in several countries [1, 2]. As with any procedure, safety, efficacy and costs remain a concern [1, 2]. Current evidence regarding the efficacy and safety of RDN is primarily based on studies with follow-up (FU) periods of up to three years, during which no serious adverse events have been reported. However, data on the long-term effects and safety remain limited [3–10]. The available long-term FU studies are mostly based on selected subgroups of patients who underwent RDN several years ago [6–10]. Although the absolute reduction in blood pressure (BP) after RDN shows considerable variability, an average decrease of approximately 6–10 mmHg in systolic blood pressure (SBP) has been observed [2, 11].

No clinical or biochemical markers have yet been found to reliably predict the response to RDN, but the most substantial BP reductions are typically observed in patients with initially very high office BP. In addition, some reports suggest that RDN treatment may lead to a gradual, progressive reduction in office BP during extended FU periods [6, 10].

According to recent randomized controlled trials (RCTs), even a modest reduction of 5 mmHg in systolic BP is associated with a 10–15% risk reduction for major cardiovascular events [11–14]. The potential benefits of even modest BP reductions emphasize the importance of achieving effective BP control, especially in patients with uncontrolled hypertension.

Office BP measurement remains central to the management of elevated BP and continues to guide both the intensification and de-escalation of antihypertensive therapy in the real world. In this study, we aimed to assess individual long-term changes in office BP in a cohort of consecutive patients who underwent RDN for resistant hypertension ten years ago at a single center in Denmark.

RDN was performed using the first-generation radiofrequency (RF) ablation catheters, the Symplicity Flex Catheter System (Medtronic, Palo Alto, CA, USA), via the femoral route [4]. A single operator performed all RDN procedures. We aimed to perform at least four circumferential ablations separated longitudinally by 4–5 mm, in each main renal artery proximally to the first bifurcation. All patients were heparinized during the ablation procedure. Energy was delivered for two minutes from a RF generator (part of The Symplicity Renal Denervation System, Medtronic—Ardian 2011) according to an algorithm controlling temperature and impedance. All renal arteries measuring > 4 mm in diameter were targeted for ablation. Procedure-induced visceral pain was treated with synthetic opioids (Fentanyl).

The patients were discharged on low dose acetylsalicylic acid for 3 months and continued routine FU in their respective outpatient clinics for at least one year. During the first year of FU, the investigator received information on any events concerning efficacy and safety. Further clinical FU was recorded through our local electronic patient journal, gathering all accessible health information. A full medical history was available for all surviving patients 10 years after RDN treatment.

The primary endpoint was the median change in systolic and diastolic office BP from pre-RDN (baseline) to the final year of FU. A secondary endpoint was the corresponding change in office BP from baseline to the mean office BP during the entire FU period. For each year of FU, a mean BP based on all available measures was calculated. All office BP measurements were assessed using the OMRON M6 automatic blood pressure monitor (Omron healthcare Europe B.V. The Netherlands) and registrations were performed by physicians or nurses not involved in this publication.

Baseline office BP was defined as the median BP recorded prior to RDN and was compared with the referral office BP. The office BP at the final year of FU was defined as the median of all available measurements during the final year of observation. The mean office BP during the total FU was calculated from all available office BP measurements. If a patient developed a medical condition that could influence BP (e.g. heart failure, hemodialysis, bleeding or bacterial sepsis), the respective BP measurements were censored up to the year before the condition was documented in the patient file.

To assess whether the changes in BP could be attributed to changes in the given antihypertensive medication over time, both the total number of antihypertensive medications was recorded as well as the total sum of their corresponding Daily Defined Doses (DDD) for all patients at the time of referral and at the end of the FU period. The latter expresses the change in antihypertensive medication load. The DDD for each medication was calculated using the ATC/DDD Index, as provided by the WHO [15]. Antihypertensive treatment data were sourced from patient records or the Danish National Prescription Registry, which includes all prescription redemptions in Denmark since 1995, classified by ATC codes.

Due to the small sample size, data were summarized using medians and interquartile ranges (IQR). Changes in BP were assessed using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test. To assess whether baseline BP was associated with the magnitude of BP reduction, both linear regression and Spearman’s rank correlation were used. To address coupling bias, Oldham’s correction was additionally applied in the regression analysis. The mean change from baseline to the averaged post-RDN follow-up BP was estimated using generalized estimating equations (GEE) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) by least squares means. This method accounts for an unequal number of observations per patient and inter-individual correlations of repeated measures. Changes in the total sum of DDD per patient was calculated using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

## 结果 / Results

Between January 2012 and October 2013, 19 patients underwent RDN treatment (10 male) with a median age of 63 years (range 35–74 years). The median numbers of antihypertensive drugs at baseline were 5 (range 1–7). Comorbidities included ischemic heart disease (n = 1), minor stroke without disability (n = 2), and mild to moderate renal dysfunction (n = 3).

The baseline median systolic and diastolic office BP were 175 mmHg (range 160–212 mmHg) and 96 mmHg (range 84–126 mmHg), respectively. We compared the referral office BP with baseline office BP measured during hospitalization prior to RDN using the Bland Altman test. Both measurements were virtually identical (relative ratio 0.97). Seventeen patients completed a 24 h-ABPM; the median 24 h-ABPM measurement was 169/93 mmHg (range 146–200/81–110 mmHg). In two patients, the 24 h-ABPM was incomplete. Likewise, no significant difference in median office BP was observed between baseline and 3–6 months after RDN.

CT-angiography revealed more than two renal arteries (range 3–5) in 11 patients (58%). The median number of ablations in each renal artery was seven (range 0–15). In one case, only unilateral ablation was performed due to anatomical constraints.

In five patients, short-lived spasm was observed, but no patient developed persistent arterial stenosis or worsened kidney function during the first year of FU. In two patients, the number of antihypertensive drugs was reduced prior to discharge due to a significant, persistent drop in office BP following the ablation procedure. One patient was discharged without antihypertensive treatment, but treatment was later resumed.

Two patients, whose BP had normalized after RDN, were discharged from the outpatient clinic after almost two and three years of FU. Regression analyses showed no statistically significant association between baseline office BP and the magnitude of BP reduction. While a weak, non-significant positive trend was observed for both systolic BP (Beta ≈ 0.29, p ≈ 0.14, R2 ≈ 0.12) and diastolic BP (Beta ≈ 0.15, p ≈ 0.35, R2 ≈ 0.05), this trend disappeared after applying Oldham’s correction. Here, the association between baseline BP and change in BP was essentially null, for both systolic BP (Beta ≈ −0.05, p ≈ 0.81, R2 ≈ 0.004) and diastolic BP (Beta ≈ −0.08, p ≈ 0.65, R2 ≈ 0.01).

Regarding changes to antihypertensive medication over time, the total number of antihypertensive agents was reduced by one to four in seven patients at the end of FU, increased by two drugs in two patients, and remained unchanged in the remaining ten patients.

With respect to changes in the total sum of DDD per patient, a median decrease of −2.12 (range: −9.33 to −0.17 DDD) was found in 12 patients (63%). In contrast, 6 patients (32%) exhibited a median increase of 3.72 (range: 0.33 to 21.08 DDD), including one patient who had developed renal failure during FU with a substantial increase in furosemide dosage (i.e. 750 mg versus 40 mg daily), corresponding to an increase of 18,75 DDD from this drug alone.

## 讨论 / Discussion

Only a few studies have been published which try to elucidate the long-term effect and safety of RDN in patients with resistant hypertension [6–10]. Despite many limitations in the present study, a substantial reduction in BP was observed in most patients who had RDN performed 10 years earlier, although the response showed substantial intraindividual variation. Initial regression analyses suggested a weak, non-significant trend indicating that patients with higher baseline BP might experience greater BP reductions following RDN, consistent with prior findings [6, 10]. However, after applying Oldham’s correction, this association was no longer evident, and R2 values were minimal. These results suggest that neither baseline systolic nor diastolic BP reliably predicts the magnitude of BP reduction.

Another notable finding was that the BP reduction appeared to be progressive over time and could not be explained by changes in the prescribed antihypertensive treatment – neither in terms of the total number of medications nor in medication load expressed in DDDs. The underlying mechanisms remain speculative but may possibly involve delayed degeneration of renal sympathetic nerve fibers [6, 10].

As in other long-term FU studies [7–10], we lacked a control group and cannot determine the true causal effect of RDN. However, the persistent BP reduction observed in most patients — despite adjustments in prescribed medications — may suggest some sustained treatment effect. Several recent RCTs have demonstrated that RDN is efficacious in reducing BP in untreated patients with hypertension. In the SPYRAL HTN-OFF MED trials [4, 16] using the newer multielectrode Symplicity Spyral system, a reduction in 24 h-ambulatory systolic BP by 4 to 5 mmHg was demonstrated in drug-native patients compared with the sham control arm. Similar results have been demonstrated in the RADIANCE HTN SOLO and RADIANCE II trials [5, 17] using a different ablation approach. In patients treated with prescribed medication for hypertension, however, the efficacy of RDN has been less clear. Several explanations have been proposed, such as changes in lifestyle and medical treatment during follow-up, compliance issues, and importantly individual response to RDN [1, 2]. Most of these trials are characterized by short FU periods.

During FU, the BP measurement protocol was not changed or improved due to newer or more rigid measurement techniques. BP was measured routinely during outpatient visits or in connection with hospital admissions, although no explicitly standardized measurement protocol was applied. The present results reflect the clinical everyday life of patients followed in an outpatient clinic for resistant hypertension, who had RDN performed 10 years earlier with the aim of improving the control of their markedly elevated BP. Data on the prescribed antihypertensive medications were available throughout the FU in all surviving patients. In accordance with Sesa-Ashton et al. [8], only minimal medication adjustments were made during FU. The reductions in both systolic and diastolic office BP were greater than those reported in previous long-term FU studies [6–9], which may partly be due to differences in endpoints. Sesa-Ashton et al. [8] and Al Ghorani et al. [9] also presented long-term FU data (> 8 years) after RDN. However, both studies included less than half of the original RDN treated population, which may introduce selection bias.

A major limitation of the results from the SYMPLICITY HTN-3 study was the lack of operator experience, which meant that relatively few patients technically underwent complete circumferential ablation of the renal arteries [18]. Newer technology with the use of multi-electrodes as well as a more aggressive approach will probably lead to a more effective destruction of the renal sympathetic nervous system, and thus greater effect on blood pressure, but future and larger studies are needed to elucidate the long-term efficacy of RDN and the clinical trajectory of patients with resistant hypertension. Considering the observations from this long-term study, the future will show whether RDN will become a more widespread treatment option for patients with poorly controlled hypertension, including resistant hypertension.

Several limitations of the present study should be acknowledged. First, the study population was small. Second, the data was not derived from a prospective, placebo-controlled design. Third, the observed BP changes were not based on 24 h-ABPM, despite its use in all patients at baselineThe referral of patients with resistant hypertension to our center stopped at the end of 2013, as physicians awaited the results of the SYMPLICITY HTN-3 study. When the negative result of the study became known (April 2014), all Danish centers chose to stop performing the RDN treatment. The planned protocolized follow-up including repeated 24-h ABPM in all patients was abandoned, and subsequently, measurement of 24 h-ABPM was based on clinical indication.

Although 24 h-ABPM measurements might be the most reliable and have some prognostic implication, these measurements were thus only available in few patients during further FU in the outpatient clinic. We therefore chose to calculate the average annual office BP, based on values recorded by individual clinicians and documented in medical records, to illustrate overall trends. Office BP remains central in the BP monitoring of most patients with hypertension and is used to both up- and down-regulate antihypertensive medication. The use of an averaged BP for each year of FU in the current study without a control group was an attempt to minimize the influence of a potential placebo effect. Although regression to the mean may contribute to our significant BP reduction findings, the progressive decline observed over multiple years as shown in Fig. 3 argues against this being the sole explanation. Moreover, the decrease in BP was evident in patients who only had minimal changes in antihypertensive medication over time following RDN. In addition, BP tends to increase with age, especially systolic BP, due to age-related changes in the vascular wall [19], which only reinforces the findings of the current study.

Fourth, we acknowledge that BP recordings during the FU occurred under different varying conditions and, for some patients, at irregular time intervals, which on the other hand reflects everyday clinical practice. Approximately 80% of the patients were followed in outpatient clinics for 8–11 years, while the others were transferred to their general practitioner due to normalization of BP. Two patients were followed for only 2 and 3 years after RDN, respectively. In both cases, their BP had normalized, and they requested continued FU and BP monitoring at their general practitioner. Neither of the two patients had ever been hospitalized. Fifth, validation of medication adherence in primary care was not performed.

Finally, heart failure is a well-known confounder. We therefore reviewed the medical records and excluded BP measurements from the time when a patient developed clinical heart failure or experienced a decline in left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) as assessed by echocardiography. Two patients, aged 67 and 78 years, developed heart failure 11 and 7 years after the RDN, respectively, and their BP measurements were subsequently censored. In both patients, BP was low at the time of heart failure.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In this cohort of patients with resistant hypertension, office BP was significantly reduced ten years after catheter-based renal denervation. The reduction appeared to persist and even progress over time, despite minor changes in prescribed medical treatment. The present study is unable to demonstrate a specific causal relationship between RDN and the progressive reduction in office BP during FU. Considering the present observations, further long-term sham-controlled follow-up studies will be needed, especially given the high prevalence of uncontrolled hypertension in the general population and the fact that many patients fail to achieve recommended BP targets in parallel with lifestyle and medical interventions.
