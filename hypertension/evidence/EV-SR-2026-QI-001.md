## English Abstract

Hypertension is a leading cause of cardiovascular and cerebrovascular disease-related morbidity and mortality. This study aimed to investigate the prevalence, treatment rate and control rate of hypertension among Chinese adults from 2019 to the present through a meta-analysis, providing evidence for the development of strategies and measures for hypertension prevention and control at the national level.

We systematically searched PubMed, Embase, The Cochrane Library, CNKI, Wanfang and VIP to identify cross-sectional studies published from 2019 onwards that reported the prevalence, treatment rate and control rate of hypertension among Chinese adults aged ≥ 18 years. Study quality was assessed using a measurement tool developed by the U.S. Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ). Statistical analyses were performed using Stata 18.0. Pooled prevalence, treatment, and control rates were calculated via random-effects models, with meta-regression analysis and subgroup analyses conducted to assess heterogeneity.

Twenty-seven studies involving 465,805 participants were included. The pooled age-standardized rates (2016–2022) were as follows: hypertension prevalence, 33.1% (95% CI: 29.3%, 37.0%); treatment rate, 32.8% (95% CI: 28.9%, 36.7%); and control rate, 14.9% (95% CI: 11.1%, 18.7%). Geographically, prevalence was highest in Northeast China (47.3%) and lowest in the West (33.3%), while the control rate showed an inverse pattern (West: 19.6%; Northeast: 12.6%) (both p < 0.001). Compared with rural residents, urban residents had a higher treatment rate (49.2% vs. 38.1%, p = 0.008). All three rates increased with age, reaching 59.4%, 42.9%, and 16.8% for prevalence, treatment, and control rates, respectively, in adults aged ≥ 60 years. Men had a higher prevalence than women (39.5% vs. 33.7%, p = 0.038), whereas women had a higher treatment rate (47.4% vs. 41.1%, p = 0.011).

Hypertension remains a major public health challenge in China. It is essential not only to improve hypertension prevention strategies but also to optimize hypertension control measures while accounting for regional variations and age-related differences.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension remains a leading cause of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality worldwide. The 2023 World Health Organization (WHO) Global Report on Hypertension revealed that the number of adults with hypertension has doubled over the past three decades, reaching 1.3 billion in 2019, yet only 21% of those aged 30–79 achieved blood pressure control [1]. In China, data from the Chronic Disease and Risk Factors Surveillance indicate that the age-standardized prevalence of hypertension among adults aged 18–69 increased from 20.8% in 2004 to 24.7% in 2018 [2]. Despite this upward trend, the standardized treatment and control rates remained notably low at 34.6% and 12.0%, respectively [2]. Approximately 274 million adults in China are estimated to have hypertension, of whom approximately 240 million have inadequately controlled blood pressure [2]. Although China has implemented national initiatives such as the National Basic Public Health Services Program and ‘Healthy China 2030’ Plan to improve hypertension management, the control rate has plateaued at a relatively low level since 2015, with widening disparities across regions [3–5]. To synthesize the evolving evidence and systematically evaluate the temporal trends, regional variation and population characteristics of hypertension prevalence, treatment rates, and control rates in China after 2018, we conducted a meta-analysis to offer an evidence-based overview to inform policy and practice.

## 方法 / Methods

The inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) Cross-sectional studies reporting the prevalence, treatment rate, and/or control rate of hypertension among community-based Chinese adults aged ≥ 18 years. (2) Study period: studies whose data collection was completed on or after January 1, 2019. To maximize data utility and avoid excluding valuable contemporary studies, studies whose data collection began before 2019 but extended into or beyond 2019 were retained in their entirety. (3) Hypertension diagnostic criteria: hypertension was defined as a systolic blood pressure ≥ 140 mmHg and/or a diastolic blood pressure ≥ 90 mmHg and/or current use of antihypertensive medication [6]. (4) Measurement indicators: hypertension prevalence: the proportion of the surveyed population with SBP ≥ 140 mmHg and/or DBP ≥ 90 mmHg or with a previous diagnosis of hypertension and on medication during the past two weeks [7]. hypertension treatment rate: the proportion of hypertensive patients who had taken antihypertensive medication during the past two weeks [7]. hypertension control rate: among hypertensive patients, the proportion whose measured blood pressure was < 140/90 mmHg [7].

The exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) Studies published before 2019; (2) Study populations aged below 18 years; (3) Studies surveying nongeneral populations (e.g., specific occupational groups or populations with particular medical conditions); (4) Duplicate publications (when duplicates existed, lower-quality versions were excluded); (5) Studies for which the required outcome data could not be obtained or reliably estimated; (6) Review articles and conference abstracts; (7) Original studies with quality assessment scores below 4 points; and (8) Studies not published in Chinese or English.

A systematic literature search was conducted in both English and Chinese databases, including PubMed, Embase, The Cochrane Library, China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI), Wanfang data (WanFang), and VIP Chinese Journal Database (VIP), to identify literature reporting hypertension prevalence, treatment and control rates among Chinese adults. the search timeframe was limited to publications from January 1, 2019, to January 1, 2025. the Chinese search terms included “高血压” (hypertension), “患病率” (prevalence), “治疗率” (treatment rate), and “控制率” (control rate), while the English search terms included “hypertension”, “prevalence”, “treatment rate”, “control rate”, and “Chinese people”, along with their relevant synonyms. Searches of Chinese databases were conducted using “title + keyword” fields, while English database searches used subject headings (MeSH/Emtree), supplemented by manual screening of reference lists of the included studies. the complete search strategies for the databases are provided in appendix S1.

Two reviewers independently screened the literature, extracted data, and cross-verified the results. Discrepancies were resolved through discussion or consultation with a third arbitrator. The screening process involved an initial title/abstract review to eliminate duplicates and clearly irrelevant studies, followed by a full-text assessment to exclude publications that failed to meet the predefined eligibility criteria. For studies lacking essential data, corresponding authors were contacted by email or telephone to obtain missing information. The extracted dataset included (1) Basic study characteristics (first author, study period, geographic location detailed to the provincial level, with some specified to the City level, specific sampling methodology); (2) Participant demographics (age, sex, ethnicity, educational attainment); (3) Information necessary to conduct risk-of-bias assessment using the AHRQ tool; and (4) Outcome measures, including hypertension prevalence, treatment rates, control rates, and diagnostic thresholds. Regarding the outcome rates, we primarily extracted the unadjusted rates as reported to maximize consistency across studies.

The methodological quality of cross-sectional studies was assessed using the 11-item evaluation criteria recommended by the U.S. Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ). Each item was rated as “Yes” (1 point), “No” (0 points), or “Unclear” (0 points), yielding total scores ranging from 0 to 11. Based on total scores, studies were categorized into three quality levels: low quality (0–3 points), moderate quality (4–7 points), and high quality (8–11 points) [8].

Meta-analysis was performed using Stata 18.0 software. Pooled estimates of hypertension prevalence, treatment and control rates were calculated using DerSimonian–Laird random effects models. The Freeman–Tukey double arcsine transformation was applied to stabilize variances of study-specific rates before pooling. Study eligibility for each synthesis was determined by comparing study characteristics against predefined subgroups, including time period (2016–2019 vs. 2020–2022), region (eastern, western, central, and northeastern China), urban–rural setting, age groups (18–34, 35–59, and ≥ 60 years), and sex. Heterogeneity across studies was assessed using I² statistics and Cochran’s Q test. Meta-regression and subgroup analyses were conducted to explore potential sources of heterogeneity, and subgroup differences were compared using Z tests to determine statistical significance. Notably, these subgroup analyses were exploratory in nature, and their results (particularly p values) should therefore be interpreted with caution. A Bonferroni correction was applied to account for multiple comparisons in the pairwise subgroup analyses (adjusted significance level α = 0.05/number of comparisons). Sensitivity analysis was performed by sequentially excluding individual studies to assess the robustness of the results. Publication bias was evaluated using Egger’s test and funnel plots (α = 0.05). Additionally, standardized rates were calculated using age-specific population composition data from the 2018 China Statistical Yearbook, published by the National Bureau of Statistics of China. Regional rate visualization was generated using R software version 4.4.1.

## 结果 / Results

The initial literature search identified 5714 publications (2507 in Chinese and 3207 in English). After removing 994 duplicate records, 4681 articles were excluded through title/abstract screening. Following full-text review of the remaining 39 articles, 12 were excluded, resulting in 27 studies ultimately being included in the analysis. The detailed selection process is illustrated in Fig. 1.

The meta-analysis included regional subgroup analyses with the following study distributions: for eastern China, 9 studies reported hypertension prevalence while 8 studies each reported treatment and control rates; for central China, 7 studies reported prevalence and 3 studies each reported treatment and control rates; for western China, 9 studies reported prevalence and 8 studies each reported treatment and control rates; and for Northeast China, 2 studies reported hypertension prevalence, with 1 study each reporting treatment and control rates. A larger study conducted across four cities in Liaoning Province (n = 9,524; multistage cluster random sampling) reported a prevalence of 51.9% (95% CI: 46.8%, 57.1%), a treatment rate of 41.8% (95% CI: 40.4%, 43.3%), and a control rate of 11.9% (95% CI: 10.9%, 12.8%). Hypertension prevalence was markedly higher in the northeast than in the eastern, central and western regions (all p < 0.001), while control rates were lower than those observed in the other regions (all p < 0.001). In contrast, pairwise comparisons among the eastern, central and western regions revealed no significant differences in prevalence, treatment or control rates (all p > 0.05) (Fig. 5).

Data on hypertension prevalence, treatment and control rates in urban and rural populations were available from 14, 10, and 10 studies, respectively. The pooled prevalence was 40.8% (95% CI: 35.9%, 45.7%) in urban areas and 42.7% (95% CI: 38.2%, 47.3%) in rural areas, with no significant difference (p = 0.569). The pooled treatment rate was significantly higher in urban areas (49.2%; 95% CI: 42.5%, 56.0%) than in rural areas (38.1%; 95% CI: 33.5%, 42.8%) (p = 0.008). For control rates, the urban and rural estimates were 18.0% (95% CI: 12.5%, 24.3%) and 12.5% (95% CI: 9.3%, 16.2%), respectively; this difference was not significant (p = 0.104).

Data on hypertension prevalence, treatment and control rates across age groups were extracted from 14, 11, and 11 studies for adults aged 18–34 years; from 23, 17, and 17 studies for adults aged 35–59 years; and from 23, 17, and 17 studies for adults aged ≥ 60 years, respectively. The pooled prevalence increased markedly with age: 10.8% (95% CI: 7.0%, 15.4%) in the 18–34 age group, 34.4% (95% CI: 30.3%, 38.7%) in the 35–59 age group, and 59.4% (95% CI: 55.6%, 63.2%) in the ≥ 60 age group. The difference across age groups was significant (p < 0.001). Similarly, the pooled treatment rate significantly increased with age: 8.2% (95% CI: 4.2%, 13.3%) in the 18–34 age group, 38.4% (95% CI: 33.3%, 43.7%) in the 35–59 age group, and 42.9% (95% CI: 34.1%, 52.0%) in the ≥ 60 age group (p < 0.001). In contrast, the pooled control rate did not differ significantly across age groups: 8.5% (95% CI: 3.1%, 15.9%) in the 18–34 age group, 17.8% (95% CI: 13.8%, 22.2%) in the 35–59 age group, and 16.8% (95% CI: 10.3%, 24.4%) in the ≥ 60 age group (p = 0.117).

Data on hypertension prevalence, treatment and control rates for males and females were available from 25, 17, and 17 studies, respectively. The pooled hypertension prevalence was significantly higher in males (39.5%; 95% CI: 35.2%, 44.0%) than in females (33.7%; 95% CI: 30.3%, 37.1%) (p = 0.038). Conversely, the pooled treatment rate was significantly higher in females (47.4%; 95% CI: 43.7%, 51.1%) than in males (41.1%; 95% CI: 37.9%, 44.3%) (p = 0.011). For control rates, the pooled estimate was 15.5% (95% CI: 12.0%, 19.5%) for males and 19.3% (95% CI: 14.9%, 24.1%) for females. This difference was not significant (p = 0.211).

Multivariable meta-regression analysis revealed that older age (β = 1.025, 95% CI: 0.745, 1.305; p < 0.001) and rural residence (β = 0.360, 95% CI: 0.026, 0.694; p = 0.035) were significantly associated with increased hypertension prevalence. No other covariates (sex, middle age, survey period, or regional distribution) were significantly associated with prevalence, treatment or control rates (all p > 0.05).

Sensitivity analysis demonstrated robust pooled estimates for hypertension prevalence (38.0%, range: 37.2%–38.6% after sequential exclusion), treatment rate (45.0%, range: 44.1%–45.9%), and control rate (19.8%, range: 17.1%–20.6%), indicating that no single study disproportionately influenced the overall results. Funnel plot analysis suggested potential publication bias for the pooled estimates of hypertension prevalence (Egger’s test, p = 0.007) and treatment rate (Egger’s test, p = 0.028). In contrast, no significant publication bias was detected for the pooled estimate of control rate (Egger’s test, p = 0.716). The publication bias funnel plots are presented in Appendix S1, Figure S1.

## 讨论 / Discussion

This meta-analysis provides an updated synthesis of hypertension epidemiology in China from 2016 to 2022. The principal finding is a persistently high burden juxtaposed with inadequate management: while the age-standardized prevalence remained elevated at 33.1%, treatment and control rates stagnated at concerningly low levels of 32.8% and 14.9%, respectively. This persistent “high prevalence, low control” pattern highlights hypertension as an unrelenting public health crisis in China, necessitating a critical re-evaluation of current prevention and management strategies. Compared with 2018 data (27.5% prevalence, 45.8% treatment rate, and 16.8% control rate) [6], the prevalence has increased, while the treatment and control rates have declined.

Our findings align closely with the most recent national estimate, which reported a prevalence of 31.2%, a treatment rate of 38.7%, and a control rate of 12.9% for 2021–2022 [36]. This remarkable consistency validates our methodological approach and confirms the severe national burden. More importantly, this study reveals significant disparities in hypertension management across different regions of China through the synthesis of regional data—a finding that is critical for localized policy-making. The highest prevalence was observed in Northeast China (47.3%) and the lowest in the West (34.3%), while control rates showed an inverse pattern (West: 19.6%, Northeast: 12.6%). Central China underperformed in both treatment and control. This granular panorama highlights the need for regionally tailored interventions.

The overall hypertension prevalence among Chinese adults (33.1%) from 2016 to 2022 was significantly higher than the prevalence (28%) reported for adults in the Western Pacific region in the WHO 2019 report [1], a difference that may be attributed to multiple factors. First, the average daily salt intake among Chinese residents (approximately 17 g) is significantly higher than the WHO-recommended limit of 5 g [1], which may contribute to the increased risk of hypertension. Second, China’s rapidly ageing population may also be a critical influencing factor. The data reveal that China transitioned from an ageing society (2001) to an aged society (2021, with ≥ 14.20% of the population aged ≥ 65 years [37]) in just 20 years—a rate substantially higher than comparable rates in France (126 years) and the United Kingdom (46 years) [38]. Lower treatment (32.8%) and control rates (14.9%) compared with the Western Pacific region (41% and 18%) [1] may also stem from inconsistent implementation at the primary care level, high out-of-pocket costs for newer antihypertensive drugs (e.g., ARBs), and urban–rural disparities in health care resources [39].

The attenuated urban‒rural disparity in control rates, in contrast to the persistent gap in treatment rates, is a notable finding. This pattern may reflect the tangible impact of the National Essential Public Health Service Package (NEPHSP), a primary care-led initiative launched in 2009 [40]. The NEPHSP has systematically expanded hypertension screening and follow-up management in rural communities, potentially increasing control rates closer to those in urban communities [3, 41]. However, the persistent lower treatment rate in rural areas suggests that barriers to initial health care access and medication initiation—such as fewer health care resources, financial constraints, and lower health literacy—remain unaddressed [42, 43]. This contrast suggests that public health management must not only strengthen standardized care for diagnosed patients but also shift resources and policy focus upstream to the initial phase of health care access. The priority should be addressing the fundamental barriers that prevent patients, particularly rural residents, from taking the first critical step in seeking treatment [44, 45].

Hypertension prevalence increases predictably with age, a universal epidemiological pattern validated by our data. The critical public health challenge, however, lies in the dissociation between rising prevalence and stagnantly low treatment and control rates across all adult age groups [1], revealing systemic management failures. Alarmingly low rates among young adults (18–34 years) are driven by low risk perception and infrequent health care engagement [46, 47]. In middle-aged adults (35–59 years), initial treatment gains plateau due to lack of awareness, behaviour dissociation and competing life priorities [48], highlighting gaps in workplace and community health management [49]. For older adults (≥ 60 years), the complexity of multimorbidity [47], polypharmacy, and age-related physiological decline complicates guideline implementation, fostering therapeutic inertia and nonadherence [50, 51]. The convergence of suboptimal control rates after age 35 underscores a system-wide inability to deliver continuous, effective, and patient-centred care tailored to life-stage-specific needs. In addition, hypertension prevalence is higher among men than women, but treatment and control rates are similar—consistent with prior studies [52–55]—likely due to greater work stress, unhealthy lifestyles (smoking, alcohol consumption, poor sleep), and lower medication adherence among men [56].

This study has notable strengths. First, we systematically reviewed 27 studies covering 20 provinces across China’s four major economic zones, ensuring reasonable national representativeness. Second, our temporal trend analysis revealed a sustained increase in hypertension prevalence alongside persistently low treatment and control rates among Chinese adults—a finding that underscores the urgent need to strengthen national prevention and control programs. However, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, substantial heterogeneity among the included studies, despite the use of random effects models, may still affect the reliability of the pooled estimates. Second, despite broad geographical coverage, only two studies represented northeastern China, limiting a comprehensive assessment of hypertension in this region. Third, the search was restricted to Chinese and English language publications, which may introduce language bias by omitting relevant data from local minority-language journals. Furthermore, while the Chinese dissertation database (CNKI) was searched to capture relevant grey literature, the international clinical trial registries were not systematically searched. This approach may have limited the comprehensiveness of the evidence base. Finally, although focused on post-2018 evidence, the inclusion of data from as early as 2016 (due to reporting constraints in source studies) may affect the precision of estimates for the most recent period. These factors should be considered when generalizing the findings. Future research should prioritize addressing these evidence gaps through more focused studies in underrepresented regions, particularly northeastern China.

In conclusion, hypertension in China is characterized by high and growing prevalence coupled with stagnantly low treatment and control rates, with significant disparities across regions, urban–rural areas, age groups, and sexes. Concerted efforts must focus on implementing evidence-based, tailored strategies—particularly in high-burden regions and among underserved populations—to translate awareness into effective, equitable management and curb the escalating cardiovascular disease burden.
