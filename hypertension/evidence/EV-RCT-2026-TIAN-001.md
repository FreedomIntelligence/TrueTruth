## English Abstract

Hypertension and depression have a well-recognized bidirectional association, with comorbidity reducing antihypertensive medication adherence and elevating adverse health outcomes; depression affects 30% to 40% of hypertensive patients, far higher than the general population. However, validated, hypertension-specific depression screening tools based on machine learning (ML) are currently lacking. This study aimed to develop, validate, and visualize an ML-based depression screening tool for adult hypertensive patients. A cross-sectional study was conducted using 2005–2020 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey data of 6687 hypertensive patients aged ≥20 years (depression defined by Patient Health Questionnaire-9 score ≥ 10). Least absolute shrinkage and selection operator regression was used for feature selection, data were split into a 7:3 training/test set, and 7 ML models were constructed. Model performance was assessed by area under the receiver operating characteristic curve, calibration, and clinical utility (decision curve analysis); SHapley Additive exPlanations values ensured interpretability, and a web-based tool was built from the optimal model. The random forest model was identified as the optimal screening tool (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve = 0.761), with good calibration and favorable net clinical benefit (threshold probability: 0.07–0.39). Twelve key predictors were established, with sleep disturbances as the top factor, followed by poverty-to-income ratio and age. An interactive online screening tool (https://chunyuzhang28.shinyapps.io/Hypertension/) was developed, enabling rapid depression risk prediction for hypertensive patients via input of the 12 predictors. This random forest-based ML screening tool is a valid and practical tool for clinical depression risk assessment in adult hypertensive patients. Clinicians can use it to stratify depression risk in hypertensive patients, identify high-risk subgroups (e.g., those with sleep disturbances or low poverty-to-income ratio), and implement targeted early intervention and integrated management strategies for hypertension and depression, thus optimizing clinical decision-making for comorbidity care.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension and depression are major contributors to the global disease burden, with a well-documented bidirectional association that exacerbates clinical outcomes and elevates the risk of adverse cardiovascular events.[1,2] A large UK Biobank cohort study has confirmed this bidirectionality: depressive symptoms increase hypertension risk (hazard ratio = 1.27), and hypertension elevates subsequent depressive risk (hazard ratio = 1.65).[3] Mendelian randomization studies have further provided genetic evidence for the causal direction of this association, with multiple studies supporting depression as a causal risk factor for hypertension and weaker evidence for reverse causality.[3–5] In Peru, the prevalence of depression among adult patients with hypertension and diabetes mellitus reaches as high as 34.96% and 35.56%, respectively.[2] In Saudi Arabia, 23.8% of hypertensive patients who are aware of their condition and receiving treatment have an elevated risk of depression.[6] In addition, a large cohort study of young and middle-aged adults has verified that depressive symptoms are associated with an increased risk of hypertension with a significant dose–response relationship.[7]

The mechanisms by which depression promotes the onset of hypertension are complex, involving direct physiological pathways and indirect effects of health behaviors. In terms of physiological mechanisms, depression is associated with heightened activity of the renin-angiotensin system, as well as increased neuroinflammation and oxidative stress, all of which may directly lead to elevated blood pressure.[8] Behaviorally, depression is often accompanied by unhealthy lifestyles such as physical inactivity, which is a major attributable risk factor for noncommunicable diseases, including hypertension, worldwide.[1] Depression may also indirectly increase the risk of hypertension by influencing dietary patterns, for instance, reducing adherence to healthy dietary models such as the Mediterranean diet.[9] A large-scale survey of hypertensive patients across 5 European countries demonstrated that emotional factors such as depression and stress are significantly associated with an elevated risk of poor treatment adherence.[10]

Accumulating evidence has indicated that chronic low-grade systemic inflammation serves as a core convergent pathway linking depression and hypertension. Inflammatory mediators, including interleukin-6, tumor necrosis factor-α, and C-reactive protein, can impair vascular endothelial function and reduce the bioavailability of nitric oxide, thereby increasing vascular contractility and stiffness and directly promoting the elevation of blood pressure.[11] Hypertension induces vascular endothelial injury, and the damaged endothelial cells release proinflammatory cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor-α and interleukin-1β. These factors penetrate the impaired blood–brain barrier to trigger central neuroinflammation and activate the Toll-like receptor 4/NF-κB signaling pathway, ultimately leading to hippocampal neuronal injury and dysregulation of monoaminergic neurotransmitters, which directly induce depressive symptoms such as low mood and anhedonia.[12]

Currently, clinical practice and research primarily rely on traditional statistical methods (e.g., logistic regression [LR], Cox proportional hazards model) combined with limited clinical variables and depression screening scales (e.g., Patient Health Questionnaire-9 [PHQ-9]) for assessment.[13] For instance, the PHQ-9 and its abbreviated version (Patient Health Questionnaire–2) are widely used for rapid screening of depressive symptoms in hypertensive or diabetic patients in primary care settings.[14] Prediction models constructed based on traditional statistical methods, such as nomograms, have been employed to identify hypertensive patients at high risk of depression. The incorporated predictors typically include demographic characteristics (e.g., age, gender, educational level), lifestyle factors (e.g., smoking, physical activity), comorbidities (e.g., diabetes mellitus, stroke), and blood pressure control status.[13,15] However, these approaches exhibit significant limitations in addressing the complex heterogeneity of the hypertensive population, processing high-dimensional data, and enabling real-time dynamic prediction. Traditional models often fail to capture complex nonlinear relationships and interactions between variables, and their feature selection relies heavily on researchers’ a priori hypotheses.

With the advancement of medical informatization and artificial intelligence technologies, machine learning (ML) methods have offered new opportunities to overcome these limitations. ML can handle large-scale, multisource heterogeneous medical data and automatically mine deep-seated features and complex patterns, thereby potentially providing more accurate risk prediction. Preliminary studies have demonstrated that ML algorithms such as random forest (RF), extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost), and support vector machines (SVMs) outperform traditional LR models in predicting depressive risk in hypertensive patients.[16,17] For example, a study based on data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study found that the LR model achieved the optimal performance in predicting 3-year depressive risk in middle-aged and older hypertensive patients.[16] Another study demonstrated that by integrating oxidative stress biomarkers, demographic data, and comorbidity information, the RF algorithm achieved up to 92% accuracy in distinguishing varying severities of depressive symptoms.[18] These advances highlight the potential of ML in integrating multidimensional information and improving prediction accuracy.

To address the current research gap of validated and interpretable ML-based depression risk assessment tools for hypertensive patients, this study developed and validated 7 interpretable ML models using data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) spanning 2005 to 2020, aiming for the early identification of hypertensive individuals at high risk of depression. We comprehensively evaluated model performance, visualized feature importance and their predictive contributions via SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) summary plots, and developed an associated web-based application to translate the optimal model into a clinically practical tool.

## 方法 / Methods

This was a cross-sectional study conducted using data from the NHANES database, which is maintained by the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and covers the survey cycles from 2005 to 2020. Demographic, laboratory, and questionnaire data from each individual NHANES cycle were merged by participant ID to construct a comprehensive dataset containing all variables required for the present study.[19] All NHANES data are publicly and freely accessible via the official U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention website (https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes). The NHANES study protocol was approved by the National Center for Health Statistics Ethics Review Board, with the approved protocol numbers including Continuation of Protocol #2011-17 and Protocol #2018-01, and the study was conducted in strict accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. All participants enrolled in the NHANES provided written informed consent prior to data collection and sample testing.

The detailed flowchart of participant screening and the overall study design is presented in Figure 1. A total of 76,496 individuals were initially identified from the 2005 to 2020 NHANES database as the primary study population. Non-hypertensive individuals and those with missing examination data or incomplete clinical information related to study predictors and outcomes were subsequently excluded from the analysis, with a total of 69,809 participants removed through these exclusion criteria. Finally, the study included a total of 6687 hypertensive patients aged ≥20 years as the final research cohort.

To be eligible for inclusion in the study, the participants had to meet the following inclusion criteria: have been diagnosed with hypertension by a physician or healthcare professional, and be aged >20 years. Participants were excluded from the study if they met any of the following exclusion criteria: had missing examination data, and/or had incomplete clinical information regarding predictors or outcomes.

All NHANES protocols were approved by the NCHS Ethics Review Board and conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, with the corresponding Ethics Review Board protocols: #2005-06 (2005–2010), Continuation of #2011-17 (2011–2017), and #2018-01 (2017–2020). All original NHANES participants provided written informed consent for data collection. As this study only used preexisting de-identified public datasets, no additional ethical review or informed consent was required.

Depressive symptoms experienced by participants over the past 2 weeks were assessed using the PHQ-9.[20] This validated screening tool was developed based on the diagnostic criteria for depressive disorders outlined in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fourth Edition,[21] and it has been extensively validated for the screening of depressive symptoms in populations with various chronic diseases in clinical and epidemiological research.[21]

The PHQ-9 is a widely validated self-report instrument for assessing depression severity based on Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fourth Edition and Fifth Edition diagnostic criteria. Psychometric evidence supports its excellent internal consistency, with meta-analytic studies reporting Cronbach α coefficients ranging from 0.85 to 0.86 across diverse populations and clinical settings.[22] Confirmatory factor analysis studies have predominantly supported a unidimensional factor structure, with adequate to excellent model fit indices (comparative fit index > 0.95, root mean square error of approximation < 0.08) observed in various cultural contexts, including Spanish-speaking, Aymara, and Quechua populations.[23] However, some studies have identified alternative 2-factor structures (cognitive-affective and somatic dimensions), particularly in cardiovascular disease patients and specific cultural samples.[24] A comprehensive systematic review of 98 psychometric studies across 40 countries concluded that sufficient high-quality evidence exists for the structural validity of the unidimensional model when using a 4-point Likert scale, with pooled internal consistency of α = 0.85 and robust measurement invariance across sex, age, education level, marital status, and income groups.[25] The PHQ-9 also demonstrates strong convergent validity, evidenced by moderate to high correlations with related constructs such as anxiety (generalized anxiety disorder-7: ρ = 0.47–0.82) and inverse associations with psychological well-being (World Health Organization five well-being index: ρ = −0.11 to −0.70).[26] Diagnostic accuracy meta-analyses indicate optimal screening performance at a cutoff score of ≥10, with sensitivity and specificity both approximating 88% for detecting major depression, and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.95.[27] Furthermore, a large population-based study (N = 30,449) confirmed strict measurement invariance across age groups, sex, education level, socioeconomic status (SES), marital status, and residence area (Δcomparative fit index < 0.01), supporting the use of PHQ-9 total scores for meaningful between-group comparisons without bias.[28]

Covariates were selected based on established evidence of their associations with depression risk. Demographic and socioeconomic variables included gender, age, race, body mass index, the family poverty-to-income ratio (PIR), marital status, education level, alcohol consumption, and smoking status.[29–33] The health information variables included the presence of diabetes, asthma, heart failure, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), coronary heart disease, tumors, stroke, and sleep disturbances as defined by the Sleep Questionnaire.[29,31,34–37] Laboratory test indicators included plasma fasting glucose, high-density lipoprotein cholesterol, low-density lipoprotein cholesterol, triglyceride, total cholesterol, and the triglyceride-glucose index.[30,35]

Least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) regression was performed using R software (version 4.4.2) to identify potential risk factors for depression in hypertensive patients based on the optimal regularization coefficient λ.[38] The LASSO regression method helps to avoid collinearity between the covariates and to screen out the best predictive risk factors. Ten-fold cross-validation was used to ascertain the optimal number of variables, gradually introducing independent variables until certain coefficients were reduced to zero, thereby selecting the features most strongly associated with depressive symptoms.

Data extraction, cleaning, descriptive statistics, and analyses were conducted on the NHANES data using R software (version 4.4.2). The continuous variables that followed a normal distribution are reported as the mean ± standard deviation, while those that did not follow a normal distribution are presented as the median (interquartile range); the categorical variables are expressed as the percentage. Independent-samples t tests or Mann–Whitney U tests were applied to the continuous variables, while chi-square tests or Fisher exact tests were applied to the categorical variables. All statistical tests were two-tailed, with a significance threshold established at P < .05.

As illustrated in Figure 1, the final study cohort of 6687 hypertensive patients was randomly divided into a training set and a validation set at a ratio of 7:3. The training set included 4680 participants and was used for model development and parameter optimization, while the validation set consisted of 2007 participants and was exclusively used for the independent evaluation of model predictive performance. Seven different ML algorithms were used to construct predictive models for depression risk in hypertensive patients, including light gradient boosting machine, SVM, RF, decision tree, XGBoost, LR, and K-nearest neighbor.

Ten-fold cross-validation was employed in the model training process to ensure the stability and reproducibility of model performance across different subsets of the training data. Model performance was comprehensively evaluated using a series of complementary metrics to assess different aspects of predictive ability. Discrimination ability, which reflects the model’s capacity to distinguish between patients with and without depression, was measured using the AUC. Overall predictive accuracy was quantified using a set of classic indices, including overall accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, negative predictive value, F1 score, and Matthews correlation coefficient. The clinical utility of the developed models was assessed via decision curve analysis, which quantifies the net clinical benefit of the models across a range of threshold probabilities.[39] Calibration, which represents the consistency between the model’s predicted depression risk and the actual observed depression status, was evaluated using calibration curves that compare predicted and actual depression risk in the study population.[40] SHAP values were employed to identify the best-performing ML model. In addition, for the best-performing ML model, an interactive web calculator based on the Shiny package was developed for convenient model use.[41] All the statistical analyses, model building, and validation in this study were conducted using R software (version 4.4.2).

## 结果 / Results

A total of 6687 adult hypertensive patients aged ≥20 years were enrolled in the study, with an overall depression prevalence of 10.86% (726/6687) as diagnosed by PHQ-9 score ≥10. After random 7:3 stratification, the training set (n = 4680) and validation set (n = 2007) showed no statistically significant difference in depression prevalence (10.47% vs 11.76%, P > .05), indicating well-balanced baseline characteristics between the 2 cohorts (Table S1, Supplemental Digital Content). Based on the optimal RF model, an interactive web-based depression risk screening tool for hypertensive patients was developed (accessible at: https://chunyuzhang28.shinyapps.io/Hypertension/). The tool supports rapid input of the 12 core predictive variables and automatically generates a personalized depression risk prediction result for clinical use. A clinical example verification showed that after inputting the information of an 85-year-old married male smoker with high school education, low PIR, a history of stroke, sleep disturbances, and no other comorbidities, the tool accurately identified the patient as a high-risk population for depression (Fig. 8), verifying the good usability and clinical practicability of the online tool for rapid depression risk stratification in hypertensive patients.

## 讨论 / Discussion

This study developed and validated an ML-based depression risk screening tool for adult hypertensive patients using 2005 to 2020 NHANES data. Among 7 ML algorithms, the RF model achieved optimal performance with an AUC of 0.761, alongside good calibration and clinical utility (net benefit at threshold probabilities of 0.07 to 0.39). SHAP value analysis identified 12 core risk factors, ranked by importance as sleep disturbances, PIR, age, marital status, COPD, education level, smoking status, stroke, gender, asthma, diabetes, and heart failure. These findings confirm ML’s feasibility for precise risk stratification and highlight context-specific patterns unique to hypertensive patients, addressing the gap in hypertension-specific depression screening tools.

Sleep disturbances emerged as the strongest predictor of depression in hypertensive patients, reflecting a mutually reinforcing cycle between sleep, blood pressure control, and mental health. Obstructive sleep apnea participates in the occurrence and progression of hypertension through multiple mechanisms such as sympathetic activation, inflammation, and oxidative stress, which form a vicious circle.[42] A meta-analysis and trial sequential analysis demonstrated that short sleep duration is a definitive risk factor for incident hypertension.[43] A study of 703 patients with major depression and secondary insomnia conducted at Erasme Hospital (Belgium) showed a 32.01% prevalence of hypertension, with severe objective insomnia and low self-reported insomnia complaints identified as core risk factors, providing large-sample epidemiological evidence for the association between insomnia and hypertension.[44] A study by the University of Chicago, utilizing the gold-standard technique of microneurography, further confirmed that patients with chronic insomnia exhibit abnormal sympathetic neural regulation (impaired baroreflex function and augmented cardiovascular reactivity to stress), offering direct neurophysiological mechanistic support for this association.[45] Chronic insomnia is associated with sustained activation of the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal (HPA) axis, which places insomniacs not only at risk of mental disorders such as depression and anxiety but also at increased risk of physical illnesses, including hypertension, obesity, metabolic syndrome, and osteoporosis.[46] There is an established bidirectional association between sleep disturbance and depression, whose interaction is mainly mediated by the activation of inflammatory pathways, dysregulation of monoaminergic neurotransmitters, genetic overlap, and circadian rhythm disruption.[47] Early intervention for sleep disturbance holds significant clinical implications for the prevention, treatment, and recurrence prevention of depression. A meta-analysis confirmed that sleep disturbances increase depression risk by 2.27-fold.[48]

PIR, the SES core measure, ranked second in this study, highlighting the unique socioeconomic burden of patients with hypertension and depression. A meta-analysis has shown that individuals with low SES not only have a higher prevalence of depression but also exhibit a greater tendency for depressive episodes to progress to chronicity.[49] There is a globally consistent and significant association between low SES and an increased risk of depression, among which income level exhibits the strongest magnitude of association, with the risk nearly doubled in low-income groups.[50] A study based on U.S. adults aged 20 years and older demonstrated that compared with the high PIR group, the very low PIR group was associated with a 34% increased risk of depressive symptoms, the low PIR group with a 21% increase, and the medium PIR group with a 16% increase.[51] A study conducted among hypertensive middle-aged and older adults in China revealed that the association between hypertension and depressive symptoms was significantly present in individuals with low SES.[52] A study based on the Prime Time Sister Circles Randomized Clinical Trial found that more than half of middle-aged and older African American women with low SES and uncontrolled hypertension had depressive symptoms, highlighting the need to integrate depression screening into the routine management of hypertension.[53]

With the acceleration of the global population aging process, the prevalence of both hypertension and depression has increased significantly. These 2 conditions are highly comorbid among older adults, severely impairing patients’ quality of life and functional status while increasing the risk of cardiovascular events and all-cause mortality.[54,55] Epidemiological evidence indicates that the prevalence of depression in patients with hypertension is significantly higher than that in the general population. For example, a study based on the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study found that among 11,519 middle-aged and older participants, the comorbidity rate of depression in hypertensive patients was as high as 42.13%, which was significantly higher than the 34.13% in non-hypertensive individuals.[33] Another study focusing on middle-aged and older Chinese patients with hypertension also confirmed that patients with hypertension combined with other chronic diseases had a 2-fold higher risk of developing depressive symptoms compared with those with hypertension alone.[56] Age is not only an independent risk factor for both hypertension and depression but also a key effect modifier, which may exacerbate the vicious cycle between depression and hypertension through multiple pathways such as neuroendocrine, immunoinflammatory, autonomic nervous function, and behavioral patterns.[57] Studies have shown that the comorbidity of hypertension and depressive symptoms is significantly associated with elevated levels of high-sensitivity C-reactive protein, and this association is more pronounced in women and older adults, suggesting that age and gender may influence the interaction between the 2 by regulating inflammatory pathways.[57]

In older adults in China, smoking has been identified as an independent risk factor for hypertension comorbid with depression.[33] In a study conducted in Ethiopia, hypertensive patients with a smoking history had a significantly increased risk of developing depression.[58] In addition, a study focusing on low-income African American women in the United States found that smoking was significantly associated with depressive symptoms among women with uncontrolled hypertension.[53] One study demonstrated that smoking exerted a significant mediating effect in the bidirectional association between depression and hypertension, with a mediation proportion of 27.7%.[59] Chronic sympathetic activation induced by smoking is a key pathway linking hypertension and depression: it leads to the sustained release of catecholamines, which not only directly elevates blood pressure but also participates in the regulation of emotional circuits (e.g., amygdala, prefrontal cortex), thereby exacerbating depressive symptoms.[60] Both hypertension and mood disorders can further increase the risk of stroke; post-stroke, individuals are prone to developing or worsening mood disorders, which in turn deteriorate blood pressure control and stroke prognosis, forming a mutually reinforcing cycle.[61] A cohort study demonstrated that the control rate of hypertension post-stroke suboptimal, and the presence of a primary care physician, stroke severity, and depressive symptoms are key predictors of blood pressure control.[62] Hyperactivation of the HPA axis post-stroke is a key pathophysiological basis for the development of depression, which induces depressive symptoms through multiple pathways, including dysregulation of the “corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH)-adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH)-glucocorticoids (GCs)” axis, inflammatory responses, and gut–brain axis dysregulation.[63] In rural areas of Bangladesh, the prevalence rates of generalized anxiety disorder and depression among adults with comorbid diabetes and hypertension were 7.24% and 17.83%, respectively.[32] Depression and type 2 diabetes exhibit a bidirectional causal relationship through multiple pathways, including brain insulin resistance, inflammation, oxidative stress, and HPA axis dysregulation.[64] When hypertension and dyslipidemia coexist with other cardiometabolic diseases (including stroke, heart disease, and diabetes mellitus), their association with depressive symptoms is more pronounced.[65]

Multiple studies have indicated that the prevalence of depression among female patients with hypertension is generally higher than that among their male counterparts.[66,67] Research has demonstrated that the prevalence of hypertension in young women aged 18 to 44 years is lower than in age-matched men, whereas the prevalence of depression is considerably higher.[68] Estrogen protects emotion-related brain regions such as the hippocampus and amygdala by regulating neurotransmitters, neurotrophic factors, and autophagic pathways; postmenopausal estrogen deficiency disrupts this balance, promoting tau protein accumulation and neuroinflammation, thereby increasing susceptibility to depression and other emotional disorders.[69] In older adults in China, the association between hypertension comorbid with depressive symptoms and elevated high-sensitivity C-reactive protein levels is more pronounced in females.[57] In addition, gender roles and social expectations may influence health behaviors, healthcare-seeking patterns, as well as the perception and management of diseases, thereby shaping distinct disease trajectories.[70] Lower educational attainment is generally associated with a higher risk of depression and poorer hypertension control.[71,72] For instance, in rural areas of Bangladesh, educational level (grades 5–9) is a significantly associated factor for generalized anxiety disorder among patients with comorbid hypertension and diabetes mellitus.[32] Patients with lower educational attainment may have insufficient understanding of disease knowledge and treatment regimens, as well as limited ability to access and utilize health information, which directly impacts treatment adherence and self-management efficacy, thereby leading to poorer clinical outcomes.[73,74]

Future mechanistic studies should focus on exploring whether autonomic dysfunction, particularly sympathetic hyperactivity, constitutes the core common pathway linking hypertension, sleep disturbances, and depression. A critical scientific question requiring urgent validation is whether interventions targeting this pathway, such as non‑pharmacological therapies aimed at improving autonomic reactivity, can simultaneously improve blood pressure control and sleep quality as well as alleviate depressive symptoms. At the clinical level, quarterly assessments should be conducted for high-risk patients with comorbid sleep disturbances, low PIR, and other chronic conditions. Integrating multimodal data, including electronic health records and wearable device data, will facilitate the development of a more comprehensive risk assessment system. Meanwhile, individualized medical management should be implemented: prioritize the use of mood-friendly antihypertensive or antidiabetic medications, and target modifiable factors through continuous positive airway pressure therapy or smoking cessation; conduct targeted psychological interventions, including gender-sensitive support for postmenopausal women and accessible, easy-to-understand health education for patients with low educational attainment; and, in addition, address health disparities through system-level improvements, such as expanding insurance coverage and providing management training for primary care physicians.

## 结论 / Conclusion

This study developed and validated an RF-based depression risk screening tool for adult hypertensive patients using 2005–2020 NHANES data, which achieved good performance (AUC = 0.761) with favorable calibration and clinical utility. Twelve core risk factors were identified, with sleep disturbances, PIR, and age as the top predictors, highlighting context-specific risk patterns unique to hypertensive patients. Our findings confirm the feasibility of ML for precise risk stratification in this population and address the gap in hypertension-specific depression screening tools.
