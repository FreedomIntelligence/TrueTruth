## English Abstract

Hypertension is a leading contributor to cardiovascular morbidity and mortality. Trace elements such as cadmium (Cd), copper (Cu), iron (Fe), magnesium (Mg), selenium (Se), and zinc (Zn) modulate blood pressure through oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, and metabolic regulation. Although previous studies have largely focused on individual elements, the combined effects of trace elements remain poorly understood. This systematic review summarizes observational evidence regarding associations between blood trace element concentrations and hypertension, and evaluate potential interactive effects among multiple trace elements. We systematically searched PubMed, Wiley, Google Scholar, and Web of Science for studies published between 1970 and 2025 reporting blood trace element concentrations in hypertensive and normotensive adults. Sixteen studies met the inclusion criteria. Pooled mean differences (MD) and standardized mean differences (SMD) were calculated using random-effects models. Heterogeneity, publication bias, and potential interactions among trace elements were assessed. A value p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. Cd and Cu concentrations were higher in hypertensive individuals (Cd: MD = 4.67, 95% CI: 0.25–9.10; SMD = 2.48, 95% CI: 0.18–5.25; I2 = 99.4%; Cu: MD = 19.18, 95% CI: 4.48–33.87; SMD = 0.86, 95% CI: 0.26–1.46; I2 = 99.6%), although heterogeneity was extremely high. No consistent associations were observed for Zn, Fe, Mg, or Se. Very few studies assessed interactions between multiple trace elements. Evidence suggested no significant difference in the Zn/Cu ratio between hypertensive and normotensive groups.. Elevated blood concentrations of Cd and Cu are statistically associated with adult hypertension; however, these association should be considered hypothesis-generating, as evidence for other trace elements remains inconclusive. The limited research on co-exposures highlights the need for future studies employing multivariate and interaction models to better understand the role of trace element interactions in hypertension.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension (HT) is a major cardiovascular condition affecting over one billion people worldwide and contributing to millions of deaths annually (WHO 2023) (Mancia et al. 2023). Sustained elevations in systolic blood pressure (BP) (> 110–115 mmHg) increase the risk of cardiovascular complications (GBD 2019). While prevalence remains stable or declining in high-income countries (Mancia et al. 2023), HT continues to rise in low- and middle-income countries, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa (SSA), where over 46% of adults aged ≥ 25 years are hypertensive, with projections indicating a doubling by 2030 (Beaney 2020; Musung et al. 2021; Ataklte et al. 2015). This burden is driven by factors such as inadequate non-communicable disease programs, population ageing, urbanization, increasing obesity prevalence, poor socioeconomic conditions, and genetic predisposition in African populations (Mancia et al. 2023; Ataklte et al. 2015). Hence, early-onset HT in SSA contributes to premature cardiovascular morbidity and mortality (Ataklte et al. 2015).

Trace elements (TEs) are inorganic elements naturally present in minute quantities in human tissues but essential for numerous physiological processes, including enzymatic reactions, vascular function, oxidative metabolism, and BP regulation (Loyke 1991; Zhengduo et al. 2022; Liu et al. 2004; Fraga et al. 2005). Imbalances in TEs, whether due to deficiency or environmental exposure, can induce oxidative stress, inflammation, renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system dysregulation, and endothelial dysfunction, all key mechanisms in HT development (Kazi et al. 2008; Carpenter et al. 2002, 2013; Satarug et al. 2005; Valera et al. 2009).

Given the rising global prevalence of HT and its major contribution to cardiovascular morbidity and mortality, understanding both modifiable and biological risk factors is crucial. However, most meta-analyses have only focused on one or two TEs, limiting insights into their combined or comparative effects. To address this gap, the present systematic review and meta-analysis integrates data on six major TEs, cadmium (Cd), copper (Cu), iron (Fe), magnesium (Mg), selenium (Se), and zinc (Zn), all known to be essential for cardiovascular and metabolic homeostasis or known environmental toxins with documented effects on BP, thus providing a comprehensive and clinically interpretable assessment of their associations with HT. By standardizing measurement units and applying robust statistical methods, this review offers novel insights into the collective impact of multiple TEs in HT.

## 方法 / Methods

The systematic review and meta-analysis were conducted following a registered protocol on PROSPERO (CRD420251042661) and reported according to the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) guidelines (Page et al. 2021).

A comprehensive literature search of PubMed, Wiley Online Library, Google Scholar, and Web of Science electronic databases, as well as grey literature and citation tracking, was conducted to identify studies published between 1 January 1970 and 31 August 2025 investigating TE concentrations in patients with HT and compared to healthy controls. In the literature search, we used the following MeSH terms: (Hypertension [MeSH Terms]) AND (trace elements [MeSH Terms]). The related keywords applied to all databases were: (“trace elements” OR “zinc” OR “iron” OR “copper” OR “magnesium” OR “selenium” OR “cadmium”) AND (“hypertension” OR “high blood pressure” OR “elevated blood pressure”) AND (“blood” OR “serum” OR “plasma” OR “circulating”). In addition, the reference lists of the retrieved studies were screened to identify further relevant articles. Searches were performed in the title and abstract fields where applicable.

Titles, abstracts, and full texts were independently reviewed by three authors (JMM, JB, and MCK). Any discrepancy was solved through discussion with a fourth author (OM). Two authors independently collected the data, and final agreement was reached through consensus. The quality of the included studies was assessed using the Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) critical appraisal tools (Munn et al. 2020).

Studies were eligible for inclusion if they were cross-sectional or case–control in design, reported whole blood, plasma, or serum concentrations of Cd, Cu, Fe, Mg, Se, or Zn in patients with HT and in healthy controls, and defined HT as systolic BP ≥ 140 mmHg and/or diastolic BP ≥ 90 mmHg, or current use of antihypertensive medication. No language restrictions were applied during the selection process. Studies were excluded if they did not provide sufficient data to calculate effect sizes or if they reported combined statistics without stratification between hypertensive and non-hypertensive groups.

All included articles were screened according to the above criteria, and disagreements were resolved through discussion and consensus.

For each eligible study, we extracted the author, year of publication, and country, along with the reported mean and standard deviation of TE concentrations in both hypertensive and normotensive groups, and the corresponding sample sizes. Where studies reported TE concentrations in different measurement units, values were systematically converted into uniform units before statistical analyses to allow direct comparisons.

Specifically, Cd, Zn, Fe, Cu, and Se were expressed in μg/dL, whereas Mg was expressed in mg/dL. Such standardization procedures are commonly recommended in meta-analyses of biochemical markers to ensure consistency and clinical interpretability across studies (Higgins et al. 2024).

The primary effect size was defined as the mean difference (MD) in TE concentrations between hypertensive and normotensive individuals. This choice was justified by the uniformity of measurement units across studies, enabling clinically interpretable results (Andrade. 2020; Jing et al. 2024). For sensitivity analyses, the standardized mean difference (SMD) using Hedges’s g correction was also computed to account for potential differences in measurement variability and to mitigate small-sample bias (Lin et al. 2021).

Meta-analyses were conducted under a random-effects model, given the expected clinical and methodological heterogeneity across studies. The between-study variance (τ2) was estimated using the Restricted Maximum Likelihood (REML) method, and Hartung–Knapp–Sidik–Jonkman (HKSJ) correction was applied to derive more robust confidence intervals, particularly relevant given the limited number of included studies (Lin et al. 2021). Heterogeneity was assessed using the Q statistic, the I2 statistic (with thresholds of 25%, 50%, and 75% representing low, moderate, and high heterogeneity, respectively), and the τ2 estimate.

Small-study effects and potential publication bias were explored by Begg’s rank correlation test and Egger’s regression test. Given the limited number of included studies, the results of these tests were interpreted with caution.

All statistical analyses were performed using Stata version 16.0 (StataCorp, College Station, TX, USA) (StataCorp. 2019). P-values < 0.05 (two-sided) were considered statistically significant.

## 结果 / Results

A total of 2,385 records were identified through database searches, including PubMed (n = 1,842), Web of Science (n = 506), Wiley Online Library (n = 20), and Google Scholar (n = 17). Additional records were identified through citation tracking (n = 5) and grey literature (n = 2). References removed (n = 18) included duplicates identified manually (n = 4), duplicates identified by Covidence (n = 11), and records marked as ineligible by automation tools (n = 3). After removal, a total of 2,374 studies were screened by title and abstract. Of these, 2,131 irrelevant studies were excluded, and 3 could not be retrieved. A total of 240 studies were assessed for eligibility. Among these, 224 were excluded for the following reasons: use of non-blood samples (n = 18); inclusion of pregnant women (n = 23); non-comparable populations such as individuals with diabetes (n = 11), menopausal women (n = 3), alcohol-dependent/smoking populations, children, or cancer patients (n = 43), hypertensive-only populations without normotensive controls (n = 42), inappropriate study design (n = 47), inappropriate grouping (n = 24), or unavailable data (n = 13). Ultimately, 16 studies met the inclusion and exclusion criteria and were included in the meta-analysis. The mean age of hypertensive participants ranged from the mid-30 s to late 60 s across studies (Bartolin et al. 1985; Bergomi et al. 1997; Berthold et al. 2012; Canatan et al. 2004; Serinkan et al. 2020; Darroudi et al. 2018; Fatima et al. 2019; Frithz et al. 1979; Gouaref et al. 2016; Granadillo et al. 1995; Li et al. 1999; Rubio-Luengo et al. 1995; Tang et al. 2003; Thind et al. 1976; Vivoli et al. 1987; Zhang et al. 2022), with comparable mean ages in normotensive controls, ensuring age-matched comparisons. Sample sizes varied considerably, from fewer than 50 participants in early case–control studies (Bartolin et al. 1985; Frithz et al. 1979; Li et al. 1999; Thind et al. 1976; Vivoli et al. 1987) to large-scale cohorts involving several thousand individuals (Darroudi et al. 2018; Zhang et al. 2022). The TEs assessed included essential micronutrients such as Zn (Bartolin et al. 1985; Bergomi et al. 1997; Canatan et al. 2004; Serinkan et al. 2020; Fatima et al. 2019; Frithz et al. 1979; Gouaref et al. 2016; Granadillo et al. 1995; Li et al. 1999; Rubio-Luengo et al. 1995; Tang et al. 2003; Thind et al. 1976; Vivoli et al. 1987), Cu (Bergomi et al. 1997; Canatan et al. 2004; Serinkan et al. 2020; Fatima et al. 2019; Gouaref et al. 2016; Granadillo et al. 1995; Rubio-Luengo et al. 1995; Tang et al. 2003; Vivoli et al. 1987 and Zhang et al. 2022), Fe (Serinkan et al. 2020; Fatima et al. 2019; Tang et al. 2003; Zhang et al. 2022), Mg (Fatima et al. 2019; Granadillo et al. 1995; Li et al. 1999; Zhang et al. 2022), and Se (Berthold et al. 2012; Serinkan et al. 2020; Gouaref et al. 2016; Zhang et al. 2022), as well as toxic metals such as Cd (Bartolin et al. 1985; Tang et al. 2003; Thind et al. 1976; Zhang et al. 2022). Zn and Cu were the most frequently studied elements, often investigated together (Bergomi et al. 1998; Canatan et al. 2004; Serinkan et al. 2020; Fatima et aal. 2019; Gouaref et al. 2016; Granadillo et al. 1995; Rubio-Luengo et al. 1995; Tang et al. 2003; Vivoli et al. 1987; Zhang et al. 2022), whereas Mg and Se were less consistently evaluated.

Overall study quality, assessed using the JBI checklist, ranged from moderate to high, with scores varying between 62.5% and 100% (Bartolin et al. 1985; Bergomi et al. 1997; Berthold et al. 2012; Canatan et al. 2004; Serinkan et al. 2020; Darroudi et al. 2018; Fatima et al. 2019; Frithz et al. 1979; Gouaref et al. 2016; Granadillo et al. 1995; Li et al. 1999; Rubio-Luengo et al. 1995; Tang et al. 2003; Thind et al. 1976; Vivoli et al. 1987; Zhang et al. 2022). Variability in study design, sample type and analytical method likely contributed to the substantial heterogeneity observed in pooled estimates. Nevertheless, the wide geographical coverage and methodological diversity strengthen the generalizability of the findings, while underscoring the need for greater.

standardization in future TE research related to HT.

A total of 10 studies including 7177 hypertensive and 10245 normotensive individuals were included in the meta-analysis of serum Cu levels (Fig. 3). The Zn/Cu ratio is considered a biomarker of oxidative stress and inflammation, both involved in the pathophysiology of hypertension. The meta-analysis included four studies (Canatan et al. 2004; Darroudi et al. 2018; Gouaref et al. 2016; Tang et al. 2003) comparing serum Zn/Cu ratio in 4085 hypertensive versus 5897 normotensive individuals. The pooled MD was −0.22 (95% CI: −0.61 to 0.16; p = 0.26), while the pooled SMD (Hedges’s g) was −0.77 (95% CI: −2.04 to 0.49; p = 0.23). Neither approach demonstrated a statistically significant association between the exposure and the outcome. Heterogeneity was extremely high (MD: I2 = 98.8%; Hedges’s g: I2 = 98.8%), indicating strong inconsistency across the studies. ## 讨论 / Discussion

The present systematic review and meta-analysis evaluated the association between blood TE concentrations and adult HT across 16 observational studies. Overall, hypertensive individuals showed significantly higher levels of Cd and Cu compared with normotensive controls, while evidence for Zn, Fe, Mg, Se, and Zn/Cu ratio was inconsistent or non-significant. Although the effect sizes for Cd and Cu were modest, they were statistically significant. Importantly, the analyses revealed very high heterogeneity, indicating caution in interpretation. These findings suggest that Cd and Cu may play a role in hypertension, but given the very high heterogeneity and modest effect sizes, these results should be considered hypothesis-generating rather than definitive. Well-designed prospective studies are needed to confirm these associations and clarify the potential causal role of Cd and Cu in hypertension. The roles of other trace elements appear more variable and context-dependent, influenced by environmental exposures, diet, and individual susceptibility.

While the pooled MDs for Cd (MD = 4.67 μg/dL) and Cu (MD = 19.18 μg/dL) reached statistical significance, the absolute magnitudes were relatively modest, raising questions about their clinical relevance. Although these differences are modest, they may still be biologically meaningful. Cd accumulates in tissues over time and contributes to oxidative stress and endothelial injury even at low concentrations, while small elevations in Cu may promote vascular oxidative stress and endothelial dysfunction. Nevertheless, these modest differences do not necessarily exceed known clinical thresholds, and their relevance should be interpreted cautiously. More striking, was the consistently high heterogeneity (I2 > 95% across most analyses). This variability likely reflects genuine differences and could be attributed to several factors, including study design (cross-sectional vs. case–control), population characteristics (age, sex distribution, comorbidities, lifestyle, socioeconomic status, and occupational exposures such as mining or industrial work), environmental exposures, sample size, and methodological differences in TE assessment (serum vs. plasma, analytical techniques, laboratory calibration). Such diversity is not uncommon in meta-analyses of biochemical markers (Choi et al. 2025; Mikolajewicz et al. 2019), where concentrations are highly sensitive to contextual factors such as diet, soil composition, and pollution sources. Nonetheless, the magnitude of heterogeneity observed here underscores the need for cautious interpretation, and conclusions should be considered hypothesis-generating rather than definitive.

Heterogeneity in TE in HT studies likely reflects differences in population characteristics, including age, sex, comorbidities and socioeconomic factors, which varied across the included cohorts. Chinese populations exhibited age- and sex-related differences in serum TE concentrations (Zhang et al. 2022), while reference blood data confirmed sex differences for Cu, Se and Zn, with age-related increases in lead, Se, and Zn (Komarova et al. 2021). Genetic factors also play a substantial role, as demonstrated by heritable effects on TE concentrations, including Cd, Cu, Se and Zn (Whitfield et al. 2010). Given the multifactorial nature of HT, lifestyle and overall health status are additional contributors to variability between studies. TE status is further influenced by environmental and dietary factors. Se concentrations depend on soil content, which varies geographically (Kafai et al. 2003; Rembert et al. 2017). Cu and Zn intake are largely determined by diet and supplementation (Babaali et al. 2020), while manganese status shows regional and seasonal variation among Chinese adults with HT (Chen et al. 2022). Such geographic and dietary differences likely contribute substantially to the observed heterogeneity across studies. Methodological variability in TE measurement is another key source of heterogeneity. Differences in sample type, whether serum or plasma, and the choice of analytical method (e.g., AAS, ICP-OES, or ICP-MS) can yield discrepant results (Hall et al. 2022). Cu and Se concentrations are often lower in serum than plasma, partly due to sequestration during clotting (Arnaud et al. 2008; Luna et al. 2019). Modern ICP-MS techniques allow precise quantification of multiple TEs from small serum or whole blood volumes, but methodological differences persist across laboratories (Hall et al. 2022; Laur et al. 2020). Taken together, variations in population characteristics, environmental and dietary context, laboratory methodology, and sample size provide plausible explanations for the high heterogeneity observed in TE–HT studies and underscore the need for cautious interpretation of pooled estimates.

Our meta-analysis of nearly 7,000 participants demonstrated significantly higher serum Cd concentrations in hypertensive individuals compared to normotensive controls. Cd is a non-essential toxic metal that accumulates in human tissues, particularly the kidney and vascular wall, and contributes to oxidative stress, inflammation, and endothelial injury (Messner et al. 2010). Experimental and narrative evidence further indicate that Cd-induced endothelial dysfunction is largely mediated by impaired nitric oxide bioavailability and increased reactive oxygen species generation, resulting in vascular stiffness and altered vasomotor regulation (Martins et al. 2021; da Cunha Martins et al. 2018). Epidemiological evidence further shows that blood Cd levels are associated with elevated BP (Tellez-Plaza et al. 2008) and with an increased risk of peripheral arterial disease, highlighting its vascular toxicity beyond HT (Navas-Acien et al. 2004; Bo et al. 2008). In addition to endothelial injury, Cd exposure has been linked to disturbed vascular smooth muscle Ca2⁺ signaling and activation of the renin–angiotensin system (da Cunha Martins et al. 2018). Cd also exerts direct nephrotoxic effects. Following gastrointestinal absorption, Cd binds to metallothionein and accumulates in proximal tubular cells, where it induces oxidative stress and tubular injury. Cd exposure has been shown to alter cytochrome P450–mediated 20-hydroxyeicosatetraenoic acid production, thereby disrupting renal sodium handling and impairing pressure natriuresis (Satarug et al. 2017). Taken together, these mechanistic and epidemiological data strongly support Cd as a plausible contributor to HT risk.

Our meta-analysis of 10 studies, including 7,177 hypertensive and 10,245 normotensive individuals, demonstrated significantly higher serum Cu concentrations in hypertensive participants. However, some individual population-based studies have reported lower or inconsistent serum Cu levels in hypertensive subjects, underscoring the variability across settings (Taneja and Mandal 2007). Despite extreme heterogeneity, sensitivity analyses confirmed this association. Cu is an essential cofactor for enzymes such as superoxide dismutase, supporting antioxidant defense. However, excessive Cu exhibits pro-oxidant activity by generating reactive oxygen species, reducing nitric oxide bioavailability, and promoting endothelial dysfunction (Bo et al. 2008; Uriu-Adams et al. 2005). Elevated serum Cu has also been linked to lipid peroxidation, inflammation, and vascular damage, providing biological plausibility for its role in HT (Bo et al. 2008; Salonen et al. 1991). These findings highlight a dual role of Cu, where physiological levels support vascular protection, but excess exposure may drive oxidative stress and BP elevation. Mechanistically, hypertension reflects complex interactions between essential and toxic trace elements. Exposure to cadmium, lead, and arsenic induces oxidative stress, inflammation, and endothelial dysfunction through reduced nitric oxide bioavailability and increased reactive oxygen species, contributing to vascular injury and elevated BP (Doligalska Dolina et al. 2025; Angeli et al. 2013; da Cunha Martins et al. 2018). Imbalances in Zn and Cu further disrupt antioxidant enzyme activity and vascular homeostasis (Doligalska Dolina et al. 2025).

As comprehensively reviewed by Joshi. et al. (2024), macro- and micro-elements regulate BP through coordinated effects on the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system, oxidative and nitrosative stress pathways, endothelial integrity, vascular smooth muscle cell (VSMC) calcium signaling, and sympathetic activity. Dysregulation of Na⁺/K⁺ and Ca2⁺/Mg2⁺ homeostasis alters natriuresis, vascular tone, and RAAS activation, while toxic metals further impair soluble guanylate cyclase–cGMP signaling, increase intracellular Ca2⁺ in VSMCs, and promote arterial stiffness and pro-inflammatory cytokine production (Joshi et al. 2024). These converging mechanisms provide a coherent biological rationale for the observed associations of Cd and Cu with hypertension, despite modest effect sizes.

Our meta-analysis demonstrates that associations between essential TEs and HT are inconsistent. Cd and Cu showed relatively robust positive associations with HT, whereas Zn, Fe, Mg, and Se did not display significant pooled effects, despite well-described biological plausibility. Zn is essential for vascular homeostasis, supporting endothelial nitric oxide synthesis and BP regulation. Both deficiency and excess can impair vascular function, while Zn deficiency may further contribute to HT through altered renal sodium handling via upregulation of the Na⁺-Cl⁻ cotransporter (Tubek. 2007; Williams et al. 2019). Fe influences vascular biology through oxidative stress: deficiency and overload both impair endothelial function, promote reactive oxygen species, and alter vascular resistance, consistent with a U-shaped relationship with BP (Fatima et al. 2019; Mele et al. 2024). Mg acts as a natural calcium antagonist, promoting vascular relaxation. Some studies report lower serum Mg in hypertensive individuals (Fatima et al. 2019), while others show higher levels or no significant association (Granadillo et al. 1995; Zhang et al. 2022), highlighting inconsistent effects across populations. Se, as a cofactor for antioxidant enzymes, has a particularly complex role: low Se impairs antioxidant defense, whereas excess Se has been linked to increased HT risk, particularly in women, suggesting narrow safe ranges and context-dependent effects (Berthold et al. 2012; Zhang et al. 2022; Wu et al. 2018).

Our pooled analysis of Zn/Cu ratios, including 4,085 hypertensive and 5,897 normotensive participants, revealed no significant association (MD = − 0.22, 95% CI − 0.61 to 0.16; Hedges’s g = − 0.77, 95% CI − 2.04 to 0.49). This finding suggests that inter-element interactions may obscure individual associations. Zn and Cu share absorption and transport pathways, and imbalances in one can modulate the vascular impact of the other (Canatan et al. 2004; Darroudi et al. 2018; Gouaref et al. 2016; Tang et al. 2003). Prior studies reported that both serum Zn and Zn/Cu ratios influence lipid metabolism and cardiovascular risk, with higher Zn/Cu ratios linked to reduced HDL and increased LDL, triglycerides, and total cholesterol, thereby promoting atherosclerosis (He et al. 1994). Conversely, decreased Zn and Zn/Cu ratios have been observed in hypertensive patients (Bergomi et al. 1997; Canatan et al. 2004), reinforcing the context-dependent and possibly nonlinear nature of these relationships. Overall, these findings underscore the complexity of TE–BP interactions. Future research should adopt integrated approaches that measure multiple elements simultaneously, explore nonlinear dose–response patterns, and account for synergistic or antagonistic interactions to clarify the role of TEs in HT pathophysiology.

This meta-analysis presents several strengths. It is the first in its kind that objectively synthesize observational studies examining serum and plasma TEs in relation to HT. Measurement units were standardized across studies, enabling meaningful comparisons, and robust statistical approaches were applied, including random-effects modelling, REML estimation, and HKSJ corrections. Sensitivity analyses based on both MD and SMD further reinforced the validity of findings.

Nonetheless, some limitations must be acknowledged. The number of available studies was limited, particularly for Mg and Se, and all included investigations were observational, mostly cross-sectional or case–control, which restricts causal inference. Given the predominance of these study designs, reverse causation cannot be excluded; hypertension itself or its treatment may influence blood Cd and Cu levels, rather than the reverse. Residual confounding is likely, as many studies did not adequately account for key factors such as diet, renal function, or smoking. The extremely high heterogeneity observed across analyses further reduces confidence in pooled estimates and suggests that effect sizes are highly context-dependent. No significant publication bias was detected by Egger’s or Begg’s tests for any of the TEs; however, the small number of studies per element and the required unit conversions may have introduced minor inaccuracies.

Despite these limitations, the review provides important insights. The consistent associations identified for Cd and Cu highlight the potential contribution of environmental and dietary exposures to HT. Reducing Cd exposure, particularly through tobacco control and environmental regulation, may yield substantial public health benefits. Monitoring Cu status could also prove informative, though the clinical relevance of modest elevations remains uncertain.

Future research should prioritize longitudinal cohort designs to clarify temporality and causality, and adopt standardized laboratory methods and reporting practices to improve comparability. Simultaneous assessment of multiple TEs will be critical to elucidate synergistic or antagonistic interactions. Dose–response analyses and mechanistic studies are needed to identify thresholds at which TEs transition from protective to harmful. Ultimately, translational research integrating epidemiological, experimental, and clinical data will be essential to determine whether modulating TE status can contribute to the prevention or management of HT.

## 结论 / Conclusion

This meta-analysis indicates that elevated blood concentrations of Cd and Cu may play a role in HT, whereas evidence for Zn, Fe, Mg, and Se remains inconclusive. The findings support a plausible biological role of TEs in BP regulation, but are limited by very high heterogeneity, potential residual confounding (including socioeconomic and occupational factors), and the predominance of observational studies, which cannot exclude reverse causation. These results should therefore be considered hypothesis-generating. Future well-designed prospective studies, using standardized methods and multielement approaches, are needed to clarify causal pathways and assess the clinical and public health implications of TE status in HT.
