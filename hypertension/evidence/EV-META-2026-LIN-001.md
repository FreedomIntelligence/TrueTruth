## English Abstract

The systematic review and meta‐analysis aimed to examine the dose‐response relationship between isometric handgrip (IHG) training and blood pressure outcomes in normotensive and hypertensive populations. Three databases (EMBASE, MEDLINE/PubMed, and Cochrane CENTRAL) were searched from inception to June 2025. Thirty‐one studies with 905 participants (51% male) met the criteria. Primary outcomes were resting systolic (SBP) and diastolic blood pressure (DBP). Study quality was assessed using the PEDro scale and RoB‐2. Meta‐regression was used to evaluate dose‐response effects on SBP and DBP. The included studies demonstrated fair to good methodological quality (5.9 ± 1.0). Moderate evidence indicated that IHG reduced SBP (–5.38 mmHg, 95% CI –6.91 to –3.85; p < 0.001; I
2 = 86% and DBP (–2.71 mmHg, 95% CI –3.75 to –1.66; p < 0.001; I
2 = 90%), with greater effects observed in hypertensive compared to normotensive groups. For meta‐regression, lower training intensity protocols (<30% of maximal voluntary isometric contraction [MVIC]) yield smaller blood pressure‐lowering effects in both SBP (p = 0.043) and DBP (p = 0.032). Higher weekly frequency (e.g., >3 to 7 days/week) was associated with a diminished effect in SBP (p = 0.032). In conclusion, the IHG training significantly lowers SBP and DBP in both normotensive and hypertensive individuals, offering preventive benefits for normotensive individuals at risk for hypertension and therapeutic potential for hypertensive patients. The evidence‐informed protocol might appear to be four sets of 2‐min unilateral contractions at ≥30 MVIC, performed ≤3 times per week for a minimum of 8 weeks. These findings support IHG as a practical non‐pharmacological strategy for blood pressure management.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension is the most common cardiovascular risk factor and is strongly associated with adverse outcomes, such as stroke [1] and heart disease [2]. It is estimated that approximately 7.5 million deaths each year, accounting for about 12.8% of all global deaths [3], are attributable to elevated blood pressure (BP). Therefore, effective control of hypertension is essential for managing risk for cardiovascular diseases. Emerging evidence also supports the efficacy of isometric handgrip (IHG) in lowering BP [4, 5, 6, 7, 8], which has led to its inclusion in recent clinical guidelines [9, 10].

Despite a consensus on the benefits of IHG for BP management, variations in IHG protocols exist between clinical guidelines. European Society of Cardiology (ESC) [9] recommends low‐to‐moderate‐intensity with three sets of 1–2 min isometric contractions, whereas the American Heart Association (AHA) [10] specifies a protocol of four 2‐min isometric contractions at 30%–40% of maximal voluntary isometric contraction (MVIC) with a 1‐min rest interval between sets, performed three times per week for 8–10 weeks. Furthermore, although a recent systematic review [11] supports the efficacy of low‐intensity IHG training in reducing BP among adults with prehypertension and hypertension, the definition of “low intensity” remains largely unclear. Specifically, among the studies included in the review [11], 10 studies employed a training intensity of 30% MVIC and only one utilized 20% MVIC. In addition to differences in training intensity, there was considerable variability in other key parameters of isometric exercise protocols, including the number of weeks of intervention (ranging from 6 to 12 weeks), weekly training frequency (ranging from 3 to 7 days), and the duration of each contraction (ranging from 10 to 120 s). Such inconsistencies in existing IHG protocols present a challenge for establishing standardized exercise prescriptions and hinder the formulation of clear clinical guidelines [12].

Previous studies [13, 14, 15, 16, 17] have investigated the effects of varying IHG training intensities and durations on BP changes, but their findings remain inconclusive due to differences in training intensity and participant populations. A randomized controlled trial (RCT) [14] in hypertensive men found that both 30% MVIC (four 2‐min contractions) and high‐intensity training at 60% MVIC (eight 30‐s contractions), performed three times per week for 8 weeks, significantly improved resting BP, with statistically similar reduction of SBP but greater reductions observed in the high‐intensity group of DBP. Another RCT study [15] demonstrated that a 12‐week IHG training protocol at 15% of MVIC, performed three times per week, significantly reduced SBP by approximately 4.0 mmHg in hypertensive adults. However, another RCT [16] found that performing 2‐min IHG contractions at 5% of MVIC, three times per week for 8 weeks, did not significantly reduce SBP in hypertensive individuals, in contrast to a greater reduction observed with 30% MVIC. On the other hand, one study [13] involving normotensive adults investigated low‐intensity IHG training at 5% or 10% MVIC (four 2‐min contractions, three times per week for 6 weeks) and observed a modest reduction in SBP. Although these changes did not reach a statistical significance, they were considered clinically meaningful. However, variations in training dosage, including the intensity, intervention length, frequency, and contraction duration, limit the comparability and generalizability of the findings, given that these parameters differed between studies.

Most previous studies [15, 18] have employed IHG interventions lasting between 4 and 8 weeks, with a frequency of 2–4 times per week in hypertensive individuals. However, this wide range makes it difficult to establish clear guidelines for clinical application. A study [19] reported a significant decrease in SBP as early as the fourth week of IHG training (four 2‐min contractions at 30% MVIC, performed three times per week), with a reduction of 11.2 mmHg observed in hypertensive patients. Regarding the training frequency, no studies to date have directly compared different session frequencies of IHG training in hypertensive patients. However, one RCT study  [20] conducted in normotensive adults compared four 2‐min IHG contraction at 30% of MVIC, performed either two or four times per week over a 4‐week period. The results demonstrated significant reductions in SBP in both groups for two times per week and four times per week, with no significant difference between the two intervention frequencies. However, discrepancies in participant characteristics, namely normotensive versus hypertensive populations, limit the generalizability of the finding, as the underlying physiological mechanisms of BP regulation may differ. Therefore, a more systematic investigation is needed to clarify the dose‐responses relationship between IHG training intensity and BP outcomes across normotensive and hypertensive populations.

Currently, there is no standardized clinical guideline regarding the dosage of IHG exercise for lowering BP, including key parameters such as the percentage of MVIC, duration, frequency, and intervention length. Therefore, establishing a clear and evidence‐based guideline is essential for clinical practice. The purpose of current systematic review and meta‐analysis was to investigate the evidence‐informed dosage of IHG for BP reduction, including determining appropriate IHG exercise dosage for hypertensive and normotensive populations, respectively.

## 方法 / Methods

The systematic review was conducted in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta‐Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines [21] by two of the authors and was prospectively registered in the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews. A comprehensive search of EMBASE, MEDLINE/PubMed, and Cochrane CENTRAL was conducted from database inception through June 2025, using a combination of relevant Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) and free‐text keywords to identify studies addressing the participants, interventions, and outcomes described in Table S1.

Study selection followed the PICOS framework (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, and Study design. (1) Population: adults aged ≥18 years, including healthy control, prehypertension, and hypertension. Studies involving pregnant women or individuals with stroke, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease were excluded. (2) Intervention: exclusively used the IHG exercise, excluding those that combined it with any other type of exercise or any other therapy. The duration was restricted ≥2 weeks. (3) Comparison: baseline (pre‐intervention) values served as comparators for evaluating the effects of the intervention. (4) Outcomes: studies were required to report both baseline and post‐intervention BP measurements for SBP and DBP. (5) Study design: encompassed all human studies, including RCTs. Systematic reviews, meta‐analyses, conference abstracts, case reports, dissertations, observational studies, quasi‐experimental designs and studies not published in English were excluded.

Two reviewers (C.W.L. and L.M.) independently screened the titles and abstracts of all retrieved records to identify studies that met the inclusion criteria. Full‐text articles were retrieved for potentially eligible studies. All references were managed in EndNote 21 (Clarivate, Philadelphia, PA, USA), where duplicate entries were removed. Any discrepancies were resolved by consensus, with a third reviewer (Y.K.J.) consulted when necessary. Data extraction was performed by one reviewer (C.W.L.) using a standardized form, which included study identifiers (authors and year), allocation methods, inclusion criteria, participant characteristics, intervention characteristics, and training content.

Data were extracted using a standardized form to systematically collect relevant information from each study. Extracted variables included the first author, year of publication, sample size, and baseline and post‐intervention means and standard deviations (SDs) for each outcome. To determine the specific IHG dose per session, only the minutes of the training phase were considered, excluding warm‐up and cool‐down periods. When session duration was reported as a range (e.g., 1–3 min), the midpoint value (e.g., 2 min) was used as the estimated session length.

Risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool for Randomized Controlled Trials (ROB‐2) [22]. It evaluates five domains: the randomization process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, measurement of outcomes, and selection of the reported results. Each domain was rated as “low risk of bias,” “some concerns,” or “high risk of bias.” The overall risk of bias judgment for each study was determined by the highest risk rating across the five domains. Two researchers (A.B. and I.J.) independently reviewed the trials, and any discrepancies were resolved by a third researcher (C.W.L.).

The methodological quality of the included studies was evaluated using the Physiotherapy Evidence Database (PEDro) scale [23]. This 11‐item tool assesses aspects such as eligibility criteria, random allocation, concealed allocation, baseline comparability, blinding of participants, therapists, and assessors, adequacy of follow‐up, intention‐to‐treat analysis, between‐group comparisons, and reporting of point estimates with variability measures. The total PEDro score is derived from the final 10 items. Studies were categorized by quality as excellent (9–10), good (6–8), fair (4–5), or poor (≤3). When available, PEDro scores were extracted directly from the PEDro database, which contained committee‐assigned ratings for three of the included studies. For studies not listed in the database, two reviewers (I.B. and V.L.) independently conducted assessments according to PEDro guidelines [23]. Any disagreements were resolved through by third reviewer (C.W.L.).

Based on the Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) approach, two researchers (C.W.L. and A.B.) independently evaluated the certainty of the evidence and any discrepancies were resolved by a third researcher (Y.K.J.). The certainty of evidence was categorized into four levels: very low, low, moderate, and high. The criteria for downgrading the certainty of evidence were as follows. (1) Risk of bias: downgraded by one level if ≥25% of the included studies for an outcome were rated as high risk of bias according to ROB‐2. (2) Inconsistency: downgraded by one level if substantial heterogeneity was present (I2
 ≥ 75%). (3) Indirectness: downgraded by one level if important differences existed in participants, interventions, outcome measures, or indirect comparisons. (4) Imprecision: downgraded if the 95% confidence interval included the null effect and/or if the sample size was small (n < 300). (5) Publication bias: downgraded if evidence of asymmetry was detected through visual inspection of funnel plots.

Data synthesis and analysis were conducted using Review Manager (RevMan, version 5.4; Nordic Cochrane Centre, Cochrane Collaboration, Copenhagen, Denmark). All analyses were performed using a random‐effects model. Standardized mean differences (SMDs), mean difference (MDs) of SBP and DBP and their 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were obtained. The SMDs interpreted according to the following thresholds: trivial (<0.2), small (0.2–0.6), moderate (0.6–1.2), large (1.2–2.0), very large (2.0–4.0), and extremely large (>4.0). Statistical heterogeneity was assessed using the I2
 statistic, categorized as unimportant (0%–40%), moderate (30%–60%), substantial (50%–90%), or considerable (75%–100%). The calculation of the risk of publication bias across all the studies was indicated by using funnel plots/graphs. The sensitivity analyses were conducted by restricting the meta‐analysis to studies with low risk of bias.

Exercise intervention parameters were classified according to recent literature [24]. These classifications were based on their influence on the dosage design of IHG exercise and are commonly used to guide training protocols. The parameters included sets, minutes per contraction, laterality, %MVIC, rest interval, days per week, and total intervention weeks. The SMDs values with 95% CIs that did not cross zero were defined as significant, whereas those with 95% CIs crossing zero were defined as non‐significant, and the frequencies were tabulated accordingly.

To evaluate the relationship between exercise dose (set, minutes per contraction, laterality, %MVIC, rest interval, days per week, and total intervention weeks) and SMD of SBP and DBP, the meta‐regression was conducted. Scatterplot diagrams were used to plot data and visually determined the types of association between independent and dependent variables. For linear associations, data were analyzed using their raw values. In cases where curvilinear associations were identified, data were recalculated using logarithmic transformations (for log‐shaped associations) or Taylor series approximations (for U‐shaped associations) to achieve linearity for the regression analysis. Random‐effects meta‐regression models with random study intercepts and fixed moderator slopes, estimating the between‐study variance (τ2) via restricted maximum likelihood (REML). The dependent variables were SMD of SBP and DBP, respectively, while the independent variables were: set [minutes, log‐transformed], minutes/set [minutes], MVIC [%], rest interval [minutes, U‐shaped], laterality [factor], days/week [days, log‐transformed], and duration [weeks]. The partial meta‐regression coefficients (B) with 95% CIs and p values were estimated. The statistical analyses were performed in R software (version 4.2.1), with the level of significance set at p < 0.05.

## 结果 / Results

A total of 2766 were retrieved from the three databases. After removing 912 duplicates, 1854 articles remained for screening. Two reviewers (C.W.L. and L.M.) independently screened the titles and abstracts, of which 118 articles met the inclusion criteria for full‐text review. Two full texts could not be retrieved. Among the 116 full‐text articles assessed, 84 studies were excluded due to reasons such as the absence of post‐intervention BP measurement, lack of BP data, publication in a non‐English language, inappropriate study design, wrong population, or being only a study protocol. In addition, five records were identified from citation searching. Ultimately, 38 records and identified 34 studies were included in the final systematic review (Figure 1). Three studies did not present the values of the outcome measures and were therefore excluded from the following analysis, leaving 31 studies in the current study.

## 讨论 / Discussion

The systematic review included 31 studies, with study quality ranging from fair to good on the PEDro scale. A total of 905 IHG participants were analyzed comprising 51% males. Among the included groups, 13 consisted of normotensive participants, and 25 consisted of hypertensive participants. Moderate evidence indicated that IHG significantly reduced both SBP and DBP. In subgroup analyses, significant reductions in SBP were observed in the normotensive (MD: –4.81 mmHg) and hypertensive (MD: –5.73 mmHg) groups. For DBP, significant reductions were found in the normotensive (MD: –2.45 mmHg) and hypertensive (–2.95 mmHg) groups.

With respect to dose parameters for both SBP and DBP, lower training intensity (<30% MVIC) was associated with smaller BP‐lowering effects. Higher weekly frequency (e.g., >3 to 7 days/week) was associated with a diminished effect, suggesting that fewer training days per week may be more effective for reducing SBP. Although based on relatively few studies and therefore interpreted cautiously, a lower number of sets (e.g., <4 sets) showed a trend toward greater reductions in both SBP and DBP. For DBP in particular, greater reductions appeared to be associated with longer contraction duration (>2 to 3 min per set). However, given the relatively low explained variance (SBP R
2 = 17.65%; DBP R
2 = 39.91%), these dose‐response interpretations should be made cautiously.

The findings indicate that longer contraction durations (minutes per set) might be associated with greater reductions in DBP. Regarding the underlying mechanisms, although the typical IHG training protocol usually consists of four sets of 2‐min contractions [10], prolonged contraction time may enhance local vascular shear stress, thereby increasing the bioavailability of nitric oxide (NO) and improving endothelial function [51, 52]. Such vascular adaptations may represent one of the mechanisms underlying the BP‐lowering effects of IHG. However, no long‐term studies have directly examined the influence of contraction duration on BP outcomes; thus, these interpretations were based primarily on short‐term effects.

One study [53] reported that BP reactivity was dependent on handgrip contraction duration, with sustained contractions lasting four 2‐min contractions eliciting greater peak responses than those lasting only sixteen 30‐s contractions. Moreover, the four 2‐min protocol maintained elevated BP throughout the entire 30‐min recovery period, whereas the short‐duration protocol allowed BP to return to baseline within 15 min. These findings suggest that longer sustained contractions may prolong cardiovascular stress or delay recovery mechanisms. Taken together, these results suggest that prolonged contractions may elevate local vascular shear stress, thereby augmenting NO availability and ultimately contributing to BP reduction. Nevertheless, the contraction duration inference was based on a relatively small number of studies in the current regression analyses, and future well‐designed trials are needed to directly test the effect of contraction duration on long‐term BP outcomes.

Lower training intensity (<30% MVIC) may yield smaller BP‐lowering effects in both SBP and DBP. This finding is consistent with three previous studies. Two studies [16, 43] demonstrated that IHG training at 30% MVIC significantly reduced both SBP and DBP, whereas training at 5% MVIC showed no clear effect. Another study [13] comparing 5% and 10% MVIC found no reduction in DBP in either group. These results suggest that DBP may exhibit a dose‐response relationship to training intensity. Importantly, very low‐intensity MVIC protocols may function more like a sham condition and therefore may be insufficient to elicit meaningful BP reductions. However, no long‐term IHG interventions have investigated different intensities above the commonly studied 30% MVIC. Future RCTs are needed to further clarify the effects of different intensity levels on BP.

A finding is the inverse effect of training sets, whereby increasing the number of sets per session was associated with attenuated SBP and DBP reductions, suggesting that fewer sets may be more effective. This indicates that vascular pressure regulation progressively accumulates with additional sets. One study [54] indicated that the total power spectral density of heart rate variability (PSD‐RRI) showed a stepwise decline across sets, reflecting sustained withdrawal of vagal activity. It suggested that exceeding a certain threshold of training volume may lead to excessive sympathetic activation or muscle fatigue, thereby limiting long‐term reductions in TPR and hindering improvements in BP. Although the traditional IHG protocol typically employs four sets, the present findings suggest that alternative set prescriptions may warrant consideration. However, only five included studies used protocols other than four sets, and thus this inference should be interpreted cautiously and requires confirmation in future trials. Future research using direct measures of autonomic activity is warranted to validate this mechanism.

Although our analyses suggest that higher weekly frequency may be associated with smaller SBP reductions, direct evidence supporting a detrimental effect of higher‐frequency IHG training remains limited. Instead, the existing IHG literature more consistently indicates no additional benefit with increased frequency, consistent with a plateau pattern. For example, Chen et al. [20] compared two sessions/week versus four sessions/week over 4 weeks and observed SBP reductions in both groups with no between‐group difference. Similarly, Cohen et al. [19] reported that after achieving a significant SBP reduction with three sessions/week for 12 weeks, reducing the frequency to one session/week for an additional 12 weeks largely maintained the SBP improvement, suggesting that lower training frequency may be sufficient, at least for maintaining BP‐lowering effects. Future study should compare different frequencies in the population to fully understand the dosage effects on BP.

This study has several limitations. First, as a systematic review, it was not possible to obtain detailed information regarding patients’ compliance with the IHG protocol. Second, the included studies demonstrated relatively high heterogeneity, which reduced the overall level of evidence. Third, the low explanatory power of the SBP model suggests that factors other than dosage (e.g., genetics, diet, and adherence) play a major role. In addition, the high heterogeneity is a major caveat, indicating considerable between‐study variability. Factors beyond exercise dosage (e.g., medication use, adherence, baseline BP, and lifestyle factors) likely contribute substantially to between‐study differences. As a result, the SBP dose‐response estimates should be interpreted cautiously. ## 结论 / Conclusion

This systematic review and meta‐analysis, encompassing 31 studies with 905 participants, provides moderate evidence that IHG effectively reduces both SBP and DBP. Significant BP reductions were observed across normotensive and hypertensive groups. The DBP reductions were pronounced in normotensive and hypertensive participants. In addition, slight benefits were observed in hypertensive individuals compared with normotensive participants.

Traditionally, the most common IHG protocol has involved four 2‐min unilateral contractions at 30% MVIC with 1‐min rest interval, three times per week for at least 8 weeks. However, the present pooled meta‐analysis has identified specific intervention characteristics to explore whether specific intervention characteristics might further optimize outcomes. The finding suggests that within the dosage ranges represented in the included studies, variations in training prescription do not appear to meaningfully modify SBP reduction. In contrast, lower training intensity (<30% MVIC) may have less effects on both SBP and DBP reduction. Higher weekly frequency (e.g., >3 to 7 days/week) was associated with a diminished effect, suggesting that fewer training days per week may be more effective for reducing SBP. Overall, the evidence supports a pragmatic, literature‐consistent prescription for an individual with hypertension or normotension (at risk for hypertension) would be four sets of 2‐min unilateral contractions at ≥30% MVIC, performed ≤3 times per week for a minimum of 8 weeks. Note that these recommendations should not be interpreted as prescriptive clinical guidelines but rather as summary patterns observed across heterogeneous studies. Nevertheless, it should also be acknowledged that BP responses may be driven largely by factors beyond dosage (e.g., genetics, diet, medication use, and adherence), rather than by the relatively modest dosage differences evaluated in this meta‐analysis.
