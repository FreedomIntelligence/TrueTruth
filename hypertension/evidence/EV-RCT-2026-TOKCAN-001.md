## English Abstract

Sympathetic overactivity is associated with hepatic steatosis. Renal denervation (RDN) is an approved treatment for uncontrolled hypertension through sympathetic modulation; however, its hepatic effects are unknown. This study aimed to assess the effects of RDN on noninvasive tests for hepatic steatosis.

This single-center study included patients with uncontrolled hypertension and cardiometabolic comorbidities undergoing RDN (n=32) or a sham procedure (n=10). Noninvasive tests for hepatic steatosis, including the hepatic steatosis index (HSI) and fatty liver index (FLI), were calculated at baseline and follow-up visits. An external cohort from the UK Biobank was used to validate the correlation between proton density fat fraction magnetic resonance imaging scans of the liver and these surrogates.

Compared with the control group, RDN significantly reduced HSI at 3 months (0.4±0.5 versus −1.3±0.3; P=0.009), 6 months (0.0±0.9 versus −2.6±0.5; P=0.027), and 12 months (0.0±0.6 versus −2.1±0.5; P=0.013), as well as FLI at 3 months (2.6±2.2 versus −3.8±1.2; P=0.021), 6 months (4.1±2.4 versus −5.7±1.2; P=0.002), and 12 months (2.0±2.7 versus −6.2±1.3; P=0.018). No significant differences were found in HSI (−1.9±0.7 versus −2.2±0.7; P=0.77) and FLI (−7.1±1.8 versus −5.3±1.7; P=0.49) between subjects in the intervention group whose office blood pressure decreased above or below the median of 19.5 mm Hg after 12 months. In the UK Biobank, correlation analysis showed a significant relationship between proton density fat fraction magnetic resonance imaging and HSI (r=0.40; P<0.0001) and FLI (r=0.27; P<0.0001).

RDN significantly reduced HSI and FLI in patients with uncontrolled hypertension and cardiometabolic comorbidities, suggesting a potential role of sympathetic modulation in metabolic disorders.

## 方法 / Methods

The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.

Forty-two patients were retrospectively included in the analyses. All patients were >18 years old and had uncontrolled hypertension, defined as an office systolic blood pressure >140 mm Hg and diastolic blood pressure >90 mm Hg at the time of study entry. In addition, patients were included in the analysis if they had at least 2 of the following comorbidities in addition to uncontrolled hypertension: history of type 2 diabetes, obesity (body mass index [BMI] >30 kg/m2), and dyslipidemia. The presence of diabetes was confirmed by diagnosis in previous medical reports, and the presence of dyslipidemia was confirmed in patients receiving lipid-lowering therapy at baseline or a baseline total cholesterol >200 mg/dL. Patients with liver disease (cirrhosis, carcinoma), an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) <40/mL/min per 1.73 m2, albuminuria grade A3 (300 mg/g or 30 mg/mmol), or secondary causes of hypertension other than sleep apnea and CKD were excluded. Only patients with complete laboratory and clinical data for the calculation of the hepatic steatosis index (HSI) and the fatty liver index (FLI) at baseline and at all follow-up visits were included in the analyses. Of the 42 patients enrolled, 32 underwent RDN and 10 patients received sham treatment as part of randomized controlled trials and were assigned to continue medical therapy (Spyral HTN On and Off Med (REGISTRATION: URL: https://www.clinicaltrials.gov; Unique identifier: NCT02439775 and REGISTRATION: URL: https://www.clinicaltrials.gov; Unique identifier: NCT02439749), Radiance HTN Solo and Trio (REGISTRATION: URL: https://www.clinicaltrials.gov; Unique identifier: NCT02649426 and REGISTRATION: URL: https://www.clinicaltrials.gov; Unique identifier: NCT03614260). Changes in antihypertensive drug regimen were allowed at the investigators’ discretion after 3 months in the SPYRAL HTN-OFF MED trial and after 6 months in the ON MED trial and as an escape criterion. All patients were treated at Saarland University Hospital (Homburg, Germany) and provided written informed consent. The study complied with the Declaration of Helsinki, was approved by the local ethics committee, and was registered at Clinicaltrials.gov (REGISTRATION: URL: https://www.clinicaltrials.gov; Unique identifier: NCT01888315).

All patients underwent a complete medical history, physical examination, office BP measurements, and routine blood chemistry at baseline and at 3, 6, and 12 months. BP was measured using an automated oscillometric device (Omron HEM-705 monitor; Omron Healthcare, Vernon Hills, IL) with an appropriately sized cuff according to current guidelines.9,10 RDN was performed by experienced interventionalists using radiofrequency (Symplicity Flex and Symplicity Spyral catheter systems; Medtronic, Santa Rosa, CA) or ultrasound (Paradise catheter system; Recor Medical, Palo Alto, CA) catheter systems as previously described.11,12,20

NITs were calculated at baseline and each follow-up visit. The required laboratory and clinical parameters (triglycerides [mg/dL], gamma-glutamyl transferase [U/L], waist circumference [cm], aspartate aminotransferase [U/L], alanine aminotransferase [U/L], and BMI [kg/m2]) were collected at each time point. As our study did not collect morphological data on liver images, the correlation of the NITs included in this study with the well-established quantitative hepatic steatosis imaging marker, PDFF MRI, was validated in a large external cohort from the UK Biobank. The UK Biobank is a large-scale, detailed, prospective cohort study comprising over 500 000 participants aged 40 to 69 years, recruited from across the UK between 2006 and 2010.23 The research was conducted using data obtained via UK Biobank Access Application number 71300. The North West Multi-Centre Research Ethics Committee (11/NW/0382) approved the UK Biobank, and all participants gave written informed consent before the study. MRI-derived PDFF maps and raw liver images were described here,24 and PDFF was obtained from the UK Biobank imaging subcohort (field 40061). Representative cases with high (PDFF=0.36) and low hepatic fat content (PDFF=0.02) were selected for visualization (Figure 1A and 1C). To assess the correlation between PDFF and NITs for hepatic steatosis, we included participants with a diagnosis of arterial hypertension according to the International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision (ICD-10: I10, n=111 685) documented up to the date of MRI acquisition. The mean time from baseline to MRI was 8.5 years (range: 4–13 years). In this subset (n=4886), the HSI and FLI were calculated using standard formulas based on baseline laboratory and anthropometric parameters as described above. Pearson correlations between MRI-PDFF and each score were computed and visualized as scatterplots with linear regression (Figure 1B and 1D).

Shapiro-Wilk and Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests were used to assess normal distribution. Categorical variables are presented as frequencies (%) and continuous variables with normal distribution as mean±SEM. For skewed variables, median and interquartile range (IQR) were used. Mann-Whitney U test was used to compare baseline data between renal denervation and control groups. Fisher exact test was used to compare categorical variables. Paired t tests and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests were used for within-group baseline–12-month changes in blood pressure and antihypertensive medication use, respectively. Within-group changes in NITs for hepatic steatosis and laboratory parameters from baseline to 3, 6, and 12 months were analyzed using 1-way repeated-measures ANOVA for normally distributed variables and the Friedman test for nonnormally distributed variables. Between group differences at each follow-up time point were analyzed using 2-way repeated-measures ANOVA. Post hoc pairwise comparisons were performed using Bonferroni multiple comparisons test or Dunn multiple comparisons test as appropriate and multiplicity-adjusted P values are reported. Because the control group included no female participants, adjustment for sex was not statistically identifiable due to lack of overlap. We performed sensitivity analyses using multivariable linear regression models of changes in NITs for hepatic steatosis, adjusting for baseline imbalances between groups (age, eGFR, and antihypertensive medication burden). We additionally conducted a male-only sensitivity analysis using 2-way repeated-measures ANOVA to further address the sex imbalance. All tests were 2-sided, and P<0.05 was considered statistically significant. UK Biobank analyses were conducted in R (version 4.2.3). All other statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism (version 10.0.1; GraphPad Software, Boston, MA).

## 结果 / Results

The patient characteristics of the intervention and control groups at baseline are summarized in Table 1. The majority of patients were men, with 14 (44%) female patients in the intervention group and none in the control group. The median age was 62 years (IQR, 56–68) and the mean BMI was 32.4±0.6 kg/m2, with 76% of patients having a BMI >30 kg/m2. Thirteen (31%) patients had type 2 diabetes. There were no significant differences in most baseline clinical parameters, especially NITs for hepatic steatosis and the parameters required for their calculation. Compared with the RDN group, patients in the control group were younger (65 [IQR, 57–71] versus 56 years [IQR, 43–61]; P=0.004), had higher eGFR (69.9±2.6 versus 90.4±4.0 mL/min per 1.73 m2; P=0.0003), and took fewer antihypertensive drugs (5 [IQR 3–6] versus 0 [IQR 0–3]; P<0.0001).

Baseline Patient Characteristics

Among 4886 UK Biobank participants with hypertension and available liver PDFF MRI, we evaluated the association between PDFF (as an imaging-based reference standard for hepatic fat quantification) and established NITs for hepatic steatosis. Figure 1A illustrates representative examples of the corresponding MRI images and fat fraction maps in a participant with hepatic steatosis (top) and without hepatic steatosis (bottom). PDFF showed a modest positive correlation with HSI (r=0.40; P<0.0001) and a weaker correlation with FLI (r=0.27; P<0.0001; Figure 1B).

At baseline, there were no significant differences in NITs for hepatic steatosis between the groups. The HSI at baseline was 43.3±0.8 in the RDN group and 43.1±1.7 in the control group (P=0.89). FLI at baseline was 82.8±2.8 in the intervention group and 72.0±6.0 in the control group (P=0.076). In the RDN group, HSI decreased significantly from baseline by −1.3±0.3 at 3 months, −2.6±0.5 at 6 months, and −2.1±0.5 at 12 months (P=0.001 versus baseline at 3 months; P<0.001 versus baseline at 6 months; P<0.001 versus baseline at 12 months). No significant decrease from baseline was observed in the control group at 3 months (+0.4±0.5), 6 months (0.0±0.9), and 12 months (0.0±0.6; P versus baseline: 3 months: P>0.99; 6 months: P>0.99; 12 months: P>0.99). Comparing the HSI between the 2 groups at the respective follow-up times showed significant reductions in the RDN group at 3 months (P=0.009), 6 months (P=0.027), and 12 months (P=0.013; Figure 2A). FLI also decreased significantly from baseline by −3.8±1.2 at 3 months, −5.7±1.2 at 6 months, and −6.2±1.3 at 12 months (P versus baseline: 3 months: P=0.011; 6 months: P<0.001; 12 months: P<0.001). No significant decrease from baseline was observed in the control group at 3 months (+2.6±2.2), 6 months (+4.1±2.4), and 12 months (2.0±2.7; P versus baseline: 3 months: P=0.78; 6 months: P=0.34; 12 months: P>0.99). Comparing the FLI between the 2 groups at the respective follow-up times showed a significant reduction in the RDN group at 3 months (P=0.021), 6 months (P=0.002), and 12 months (P=0.018; Figure 2B). Sensitivity analyses using multivariable linear regression, adjusted for baseline imbalances between groups (age, eGFR, and antihypertensive medication burden), demonstrated robust treatment effects across follow-up with greater reductions in the RDN group (Table S1) and findings were consistent in the male-only sensitivity analysis (Table S2).

Looking at the parameters used to calculate the NITs after 12 months in the RDN group showed that, compared with baseline, a significant decrease in aspartate aminotransferase (−2.5 U/L [IQR, −5.8 to 1.0]; P=0.041), alanine aminotransferase (−6.5 U/L [IQR, −12.0 to −0.25]; P<0.001), BMI (−0.9±0.2 kg/m2; P<0.001), triglycerides (−25.6±9.4 mg/dL; P=0.031), and gamma-glutamyl transferase (−3.5 U/L [IQR, −14.8 to 0.8]; P=0.004) was observed, while waist circumference (−1.0 cm [IQR, −5.8 to 2.0]; P=0.57) decreased numerically (Table 2). Renal function, as assessed by eGFR, did not differ significantly from baseline after 12 months in the RDN group (−2.2±1.8 mL/min per 1.73 m2, P=0.52). In the control group, a numerically decreasing trend in eGFR over time was observed without reaching statistical significance (−6.0±3.0 mL/min per 1.73 m2, P=0.12). In the study cohort, eGFR was significantly inversely correlated with FLI at baseline (r=−0.32; P=0.040) and 12 months (r=−0.37; P=0.015), with a similar nonsignificant trend at 3 (r=−0.27; P=0.075) and 6 months (r=−0.20; P=0.20). No significant correlation was observed between eGFR and HSI (Figure S1A and S1B). For a proportion of patients in the RDN group with extended follow-up data, HSI and FLI remained numerically reduced by up to 36 months after the intervention, compared with the baseline values (Figures S2 and S3).

Changes in Parameters Associated With HSI or FLI Compared With Baseline

The RDN group was stratified into baseline HSI and FLI quartiles to assess whether the magnitude of change after RDN varied across different baseline levels of NITs of hepatic steatosis (Table S3). Compared with baseline HSI, no significant decrease was observed in the first quartile at any follow-up time point. The second quartile showed a significant decrease at 6 months (P=0.030). In the third quartile, HSI decreased significantly at 3 months (P=0.048) and 12 months (P=0.008), whereas in the fourth quartile significant decreases were observed at 6 months (P=0.022) and 12 months (P=0.026; Figure S4A). With respect to FLI, no significant decrease from baseline was observed in the first, third, or fourth quartile at any follow-up time point. A significant decrease was observed only in the second quartile at 3 months (P=0.037; Figure S4B).

At 12 months, office systolic blood pressure decreased by −19.3±3.7 mm Hg in the RDN group (P versus baseline <0.001) and by −10.8±5.3 mm Hg in the control group (P versus baseline =0.071). The median number of antihypertensive medications was significantly reduced in the RDN group from 5 (IQR, 3–6) at baseline to 3 (IQR, 0–5) at 12 months (P=0.006). In the control group, the median number of antihypertensive medications increased numerically, from 0 (IQR, 0–3) at baseline to 3 (IQR, 1.5–3.3) at 12 months, without reaching statistical significance (P=0.063). Notably, randomized comparisons in SPYRAL OFF and SPYRAL ON ended at 3 and 6 months, respectively. After completion of follow-up as planned, antihypertensive medication was left at the discretion of the investigators.

To assess whether changes in NITs of hepatic steatosis varied with the magnitude of blood pressure reduction after RDN, we stratified the RDN group by the median 12-month reduction in office systolic blood pressure (19.5 mm Hg) and examined HSI and FLI trajectories in the 2 subgroups. Regarding the HSI, no significant differences were observed between the group with a decrease >19.5 mm Hg and the group with a decrease <19.5 mm Hg after 3 months (−1.8±0.5 versus −0.7±0.3; P=0.10), 6 months (−2.9±0.8 versus −2.2±0.8; P=0.52) and 12 months (−1.9±0.7 versus −2.2±0.7; P=0.77; Figure 3A). Similarly, there were no significant differences in the FLI between the group with a decrease >19.5 mm Hg and the group with a decrease <19.5 mm Hg after 3 months (−4.2±2.1 versus −3.5±1.4; P=0.80), 6 months (−5.8±1.5 versus −5.5±1.9; P=0.92) and 12 months (−7.1±1.8 versus −5.3±1.7; P=0.49; Figure 3B).

## 讨论 / Discussion

In our study, patients with uncontrolled arterial hypertension and cardiometabolic risk factors clearly exceeded the cutoff values of 36 for the HSI and 60 for the FLI, respectively, suggesting the presence of hepatic steatosis. Compared with the control group, RDN significantly reduced HSI and FLI at 3, 6, and 12 months. The greatest numerical decrease in HSI was observed after 12 months in the fourth quartile (ie, subjects with the highest baseline value). The reduction in HSI and FLI was achieved independently of blood pressure reduction and was greater in patients with type 2 diabetes than in nondiabetics.

The link between metabolic syndrome and hepatic steatosis has been proven, suggesting that hepatic steatosis can be considered a liver manifestation of metabolic syndrome.25,26 This is also reflected in the new definition of metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease, which states that in addition to hepatic steatosis, patients must have at least one of the following cardiometabolic risk factors: obesity, diabetes/prediabetes, hypertension, or dyslipidemia.25,27

The most commonly used noninvasive clinical method for identifying hepatic steatosis is ultrasound imaging.28 As our study did not include imaging data, we used established serum biomarkers that can reliably predict the presence of fatty liver disease, particularly in individuals with a high pretest probability.21,22,29,30 Liver fat content can be objectively and precisely measured using PDFF MRI, a quantitative imaging biomarker.31 PDFF is defined as the ratio of the density of mobile protons from triglycerides to the total density of protons from combined triglycerides and mobile water.31 Previous studies have shown that PDFF-MRI is a reliable noninvasive marker for determining the severity of hepatic steatosis when compared with liver biopsy results.31 In our analysis, we observed weak-to-moderate correlations between the NITs and PDFF. The stronger correlation between HSI and PDFF-MRI suggests that HSI may better discriminate across different grades of hepatic steatosis than FLI. The modest correlations may, in part, reflect the substantial time lag between blood sampling (used to calculate the NITs) and PDFF-MRI acquisition in the UK Biobank (mean 8.5 years; range 4–13 years). The inverse correlation between FLI and eGFR observed in our cohort is in line with previous population-based studies demonstrating that FLI, as a surrogate marker of hepatic steatosis, is associated with prevalent and incident CKD. This relationship is likely mediated by shared metabolic pathways.32,33

As the kidney communicates with the central sympathetic nervous system via renal sympathetic afferent nerve fibers,34 disrupting this connection decreases central sympathetic activity6,35 and whole-body norepinephrine spillover by 42%.14 Extensive research has confirmed that RDN effectively lowers blood pressure in patients with hypertension,11,12 including those with various comorbidities.36 Although prolonged time within the therapeutic systolic blood pressure range following RDN has been associated with reduced major adverse cardiovascular events,37 the potentially beneficial pleiotropic effects of RDN beyond blood pressure control remain inadequately explored.

This study demonstrates that modulating sympathetic activity by RDN significantly reduces HSI by 5% and FLI by 7.5% after 12 months, compared with controls undergoing a sham procedure. These effects appeared to be independent of blood pressure reduction, as there were no significant differences in the decreases in HSI and FLI between subjects in the intervention group whose blood pressure decreased above or below the median. The largest decrease in HSI was evident in the quartile with the highest baseline values. The absence of this effect for FLI could potentially be explained by the higher correlation of HSI with PDFF-MRI. The decrease in HSI and FLI was mainly achieved by reducing BMI, transaminase levels, and triglyceride levels in the intervention group. Recent evidence indicates that sympathetic nervous system activity contributes to metabolic dysregulation by inhibiting GLP-1 secretion from enteroendocrine L cells and lowering circulating adiponectin concentrations.38 Higher circulating adiponectin concentrations were observed in patients following RDN, suggesting that RDN may alleviate this inhibitory effect and thereby potentially enhance metabolic control.39–41 Further consideration of this hypothesis in prospective trials is necessary.

An impaired autonomic balance characterized by increased sympathetic activity is recognized as a significant contributor to the development of metabolic syndrome and hepatic steatosis.7 Preclinical studies have shown that mice with central sympathetic hyperactivity develop metabolic syndrome and hepatic steatosis by 4 months of age, even without a high-fat diet.42 Another preclinical study16 recorded hepatic sympathetic nerve activity in mice fed a high-fat diet or normal chow, showing that diet-induced hepatic steatosis was associated with a firing rate of efferent hepatic sympathetic nerves that was almost double that observed in control mice. Two retrospective studies in humans43,44 showed that increased sympathetic activity is associated with an increased risk of hepatic steatosis and that metabolic syndrome is significantly associated with increased sympathetic activity.43,44 Therapeutic interventions by hepatic sympathetic nerve denervation in a mouse model of diet-induced fatty liver disease resulted in reduced fatty liver phenotype19 and improved lipid profiles in obese Zucker rats.15

Our findings further suggest that particularly patients with type 2 diabetes may derive greater metabolic benefit from RDN. The reduction in HSI was more pronounced in type 2 diabetics compared with patients without diabetes; a similar trend was also observed for the FLI. Given the bidirectional relationship between hyperinsulinemia and sympathetic activation,45,46 as well as prior data showing improvements in glucose metabolism following RDN,17 our results support the hypothesis that targeting sympathetic outflow may be particularly beneficial in metabolically vulnerable populations with a high-risk profile. A first-in-human study investigating combined hepatic and renal denervation is currently ongoing (NCT06907147) and will provide further insights.

This study is hypothesis-generating as it has limitations. The sample size was not based on a priori power calculations, and data need confirmation by larger prospective trials, such as the currently ongoing (NCT06907147). Medication changes occurred during the 12-month follow-up period to achieve blood pressure control. Although unlikely, we cannot entirely exclude the possibility that these medication changes may have influenced the observed metabolic parameters. The used NITs are surrogate scores of hepatic steatosis, and misclassification of patients is possible. The mean time lag of 8.5 years between blood sampling and PDFF-MRI acquisition in the UK Biobank may have attenuated the observed correlations. Lastly, because randomization between the RDN and sham groups was not preserved in this post hoc analysis, baseline imbalances occurred, including the absence of female participants in the control group. Consequently, sex adjustment was not feasible and the generalizability of our findings to women is limited. Nevertheless, covariate-adjusted and male-only sensitivity analyses produced concordant findings, supporting the robustness of the observed associations within the available data set.

Autonomic modulation by RDN significantly reduced NITs associated with hepatic steatosis in patients with uncontrolled hypertension and cardiometabolic comorbidities compared with a control group undergoing sham procedures. These findings emphasize the potential metabolic effects of RDN and its potential for the treatment of metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease. Further prospective studies investigating combined renal and hepatic denervation are ongoing to scrutinize the effect of sympathetic denervation on blood pressure, metabolic, and hepatic parameters.
