## English Abstract

This study aimed to assess the efficacy and safety of a combination therapy of Allisartan Isoproxil 240 mg and Amlodipine 5 mg (ALI/AML) compared to AML 5 mg monotherapy in patients with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension. In this phase III, multicenter, double‐blind, parallel‐group, randomized controlled trial, patients aged 18–70 years with mean sitting systolic blood pressure (msSBP) between 140 and <180 mmHg and mean sitting diastolic blood pressure (msDBP) between 90 and <110 mmHg, following a 4‐week treatment with AML 5 mg, were randomized 1:1 to receive either ALI/AML or AML once daily for 12 weeks. This 12‐week double‐blind period was followed by an open‐label extension of ALI/AML treatment through week 52. A total of 300 patients were enrolled, with 149 and 151 patients randomly assigned to ALI/AML and AML groups, respectively. Of these, 257 patients completed the study. Baseline demographics and characteristics were comparable between groups. After 12 weeks, the reduction in msSBP (the primary endpoint) was significantly greater in the ALI/AML group compared to the AML group (–15.7 vs. –10.2 mmHg, p = 0.0019). Similarly, reductions in msDBP (–5.7 vs. –2.4 mmHg, p < 0.001) and 24‐h mean ambulatory SBP and DBP (–10.4 and –7.7 mmHg vs. –5.6 and –3.8 mmHg) were more pronounced in the ALI/AML group. Additionally, a higher proportion of patients achieved both a BP response and target office BP in the ALI/AML group compared to the AML group (51.4% vs. 37.4%, 42.5% vs. 30.6%, both p < 0.05). The ALI/AML combination was generally well tolerated, and the antihypertensive effect was maintained for up to 52 weeks. In patients with essential hypertension inadequately controlled by AML, the ALI/AML combination provided superior reductions in msSBP and was significantly more effective than AML monotherapy. This once‐daily single‐pill combination demonstrated promising efficacy and tolerability.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension affects over 1 billion people globally, and this number is projected to increase to 1.5 billion by 2025 [1, 2]. In China, data from the Chinese Hypertension Survey (CHS) conducted between 2012 and 2015 indicated that 27.9% of adults aged 18 years and older suffer from hypertension [3]. Rates of awareness, treatment, and control among hypertensive patients were reported as 51.6%, 45.8%, and 16.8%, respectively [4]. Based on estimates from the 2010 National Population Census, approximately 270 million individuals in China were living with hypertension [5]. Hypertension remains the most significant risk factor for cardiovascular disease in China, contributing extensively to both morbidity and mortality [6]. Hypertension is strongly associated with major complications such as stroke, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease, leading to numerous cases of cardiovascular disability and death [7, 8, 9].

Hypertension management strategies include both non‐pharmacological and pharmacological interventions. Non‐pharmacological approaches emphasize lifestyle modifications, particularly in diet [10], while pharmacological therapy remains central to achieving effective blood pressure (BP) control [11]. Commonly used antihypertensive medications include calcium channel blockers (CCBs), angiotensin‐converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs), angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs), diuretics, and β‐blockers, as well as fixed‐dose combinations of these agents [12]. Combining dihydropyridine CCBs with ARBs is known to exert a synergistic antihypertensive effect. Importantly, ARBs can reduce the incidence of common side effects associated with CCBs, such as ankle edema [13]. Evidence suggests that this combination therapy enhances BP control rates [14]. As a result, the dihydropyridine CCB and ARB combination is recommended as one of the optimized treatment strategies for hypertension management, as outlined in the European Society of Hypertension (ESH) [15] and Chinese guidelines [16].

Amlodipine (AML), a CCB, exerts its antihypertensive effect by inhibiting calcium influx in vascular smooth muscle cells, resulting in vasodilation [17]. Extensive clinical studies have demonstrated that AML not only effectively reduces BP but also significantly lowers the risk of cardiovascular events such as myocardial infarction and stroke [18]. Its efficacy in BP management is well‐established in clinical practice, and it is commonly used in combination with other antihypertensive agents, including ACEIs, ARBs, and diuretics.

Allisartan Isoproxil (ALI), a member of the ARB class, shares the active metabolite EXP3174 with losartan and has been shown to possess substantial antihypertensive properties. Clinical evidence indicates that a once‐daily oral dose of 240 mg of ALI leads to sustained BP control, as confirmed by ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM), which shows stable reductions in both 24‐h and nocturnal BP [19, 20, 21]. Additionally, ALI has been found to provide cardiovascular protection, with studies demonstrating its beneficial effects on the heart, kidneys, and endothelial function in patients with mild to moderate essential hypertension [22].

This study aimed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of a single‐pill combination (SPC) of ALI 240 mg and AML 5 mg in patients with essential hypertension that remained inadequately controlled following 4 weeks of AML monotherapy.

## 方法 / Methods

This was a phase III, 52‐week, multicenter, double‐blind, parallel‐group, randomized controlled clinical trial conducted across 42 clinical sites (Table S1). Patients with essential hypertension who were either treatment‐naïve or had previously received irregular antihypertensive treatment with AML were initially treated with AML monotherapy (5 mg/day) for 4 weeks as the run‐in period. Those with uncontrolled BP after 4 weeks were subsequently enrolled in a 12‐week double‐blind treatment period. Other participants already on a stable regimen of AML (5 mg/day) for at least 4 weeks prior to screening who exhibited uncontrolled BP were directly randomized into the 12‐week double‐blind period.

Randomization was conducted using a stratified block randomization method (block size = 4), with the randomization sequence generated by SAS (version 9.4) software. Patients were stratified based on baseline systolic blood pressure (SBP) (<160 or ≥160 mmHg) and randomized in a 1:1 ratio to either receive AML 5 mg (plus placebo matching the ALI/AML combination) or the SPC of ALI 240 mg and AML 5 mg, along with a placebo matching AML.

Blinding was maintained through standardized packaging. Each participant was provided with a large box containing a 5‐week supply of medication, subdivided into five smaller boxes, each holding a 1‐week supply. Each smaller box contained seven daily dose sachets. For participants in the ALI/AML group, each sachet contained one 240 mg ALI/AML tablet and one placebo tablet resembling AML. In the AML monotherapy group, each sachet contained one 5 mg AML tablet and one placebo tablet resembling the ALI/AML combination. Identical packaging, color, appearance, smell, and labeling were employed across groups to ensure proper blinding.

A cohort of 60 participants was scheduled for the ABPM sub‐study. Eligibility for this sub‐study required participants to have uncontrolled BP following 4 weeks of AML monotherapy and a 24‐h mean ambulatory BP of ≥130/80 mmHg.

After the completion of the double‐blind treatment period, all participants entered an open‐label extension period where they received the ALI/AML combination therapy until Week 52 to assess long‐term safety and tolerability.

The study protocol received approval from the Ethics Committees of all participating centers and was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and Good Clinical Practice (GCP) guidelines.

The primary inclusion criteria were: (1) adult patients aged 18–70 years with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension; (2) untreated patients, either newly diagnosed or with a history of hypertension, who had not taken any antihypertensive medications for at least 2 weeks prior to screening, with mean sitting systolic blood pressure (msSBP) between 150 and <180 mmHg and mean sitting diastolic blood pressure (msDBP) <110 mmHg; patients with a history of irregular AML treatment (5 mg once daily for <4 weeks) or those who had missed doses for ≥5 days within the previous 4 weeks, with msSBP between 140 and <180 mmHg and msDBP < 110 mmHg; patients who had been receiving other antihypertensive agents for at least 2 weeks with msSBP between 140 and <180 mmHg and msDBP < 110 mmHg, deemed suitable for a switch to AML 5 mg/day by the treating clinician; and patients who had been treated with AML at a dose of 5 mg/day for ≥4 weeks with msSBP between 140 and <180 mmHg and msDBP < 110 mmHg; (3) patients with msSBP between 140 and <180 mmHg and msDBP < 110 mmHg before randomization; and (4) participants enrolled in the ABPM sub‐study with 24‐h mean ambulatory BP of ≥130/80 mmHg following 4 weeks of monotherapy.

Key exclusion criteria included: (1) secondary hypertension; (2) msSBP ≥ 180 mmHg and/or msDBP ≥ 110 mmHg, or a history of hypertensive emergencies or urgencies; (3) history of New York Heart Association (NYHA) Class III or IV heart failure, acute coronary syndrome, percutaneous coronary intervention, or other severe cardiac conditions such as cardiogenic shock, moderate‐to‐severe valvular heart disease, II‐III degree atrioventricular block, bradycardia with a heart rate below 50 beats per minute, or severe arrhythmias within the past 6 months; (4) known allergies or intolerance to study medications (e.g., angioedema); (5) history of severe cerebrovascular events, including hypertensive encephalopathy, cerebrovascular injury, stroke, or transient ischemic attack, within the last 6 months; (6) large aneurysm, aortic dissection, or dissecting aneurysm; (7) renal artery stenosis or severe renal insufficiency; (8) active viral hepatitis (including hepatitis B and C), severe liver disease, or hepatic dysfunction; and (9) abnormal laboratory findings, including creatinine >1.5 times the upper limit of normal (ULN), potassium >5.5 mmol/L, alanine aminotransferase (ALT) or aspartate aminotransferase (AST) >2.5 times ULN, or total bilirubin (TBIL) >2 times ULN. Detailed eligibility criteria can be found in the Supporting Information. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants before their inclusion in the study.

The primary endpoint of the study was the change in msSBP from baseline to Week 12. Secondary endpoints included changes in msDBP from baseline after 12 weeks of double‐blind treatment; changes in msSBP and msDBP from baseline after 4 and 8 weeks of double‐blind treatment; the proportion of responders (defined as achieving msSBP/msDBP <140 mmHg/90 mmHg, or a reduction in msSBP of >20 mmHg and/or msDBP reduction of >10 mmHg) after 12 weeks; and the proportion of patients attaining target office BP (defined as msSBP/msDBP <140/90 mmHg) at 4, 8, and 12 weeks. Furthermore, the proportion of patients reaching the target office BP at Week 32 and at the end of treatment was also evaluated. Exploratory endpoints encompassed changes in 24‐h, morning, daytime, and nighttime ambulatory SBP/DBP after 12 weeks of treatment.

Office BP measurements were performed following standard hypertension management guidelines (Verification Regulation of Non‐invasive Automated Sphygmomanometers JJG 692–2010) using a validated, calibrated upper‐arm electronic BP monitor (model: AND UA‐651BLE‐W). Morning BP was measured twice at 1–2 min intervals, and the mean value was recorded. If the difference between the SBP and/or DBP readings was ≥5 mmHg, an additional measurement was conducted. Orthostatic BP was measured after the final SBP reading, with a 1‐min delay after transitioning to a standing position. Orthostatic hypotension was defined as a decrease in SBP of ≥20 mmHg and/or a decrease in DBP of ≥10 mmHg.

ABPM was performed using the AND TM‐2430 device in accordance with hypertension management protocols (Verification Regulation of Non‐invasive Automated Sphygmomanometers JJG 692–2010). Prior to monitoring, clinic BP was assessed in both arms. If the difference between arms was ≥10 mmHg, the higher arm was selected; otherwise, the non‐dominant arm was used. ABPM readings were captured every 20 min during daytime (awake hours) and every 30 min during nighttime (sleep hours). The early morning period was defined as the first 2 h after awakening, based on each participant's individual schedule. Awake hours and sleep hours were determined through participant self‐reports. For valid ABPM results, a minimum of 20 daytime readings, seven nighttime readings, and 70% of the expected 24‐h readings (with at least one reading per hour) were required. Changes in SBP and DBP from baseline during the morning, daytime, and nighttime were analyzed. A total of 60 participants were planned for inclusion in the ABPM analysis, all of whom were required to have uncontrolled BP after 4 weeks of monotherapy and a 24‐h mean ABPM ≥130/80 mmHg.

Adverse events (AEs) and serious adverse events (SAEs) were recorded from the time of informed consent. Regular assessments included hematological, biochemical, and urinalysis parameters, as well as body weight, vital signs, physical examination results, electrocardiograms, and other relevant clinical assessments. Laboratory evaluations were conducted at screening and subsequently at Weeks 0, 12, 32, and 52 after randomization.

The sample size calculation was conducted to achieve 80% power to detect a between‐treatment difference of 4 mmHg, assuming a standard deviation (SD) of 11 mmHg [23], at a one‐sided significance level of 0.025. Accounting for an anticipated dropout rate of 20%, the final required sample size was estimated to be 150 completed patients per treatment group. This calculation was performed using PASS 16 software.

The primary efficacy endpoint was analyzed using the full analysis set (FAS), with supplementary analyses conducted on the per‐protocol set (PPS). To evaluate the primary efficacy endpoint, a mixed model repeated measures (MMRM) approach was employed. This model incorporated changes from baseline in msSBP at each study visit during the double‐blind treatment period as the dependent variable, with treatment group, visit, and the interaction between treatment group and visit as fixed effects. Baseline msSBP was included as a covariate. The difference between the two treatment groups for the primary efficacy endpoint, along with the corresponding 95% confidence interval (CI), was computed. Additionally, a subgroup analysis of the primary efficacy endpoint was carried out based on the randomization stratification factors and other relevant baseline characteristics that could potentially influence the results.

Secondary efficacy endpoints were analyzed using the FAS and PPS. Least square mean (LSM) changes from baseline and between‐group differences, along with their 95% CIs, were calculated using the MMRM, with baseline values included as covariates. Logistic regression was applied to assess the proportions. Safety assessments were conducted using the safety set (SS), with particular attention to the frequency and incidence of AEs. All statistical analyses were performed using SAS software, version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc.).

## 结果 / Results

A total of 490 individuals were screened for eligibility, of which 300 were randomized into the study. Of the remaining 190, 176 participants did not meet the inclusion criteria, and 14 declined to participate further. A total of 277 participants successfully completed the double‐blind treatment period, as depicted in Figure 1. The FAS comprised 293 patients, with 146 assigned to the ALI/AML group and 147 to the AML group. The SS included 296 patients, with 147 in the ALI/AML group and 149 in the AML group. The majority of participants were male (58.4%) and under the age of 65 (77.1%). Of the total population, 18.8% had a history of diabetes mellitus. During the double‐blind period, the incidence of AEs was 39 (26.5%) in the ALI/AML group and 44 (29.5%) in the AML group, with the majority of AEs being mild in both treatment arms. In the open‐label period, when all participants received ALI/AML, the overall incidence of AEs increased to 188 (67.9%), with most events classified as mild.

In the double‐blind period, the most commonly reported AEs in the ALI/AML group were upper respiratory tract infections and nasopharyngitis, each affecting 4 (2.7%) patients. In the AML group, sinus bradycardia was the most frequent AE, occurring in 4 (2.7%) patients (Table 2). During the open‐label period, hyperuricemia in 35 (12.6%) patients and hyperlipidemia in 31 (11.2%) patients were the most commonly reported AEs.

The incidence of drug‐related AEs during the double‐blind period was 8 (5.4%) in the ALI/AML group and 12 (8.1%) in the AML group. In the ALI/AML group, the most frequent drug‐related AEs were headache 2 (1.4%) and hypotension 2 (1.4%), whereas in the AML group, dizziness 2 (1.3%) and urinary protein detection 2 (1.3%) were the most common. During the open‐label period, the incidence of drug‐related AEs increased to 41 (14.8%), with hyperuricemia being the most frequent 7 (2.5%).

The incidence of AEs leading to withdrawal from the study during the double‐blind period was 1 (0.7%) in the ALI/AML group and 3 (2.0%) in the AML group. In the ALI/AML group, the event leading to withdrawal was ventricular extrasystole 1 (0.7%), whereas in the AML group, withdrawals were due to sinus bradycardia 2 (1.3%), second‐degree atrioventricular block 1 (0.7%), dizziness 1 (0.7%), brainstem infarction 1 (0.7%), and a prolonged RR interval on electrocardiogram 1 (0.7%). During the open‐label period, AEs leading to withdrawal occurred in 2 (0.7%), including carcinoma of the rectum 1 (0.4%), which was deemed unrelated to the study drugs, and chest discomfort 1 (0.4%).

SAEs were observed in 2 (1.4%) in the ALI/AML group during the double‐blind period, including a transient ischemic attack and acute coronary syndrome. In the AML group, SAEs occurred in 3 (2.0%), consisting of brainstem infarction, drug‐induced liver injury, and a benign tongue tumor. None of these SAEs were considered drug‐related. Importantly, no patients in the ALI/AML group discontinued treatment due to SAEs. During the open‐label period, 18 (6.5%) experienced SAEs, all of which were unrelated to the study medications. No deaths occurred throughout the study.

Hypotension was reported in 2 (1.4%) in the ALI/AML group during the double‐blind period, with no cases observed in the AML group. No instances of orthostatic hypotension were noted during this period. Peripheral edema related to AEs was reported in 1 (0.7%) in both groups, and no cases of hyperkalemia were reported. In the open‐label period, hypotension occurred in 1 (0.4%), and orthostatic hypotension was reported in 2 (0.7%). No peripheral edema‐related AEs were reported during the open‐label period, and the incidence of hyperkalemia was 2 (0.7%).

No major differences were observed between the treatment groups in terms of hematological and clinical chemistry parameters. Abnormalities in laboratory values were infrequent and comparable across both groups (Table 3). The majority of participants maintained blood potassium levels within the normal range of 3.5‐5.5 mmol/L during the double‐blind period. Baseline uric acid levels were 342.9 µmol/L in the ALI/AML group and 329.9 µmol/L in the AML group. At Week 12, the reductions in uric acid were 11.5 and 11.6 µmol/L, respectively. During the open‐label period, most participants in the ALI/AML group had potassium levels within the normal range, with no significant changes observed in potassium levels between the double‐blind and open‐label periods (Table 3).

## 讨论 / Discussion

This phase III, multicenter, double‐blind, parallel‐group, randomized controlled clinical trial was conducted to assess the efficacy and safety of a SPC of ALI/AML in patients with mild to moderate essential hypertension that remained uncontrolled after 4 weeks of AML monotherapy. The results indicated that the ALI/AML combination therapy was superior to AML monotherapy in reducing msSBP from baseline to Week 12. Subgroup analyses, stratified by age, gender, duration of hypertension, and baseline BP levels, consistently demonstrated greater reductions in msSBP in the ALI/AML group across all subgroups. These findings were consistent with the overall trends observed for the primary efficacy endpoint. Treatment adherence was high and comparable between both groups (98.0% vs. 97.2%). The antihypertensive effect of ALI/AML was sustained for up to 52 weeks, with both groups achieving target office BP in over 45% of patients by the end of the study. This represents an improvement over the 42.5% target achievement rate in the ALI/AML group at Week 12, indicating durable efficacy with continued treatment.

Hypertension is recognized as one of the most significant modifiable risk factors for cardiovascular disease. The findings from the Blood Pressure Lowering Treatment Trialists’ Collaboration (BPLTTC) study demonstrated that a reduction of 5 mmHg in SBP can reduce the risk of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) by approximately 10% [24]. Likewise, a reduction of 3 mmHg in DBP has been associated with a decreased risk of MACE across hypertensive patients of all age groups [25]. In the current study, following 4 weeks of treatment in the double‐blind period, the LSM changes in msSBP and msDBP in the ALI/AML group were −12.1 and −4.5 mmHg, respectively, compared to −10.1 and −2.9 mmHg in the AML group, resulting in a between‐group difference of −2.0 and −1.6 mmHg. At 12 weeks, the LSM changes in msSBP and msDBP from baseline in the ALI/AML group were −15.7 and −5.7 mmHg, respectively, while the AML group demonstrated reductions of −10.2 and −2.4 mmHg, yielding a between‐group difference of −5.4 and −3.3 mmHg.

The 24‐h BP profile is a well‐established predictor of cardiovascular and cerebrovascular risk [26]. In this study, by Week 12, the ALI/AML group exhibited obvious and sustained antihypertensive effects over 24 h, with greater reductions in ambulatory SBP/DBP compared to the AML monotherapy group, highlighting the clinical efficacy of ALI/AML. Reductions in daytime and morning ambulatory SBP/DBP were also more pronounced in the ALI/AML group than in the AML group. Morning hours represent a high‐risk period for cardiovascular and cerebrovascular events, with excessive elevations in morning BP identified as a critical factor contributing to this risk. An increase of 10 mmHg in morning BP has been associated with a 44% higher risk of stroke [27]. In this trial, ABPM within the first 2 h after awakening revealed that, at Week 12 of the double‐blind period, the ALI/AML group demonstrated greater reductions in morning SBP and DBP compared to the AML group (−7.2 and −4.1 mmHg vs. 0.6 and −1.3 mmHg). It was also noted that the changes in several ABPM indicators of the ALI/AML group did not reach statistical significance compared to the AML group (except for changes in the daytime DBP between the two treatment groups, p = 0.0309). As ABPM was an exploratory endpoint and not the primary outcome, the limited sample size in this study may have reduced the power to detect statistically significant differences in these measures.

The ALI/AML combination was found to be both safe and well‐tolerated. The incidence of AEs during the double‐blind period was comparable between the ALI/AML group and the AML group (26.5% vs. 29.5%). The frequencies of headache (1.4% vs. 1.3%) and dizziness (1.4% vs. 1.3%) in the double‐blind period remained stable and did not increase significantly during the open‐label period (1.8% vs. 2.5%). Hypotension occurred in fewer than 2% of patients in both groups (1.4% vs. 0%), and no cases of orthostatic hypotension were observed during the double‐blind period. Similarly, hypotension and orthostatic hypotension were rare during the open‐label period. Peripheral edema, a dose‐dependent side effect of AML, was reported in fewer than 1% of patients in both groups during the double‐blind period, with no peripheral edema‐related AEs reported during the open‐label period. These results align with findings from international studies, such as those conducted in the United States, where the incidence of edema in patients treated with AML was approximately 3% [28]. This may be attributable to the complementary effects of ARBs like ALI, which mitigate peripheral edema by promoting venous dilation [13]. ALI, as a renin‐angiotensin‐aldosterone system (RAAS) inhibitor, carries a potential risk of hyperkalemia [29]. However, in this study, no cases of hyperkalemia were observed during the double‐blind period, and the incidence of hyperkalemia remained below 1% during the extended treatment period. Most participants in the ALI/AML group maintained normal potassium levels (3.5‐5.5 mmol/L) throughout the trial. After 52 weeks of treatment, the ALI/AML combination was confirmed to be both effective and safe in patients with hypertension. Although the overall incidence of AEs increased slightly with the duration of treatment in the open‐label period, most events were mild in nature. The incidence of AEs at Week 52 was comparable to those observed in other combination regimens with similar components, such as AML [30, 31]. In conclusion, these findings demonstrate that ALI/AML is a safe, well‐tolerated, and effective treatment option for patients with hypertension.

Hypertension and hyperuricemia frequently coexist in clinical settings and have a reciprocal relationship, collectively increasing the risk of cardiovascular events [32]. In this study, a reduction in uric acid levels was observed at Week 12 of the double‐blind period, with decreases of 11.5 µmol/L in the ALI/AML group and 11.6 µmol/L in the AML group (conversion factor: 1 mg/dL = 60 µmol/L). While there was a discernible trend toward uric acid reduction, the magnitude of the effect was modest, likely due to the limited sample size. The incidence of hyperuricemia during the double‐blind period was 1 (0.7%) in the AML group, with no cases reported in the ALI/AML group. During the open‐label period, drug‐related hyperuricemia occurred in 7 (2.5%) of participants, with no significant increase in incidence compared to the double‐blind period.

The combination of dihydropyridine CCBs and ARBs is widely recommended as an optimized therapeutic regimen for hypertension in clinical practice, particularly in China. CCBs act primarily by promoting arterial vasodilation, while ARBs and ACEIs provide vasodilation in both arteries and veins, creating a synergistic antihypertensive effect. Previous systematic reviews have demonstrated that a combination of standard‐dose CCB and ARB therapy is more effective in controlling BP and reducing the risk of AEs compared to high‐dose CCB monotherapy [33, 34]. This study corroborates these findings, showing that the combination of ALI and AML is effective in reducing BP and is well tolerated.

While this study demonstrated the efficacy and safety of the combination of ALI/AML, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the proportion of elderly patients included in the study was relatively low. Given that elderly hypertensive patients often face challenges such as reduced medication adherence and suboptimal BP control, this population warrants further attention. Future studies should focus on the efficacy and safety of ALI/AML in elderly patients to address these concerns. Second, patients already on a stable regimen of AML but with uncontrolled BP were directly randomized into the double‐blind period without a run‐in period, which limits the ability to assess their adherence prior to enrollment. As a result, a small number of patients did not complete the treatment as required. Third, the included population was relatively more restricted than the hypertensive patients typically seen in clinical practice. Specifically, patients with poorly controlled diabetes and those having a BMI greater than 30 kg/m2 were excluded from the trial. Last, the sample size for the exploratory ABPM analysis may have been insufficient to detect more differences with statistical significance between the treatment groups. Future studies with larger sample sizes for ABPM could provide more robust data on the efficacy of ALI/AML in continuous BP monitoring settings.

## 结论 / Conclusion

ALI/AML demonstrated better efficacy in reducing both office and ambulatory BP in patients who had an inadequate response to AML monotherapy. The treatment was well tolerated with once‐daily dosing. The SPC of ALI/AML offers a convenient and effective therapeutic option for BP management in hypertensive patients.
