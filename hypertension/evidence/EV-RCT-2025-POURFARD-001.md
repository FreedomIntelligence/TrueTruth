## English Abstract

Ischemic heart disease (IHD) has a substantial burden on individuals and health system infrastructure worldwide. Meanwhile, mounting evidence has declared remarkable contrasts in gut microbiota between individuals with IHD and healthy counterparts. The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) might be one of the most reliable strategies to manage cardiometabolic risk factors. Nonetheless, no experiment has tested the impact of a DASH diet and exercise on gut microbiota composition in IHD individuals. Hence, we aim to explore the effects of a DASH diet and concurrent exercise on gut microbiota, oxidized LDL, and cardiometabolic risk factors among men.

In total, 48 men aged 40–70 years with IHD will be recruited in this parallel randomized, controlled, 2 × 2 factorial clinical trial. These patients will be randomly assigned to the following four intervention groups: DASH diet (D), concurrent exercise (E), concurrent DASH diet and exercise (DE), and control (usual care) (C) for 8 weeks. At baseline and the end of the study, stool samples and serum levels will be gathered to detect gut microbiota (Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Bifidobacteria, clostridium, Faecalibacterium prausnitzi, Bacteroidetes, and Akkermansia muciniphila) and cardiometabolic risk factors, as well as anthropometric measures, blood pressure, quality of life, 6-min walking test, and Beck Depression Inventory (BDI).

Based on our information, the present study is the primary randomized controlled trial testing the impacts of short-term concurrent training and DASH diet on intestinal bacteria diversity and several cardiometabolic risk factors in men with IHD.

Iranian Registry of Clinical Trials (www.irct.ir, IRCT20181205041859N1). Registered on 29 September 2024.

## 背景 / Background

Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are the major reason for premature fatality in the world [1]. Ischemic heart disease (IHD) is the highly common form of CVD, which is the result of atherosclerosis [2]. By the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) study, IHD influences approximately 1.72% of the world’s adult people. In Iran, the incidence rate of IHD was estimated to be around 829 new cases per 100,000 persons in 2019 [3]. To decline the hazard level of IHD and boost the quality of life of individuals with IHD, closer concentration needs to be centered on the cardiometabolic risk factors as primary and secondary prevention of IHD. Evidence explains that concurrent cardiometabolic diseases (CMD) and a disadvantageous lifestyle can bring about more than twice the risk of fatality while keeping a healthy lifestyle can counteract 63% of the deleterious effects of CMD on mortality [4]. However, many patients will experience new events, notwithstanding the traditional risk factors being managed appropriately [5]. Furthermore, confronted with the absence of efficacious treatment, there is a growing concern about novel methods that assist in elucidating the pathophysiology of atherosclerosis. Additionally, recent evidence has revealed that intestinal bacteria are related to cardiovascular and various cardiometabolic diseases, namely obesity, hypertension, and type 2 diabetes [6, 7].

Over the past 10 years, evidence influenced by commensal microbes investigations has potentially proposed novel CVD prevention and management approaches [8]. Intriguingly, the pathogenic consequences of some lifestyle risk factors might be mediated through their adverse effects on the commensal bacteria [9]. A history of reciprocally favorable collaboration among the intestinal microbial community and the mammalian host influences a broad range of physiological functions [10].

Interestingly, recent evidence has indicated that the interaction of various gut microbial products with diet can influence the immune and inflammatory pathways and consequently induce the atherosclerotic process [11]. The microbiome variations and metabolite traits between individuals with and without IHD highlight their pathophysiological relevance [12]. Dysbiosis, as an imbalanced gut microbiota, is pivotal in several disease states, including atherosclerosis, hypertension, heart failure, and diabetes mellitus [13, 14]. Dysbiosis disrupts microbial diversity and increases potential pathogenic microorganisms’ abundance and distribution [15]. Several studies have shown the participation of gut dysbiosis in the expansion of various chronic diseases and immune dysfunction [14, 15].

While dietary interventions may play a role in secondary cardiovascular prevention, most of the available studies have explored the role of diet in primary prevention of cardiovascular [16–19]. It has been shown that various dietary interventions, such as energy restriction [16], plant-based diets like the vegan diet [19], the American Heart Association–recommended diet [18, 19], and the lacto-ovo-vegetarian diet [17] have improved cardiometabolic risk factors by modulating levels of inflammatory biomarkers, oxidative stress, and trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO). Different dietary factors impact the composition of the intestinal bacteria. For instance, the contribution of macronutrients to daily energy intake has attracted attention, particularly [20].

Clinical protocols advocate dietary strategies as the basis of therapy to manage and prevent cardiovascular disease [20, 21]. The dietary approaches to stop hypertension (DASH) have been revealed to mitigate blood pressure effectively [22] and cholesterol levels in clinical trials and diabetes and cardiovascular fatality in prospective cohort studies [23, 24]. Regardless of the protective associations between the intake of the DASH diet and CVDs, we know of no clinical trial exploring the impact of the DASH diet on individuals with IHD. This dietary pattern is rich in magnesium, calcium, and fiber and contains low amounts of saturated fatty acids besides a reasonable amount of protein, mainly from plant foods, fish, poultry, nuts, and low-fat dairy products. This diet also limits refined grains and sweets and recommends more whole grains. Although no study has evaluated the effects of the DASH diet in patients with IHD, the effects of a vegetarian diet (VD) or meat diet (MD) in patients with IHD were studied in a 4-week, randomized crossover trial [25]. This study showed that a vegetarian diet ameliorated cardiometabolic risk factors, reduced body weight, and influenced the relative proportion of gut commensal microbes with beneficial effects on the genus Akkermansia and Bifidobacterium, and plasma metabolites [25]. Compared with MD, consuming a VD led to beneficial effects on the quantity of the Akkermansia, similar to the effects of a diet rich in prebiotic and polyphenol-rich diets [26]. Akkermansia also favorably influenced body fat composition and fasting glycemic and lipid profile in a 6-week weight loss diet in obese subjects [27].

Exercise is also related to superior gut microbiota variety and beneficial taxa [28, 29]. So far, most exercise and intestinal microbes inspections have involved athletic or healthy people [30]. Consequently, there is scarce data regarding the impact of training on gut microbiota and physiological outcomes in pathological conditions [31]. In a cross-sectional analysis of healthy individuals, Clostridiales and Roseburia were firmly related to cardiorespiratory fitness [32]. Likewise, clinical trials revealed training-grown butyrate-synthesizing microbes such as Akkermansia and Faecalibacterium prausnitzii [33]. In this regard, Torquati and colleagues showed that in sedentary patients with type 2 diabetes, moderate-intensity exercise led to an increase in Bifidobacterium, Escherichia, and A. municiphila [31].

To our knowledge, no study has tested the impact of the DASH diet and exercise on the gut microbiome composition and cardiometabolic risk factors in individuals with IHD. Discovering the diversity and abundance of intestinal microbiota could be a promising therapy in manifesting novel strategies to treat and manage IHD. Therefore, the current study aims to reveal the effect of a concurrent DASH diet and exercise on intestinal microbiota abundance and cardiometabolic parameters in men with IHD. We also will test whether combinations of concurrent exercise and a DASH diet will be more effective than aerobic exercise with resistance exercise or a DASH diet alone.

## 方法 / Methods

The current clinical trial will be a parallel four-group randomized controlled clinical trial conducted on patients with IHD referred to Cardiac Rehabilitation Research Center in Isfahan, Iran. All participants will complete and sign an informed written consent form before completing their registration. Ethical approval has been obtained by the Research Ethics Committee of Islamic Azad University—Isfahan Branch (Khorasgan) (No. IR.IAU.KHUISF.REC.1402.322). Moreover, this intervention was registered in the Iranian Registry of Clinical Trials (www.irct.ir) on 29 September 2024 with code number IRCT20181205041859N1.

In the present single-blind randomized controlled trial, outpatients with IHD will be recruited from amongst those who underwent angiography at Shahid Chamran Hospital, Isfahan, Iran. IHD will be confirmed by a cardiologist using a PCI report and laboratory findings. This approach will recruit a representative sample of patients with IHD and increase the generalizability of results across populations.

We will include adult men, aged between 40 and 70 years, with stable IHD who underwent PCI at least 1 month prior to study initiation and were receiving common medications, including cholesterol-lowering drugs and aspirin. To mitigate loss to follow-up, patients will be recruited from those residing in Isfahan.

Patients will be excluded if they (1) were unable to provide informed consent; (2) had unstable coronary disease; (3) had musculoskeletal disease or were affected by inflammatory conditions, autoimmune disorders, kidney, and infectious diseases; (4) were already following a special diet, such as vegan diet and weight loss diet; (5) consumed supplements or medications which affect gut microbiota, such as antibiotics and symbiotic products during the past 3 months; and (6) had a known food allergy, previous bariatric surgery, and who failed to complete the rehabilitation sessions. In addition, the sample size was calculated using G*Power v3.1.9.4 (freeware, Heinrich Heine University Düsseldorf, Germany), based on a mixed between–within (group × time) repeated-measures ANOVA within a 2 × 2 × 2 factorial design, comprising two between-subject factors (exercise and diet) and one within-subject factor (time: pre/post). The input parameters included Cohen’s f = 0.25 (representing a medium effect size per Cohen), α = 0.05, power (1 − β) = 0.80, four experimental groups (2 exercise levels × 2 diet levels), and two repeated measurements (pre- and post-intervention). The choice of a medium effect size was informed by direct evidence from a randomized crossover trial in patients with IHD [25], in which an isocaloric vegetarian diet versus a meat-containing diet over 4 weeks, resulted in a significant reduction in Ox-LDL (− 2.73 U/L; P = 0.02). In that within-subject design (n = 27), the approximate effect size for Ox-LDL was d ≈ 0.48 (moderate–large), which we conservatively translated to f ≈ 0.25 for the parallel 2 × 2 × 2 design. Accordingly, based on Ox-LDL as the primary outcome for the power calculation, the required total sample size will be 48 participants (12 per group).

Figure 1 shows the flowchart of the study design. Overall, 100 men with IHD will be screened based on the inclusion criteria through advertisements on social media. Then, 48 eligible men will be enrolled in the present study. Patients will be divided into four groups using a simple randomization based on the table of random numbers. Accordingly, first, the reading position of the table numbers will be determined, and then the figures will be allocated to various groups, for example, 0 to 24 for the concurrent training group, 25 to 49 for the DASH diet group, 50 to 74 for the training and DASH group, and 75 to 99 for the control group. Finally, the researcher will randomly choose a value by touching one of the numbers and moving in one direction, which is considered before. To conceal random allocation, the approach of coded boxes with a random sequence will be applied. This approach is based on some boxes that are numbered randomly. Adequate sealing of the boxes, their similar appearance, and weight will be warranted.

The principal investigator (NRP) will register eligible men. A signed consent form will be obtained from all participants before enrolment, in which details regarding the recommended diets and exercise, duration of the project, and contact information for the principal researcher are provided. Participants will be notified that all the services provided during the study will be free of charge to them and that they can leave the study at any time all over the study. In addition, participants will be asked to keep their level of physical activity and dietary intake constant during the study based on the researchers’ recommendations.

Each person will receive a booklet, including a tailored eating plan consisting of some menus based on the DASH diet. Outcome assessors will be blinded throughout the intervention period.

Participants in the intervention groups will be given a DASH diet menu and will train, while persons in the control group will be provided with information brochures on nutritional and physical activity advice based on a Healthy Eating Plate [35] and American Heart Association, respectively. Moreover, this intervention was registered on the Iranian Registry of Clinical Trials (www.irct.ir) on 29 September 2024 with code number IRCT20181205041859N1.

All participants will pass the medical examination, namely ECG and echocardiography. After the initial assessment, patients in the control group will receive common care. The intervention consists of an 8-week, 2 to 3 times per week, supervised group-based rehabilitation programme that will be provided in the cardiac rehabilitation department of the Cardiac Rehabilitation Research Center. Following the results of the exercise test (ET), individuals will be classified into low, intermediate, and high-risk groups. Consistently, patients will reach the maximum exercise capacity in 2–7 days. If patients experience any problems during exercise training sessions, the program will be stopped, and risk classification will be reassessed considering new results gained from ET. Each session will last for 90 min: the first 20 min will be initiated with a warm-up followed by aerobic exercise for 20–40 min and finished with a 10-min cooldown. Participants will also relax for 20 min at the end of each session [36]. The type of exercise will be either cycling or walking/running using an ergo meter or a treadmill, respectively. Warm-up and cool-down phases include stretch activities. To determine the intensity of exercise, risk will be assessed using the maximum heart rate (HR) obtained between 60 and 85% of the ET [37]. A cardiologist will closely monitor and supervise patients during the first three sessions [38].

Using the ACSM guidance [39], strength training of the major muscle groups will be delivered (1 to 3 sets of 6–15 repetitions performed on training sessions). Training intensity will be set at 40% to 70% of 1-RM.

The dietary intervention will be an isocaloric DASH diet. Participants’ energy requirements will be estimated using adjusted ideal body weight and the Harris-Benedict equation. The DASH eating plan will be designed according to the plan suggested by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute [40].

Participants’ adherence to the advised diets will be examined through dietary recalls (2 weekdays and 1 weekend) collected every 2 weeks. Dietary recalls will be collected through phone interviews and household measurements by an expert dietitian. In total, 12 dietary recalls will be obtained. Household measures will be converted to grams/d and then used to estimate energy and nutrient intake using Nutritionist IV software (First Databank, San Bruno, CA, USA) adapted for Iranian dishes. We will ask participants to feel free to call the dietitian if they want to change their food consumption. We will also contact participants through the study to promote adherence and reduce forgetfulness concerning dietary and physical activity recommendations, as well as scheduled laboratory assessments.

The applied intervention will not have serious side effects on patients. However, participants will be asked to report any possible inconvenience and changes in their medications during the study. Following study completion, individual’s laboratory findings will be interpreted, and appropriate recommendations will be provided as necessary.

A standard questionnaire will be used to collect information about socioeconomic, demographic, ethnicity, and lifestyle variables (e.g., smoking status, physical activity, alcohol consumption, and dietary intake), health status, and medical history through a fully structured interview by trained health professionals. We will also measure primary outcomes including oxidized LDL, serum lipid profile concentrations, apolipoprotein A1 (Apo A1) and apolipoprotein B (Apo B) levels, fecal bacterial load, and liver enzymes, as well as secondary outcomes including anthropometric measurements and blood pressure at the beginning and end of the study.

Body weight will be measured using a balance scale while participants are barefoot and minimally clothed and will be recorded to the nearest 0.1 kg. Height will be measured using a wall-fixed meter while subjects are standing, and shoulders are in a normal position and will be recorded to the nearest 0.5 cm. Body mass index (BMI) will be calculated by dividing body weight by squared height (kg/m2). Waist circumference will be determined at the narrowest level while participants are minimally clothed using a non-stretchable tape measure and will be recorded to the nearest 0.1 cm. Systolic and diastolic blood pressures will be measured twice according to standard protocols and in a seated position. The first and fifth Korotkoff sounds will be regarded as systolic and diastolic pressures, respectively. The mean values of the two measures will be recorded as the final measure of blood pressure.

Biochemical markers will be measured using a 12-h fasting blood sample. Serum levels of lipid profile and fasting blood sugar (FBS) will be measured by using enzymatic colorimetric methods (Pars Azmoon, Tehran, Iran) adapted to a Selectra-2 autoanalyzer (Vital Scientific, Spankeren, Netherlands). Serum concentrations of high-density lipoprotein-cholesterol (HDL-C) will be determined after precipitation of non-HDL-C using dextran sulphate–magnesium chloride. To determine low-density lipoprotein-cholesterol (LDL-C), we will use the Friedwald equation where serum TG levels are less than 400 mg/dL. Oxidized LDL will be measured through the ELISA technique. Apolipoprotein A1 (Apo A1) and apolipoprotein B (Apo B) levels will be measured through an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA; Pars Azmon, Tehran, Iran) and serum C-reactive protein (CRP) will be determined using an ultrasensitive latex-enhanced immunoturbidimetric assay. Serum concentration of liver enzymes, including alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), and alkaline phosphatase (ALP), will be quantified on a BT-3000 (Biotechinica; China) autoanalyzer and using commercially available enzymatic reagents (Pars Azmoon, Iran). Serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) will be determined using the enzymatic colorimetric Bertholet method with a commercially available assay kit.

Individual fresh fecal samples will be obtained from all those involved at baseline assessment and after 8 weeks using a sterile plastic container brought to the Cardiovascular Research Institute Laboratory within 4 h of collection. Before the analysis of fecal microbiota, feces specimens will be given a code and frozen at − 80 °C immediately until final processing in the laboratory. To reduce alterations in the abundance of stool microbiota, the stool samples will be saved in one of three methods: initially, we will ask participants to attend the test room on the day of feces selection. The lab will be supplied with everything needed to gather stool samples. The next procedure requires the participant to send the specimen to the lab in less than 4 h from their fecal gathering. Lastly, if the individual cannot finish any of the designs, they ought to connect with the research staff, who will obtain the samples at home and deliver them within 4 h to the lab.

DNA isolation from frozen fecal specimens will be performed with a fecal DNA Extraction Stool Mini Kit after mechanical mixing based on the supplier’s details. The concentration of DNA separated from stool samples will be adjusted to 5 ng/μl for the following steps. All the isolated DNAs will be kept at − 20 °C freezer until more detailed analysis. Furthermore, detecting the abundance of stool bacteria, including Bifidobacteria, Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Clostridium, Bacteroidetes, and Akkermansia muciniphila as well as Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio by real-time PCR (using SYBR Green) will be performed with the Rotor-Gene Q (QIAGEN, Germany). We chose the taxa regarding their relationship with IHD and capacity to stabilize the commensal bacteria [12]. The primer sets applied in this study will be chosen based on the preceding study [41]. Specific and universal primers will be formulated according to the sequences of identical regions of 16S rRNA gene sequences and will be ordered from Bioneer, South Korea. Finally, the standard curves will calculate the quantity and relative abundance of gut bacteria.

Participants will be assured that their personal information will be stored privately and anonymously and will not be used individually. They will be informed that their information and specimens will be stored in a secure database and in a biochemistry laboratory with unique codes that are not identifiable by anyone except for the research team.

The study coordinators’ principal investigator (PI) will be NRP and MS, who will be responsible for the study design and project oversight. However, the Trial Steering Committee (TSC) will be comprised of KHJ and MS as principal members, the study physician (ESH), the study assistant and study advisor (MS), the statistician and study advisor (FT), and the principal investigator (NRP). Regular meetings of the members during the study will be held to monitor the progress of the work and resolve any possible technical and financial issues, and enhance planning efforts to meet project deadlines. In addition, Protocol amendments will be promptly reported to the Research Ethics Committee of Islamic Azad University—Isfahan Branch (Khorasgan). Since the intervention is low risk, no data monitoring committee is required. Blood and stool samples will be stored and analyzed in the laboratory of the Cardiovascular Research Institute, Isfahan University of Medical Sciences, Isfahan, Iran. PI (NRP and MS) will identify eligible participants, recruit, and obtain informed consent. Data Management Team will be comprised of MS and JALALI.

All statistical analyses will be performed using the Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS version 15, SPSS, Inc., Chicago). The Kolmogorov–Smirnov test, histogram, and Q-Q plot will be used to assess the normal distribution of variables. The log-transformed values will be used when data are not distributed normally. The general characteristics between the four groups will be compared using a one-way ANOVA test for continuous variables (age and physical activity level) and chi-square for categorical ones. Data will be presented as mean ± SD for continuous and percentage for categorical variables. Each group’s biochemical markers and anthropometric measurements before and after the intervention will be compared using independent sample t-test. Mean changes of anthropometric and biochemical measurements between four groups will be compared using analysis of variance (ANOVA) in crude and analysis of covariance in an adjusted model. Confounders will be chosen based on significant differences between groups. A significant level will be considered P < 0.05.

## 讨论 / Discussion

Ischemic heart disease is one of the principal causes of fatality worldwide [42]. Besides adopting a nutritious diet, physical activity has been demonstrated to have significant benefits in the secondary prevention and mitigating mortality rates of IHD [43]. The DASH diet, abundant in plant foods, low-fat dairy products, and less saturated fat and cholesterol, relates to a decreased odds of CVD events and has been approved by the American Heart Association to boost cardiovascular health [44]. In recent years, dysbiosis is severely involved in health issues and has manifested as a probable risk factor for various chronic diseases such as CVDs [45, 46]. The microbial intestinal community consists of more than trillions of microorganisms in the human gut [47]. Host and gut bacteria have developmental documentation, and many microbes remarkably affect health status. Nonetheless, technical developments have critically modified ecological conditions. Numerous of these alterations are highly considerable [48]. Amongst these, the associations between diet, physical activity, and intestinal microbes have obtained more consideration [49]. Current dietary shifts, including a diet low in fiber, fruit, nuts and seeds, vegetables, and whole grain, particularly in low-income countries [50], have been related to reduced microbiome diversity, increased inflammatory response, and risk of metabolic disorders, leading to IHD occurrence [50, 51]. Considering the complexity of interactions between basic reasons and complications in IHD, a practical intervention comprising diet and exercise would be effective in reducing modifiable risk factors and thereby preventing any related disability and mortality.

This study will be the first clinical trial testing the impact of the DASH diet and training on gut microbiota and cardiometabolic parameters in individuals with IHD. It is worth noting that the intervention is reasonable and can be implemented in medical conduct. Furthermore, this study is well designed, with highly controlled exercise and diet intervention. Since energy intake in intervention groups will be estimated based on actual body weight, this permits us to assess the impacts of the DASH diet and exercise on gut microbiota and cardiometabolic parameters independent of weight reduction.

The notable limitation of this trial is the limited number of bacterial species that will be detected. Another limitation is that this is a single-centre trial with a relatively small sample size, which restricts the generalizability of outcomes to all people. The short-term intervention of the study might not be enough to detect persistent alterations in gut microbiota composition and lifestyle manners.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In the present study, we hypothesize that adhering to a DASH diet combined with aerobic and resistance exercise would improve cardiometabolic components by modifying intestinal microbial composition. The results of our study may shed light on the execution of a suitable lifestyle intervention leading to better outcomes and improving quality of life in patients with IHD.

This trial is planned to begin in May 2025 and anticipated the recruitment to end in July 2025. All involved parties will be informed of the protocol modifications, and the Iranian Clinical Trials Registry will be updated accordingly (IRCT.ir).
