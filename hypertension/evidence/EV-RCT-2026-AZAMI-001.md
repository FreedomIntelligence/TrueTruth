## English Abstract

This systematic review and meta‐analysis aimed to compare the clinical outcomes of antihypertensive medication administration at bedtime versus morning, focusing on major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), mortality, and secondary cardiovascular outcomes in hypertensive patients.

We conducted a systematic search across multiple databases to identify randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comparing bedtime versus morning antihypertensive dosing. Studies were included if they evaluated MACE, all‐cause mortality, or cardiovascular mortality. Myocardial infarction, stroke, and heart failure were considered secondary outcomes. Data were extracted, and statistical analysis was performed using hazard ratios and mean differences with 95% confidence intervals. A random‐effects model with Hartung–Knapp correction was used to account for between‐study heterogeneity. Sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the robustness of the results.

Five studies with 36,477 patients (42.30% male) were included. No significant differences were found between bedtime and morning dosing for MACE (HR: 0.71, 95% CI: 0.43–1.15, p = 0.11) and mortality outcomes (all‐cause HR: 0.76, 95% CI: 0.50–1.16, p = 0.14; cardiovascular HR: 0.54, 95% CI: 0.08–3.86, p = 0.31). These findings, which showed substantial heterogeneity (I² > 90%), were consistent in sensitivity analyses. Also, for secondary outcomes, there were no significant differences observed in myocardial infarction (HR: 0.79, 95% CI: 0.49–1.29, p = 0.26), stroke (HR: 0.61, 95% CI: 0.32–1.17, p = 0.10), or heart failure (HR: 0.64, 95% CI: 0.37–1.08, p = 0.07).

This meta‐analysis found no significant difference in cardiovascular outcomes between bedtime and morning antihypertensive dosing. Within the limitations of the available evidence, a universal chronotherapy strategy does not appear to provide additional cardiovascular benefit. Once‐daily antihypertensive medications can be taken at a time that aligns with the patient's lifestyle, with adherence being the most critical factor in ensuring treatment effectiveness.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension affects nearly one‐third of adults globally, making it a widespread condition responsible for an estimated 7.5 million deaths worldwide, or about 12.8% of all deaths [1, 2]. Proper blood pressure control is crucial for reducing the risk of major cardiovascular events, including stroke, ischemic heart disease, and cardiovascular‐related deaths. Blood pressure follows a complex 24‐h cycle influenced by environmental, behavioral, and circadian factors, with endogenous circadian rhythms playing a particularly significant role [3]. These rhythms not only affect 24‐h blood pressure patterns but also influence the pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics of hypertension medications, impacting their absorption and excretion [4, 5]. Research has shown notable differences in the safety and efficacy of antihypertensive drugs when taken in the morning versus at bedtime [6]. Understanding and addressing these fluctuations is essential for managing hypertension effectively and reducing long‐term cardiovascular risks.

Studies suggest that night‐time blood pressure is the strongest predictor of cardiovascular risk, surpassing awake‐time and 24‐h measurements [7, 8]. Elevated night‐time blood pressure and a lack of nocturnal decline are linked to target organ damage, while morning blood pressure surges increase the risk of cardiovascular events, including stroke [9]. Restoring a normal dipper blood pressure profile has emerged as a therapeutic goal, with implications for optimizing antihypertensive treatment and improving patient outcomes [10]. To address these risks, achieving 24‐h blood pressure control is crucial. Bedtime dosing has been proposed as an effective strategy to normalize nocturnal dips, manage nighttime hypertension, and reduce morning surges [3, 10]. It may benefit patients with non‐dipping patterns, isolated nighttime hypertension, or exaggerated morning surges [11, 12], yet its necessity is questioned with modern 24‐h antihypertensive therapies [9]. Moreover, its evidence, clinical relevance, and indications remain controversial.

Uncertainties remain regarding the impact of antihypertensive drug chronotherapy on clinical outcomes. Earlier studies by Hermida et al. recommended bedtime dosing [13, 14], but more recent research has challenged this approach [15, 16, 17]. A 2022 systematic review by the International Society of Hypertension examined the effects of bedtime dosing on cardiovascular outcomes. Still, it highlighted limitations in existing evidence due to methodological flaws in available trials [18]. Additionally, their analysis was restricted to a systematic review without meta‐analysis. A 2023 systematic review and meta‐analysis further explored this topic but did not include the most recent and largest studies in this context. These recent studies offer new insights that could reshape our understanding [19]. Therefore, this systematic review and meta‐analysis focus on comparing bedtime versus morning dosing regarding clinical outcomes, incorporating the latest and most robust evidence.

## 方法 / Methods

This systematic review and meta‐analysis was registered on PROSPERO (ID: CRD42024627861). It was conducted in adherence to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta‐Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines [20]. A systematic search was performed across multiple electronic databases, including PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and Embase, to identify relevant studies. The initial search was conducted on September 26, 2024, and updated on August 01, 2025. The PRISMA flow diagram reflects the cumulative results of both searches. The search employed a standardized approach using the following keywords: “night‐time,” “bedtime,” “chronotherapy,” “nocturnal,” “dosing,” “dose,” “administration,” “antihypertensive,” “treatment,” “drug,” “medication,” “hypertension,” “blood pressure,” “prognosis,” “outcome,” “mortality,” “death,” “myocardial infarction,” “stroke,” “heart failure,” and “MACE.” In addition, two independent reviewers manually screened the reference lists of original studies, reviews, case series, case reports, and editorials to identify supplementary studies. Records from major scientific conferences were also reviewed to capture gray literature. The search encompassed all languages and publication periods. Detailed search strategies are provided in Supporting Information Table 1.

We included randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that evaluated patients with hypertension (HTN) who took at least one antihypertensive drug in the evening or at bedtime, compared to those who took all antihypertensive drugs in the morning or upon waking. The primary outcomes were the reporting of at least one of the following: major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), all‐cause mortality, or cardiovascular mortality. Secondary outcomes included myocardial infarction (MI), stroke/transient ischemic attack [21], heart failure (HF), morning and evening systolic and diastolic blood pressure measurements, and available subgroup analyses (e.g., by age, sex, diabetes status, and smoking). MACE was commonly defined across the included studies as cardiovascular death, MI, stroke, and HF, with slight variations in specific criteria. Studies were excluded if they were in vitro or animal studies, crossover or placebo‐controlled trials, or if they compared two different antihypertensive drugs administered at bedtime and morning.

Two authors (F.R. and A.M.) independently screened the titles and abstracts of the retrieved records for eligibility after duplicates were removed using the Rayyan online tool [22]. Following the initial screening, the full texts of the selected studies were retrieved and thoroughly evaluated for eligibility for inclusion in the meta‐analysis. Any discrepancies or disagreements during this process were resolved through discussion with a third author (P.A.).

Data extraction was performed by two reviewers (F.R. and S.F.), who recorded the following information: study characteristics, including year, country, study design, study population, inclusion and exclusion criteria, primary and secondary outcomes, composite cardiovascular event definitions, blood pressure measurement methods, follow‐up duration, and medications used. Baseline characteristics, including age, sex, number of patients per group, BMI, systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP), and relevant comorbidities (including diabetes mellitus, chronic kidney disease, essential hypertension, and coronary artery disease), were also extracted. Efficacy outcomes, including all‐cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, MACE, heart failure, myocardial infarction (MI), angina, and cerebrovascular events (stroke/TIA), were recorded. Any disagreements in data extraction were resolved through discussion with a third reviewer (P.A.).

We utilized the revised Cochrane Collaboration tool (ROB 2) to assess the risk of bias in randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and the Newcastle‐Ottawa Scale to evaluate the quality of non‐randomized studies. Four reviewers (P.A., E.Z., F.R., and S.F.) independently assessed the included studies for biases related to randomization, selection, performance, reporting, attrition, and overall risk. Any disagreements were resolved through consensus.

This meta‐analysis was performed using R Software Version 4.3.2 to pool dichotomous outcomes with hazard ratios (HR) and continuous outcomes with mean differences [23], accompanied by their respective 95% confidence intervals (CI) [24]. The random‐effects model was used for the pooled analysis due to significant heterogeneity across the included trials. HRs were pooled using the Dersimonian‐Laird method, with the Hartung‐Knapp [7] approach applied to adjust standard errors and account for the high heterogeneity among the studies. Heterogeneity was assessed using the chi‐square test and quantified with the I2. A Chi‐square p‐value of < 0.1 indicated significant heterogeneity, and heterogeneity was considered substantial when the I2 value exceeded 50%. In cases of significant heterogeneity, sensitivity analysis was performed by excluding one study at a time and reanalyzing the data to identify the source of the variability. The forest plots were created using GraphPad Prism, version 9.5.1 (528) [25]. As the number of trials included in the analysis was fewer than 10, we did not assess publication bias using funnel plots [26].

## 讨论 / Discussion

Nocturnal hypertension, a key predictor of adverse outcomes in individuals with hypertension, has spurred the hypothesis that evening dosing of antihypertensive medications could improve cardiovascular outcomes. This idea stems from the potential benefit of aligning treatment with the body's circadian rhythms to better control nighttime blood pressure. However, the topic remains controversial, with conflicting evidence and ongoing debates within the scientific and medical communities regarding the clinical efficacy and generalizability of such an approach. This study systematically reviewed and pooled the results of five randomized trials to evaluate the differences in clinical outcomes between bedtime and morning dosing of commonly used antihypertensive medications. The analysis revealed no significant differences between the bedtime and morning dosing groups in the occurrence of MACE, all‐cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, MI, HF, and stroke incidence. Moreover, subgroup analyses stratified by gender, DM, CKD, and resistant HTN showed no significant differences in clinical outcomes between the two dosing times. As anticipated, asleep SBP and DBP, and the proportion of non‐dippers were lower with bedtime dosing, while awake SBP and DBP remained similar between dosing schedules.

In the present study, we concluded that bedtime dosing of antihypertensive drugs provides no additional benefit regarding MACE, all‐cause mortality, or cardiovascular mortality compared to morning dosing. This finding aligns with the position of the International Society of Hypertension [34], which emphasized that available evidence for bedtime dosing remains limited and of low certainty, primarily due to concerns over trial design and generalizability [35]. One of the earliest meta‐analyses suggesting benefit came from Roush et al., who in 2014 reported a reduction in cardiovascular events with bedtime dosing, although their analysis was based on a small number of heterogeneous and irrelevant trials [36]. Subsequently, a systematic review by Hermida et al. summarized the evidence on chronotherapy, drawing heavily on their own group's single‐center, open‐label trials, including MAPEC (2010) [13] and Hygia (2019) [14], which reported large reductions in cardiovascular risk with bedtime dosing [37]. However, more recent and independent meta‐analyses by Maqsood et al. [38] and Lee et al. [39] found no significant benefit after excluding trials from the Hermida group or small Chinese studies, underscoring the fragility of earlier positive findings. Burnier et al., in a narrative review, also questioned the clinical relevance of the bedtime dosing strategy and highlighted the methodological concerns surrounding supportive trials [40]. Abuelazma et al. conducted a meta‐analysis and found no significant difference in major cardiovascular events between morning and bedtime dosing, except MI, though their inclusion of some older studies with differing designs may limit interpretability [41]. In contrast to these prior reviews, our analysis specifically pooled data from all five available randomized controlled trials that directly compared morning and bedtime dosing with respect to clinical cardiovascular endpoints, providing a focused and robust assessment of the chronotherapy hypothesis.

The earliest MAPEC (2010) [13] and Hygia (2019) [14] studies, which reported significant reductions in cardiovascular events and mortality with bedtime antihypertensive dosing, had notable limitations [18]. In these studies, modest changes in ambulatory blood pressure (ABP) were insufficient to explain the observed clinical outcomes [13, 14]. Moreover, unblinded protocols, treatment variability (such as inconsistent drug regimens in the randomized arm), and unreasonably low drop‐out rates introduced confounding factors [18, 42, 43]. Critically, the Hygia [14] trial changed its primary endpoint from ABP to cardiovascular outcomes after the study had begun, invalidating its primary findings [44]. Furthermore, the reported cardiovascular event rate in the control group was implausibly low compared to the enrolled population's risk profile [44]. Additionally, questions arose regarding whether the MAPEC (2010) [13] is a single RCT or a pooled analysis, and insufficient transparency in data handling raised concerns about the reliability of the results [45, 46]. The Hygia [14] investigators and independent experts have addressed some of these concerns [47, 48, 49, 50]. For example, they claim the reported cardiovascular benefits were explicitly linked to reductions in asleep systolic blood pressure, not overall ABP, consistent with established chronobiological mechanisms [50]. An independent, blinded committee conducted endpoint adjudication, and although drug regimens varied to reflect real‐world practice, treatment timing was standardized [49]. The low dropout rate (0.45%) reflects intensive primary care follow‐up rather than reporting bias. Cardiovascular outcomes were prespecified in the 2016 protocol, not added post‐hoc [33], and the low event rates in the control group were consistent with the population's low risk profile [50]. MAPEC [13] was a distinct RCT conducted in a tertiary setting before Hygia [14], not a pooled analysis [48]. Data transparency was supported by patented software, device battery logs, and acceptable Ambulatory Blood Pressure Monitoring (ABPM) failure rates [49, 50]. However, these ongoing debates and uncertainties have, so far, prevented the incorporation of the trial results into clinical practice.

Three recent trials —TIME (2022) [16], BedMed‐Frail (2025) [28], and BedMed (2025) [27] —reported consistent findings that bedtime dosing offers no advantage over morning dosing on hard clinical outcomes, including mortality. TIME [16] was a real‐world study and the largest RCT to investigate the effects of blood pressure chronotherapy on clinical outcomes. However, some concerns have been raised about its design and methodology. The study relied on fixed clock‐time dosing windows (06:00–10:00 vs. 20:00–00:00) and assumed uniform sleep‐wake cycles across participants, rather than aligning treatment with each individual's biological circadian rhythms, a fundamental principle of chronotherapy [51]. Notably, a TIME sub‐study found that dosing aligned with chronotype was associated with a reduced risk of non‐fatal myocardial infarction [52]. This limitation significantly undermines the study's ability to capture true time‐dependent treatment effects. The study's remote, self‐managed design with limited physician involvement introduces potential concerns about data accuracy and adherence. It also failed to employ ABPM, omitting key outcomes such as asleep blood pressure and sleep‐time dipping—both strong predictors of cardiovascular risk [51]. The study reported differential non‐adherence rates of 39.0% in the evening‐dosing group compared to 22.5% in the morning group, primarily due to nocturia caused by diuretics. However, its large‐scale design, inclusion of a diverse population, and blinded endpoint adjudication help contextualize its conclusions.

The latest BedMed trial's [27] design, while strengthening real‐world applicability, introduced critical limitations. Most notably, adherence was substantially lower in the bedtime group (70% at 72 months vs. 88% in the morning group), largely due to intolerable nocturia with evening diuretics [53]. This asymmetry likely diluted any true biological benefit of bedtime dosing. There was also protocol‐allowed flexibility in medication timing (bedtime to dinnertime, morning time to lunchtime). Compounding this, only 9% of participants underwent 24‐h BP monitoring, leaving the trial unable to confirm whether bedtime dosing actually improved nocturnal BP control; the core mechanism proposed for cardiovascular protection [51]. The study was also underpowered to detect modest (< 25%) but clinically meaningful risk reductions, a key concern given that realistic treatment effects may fall in the 12%–15% range. Additionally, the failure to analyze outcomes by nocturnal BP dipping status overlooked a high‐risk subgroup that might uniquely benefit from chronotherapy [53]. BedMed's pragmatic approach directly answers whether bedtime dosing is feasible and safe in actual clinical practice—not just idealized trial settings. Its design ensures findings are immediately applicable to primary care, where most hypertension management occurs. BedMed robustly demonstrated that pragmatic timing switches do not improve outcomes in primary care; however, several limitations prevent definitive conclusions about the biological potential of bedtime dosing under optimized conditions. The BedMed‐Frail trial [28] evaluated the effects of antihypertensive dosing time in older adults, but its conclusions should be interpreted with caution due to several notable limitations. First, the comparison between groups may have been partially affected by the fact that some participants in the ‘usual care’ group were already taking antihypertensives at bedtime (15.3% of doses), reducing the contrast in dosing timing between the groups. Second, the extreme frailty of participants (median age 88, 86% dementia) led to overwhelmingly high non‐cardiovascular mortality; over 90% of primary outcomes were deaths unrelated to heart disease or stroke. Third, with fewer than 20 non‐fatal cardiovascular events recorded, the trial lacked statistical power to meaningfully assess whether bedtime dosing prevents heart attacks, strokes, or heart failure. Taken together, these limitations make it challenging to draw firm conclusions about the impact of antihypertensive timing on cardiovascular outcomes in typical older adults.

No significant differences were found between bedtime and morning dosing for vascular events, such as myocardial infarction and stroke, in the current study. As mentioned before, some earlier meta‐analyses suggested that bedtime dosing may reduce cardiovascular events, but these included some older, less relevant studies [19, 54, 55]. Two older studies, FACET (1998) [56] and CONVINCE (2003) [57], which reported benefits of evening dosing, compared different antihypertensive drugs administered at night and in the morning, introducing flaws in the analysis, so they were excluded from this study. Similarly, although the HOPE (2000) [58] study supported evening dosing, it compared ramipril with placebo and did not directly evaluate medication timing or chronotherapy, limiting its relevance to the bedtime dosing hypothesis. Moreover, the Syst‐Eur (1997) [59] and Syst‐China (1998) [60] trials showed that treating older adults with isolated systolic hypertension on an evening schedule lowers stroke and cardiovascular risk. However, both trials included placebo arms and were not designed to compare bedtime versus morning dosing. In addition to clinical trial data, observational studies have also explored this issue, with mixed results. A large Spanish registry study following over 28,000 hypertensive patients for nearly 10 years found no difference in cardiovascular or all‐cause mortality between morning and bedtime dosing, supporting more recent randomized trial results in a real‐world setting despite limitations such as lack of adherence data [61]. In contrast, Sobiczewski et al.'s observational study in hypertensive patients with coronary heart disease linked omission of bedtime dosing to non‐dipping blood pressure patterns and increased mortality over 6.6 years [62]. However, the Sobiczewski study's small sample size and focus on a high‐risk subgroup reduce the generalizability of its findings, and the observational design of both studies weakens the strength of their conclusions.

Blood pressure typically follows a diurnal pattern, being higher during the day and lower at night, with a morning surge as activity begins [63, 64]. Lower nighttime systolic blood pressure is associated with improved endothelial function, including enhanced nitric oxide (NO) bioavailability and reduced arterial stiffness, contributing to cardiovascular health [65, 66]. These effects are absent during the morning surge, which contributes to the higher incidence of MI and stroke observed at this time [67, 68]. Managing nighttime blood pressure with antihypertensive drugs, such as ACE inhibitors, can enhance vascular health by reducing thrombotic risk factors, including plasminogen activator inhibitor‐1 (PAI‐1) [69, 70]. Nocturnal dipping patterns significantly impact cardiovascular outcomes [71], as a meta‐analysis of 17,312 hypertensive patients found that nocturnal SBP fall, independent of 24‐h SBP, predicts major cardiovascular events, with reverse and reduced dipping patterns associated with higher risks [72]. These physiological benefits of nighttime blood pressure control may not always result in differences in clinical outcomes, such as MI [73]. Comorbid conditions like diabetes and chronic kidney disease can disrupt circadian rhythms and hinder endothelial repair mechanisms, potentially reducing these advantages [74, 75]. Furthermore, advanced vascular remodeling or irreversible endothelial dysfunction in certain patients may further restrict the clinical impact of nocturnal blood pressure reductions [76, 77]. This discrepancy may also reflect additional factors, including the limited pharmacologic rationale for bedtime dosing when long‐acting antihypertensive agents are used [78], the influence of underlying disease processes such as obstructive sleep apnea that contribute to nocturnal hypertension independently of medication timing, and the low reproducibility of dipping patterns, which may lead to inconsistent patient classification and weaken the reliability of targeting nocturnal BP phenotypes [78].

Although long‐acting antihypertensive agents generally maintain 24‐h blood pressure control [78], studies have shown that bedtime dosing can result in greater nocturnal BP reductions and improved dipping patterns in some patients, particularly non‐dippers [13, 39]. Among the included trials, MAPEC [13] and HYGIA [14] demonstrated significantly lower asleep systolic and diastolic BP with bedtime dosing compared to morning dosing, accompanied by greater sleep‐time SBP and DBP dipping and a lower proportion of non‐dippers. In the BedMed trial [27], BP monitoring was not performed routinely, and only an intermediate‐stage ABPM assessment in a small subgroup suggested a trend toward lower asleep BP with bedtime dosing. These findings are consistent with the meta‐analysis by Hermida et al. [37], which included several randomized controlled trials. They reported an average reduction of 5.17 mmHg in asleep SBP, a 3.22% increase in dipping percentage, and 73–78% of non‐dippers converting to dipper status with bedtime dosing. Overall, 82.3% of the included trials favored bedtime dosing for improved nocturnal blood pressure control. Although some earlier trials, such as HARMONY [79] and TEMPUS [80], reported differing results, these studies had methodological limitations, including small sample sizes, short follow‐up periods, and contamination. Besides, some reviews have suggested that bedtime dosing may reduce the morning blood pressure surge, proteinuria, and left ventricular hypertrophy [37, 39].

Hypertension management is often complicated by comorbidities such as DM [81, 82], CKD [83, 84], resistant hypertension [85], and obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) [86, 87], conditions frequently associated with non‐dipping and nocturnal hypertension [88, 89], both linked to elevated cardiovascular risk [90]. These characteristics theoretically make affected patients more responsive to bedtime dosing of antihypertensive medications. Corroborating this, the Hygia trial [14] and three MAPEC sub‐studies [85, 91, 92] reported superiority of bedtime dosing in hypertensive patients with CKD [91], DM [92], and resistant hypertension [85]. In contrast, recent trials such as TIME [16], BedMed [27], and BedMed‐Frail [28] found no significant benefit of bedtime dosing on cardiovascular outcomes in subgroup analyses, possibly due to methodological differences, as previously noted. Our meta‐analysis, which pooled data from these trials, similarly found no significant reduction in MACE with bedtime versus morning dosing in subgroup analyses. These findings highlight the complexity of blood pressure regulation in such comorbid states and suggest that the timing of antihypertensive medication alone may not translate into improved long‐term cardiovascular outcomes across diverse patient groups. However, this meta‐analysis should be interpreted cautiously due to the small number of studies available for these subgroups, and larger, well‐designed trials specifically targeting these populations are needed.

Concerns have been raised about the safety of nighttime blood pressure lowering through bedtime dosing, particularly regarding cerebrovascular events, glaucoma, and postural hypotension‐related complications; however, all included trials consider it safe. Neither the BedMed [17] nor the TIME [16] studies found significant differences in decreased vision, new glaucoma diagnoses, or glaucoma‐related hospitalizations between bedtime and morning dosing groups. While earlier studies suggested that low nocturnal blood pressure might increase the risk of ischemic optic neuropathy and worsen glaucoma, these findings have made ophthalmologists cautious about prescribing antihypertensive medications at bedtime. Excessive nocturnal blood pressure reductions have been proposed as a potential concern for cerebrovascular risk [93, 94], though conclusive evidence remains lacking [95]. Moreover, major trials such as MAPEC [13] and Hygia [14] reported that nocturnal hypotension is extremely rare (0.3% of participants). The BedMed [17] and BedMed‐Frail [17] trials likewise found no significant differences in postural hypotension‐related events, such as syncope, falls, fractures, or cognitive and behavioral disorders, providing further reassurance about the safety of bedtime antihypertensive dosing, even when it lowers nocturnal blood pressure.

This meta‐analysis is the first to compare bedtime and morning dosing of the same antihypertensive drugs on clinical outcomes, excluding trials that compare different medications at different times (e.g., one drug at night and another in the morning) or placebo‐controlled trials, thereby ensuring an accurate and robust comparison. All available relevant RCTs, including those newly identified, were included in the analysis. We used pooled HR, accounting for time‐to‐event, along with the Hartung–Knapp adjustment, making this analysis more precise and representative than previous analyses evaluating relative risk.

There are some limitations in the preparation of this meta‐analysis. First, the included studies were open‐label, which increases the risk of performance bias. Additionally, the number of included studies was limited, particularly for subgroup analyses, reducing the power and generalizability of these specific findings. Moreover, there were variations among studies in the particular drugs used. The study arms also differed in dosing schedules; most defined dosing times relative to waking or bedtime, whereas one study used fixed clock times. These differences reduce direct comparability across trials and likely contributed to the observed heterogeneity and low certainty in some outcomes. These variabilities were expected given the pragmatic, real‐world designs of the included trials; we addressed this heterogeneity using a random‐effects model and sensitivity analyses, with results remaining stable across iterations. Furthermore, we did not differentiate the effects of dosing schedules between dipping and non‐dipping individuals, which could have influenced the results. Therefore, the findings of this study should be interpreted with caution.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In this systematic review and meta‐analysis, we found no significant difference in major adverse cardiovascular events—including stroke, myocardial infarction, mortality, or heart failure—or in safety outcomes when comparing bedtime versus morning administration of blood pressure–lowering medications. Based on the current evidence, once‐daily antihypertensive medications may be taken at the time that best aligns with a patient's routine and preferences, without compromising clinical effectiveness or safety. While our findings do not support a universal cardiovascular benefit of chronotherapy, they underscore the need for future research focusing on specific patient populations, incorporating ABPM, and addressing the poor reproducibility of dipping status. Well‐designed trials that target subgroups who may differentially benefit—or be harmed—from evening dosing are essential to clarify the role of chronotherapy in hypertension management.
