## English Abstract

The precise mechanism of sodium glucose co‐transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitor on reno‐protective effect has been still unclear. In this study, we hypothesised that SGLT2 inhibitor prevents diabetic kidney disease via reduction of hypoxia‐induced factors.

In this multicenter, prospective, randomised, double blinded clinical trial, people with type 2 diabetes and microalbuminuria were randomised equally to empagliflozin (10 mg/day) (n = 40) and placebo (n = 39) and followed 24 weeks. The primary endpoint was change in urinary albumin creatinine ratio (ACR) and urinary liver type fatty acid binding protein (L‐FABP) excretion from baseline to 24 weeks. Major secondary outcome was change in serum vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), angiopoietin‐like proteins 2 (ANGPTL2), angiopoietin‐like proteins 4 (ANGPTL4), and adrenomedullin (AM) levels.

Although the reduction of ACR was significantly greater in the empagliflozin group than the placebo group at 4 and 12 weeks, the difference of change at 24 weeks between the two groups was not statistically significant (Empagliflozin group—Placebo group: −0.3643, 95% CI: −0.7571 to 0.0285, p = 0.0686). There was no difference in urinary L‐FABP excretion between the empagliflozin and placebo groups. Serum VEGF and ANGPTL2 decreased significantly more in the empagliflozin group, whereas there were no significant differences in AM and ANGPTL4.

These results demonstrated that empagliflozin partially suppressed the hypoxia‐induced angiogenic factors overproduction in addition to a declining trend in ACR in the early stage of diabetic kidney disease, which might contribute to the mechanisms of reno‐protective effects of this agent (jRCTs051200147).

## 背景 / Background

Diabetic kidney disease (DKD) is a leading cause of end‐stage kidney disease, along with the increasing rate of type 2 diabetes worldwide. DKD causes proteinuria, leading to kidney dysfunction, and is an important risk factor for cardiovascular diseases.
1
 Therefore, improvements in the clinical outcomes of diabetic patients are crucial to prevent DKD progression. However, these methods have been insufficient to prevent DKD progression.

Recent clinical trials such as CREDENCE and EMPA‐KIDNEY demonstrated that sodium glucose co transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitor could prevent DKD progression including early phase beyond glycemic control
2
, 
3
 in DKD, and, therefore, American Diabetes Association (ADA) statement recommends preferential use of SGLT2 inhibitor in DKD.
4
 However, precise mechanisms of preventive effects of SGLT2 inhibitor on DKD have not been fully clarified.

In early phase DKD, kidney oxygen requirement is elevated by glomerular hyperfiltration and exacerbation of tubular reabsorption.
5
 These statuses expose kidney tissue to hypoxic injury. Indeed, a previous study showed that hypoxia is present in the kidney of people with diabetes.
6
 A previous experimental study demonstrated that induction of hypoxia‐inducible factor‐1 (HIF‐1), which induced angiogenic factor such as vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), was elicited at early phase of DKD.
7
 A previous single‐nucleus transcriptome analysis of human early DKD sample also showed up‐regulation of proangiogenic genes.
8
 Previous studies demonstrated that overexpression of angiogenic factors cause glomerular sclerosis, tubulo‐interstitial fibrosis via inflammation and induction of profibrotic cytokines. A previous report showed that overexpression of VEGF‐A in podocyte contribute to diabetic glomerular nodular sclerosis formation,
9
 and, clinically, an observational study also demonstrated that elevation of VEGF was associated with DKD.
10
 In diabetic kidney, up‐regulation of Ang‐2 which loosens the endothelial cells and synergies VEGF to promote angiogenesis, associated with decreased Tie‐2 expression, has been reported in DN animal models, although the expression of Ang‐1 was not altered.
11
 Although Angiopoietin‐like proteins (ANGPTLs) are structurally similar to angiopoietin, ANGPTLs does not bind to the angiopoietin receptors Tie2 and Tie1.
12
 However, ANGPTLs are also induced by hypoxia and involved in angiogenesis.
12
 Among them, ANGPTL2 was reported to overexpress in diabetic glomerulopathy.
13
 ANGPTL2 is expressed in podocyte and tublar cells in the kidney, and was reported to involved in renal fibrosis and podocyte dysfunction.
14
, 
15
 ANGPTL4 is expressed mainly in podocyte in the kidney, and overexpression of ANGPTL4 in podocyte causes proteinuria in nephrotic syndrome.
16
 In diabetic rodent model, it has been reported that ANGPTL4 was involved in proteinuria of DKD.
17
 Therefore, it is important for prevention of diabetic nephropathy in early stage to attenuate these hypoxia‐induced angiogenic factors overexpression.

SGLT2 inhibitor attenuates glomerular hyperfiltration via correction of tubulo‐glomerular feedback system
18
 and proximal tubular oxygen usage by reduction of proximal tubular sodium‐glucose cotransport.
19
 Additionally, increase of ketone body by SGLT2 inhibitor promotes ketone usage, which requires less oxygen as energy source.
20
 Therefore, SGLT2 inhibitor can reduce oxygen requirement of kidney tissue. Furthermore, SGLT2 inhibitor can increase oxygenation by elevation of haematocrit. A recent report showed that SGLT2 inhibitor treatment improved kidney oxygenation in diabetes with normal kidney function,
21
 suggesting that overproduction of angiogenic factors which is introduced by hypoxia might be attenuated by SGLT2 inhibitor via improvement of kidney hypoxia. Taken together, SGLT2 inhibitor may improve renal oxygenation and lower hypoxia‐associated both glomerular and tubular damage through inhibition of hypoxia‐induced overproduction of angiogenic factors. Indeed. Therefore, in the present study, we investigate the effect of SGLT2 inhibitor on urinary albumin excretion and liver type fatty acid binding protein (L‐FABP) in the early phase of DKD. L‐FABP was induced by ischemic renal injury in a rodent model.
22
 Furthermore, urinary L‐FABP excretion was associated with the expression of HIF‐1 in diabetic model mice.
23
 These studies suggest that L‐FABP might be a marker of tubular injury due to hypoxic injury. Furthermore, we investigate the change of hypoxia‐induced angiogenic factors such as VEGF level by SGLT2 inhibitor.

## 方法 / Methods

This study was a prospective, multicenter, randomised, double‐blind, placebo‐controlled comparison, investigator‐initiated study. It was conducted at 4 institutions in Japan, and it complied with the articles of the Declaration of Helsinki (as revised in October 2013) and the Clinical Trial Act in Japan. The study protocol was approved by the Nara Medical University Certified Review Board (approval number: nara0018) in accordance with the law on clinical research in Japan.

Patients were included if they gave their written informed consent to take part in the study, were aged ≥20 and <85 years (regardless of sex), had a haemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) ≥6.5% and <10.0% (86 mmol/mol), had an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ≥30 mL/min/1.73 m2, and had a urinary albumin creatinine ratio (ACR) >30 and <300 mg/gCr. We also included patients who did not change their renin‐angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitor, statin, and glycemic‐lowering agents for at least 3 months before granting informed consent.

Patients were excluded if they met any of the following criteria:Patients with body mass index (BMI) less than 18.5 kg/m2.Patients diagnosed with chronic glomerulonephritis.Patients diagnosed with acute coronary syndrome or stroke within 3 months prior to obtaining consent.Patients who have undergone coronary artery bypass surgery or percutaneous coronary angioplasty within 3 months prior to obtaining consent.Patients with contraindications to empagliflozin administration (hypersensitivity, severe ketosis, diabetic coma, severe infection, before or after surgery, severe trauma).Patients receiving SGLT2 inhibitors within 3 months prior to obtaining consent.Patients with heart failure of New York Heart Association (NYHA) cardiac function class III or higher.Patients taking non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory analgesics on a regular basis.Patients with severe hepatic dysfunction (hepatic transaminases >500 U/L).Pregnant, lactating, or attempting pregnancy at the time of consent.Patients participating in other interventional studies at the time of consent.Patients who are judged to be inappropriate to participate in this study by the investigators.

After being confirmed eligible, participants were randomized by stratified allocation with eGFR (≥60 mL/min/1.73 m2 or not) as an allocation adjustment factor. Participants received 10 mg of empagliflozin or placebo once a day for 6 months in a 1:1 ratio using block random allocation. The study medication and matching placebo were provided by ClinCloud, Japan, which had no role in the trial design and conduct. The person responsible for randomisation will manage the randomisation list until the time of key opening so that blinding can be maintained for the subjects and the principal investigator and sub‐investigators. During the study period, the target of HbA1c was below 7%, and the target of blood pressure was below 130/80 mmHg according to Japanese diabetes society recommendation.

Previous studies examining the effects of hypoglycemic drugs such as dipeptidyl peptidase‐4 (DPP‐4) inhibitor and pioglitazone on urinary albumin levels have shown a decrease in urinary ACR after 24 weeks.
24
, 
25
 Therefore, the primary endpoints were change from baseline in urinary ACR and urinary L‐FABP after 24 weeks. The secondary endpoints were change from baseline in serum VEGF, plasma adrenomedullin (AM), serum ANGPTL2, and serum ANGPTL4, and change from baseline in urinary neutrophil gelatinase‐associated lipocalin (NGAL) and urinary kidney injury molecule‐1 (KIM‐1) after 6 months.

Data were collected at 0, 4, 8, 12, 18, and 24 weeks. Venous blood samples and urinary samples were taken in the morning after an overnight fast. Blood samples were collected for measurement of total protein, albumin, total bilirubin, aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), creatinine, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), calcium, sodium, chloride, low density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL‐C), high density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL‐C), triglyceride (TG), uric acid (UA), C‐reactive protein, fasting plasma glucose, HbA1c, ketone body, VEGF, ANGPTL2, ANGPTL4, and AM. Urine was collected for measurement of ACR, L‐FABP, NGAL, KIM‐1. VEGF, ANGPTL2, and ANGPTL4 were measured by ELISA. ANGPTL2 was measured by Human ANGPTL2 Assey Kit (IBL) (sensitivity; 0.01 <ng/mL, specificity; 100%). ANGPTL4 was measured by Human ANGPTL2 Assey Kit (IBL) (sensitivity; 17.53 <pg/mL, specificity; 100%). VEGF was measured by Quantikine ELISA Human VEGF Immunoassey (sensitivity; 9.0 < pg/mL, specificity; 100%). AM was measured by immunoradiometric assay (mature AM: sensitivity; 0.5 <pmol/L specificity; 100%, total AM; 0.5 <pmol/L, specificity; 100%).
26
, 
27
 The measurements were outsourced to SRL (Japan). Vital parameters, such as body weight, abdominal circumference, systolic blood pressure, and diastolic blood pressure, were collected by standard methods.

Since there are no data evaluating the effect of SGLT2 inhibitor on urinary ACR and urinary L‐FABP, the treatment effect was estimated from the results of studies using drugs containing DPP‐4 inhibitors or RAS inhibitors.
24
, 
28
, 
29
, 
30
, 
31
 Change from baseline at week 24 in the logarithm of the urine ACR (mean ± SD) was 0.6 ± 0.7 for eGFR ≥60 and 0.75 ± 0.7 for eGFR <60 (14–16). At a two‐sided significance level of 5% and a power of 80%, 22 participants per group with eGFR ≥60 and 14 participants per group with eGFR <60 would be required. With an expected dropout rate of approximately 10%, the sample size for this study was set at 40 participants in a group (80 participants overall). This sample size also ensures power for L‐FABP differences (mean ± SD; 1.7 ± 1.7).
30
, 
31

Background characteristics were summarised as mean and standard deviation for continuous values and as number of participants and percentage for categorical values. The primary endpoints and secondary endpoints were analysed using a mixed model for repeated measures adjusted for eGFR, an allocation adjustment factor. Fixed factors were treatment arm (Empagliflozin group or Placebo group), time (visit), and baseline eGFR (below 60 mL/min/1.73 m2 or not). Random factors were patient. Interaction was treatment arm X time. For adverse events (AEs), the number and rate of AEs and the number of occurrences of each adverse event were summarised. For the confirmatory analyses of the two primary endpoints at 24 weeks, the Bonferroni method was used as the multiplicity adjustment (α = 0.025, two‐sided). For other analyses conducted for supplementary or exploratory purposes, no multiplicity adjustment was performed (α = 0.05, two‐sided). All statistical analyses were performed using SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute Japan Ltd., Tokyo, Japan).

## 讨论 / Discussion

Previous clinical trials of SGLT2 inhibitor including empagliflozin demonstrated that SGLT2 inhibitor can prevent the progression of DKD.
2
, 
3
 In the present study, the decrease of ACR in empagliflozin tended to be greater than placebo but not significant in patients with early‐stage DKD. DAPA‐CKD showed that the effects of dapagliflozin in patients with Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcome (KDIGO) moderate risk tended to be small compared with KDIGO high risk.
32
 In EMPA‐KIDNEY trial, urinary ACR reduction in microalbuminuria was smaller than macroalbuminuria although relative reduction of eGFR slope in normoalbuminuria was greatest.
33
 These previous reports suggest that the effects of SGLT2 inhibitors on ACR reduction may be slightly small in the early stage of nephropathy compared with the advanced stage. Therefore, this study might not have enough statistical power. As other possible reasons, duration of follow‐up, importantly biological variability in ACR measurements due to only spot urine test used in the study, regression to the mean, biological adaptation, or compensatory mechanisms.

In this study, urinary L‐FABP excretion in both groups was significantly increased at 24 weeks, and there was no difference in change in urinary L‐FABP excretion between empagliflozin and placebo. Furthermore, there was no significant difference in change in urinary NGAL and KIM‐1 excretion in the present study. Thus, the present study did not show apparent protective effects of SGLT2 inhibitor on tubular injury in the early stage of DKD, although L‐FABP, NGAL, and KIM‐1 are indicators of acute kidney injury rather than chronic kidney disease. The intervention period of the present study was 24 weeks. In CANVAS trial,
34
 tublar injury marker reduced by canagliflozin treatment after 52 weeks whereas the effect of dapagliflozin on tubular injury marker reduction was limited after 6 weeks
35
 Therefore, the study period was not enough to demonstrate the effect of empagliflozin on tubular damage. Therefore, the study period was not enough to demonstrate the effect of empagliflozin on tubular damage.

Excess of angiogenic factors which are induced by hypoxia play a key role in the progression of diabetic nephropathy especially in early stage. In this study, VEGF and ANGPTL2 level were decreased by empagliflozin treatment compared with placebo. Previous basic research showed that excessive VEGF activity increases blood flow to and the permeability of the glomerulus, amplifies the inflammatory cascade and causes destruction of the epithelial cells, podocytes and mesangial cells.
36
, 
37
 In a rodent model, blockade of VEGF was reported to ameliorate the diabetic glomerular injury.
38
 On the other hand, a previous study showed that podocyte‐specific VEGF‐A excision in a diabetic mice model caused the heavy proteinuria and glomerular sclerosis.
39
 These previous reports suggested that too much or too little VEGF can lead to the diabetic glomerular injury. In the present study, VEGF level was not increased by empagliflozin whereas that was increased in the placebo group, suggesting that empagliflozin treatment might inhibit excess of VEGF. ANGPTL2 also contributes to the progression of diabetic nephropathy. Previous reports showed that excess of ANGPTL2 signalling was involved in the development of atherosclerosis via proinflammatory action.
40
 Indeed, clinically, serum ANGPTL2 level was associated with albuminuria in diabetes.
41
 Therefore, attenuation of VEGF and ANGPTL2 production may contribute to the reno‐protective effects of SGLT2 inhibitor. Adrenomedullin also reported to contribute to glomerular hyperfiltration and hypertrophy in the early stage of nephropathy in a rodent model,
42
, 
43
 and it has been reported that high circulating adrenomedullin level was associated with kidney function decline.
44
 In this study, there was no significant difference in the change of mature AM between empagliflozin and placebo group. AM was mainly expressed in distal tubules and collecting duct in the kidney.
45
 Protective effects of SGLT2 inhibitor might be small in renal medulla compared with renal cortex, since SGLT2 inhibitor is expressed in proximal tubules. Indeed, a previous experimental study showed that SGLT2 inhibition lowered O2 consumption due to the reduction of Na+ reabsorption in proximal tubule whereas O2 consumption in medulla was not reduced by SGLT2 inhibition.
46
 Taken together, AM induction might not be affected by SGLT2 inhibition. There was no significant difference in the change of ANGPTL4 between empagliflozin and placebo group. ANGPTL4 is also produced in adipose tissue, and fasting conditions promote the production of ANGPTL4.
47
 SGLT2 inhibitor, which promotes glycosuria, leading to significant caloric deficit and weight loss, mimics fasting conditions. In this study, ANGPTL4 level was evaluated in serum level, and therefore, it is possible that the blood ANGPTL4 concentration more strongly reflected that it was derived from adipose tissue due to the fasting‐like state produced by SGLT2 inhibition.

On the other hand, there was no significant association between change of urinary ACR and hypoxia markers. It has been reported that there are several mechanisms for the renal protective effect of SGLT2 other than prevention of hypoxic injury, such as promoting autophagy and attenuation of sympathetic hyperactivity.
19
 Thus, the effects of reducing hypoxic injury may only account for part of the renal protective effects of SGLT2 inhibitors.

In the present study, the effects of empagliflozin on ACR reduction in patients with low eGFR were greater than in patients with preserved eGFR. Typically, the first clinical sign of classical diabetic nephropathy is considered to be the development of microalbuminuria, which gradually leads to macroalbuminuria, decreasing GFR, and finally to end‐stage kidney disease. However, it is well known that low GFR is found in some normoalbuminuric and microalbuminuric diabetes patients.
48
 These suggest that some of DKD have the component of nephrosclerosis, which involves hyalinization and sclerosis of interlobular and afferent arterioles, as well as typical diabetic nephropathy. Nephrosclerosis involves hyalinization and sclerosis of interlobular and afferent arterioles; therefore, kidney ischemia plays a key role in the progression of kidney damage in nephrosclerosis. A previous study showed that vascular lesion rather than typical glomerular lesion existed in DKD without albuminuria.
45
 These suggest that hypoxia may be involved in DKD without albuminuria rather than typical diabetic nephropathy. Together with our results, it is suggested that empagliflozin may be more effective in DKD consisting of typical diabetic nephropathy and nephrosclerosis; that is, DKD with more involvement of hypoxic injury. Indeed, the EMPA‐kidney trial showed the CKD with normoalbuminuria had a greater effect of SGLT2 inhibitor on the GFR slope than CKD with albuminuria.
33

In conclusion, the present study demonstrated that empagliflozin tended to decrease ACR in DKD with microalbuminuria after 24 weeks, although empagliflozin significantly decreased ACR in DKD with microalbuminuria and eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m2. This study also demonstrated that empagliflozin treatment suppressed VEGF and ANGPTL2, which might at least partially contribute to the mechanisms of the reno‐protective effects of this agent.
