## English Abstract

Uncontrolled hypertension is common in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD), putting these patients at high risk for adverse cardiovascular events. Radiofrequency renal denervation (RDN) lowers blood pressure (BP) in patients with hypertension. The Global SYMPLICITY Registry DEFINE enrolled over 3500 patients with uncontrolled hypertension worldwide, including patients with moderate-to-severe CKD.

Patients were categorized by their baseline estimated glomerular filtration rate (estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR], mL/min per 1.73 m2): eGFR ≥60 (no-CKD, n=2539), eGFR ≥45 to <60 (stage 3a, n=496), eGFR ≥30 to <45 (stage 3b, n=256). BP changes from baseline through 3 years were compared between groups. One-year data for 74 CKD stage 4 patients were also assessed. Safety events and eGFR were reported at all time points.

Average baseline office systolic BP was similar across the 3 comparator groups (range: 162.8±26.4–164.7±24.8 mm Hg). At 3 years, office systolic BP reductions from baseline were −17.2±28.1 mm Hg, −12.1±30.3 mm Hg, and −13.0±27.3 mm Hg in the no-CKD, CKD 3a, and CKD 3b groups, respectively (all P<0.0001). A mild decline in eGFR through 1 year occurred in the no-CKD group but not in any CKD group. Three-year mortality was 4.3%, 7.0%, and 15.2% in no-CKD, CKD 3a, and CKD 3b groups, respectively. Adverse renal events were rare (<1.0%) across all 3 groups. Antihypertensive medication burden remained high in the no-CKD and CKD 3a groups but declined over 3 years in the CKD 3b group (P=0.007).

In patients with uncontrolled hypertension and moderate-to-severe CKD, radiofrequency RDN results in clinically meaningful and durable BP reduction without renal or vascular safety concerns.

## 方法 / Methods

The authors declare that all supporting data are available within the article (and its Supplemental Material).

The GSR DEFINE is a prospective, multicenter, single-arm, open-label, observational international registry that enrolled an all-comers population of patients with uncontrolled hypertension. Patients were enrolled from Asia, Australia, Canada, Europe, Latin America, the Middle East, New Zealand, and South Africa. The GSR DEFINE assessed the safety and efficacy of radiofrequency RDN in a real-world setting that included patients with renal insufficiency. Details of the study design and RDN treatment procedure have been previously published.18,28 All study centers obtained approval for the conduct of the study by their ethics committee, and written informed consent was obtained from all enrolled patients. The study was conducted according to the Declaration of Helsinki.

Patients underwent RDN with either the first-generation, single-electrode Symplicity Flex or the current-generation, 4-electrode Symplicity Spyral catheter (Medtronic, Santa Rosa, CA). Office and 24-hour ambulatory BP as well as eGFR were assessed at baseline before RDN and at 6- and 12-months postprocedure and annually thereafter. Prescribed antihypertensive medication classes were collected at baseline and each follow-up visit.

Major adverse events including all-cause mortality, new renal artery stenosis (>70% confirmed by angiography), renal artery reintervention (performed after completion of RDN procedure and removal of guide catheter), hospitalization for hypertensive crisis/hypertensive emergency (BP ≥180/110 mm Hg, together with progressive or impending target organ damage, requiring hospitalization not related to confirmed nonadherence with medications), and vascular complications (groin hematoma, arteriovenous fistula, pseudoaneurysm, or excessive bleeding) were also recorded at each follow-up visit. Protocol-defined adverse events were adjudicated independently by the Clinical Events Committee (Cardiovascular Research Foundation, NY).

All patients with available data were categorized into stages of CKD by their baseline eGFR: eGFR >15 to <30 mL/min per 1.73 m2 (CKD stage 4), eGFR ≥30 to <45 mL/min per 1.73 m2 (CKD stage 3b), eGFR ≥45 to <60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 (CKD stage 3a), and eGFR ≥60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 (no CKD). The eGFR for each patient was calculated using the Modification of Diet in Renal Disease formula (eGFR [mL/min per 1.73 m2]=175×[sCr]−1.154×[aged]−0.203×[0.742 if female]×[1.212 if African descent]).29 Baseline characteristics and office and 24-hour ambulatory BP and eGFR changes from baseline to 6 months, and 1, 2, and 3 years were compared between groups. Baseline characteristics, office, and 24-hour ambulatory BP and safety were also assessed through 1 year for patients with CKD stage 4 (eGFR <30 mL/min per 1.73m2, excluding dialysis patients). While not included in the statistical analysis, data for the smaller groups of patients with CKD stage 4 are also reported to provide the full set of available data across the CKD spectrum.

Continuous variables are reported as mean±SD, and categorial variables are reported as percentages. Comparisons of BP reductions were by ANCOVA adjusted for baseline BP and number of antihypertensive medication classes.

To evaluate the risk for all-cause death as a function of BP reduction, a Cox regression analysis was performed, adjusted for sex, age, baseline BP, and baseline eGFR. Groups were defined based on the median BP reduction for both office and 24-hour ambulatory systolic BP.

To account for repeated measures of eGFR over time, linear mixed models were run for the no CKD group and a group of all patients with CKD stage 3. The fixed effect covariates included in the models were baseline eGFR, age, sex, diabetes, history of cardiac disease, history of vascular disease, number of prescribed antihypertensive medications at baseline, and smoking history.

SAS for Windows 9.4 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC) was used for all statistical analyses.

## 结果 / Results

A total of 3294 patients were included in the current analysis: 2539 had an eGFR ≥60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 and were defined as the no-CKD group, 496 had CKD stage 3a (eGFR ≥45 to <60 mL/min/1.73 m2) and 256 had CKD stage 3b (eGFR ≥30 to <45 mL/min per 1.73 m2). Baseline characteristics by eGFR group are compared in Table 1. CKD stage 3b patients had a higher proportion of patients with diabetes and were on more antihypertensive drug classes compared with patients in the eGFR ≥45 mL/min per 1.73 m2 groups and had the highest average 24-hour ambulatory systolic BP.

Baseline Characteristics by CKD Stage

Office systolic and diastolic BPs were significantly lower through 3 years post-RDN in all 3 eGFR groups (Figure 1A and 1B). At 3 years, the average 24-hour ambulatory systolic BP was reduced significantly and to a similar extent in the stage 3a and no-CKD groups, whereas the ambulatory systolic BP fall in the smaller group of stage 3b patients did not reach statistical significance (Figure 1C). The 24-hour ambulatory diastolic BP was significantly lower through 3 years in all groups except for the 2-year diastolic BP reduction in the stage 3b group (Figure 1D). The average number of antihypertensive medications remained unchanged in the no CKD and stage 3a groups; however, there was a significant decline in the number of prescribed antihypertensive medications in the stage 3b group through 3 years (P=0.007; Figure 2).

The 24-h ambulatory systolic (C) and diastolic (D) blood pressure measures at 6 months, 1, 2, and 3 years were significantly lower than baseline in the no chronic kidney disease (CKD) and stage 3a groups. The mean number of antihypertensive medications prescribed was unchanged or slightly declined over 3 years of follow-up for the no chronic kidney disease (CKD) and stage 3a group, but there was a significant decline in the number of prescribed antihypertensive medications in the CKD stage 3b group (P=0.007).

Renal function in patients with CKD stage 3a and 3b remained stable through 1 year, followed by a nonsignificant decline in eGFR at 2 and 3 years (Figure 3). Among patients with an eGFR ≥60 mL/min per 1.73 m2, there was a significant decline in eGFR at 6 months (−3.2±14.6 mL/min per 1.73 m2; P<0.0001) followed by smaller incremental eGFR changes through 3 years of follow-up (Figure 3). After the first year, the decline in eGFR over time in patients with measures at all time points was similar between the groups (Figure S1).

Patients with an eGFR ≥60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 experienced a decline in eGFR through 1 year, whereas renal function was stable through 1 year for both stage 3a and 3b patients. After 1 year, the decline in eGFR was similar between the 3 groups. CKD indicates chronic kidney disease.

Analysis of the longitudinal eGFR data using linear mixed models confirmed that eGFR declines to some degree over time (Table S1). Among patients with CKD stage 3, baseline eGFR, diabetes, and the number of antihypertensive drugs at baseline were significant factors associated with lower eGFR measures, but only diabetes (eGFR change estimate of −2.47 mL/min per 1.73 m2) was associated with an eGFR absolute value change >1 mL/min per 1.73 m2.

Adverse events at 3 years are summarized in Table 2. Three-year rates of death were 4.3%, 7.0%, and 15.2% in the no CKD, stage 3a, and stage 3b groups, respectively. A Cox regression analysis found no significant difference in the risk for death among patients with CKD based on office or ambulatory BP change. Renal artery reintervention or new renal artery stenosis >70% were rare, and rates were similar across all groups (Table 2). Vascular complications were also uncommon and similar across the groups. New onset end-stage renal disease and mortality were highest in the stage 3b patients.

Diabetes and cardiac disease were more common in stage 4 patients than in patients with less severe CKD. At 1 year, the average office systolic and diastolic BP was reduced, whereas the ambulatory systolic and diastolic BP were relatively unchanged (Figure 4). The average eGFR of CKD stage 4 patients remained stable through 1 year (22.2±6.3 mL/min per 1.73 m² at baseline and 20.6±10.0 mL/min per 1.73 m² at 1 year). ## 讨论 / Discussion

This analysis of patients with moderate-to-severe CKD who underwent RDN demonstrates the continued safety and effectiveness of RDN through 3 years in patients with an eGFR of at least 30 mL/min per 1.73 m2 (moderate-to-severe CKD stage 3a/b), but possibly even lower (CKD stage 4). Indeed, among the smaller groups of patients with an eGFR <30 mL/min per 1.73 m2, there was no evidence of worsening renal function or increased risk of adverse events at 1-year post-RDN. The prescribed number of antihypertensive medications remained stable or decreased through 3 years in all groups.

Early work describing the interrelationships between sympathetic overdrive, worsening renal function, and difficult-to-control high BP led to a number of small studies and case reports in patients with later-stage CKD and end-stage renal disease.23–25,30–33 It was hypothesized that the reduction of sympathetic outflow from the kidneys following RDN would both decrease BP and slow the progression of CKD that is related to increased sympathetic activation. One of the first studies of radiofrequency RDN in 15 patients with resistant hypertension and concomitant moderate-to-severe CKD (eGFR <45 mL/min per 1.73 m2) reported significant office BP reductions with no decline in eGFR or creatinine clearance at 6 or 12 months follow-up.23 Two other single-center studies of similar moderate-to-severe patients with CKD also observed effective BP reduction and preserved renal function through 1 year25 and through 3 years34 after RDN. Furthermore, an assessment of relevant biomarkers of renal damage (neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin and KIM-1 [kidney injury molecule 1]) in 47 patients found no evidence of increased renal damage following RDN.34

Although several publications from the GSR have reported data on the safety and efficacy of RDN in patients with CKD, most have focused on grouped patients with an eGFR <60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 with no separately reported data on patients with an eGFR <45 mL/min per 1.73 m2.19,20,35 In the recent analysis comparing 475 patients with CKD stage 3 (mean eGFR, 46.9±10 mL/min per 1.73m2) with 1505 patients without CKD (baseline eGFR >60 mL/min per 1.73 m2), both groups had significant reductions in BP annually through 3 years with a greater reduction in office SBP at 3 years in the CKD stage 3 group after adjusting for baseline SBP (−17.3±28.3 versus −11.7±29.9 mm Hg; P=0.009). Between baseline and 1 year, there was a greater decline in eGFR in patients without CKD compared with patients with stage 3 CKD, but thereafter the changes in eGFR were similar between the groups and not considered clinically concerning.19 Furthermore, the authors considered the initial decline as likely attributable to regression-to-the-mean.19 The potential impact of regression-to-the-mean has been well described in an analysis of BP-lowering randomized clinical trials that assessed the clinical relevance of regression-to-the-mean for clinical trials.36

A recent study of over 4000 patients, including patients from GSR, investigated whether certain patient characteristics, including renal function, had a disproportionate effect on BP changes after RDN using a linear mixed model.37 Renal function, based on eGFR, appeared to have little to no effect on the efficacy of RDN in reducing patient BP.

Although the antihypertensive medications remained unchanged over time in the no CKD and stage 3a patients, a significant decrease in the prescribed number of antihypertensive medications was observed in the stage 3b group, which may have influenced the observed BP changes over time. Furthermore, nonadherence to prescribed antihypertensive medications among patients treated on clinical trials is well known17 which may contribute more to the variability in BP change seen in the smaller stage 3b and stage 4 groups.

This observation of a steeper decline in eGFR in the no CKD group in the first year, followed by much smaller incremental changes, is consistent with our current data. Given that there is an expected progressive decline in eGFR in patients with moderate-to-severe CKD and uncontrolled hypertension, it is unclear how radiofrequency RDN may affect renal function.38,39 A study of patients with 46 hypertensive CKD looked at the progression of CKD from 60 months before the RDN procedure and at 3, 6, 12, and 24 months post-RDN. Linear mixed model analysis of eGFR demonstrated a substantial decline in eGFR in the months preceding RDN, followed by a slight increase in eGFR at 3 months that remained stable thereafter to 24 months.5 The investigators noted that the BP-lowering effect of RDN appeared to be independent of the observed renal protective effect of RDN. While the mean eGFR at baseline in these patients was 46.2±13.0 mL/min per 1.73 m2, our current study also observed minimal changes in eGFR at 2 years post-RDN in patients with a mean eGFR of 38.7±4.1 mL/min per 1.73 m2.

Further analysis of the change in eGFR over time using linear mixed models confirms expected declines in eGFR in these patients with multiple risk factors for worsening renal function. One notable exception was the impact of diabetes on eGFR reductions, which is consistent with the known association of diabetes and risk for CKD.40

Among patients followed through 3 years, we observed higher rates of all-cause and cardiovascular mortality in the CKD stage 3b group. The increased risk for death in patients with worsening CKD is in line with the natural history of patients with CKD and hypertension.41–43 An analysis of CKD and risk of death or cardiovascular events reported all-cause mortality of 7.5% for stage 3a and 22.8% for stage 3b CKD patients with a median follow-up of 2.84 years. To put those numbers into perspective, the age-standardized rates of death were 10.8 per 1000 person-years for stage 3a and 47.6 per 1000 person-years for stage 3b. In an analysis of CKD progression and mortality in 5 European countries, mortality rates ranged from 7.5 in Belgium, 4.6 to 22.5 in Italy, and 27.1/1000 person-years in Spain.42 The United States Renal Data System database reports CKD stage 3 age-adjusted mortality of 48.6/1000 person-years, and CKD stage 4 age-adjusted mortality of 106.6/1000 person-years.44 There was no evidence of increased risk for renal artery damage after radiofrequency RDN in any of the CKD groups. At 3-year follow-up, rates of renal artery reinterventions, new renal artery stenosis, and vascular complications were low and similar between the patients with no CKD and stage 3 CKD.

Although this analysis did not raise any safety concerns with RDN in CKD stage 4 patients, there was substantial variability in office and 24-hour ambulatory SBP changes at 1-year post-RDN in this subgroup. Another concern about RDN for patients with severe or end-stage renal disease is the potential for renal vessel diameters to be smaller than the minimal device specifications. These issues were reported in a feasibility study of RDN in 12 patients with end-stage renal disease and uncontrolled BP. Four of the 12 patients were unable to receive RDN because of atrophic renal arteries (<4 mm), and the investigators noted the challenge in delivering ablations in cases of insufficient blood flow in some patients.32 These studies were performed with the single electrode catheters, and the latest technological advances with multielectrode catheters may perform differently in these settings. There were, however, substantial drops in office SBP in the 8 patients who were treated. This study also confirmed marked sympathetic activation at baseline in 5 patients who underwent muscle sympathetic nerve activity before RDN. Because minimal data exist, RDN is not guideline-recommended in patients with severe renal dysfunction. Therefore, baseline imaging seems important to confirm the suitability of the renal arteries in cases where this alternative treatment is considered.

Common to observational registries, there is an incomplete 3-year follow-up, and there was often no information about reasons for patients to discontinue participation in the registry. In particular, there was a high proportion of missing 24-hour ambulatory data over time in patients with CKD, which limits our understanding of these data in the small stage 4 and 5 groups. The GSR DEFINE registry also does not include follow-up data on microalbuminuria/proteinuria or albumin-to-creatinine ratio, and the duration of dialysis treatments before enrollment in the registry is also unknown.

Radiofrequency RDN provided a clinically meaningful reduction in office and 24-hour ambulatory SBP in patients with CKD stage 3a and 3b without any increase in adverse renal events through 3 years. Among the CKD stage 3a and 3b patients, changes in eGFR were not seen for 2 years, and later declines in eGFR were consistent with the natural progression of CKD. At 1 year, there was no evidence of adverse renal effects in CKD stage 4 patients, but there is limited data to support routine consideration of RDN in this population. Our data suggest that RDN is likely to be safe and may represent an effective means of lowering BP in patients with moderate-to-severe CKD in whom other options have failed to adequately control BP.
