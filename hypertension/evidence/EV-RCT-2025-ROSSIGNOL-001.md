## English Abstract

Hypertension is a cause and consequence of chronic kidney disease (CKD). Resistant hypertension is common and often difficult to control in patients with CKD. This post hoc analysis evaluated the efficacy and safety of aprocitentan (with standardized background antihypertensive therapy) in patients with CKD and resistant hypertension, a group with high morbidity and mortality risk and limited treatment options.

The PRECISION study (Parallel-Group, Phase 3 Study With Aprocitentan in Subjects With Resistant Hypertension) consisted of part 1: 4-week, double-blind (aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg versus placebo); part 2: 32-week, single-blind (aprocitentan 25 mg); and part 3: 12-week, double-blind withdrawal (aprocitentan 25 mg versus placebo). In participants with CKD, aprocitentan’s effect on blood pressure (BP), urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio, and safety was evaluated.

Of 730 participants in PRECISION, 147 had CKD categorized as KDIGO high risk or very high risk. At week 4, aprocitentan 12.5 mg, aprocitentan 25 mg, and placebo reduced office systolic BP by −13.5, −16.6, and −4.4 mm Hg, respectively; this was maintained with aprocitentan 25 mg to week 36 (−16.4 mm Hg). At week 4, reductions in nighttime ambulatory systolic BP were −9.6, −13.8, and −2.5 mm Hg, respectively. Changes in urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio were −47.1%, −59.6%, and −2.4%, respectively, maintained with aprocitentan 25 mg to week 36 (−61.6%). Aprocitentan was generally well tolerated (no change in potassium or estimated glomerular filtration rate); early peripheral edema was the most common adverse event.

Aprocitentan was well tolerated; efficiently lowered BP, particularly nighttime ambulatory BP; and markedly reduced urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio in participants with CKD and resistant hypertension. Aprocitentan may confer considerable cardiovascular and kidney-protective benefits in these difficult-to-treat patients.

## 方法 / Methods

The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding authors upon reasonable request.

The phase 3 PRECISION trial investigated the efficacy and safety of aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg in participants with confirmed RHT.25 The study design has been described previously (Figure S1).25,28 Eligibility criteria included a sitting office SBP at trough (SiSBP) ≥140 mm Hg (unattended automated office BP), an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ≥15 mL/min per 1.73 m2, and NT-proBNP (N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide) <500 pg/mL.25,28

During screening, participants were switched from their existing antihypertensive medications (except β-blockers) to fixed-dose, triple-combination SBAT, consisting of amlodipine 5 or 10 mg, valsartan 160 mg, and hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg, which was maintained throughout the study. Participants on other diuretics at screening, including loop diuretics, had to stop these drugs and switch to hydrochlorothiazide as part of SBAT. Adherence to SBAT was ≈98% for the overall study population during the entire study.29

The active treatment phase of PRECISION consisted of 3 parts: part 1: double-blind aprocitentan 12.5 mg, aprocitentan 25 mg, or placebo to week 4; part 2: single-blind aprocitentan 25 mg to week 36; part 3: double-blind withdrawal (DB-WD) to week 48, after rerandomization at week 36 (withdrawal baseline) to placebo or aprocitentan 25 mg. A 30-day safety follow-up period completed the study (Figure S1).

Participants categorized as KDIGO ≥high risk15 included participants with eGFR ≥15 to <45 mL/min per 1.73m2, eGFR ≥45 to <60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 and urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) ≥30 mg/g, and eGFR ≥60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 and UACR >300 mg/g. Participants were also categorized by CKD stages 3 and 4 (CKD stage 3a/b: eGFR ≥30 to <60 mL/min per 1.73 m2; CKD stage 4: eGFR ≥15 to <30 mL/min per 1.73 m2) and by albuminuria levels at baseline (normal: UACR <30 mg/g; microalbuminuria: UACR ≥30 to ≤300 mg/g; and macroalbuminuria: UACR >300 mg/g). Differences in the composition of the subgroups with kidney impairment are illustrated in Figure S2.

The mean change from baseline or withdrawal baseline in unattended SiSBP and sitting office diastolic BP at trough (SiDBP) over time, the mean change from baseline to week 4 and from withdrawal baseline to week 40 in daytime and nighttime ambulatory SBP and diastolic BP (DBP), and the percentage change in UACR (measured on spot urine samples at trough) from baseline or withdrawal baseline over time were assessed. Changes in eGFR were measured from baseline to weeks 4 and 36 and from the withdrawal baseline to weeks 40 and 48 (acute changes) and between weeks 6 and 36 when all participants received 25 mg aprocitentan (chronic slope).30

The correlation between changes in SiSBP and UACR was assessed at weeks 4 and 36 (Figure S3). The correlation coefficient R, a value between −1 and 1, measures the strength of the linear relationship between the 2 quantitative variables, namely, SiSBP and UACR in our analysis. A value of R close to 1 would indicate a strong positive correlation.

Safety outcomes included all treatment-emergent adverse events, defined using the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Dictionaries (version 24.1). Bodyweight and laboratory measurements, including serum NT-proBNP, potassium, sodium, and hemoglobin, were assessed at baseline, weeks 4 and 36, withdrawal baseline, and weeks 40 and 48. Percent changes in estimated plasma volume were assessed by the Strauss formula, between baseline and week 4, and weeks 36 and 40.31,32

SiSBP, SiDBP, and UACR (log transformed) were analyzed using a mixed model with factors for treatment group, visit, and treatment by visit interaction, covariates for baseline or withdrawal baseline SBP/DBP (or log UACR), and the interaction between baseline or withdrawal baseline and visit (for further details, see the study by Schlaich et al25 and the study by Danaietash et al28). Ambulatory SBP and DBP were assessed by ANCOVA with a factor for treatment group and a covariate for baseline (or withdrawal baseline) 24-hour mean SBP/DBP. Descriptive statistics are reported as mean (SD) or mean (SE), or geometric mean ratio/ mean percent change (95% CI) for quantitative variables. Frequencies and percentages were used for qualitative data. For the generation of the Forest plots, the mixed model for the main analyses (SiSBP, daytime/nighttime SBP, and UACR) was extended with factors for the subgroup and treatment by subgroup interaction. Placebo-corrected least square means with 95% CIs were used for inference without multiplicity adjustment. All analyses were post hoc and exploratory.

## 结果 / Results

Of the 730 participants with RHT enrolled in PRECISION, 147 (20.1%) were categorized as KDIGO ≥high risk (Figure S4). Compared with the non–high-risk KDIGO group (n=583), participants with KDIGO ≥high risk were older (66.1 versus 60.6 years), more likely to be male (70.7% versus 56.6%), and Black (17.0% versus 9.8%), on ≥4 antihypertensive medications (71.4% versus 60.9%), including β-blockers (72.8% versus 59.9%), and were taken off loop diuretics at screening (20.4% versus 13.2%; Table S1). The KDIGO ≥high risk and the non–high-risk KDIGO groups differed in comorbidities at baseline, including diabetes (79.6% versus 47.7%), congestive heart failure (12.9% versus 21.3%), and sleep apnea syndrome (20.4% versus 12.5%; Table S1). Mean baseline BPs (SiSBP, SiDBP, and ambulatory SBP and DBP) were similar in both KDIGO groups (Table S1).

Participants with KDIGO ≥high risk were equally distributed across the treatment arms (49, 52, and 46 participants received aprocitentan 12.5 mg, aprocitentan 25 mg, and placebo, respectively), and baseline demographics, disease characteristics, and medical history were well balanced (Table S1).

Aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg in addition to SBAT lowered SiSBP and SiDBP in participants with KDIGO ≥high risk. From baseline to week 4 (part 1), aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg reduced mean SiSBP (SE) by −13.5 (2.3) and −16.6 (1.9) mm Hg, respectively, compared with −4.4 (2.9) mm Hg with placebo (Figure 1). At week 36, the reduction in SiSBP was −16.4 (1.9) mm Hg versus baseline. From the DB-WD baseline (week 36) to week 40, SiSBP increased by 5.5 (2.7) mm Hg in participants rerandomized to placebo compared with −0.8 (2.0) mm Hg in participants continuing aprocitentan 25 mg (Figure 1). Similar changes over time were observed when assessing SiDBP (Figure S5A).

The correlation coefficients for changes in SiSBP versus changes in UACR from baseline to week 4 across the 3 treatment groups (R range, 0.12–0.32) and from baseline to week 36 for aprocitentan 25 mg (R=0.29) were all considerably <1 (Figure S3).

Baseline eGFR was 48.3 to 50.7 mL/min per 1.73 m2 across the treatment arms in participants with KDIGO ≥high risk. Changes in mean eGFR (SD) from baseline to week 4 were 0.5 (8.1), −2.5 (8.4), and −0.4 (8.2) mL/min per 1.73 m2 with aprocitentan 12.5 mg, aprocitentan 25 mg, and placebo, respectively (Table S2). In part 3, there were minimal changes in mean eGFR from the DB-WD baseline to week 48 with aprocitentan 25 mg and placebo (−1.0 [9.4] and −1.1 [9.1] mL/min per 1.73 m2, respectively; Table S2). From weeks 6 to 36 (aprocitentan 25 mg), the least square mean (95% CI) of the chronic eGFR slope was −0.9 (−4.0 to 2.2) mL/min per 1.73 m2/y.

The efficacy of aprocitentan (12.5 and 25 mg) on BP and UACR was similar across all subgroups with kidney impairment (KDIGO ≥high risk, CKD stages 3 or 4, or microalbuminuria or macroalbuminuria: Figures 1–4, Figures S5–S8; Tables S3 and S4 list baseline demographics and disease characteristics for participants with CKD stages 3 and 4, and microalbuminuria and macroalbuminuria, respectively). In participants with macroalbuminuria, aprocitentan had the most pronounced effect in lowering mean UACR (95% CI): up to −63% (−72 to −53) with aprocitentan 25 mg during part 1 and −67% (−75 to −55) during part 2 (Figure S7B). Aprocitentan had similar or greater efficacy in participants with kidney impairment compared with the overall phase 3 population (Figure 4).

Aprocitentan was well tolerated in participants with KDIGO ≥high risk (Table S5). Edema was the most common adverse event with aprocitentan; almost all edema events were mild or moderate peripheral leg edema. They were effectively managed with or without additional diuretic therapy (Table S6). Three participants (2.1%) discontinued due to edema during the 48 weeks of the study (there were no discontinuations during PRECISION in participants randomized to aprocitentan 12.5 mg in part 1). Of participants with KDIGO ≥high risk who received aprocitentan (any dose) at any time during PRECISION (N=145), 37.9% (n=55) had at least 1 edema event during the study (Table S6). This compares with 25.4% (n=184 of 724) in the overall phase 3 population. The incidence of new or recurrent edema events measured in 4-week time intervals was 2.0% before randomization and 16.3% and 15.1% with aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg, respectively, versus 4.4% with placebo up to week 4 (placebo not shown; Figure S9). By weeks 12 to 16 (all participants receiving aprocitentan 25 mg), incidence rates returned to prerandomization rates and remained low for the remainder of the trial. Of participants with KDIGO ≥high risk who received aprocitentan (any dose) at any time during PRECISION, 29% (n=42 of 145) were switched from loop diuretics or >1 diuretic to hydrochlorothiazide as part of SBAT before randomization. The incidence of edema was higher among those participants who were switched from a more potent diuretic treatment to hydrochlorothiazide (55%; n=23 of 42; Table S6) compared with 31% (n=32 of 103) among those who did not decrease their diuretics before randomization.

Eleven participants had heart failure events: none were considered to be related to study treatment, and none led to discontinuation of the study. Of the 11 participants with heart failure events, 7 required hospitalization (part 1: n=2 receiving aprocitentan 25 mg; part 2: n=4; follow-up to part 3: n=1 receiving placebo [15 weeks after switching from aprocitentan]). Besides KDIGO ≥high risk, these 7 participants had additional risk factors for heart failure, including a medical history of diabetes (all n=7) and a medical history of heart failure (n=3). Of the 4 participants without a medical history of heart failure, 2 had elevated NT-proBNP levels at screening (385 and 1124 pg/mL, respectively). All 7 participants received β-blockers.28 Four of the 7 participants were switched from a more potent diuretic at screening to hydrochlorothiazide as part of SBAT. All 7 responded to treatment with loop diuretics and cases resolved.

There were no excess headaches, no orthostatic hypotension, and no tachycardia. No acute kidney injuries (defined based on the study by Kellum and Lameire33) occurred during PRECISION.

Potassium and sodium levels were stable throughout the study (Tables S7 and S8).

Baseline NT-proBNP levels ranged from 15.4 to 20.6 pmol/L across the treatment arms. NT-proBNP remained unchanged with aprocitentan 12.5 mg, increased by 10% with aprocitentan 25 mg, and decreased by 10% with placebo during part 1 (geometric mean ratio [95% CI] from baseline to week 4: aprocitentan 12.5 mg: 1.0 [0.8 to 1.2]; aprocitentan 25 mg: 1.1 [0.9 to 1.2]; and placebo: 0.9 [0.7 to 1.1]). NT-proBNP levels were ≈20% higher than baseline at week 36 (aprocitentan 25 mg: 1.2 [1.0 to 1.4]). From DB-WD baseline to week 40, NT-proBNP levels remained stable with aprocitentan 25 mg (1.0 [0.9 to 1.1]) and placebo (0.9 [0.8 to 1.1]; Table S9).

Hemoglobin concentrations ranged from 131.0 to 132.0 g/L at baseline (Table S10). Levels decreased by ≈8% with aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg during part 1 (baseline to week 4; mean [SD]: −10.2 [8.9] and −10.3 [6.8] g/L, respectively, versus −2.0 [7.1] g/L with placebo), stabilized during part 2 (baseline to week 36: −11.8 [8.6] g/L) and reversed upon withdrawal (DB-WD baseline to week 40: aprocitentan 25 mg: 0.0 [6.3] g/L; placebo: 9.2 [10.0] g/L; Table S10). Mean percentage changes in estimated plasma volume (SE) during part 1 were 14.6% (2.0) and 13.7% (1.4) with aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg, respectively (versus 3.2% [1.5] with placebo), and reversed upon withdrawal (aprocitentan 25 mg: 0.9% [1.2]; placebo: −9.6% [1.4]; Table S11).

The mean bodyweight at baseline ranged from 94.0 to 98.2 kg (Table S12). Transient minimal increases in mean bodyweight (SD) with aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg during part 1 (0.7 [1.5] and 0.6 [1.7] kg, respectively, versus −0.1 [1.8] kg with placebo), reversed with aprocitentan 25 mg during part 2 (−1.0 [3.3] kg). In part 3, the mean bodyweight decreased with aprocitentan 25 mg by −0.5 (2.1) kg and with placebo by −1.2 (1.6) kg from the DB-WD baseline to week 40 (Table S12).

## 讨论 / Discussion

This post hoc analysis of PRECISION in subgroups with kidney impairment and RHT showed that aprocitentan (in addition to triple-combination SBAT of amlodipine, valsartan, and hydrochlorothiazide; with or without β-blockers) was generally well tolerated (with edema being the most common adverse event) and efficiently lowered office and ambulatory SBP and DBP, with the effect on nighttime ambulatory SBP and DBP being especially pronounced. Aprocitentan also markedly reduced UACR (up to −67%) without reducing eGFR. The analysis focused on participants categorized as KDIGO ≥high risk with confirmatory results in participants with CKD stages 3 or 4 and participants with microalbuminuria or macroalbuminuria.

Consistent with previous reports,9,34,35 participants with KDIGO ≥high risk were older and a higher proportion of them were Black compared with other participants in the PRECISION study (non–high-risk KDIGO). The proportion of patients with diabetes was also considerably higher than in the non–high-risk KDIGO group.

Meta-analyses of individual participant-level data have demonstrated that a 5 mm Hg reduction of SBP denotes a 10% risk reduction in major cardiovascular events in patients with hypertension.36,37 In patients with a higher baseline risk, such as patients with CKD and RHT, a similar reduction of SBP may produce an even greater risk reduction.37 In participants with KDIGO ≥high risk, aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg reduced SiSBP by −13.5 and −16.6 mm Hg, respectively, at week 4, a decrease that was maintained with aprocitentan 25 mg to week 36 (−16.4 mm Hg versus baseline). Aprocitentan had a similar efficacy in lowering SiSBP across all subgroups with kidney impairment and RHT. Therefore, aprocitentan may significantly lower the cardiovascular risk and may present an attractive therapeutic option in these difficult-to-control, high-risk patients.38–40

Ambulatory BP monitoring is considered the gold standard among BP measurement modalities. It provides the most accurate prediction of cardiovascular outcomes compared with office BP measurements.16 Aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg showed a dose-dependent effect on nighttime ambulatory SBP and DBP, with reductions in nighttime ambulatory SBP from baseline to week 4 of −9.6 and −13.8 mm Hg, respectively, versus −2.5 mm Hg with placebo. Especially nighttime BP has a high predictive value for the risk of all-cause mortality, cardiovascular death, and kidney function decline.12–14,41,42 Aprocitentan’s nighttime BP-lowering effect is the most pronounced compared with its daytime effect underpinning its therapeutic benefit in these difficult-to-treat patients.43 The strong effect on nighttime BP may be due to sympatholytic effects of dual ERAs such as aprocitentan44 and aprocitentan’s long half-life (≈44 hours).45

Increased UACR levels are associated with poor long-term cardiovascular outcomes and kidney function decline.15,46 Reduction in albuminuria levels correlates with the degree of long-term kidney protection and a lower risk of cardiovascular disease.46 A >30% reduction in UACR has been recognized as a valid treatment surrogate for slowing CKD progression.47 Aprocitentan had a marked antiproteinuric effect in all subgroups with kidney impairment, reducing UACR up to −63% at week 4 and up to −67% at week 36. These reductions in UACR without changes in eGFR suggest a robust kidney-protective effect of aprocitentan.

The correlation analyses show that the significant reductions in BP with aprocitentan do not solely explain the observed reductions in UACR, suggesting that additional mechanisms are involved in aprocitentan’s kidney-protective effect, such as a decrease in glomerular permeability, postglomerular vasodilation, or functional tubular changes.48

Aprocitentan was generally well tolerated in participants with CKD and RHT. The safety data of our study are in line with those of the phase 3 PRECISION study25 without any additional safety signals. The most frequent adverse event with aprocitentan in the KDIGO ≥high-risk group was edema, with almost all events being of mild or moderate severity and the most common event being peripheral (leg) edema. Edema events were clinically manageable with additional diuretic therapy and lowering the dose of amlodipine. Edema events are a known side effect of ERAs.49 In addition, CKD is a risk factor for edema: compared with the general population, patients with kidney dysfunction are more prone to edema events due to their tendency to retain sodium and fluid with advancing kidney disease.49 Accordingly, a higher proportion of participants with KDIGO ≥high risk had edema events compared with the overall phase 3 population (37.9% versus 25.4%) during the 48 weeks of PRECISION. Importantly, after a transient increase in the risk of edema postrandomization to aprocitentan, the incidence of edema events returned to prerandomization rates (after ≈week 12) and remained low until study completion.

As part of the PRECISION study design, 8 weeks before randomization, participants were taken off their existing antihypertensive medication, including diuretics, and switched to SBAT, including the diuretic hydrochlorothiazide, which is known to have decreased efficacy with increasing severity of kidney impairment. In the KDIGO ≥high risk group, edema events occurred at a higher frequency (55%) in patients who were switched from a more potent diuretic therapy (loop diuretics or >1 diuretic) to hydrochlorothiazide compared with those who did not decrease their diuretic therapy (31%). Adequate control of fluid homeostasis is important in the context of treatment with ERAs, particularly in patients with type 2 diabetes and those with a history of heart failure and kidney impairment. This can be achieved by diuretic therapy with thiazide and thiazide-like diuretics, such as hydrochlorothiazide and indapamide, or loop diuretics where required (eGFR <30 mL/min per 1.73 m2).16,50 Besides the continuation of loop diuretics, the European Society of Cardiology guidelines recommend indapamide, a thiazide-like diuretic, shown to be effective in patients with CKD stages 3 and 4.16 The Summary of Product Characteristics specifies that in patients treated with loop diuretics, the loop diuretic should not be switched to a less effective diuretic at initiation of aprocitentan.27 Together, these findings and recommendations highlight the need for ensuring appropriate and optimal diuretic therapy for each patient before initiating aprocitentan in real-world clinical practice. Based on our clinical experience and data from the PRECISION trial, including data of participants with kidney impairment presented in this study, we also recommend ensuring a euvolemic state before initiation of aprocitentan. Finally, we suggest close monitoring of patients initiating aprocitentan, including consideration of the need for enhanced diuretic therapy if this is warranted.

Of the 7 participants with KDIGO ≥high risk who were hospitalized for heart failure events during PRECISION, all had a medical history of diabetes and either a medical history of heart failure or features, suggesting undiagnosed heart failure. All heart failure events resolved following treatment with loop diuretics, and none of these participants discontinued the study. The incidence of heart failure in this population is higher than in the overall phase 3 population25: participants with KDIGO ≥high risk represent ≈20% of the overall PRECISION population and contribute to the majority of hospitalizations due to heart failure (7 of 11 cases). However, study design features (such as decreased diuretic treatment when switching to SBAT before randomization) are obvious contributing factors.

As expected with ERAs, aprocitentan decreased hemoglobin concentrations (≈8%) and increased estimated plasma volume (≈14%) in participants with KDIGO ≥high risk. Both effects were reversible upon aprocitentan withdrawal (part 3).18,19 Interestingly, effects with aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg were similar, suggesting that the plasma volume expansion plateaued between these 2 doses, similar to the edema incidence. Reporting of anemia occurred more frequently in participants with KDIGO ≥high risk compared with the overall phase 3 population, in particular during part 1 of the study. This is not unexpected, as subjects with reduced kidney function typically have a lower baseline level of hemoglobin.51,52 Therefore, a mild decrease in the concentration of hemoglobin may trigger the reporting of an anemia event. Other measures, including NT-proBNP, potassium, sodium, and bodyweight, were only slightly or not affected. Baseline NT-proBNP was within expected levels for patients with CKD.53–55 A maximum change from baseline of 20% in NT-proBNP at week 36, after 32 weeks of aprocitentan 25 mg, and NT-proBNP concentrations remaining stable in both groups during part 3, is a reassuring safety observation. While measurement of NT-proBNP is currently not considered standard care in this patient population, thorough clinical assessment of volume status is recommended. Findings with parameters including bodyweight, hemoglobin, and NT-proBNP were compatible with the changes seen in plasma volume estimated by the Strauss formula. Formula-based estimates of plasma volume have been proven to provide prognostic value for clinical outcomes31,56–58; however, caution is advised in the interpretation of these estimates as a recent report in patients with clinically stable chronic heart failure demonstrated only limited reliability compared with quantitative volume measurements.59

Aprocitentan does not aggravate the risk of hyperkalemia,26,27 and there is no need for monitoring potassium levels apart from common clinical practice. In contrast, the use of other antihypertensives, including angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers, direct renin inhibitors, or MRA, can increase the risk of hyperkalemia, especially in patients with CKD.1 Indeed, a major concern with MRAs in advanced CKD is the risk of hyperkalemia and further reduction in eGFR. For example, spironolactone is not recommended in patients with an eGFR <45 mL/min per 1.73 m2 because of its increased risks of hyperkalemia and kidney function decline.10,15 While MRAs may often still be beneficial in these patients, close clinic and laboratory monitoring is required. Aprocitentan has no major impact on electrolytes and does not adversely affect eGFR aside from potential hemodynamic effects mediated by its BP-lowering capacity. Aprocitentan may represent the preferred choice in patients with uncontrolled hypertension and advanced CKD to improve BP control and reduce proteinuria if present.

CKD and RHT are both linked to an increased production of ET-1 and activation of the ET system. Aprocitentan as a dual ERA (ETA/ETB) was specifically selected for clinical development in difficult-to-treat hypertension.20 It is conceivable that treatment with aprocitentan may elicit different effects compared with ETA-selective ERAs.60–62 There is indirect evidence from clinical studies that compared with dual ERAs, ETA-selective ERAs may be associated with a higher risk of fluid retention and heart failure, and increases in NT-proBNP.23,52,63–65 However, we are not aware of any published head-to-head studies assessing dual ERAs versus ETA-selective ERAs in either hypertension or CKD,66 and data from ETA-selective ERAs cannot be extrapolated to dual ERAs or vice versa. In addition to receptor selectivity, the specific chemical and pharmacological properties of a drug need to be considered for the interpretation of differences in clinical outcomes.

Limitations of this analysis include its post hoc nature and the small sample size of the treatment arms in the KDIGO ≥high-risk group. Patients with NT-proBNP ≥500 pg/mL were excluded from the PRECISION study as per study protocol.28 Long-term data (beyond the 48 weeks of PRECISION) are needed to conclusively assess the net benefit-risk of aprocitentan in patients with CKD and RHT; this is of particular importance for the assessment of effects on eGFR trajectories and CKD progression. As per the nature of this post hoc study, the analyses were not powered for evaluating statistical significance and should be interpreted descriptively. Strengths of the study include the 3-part study design of PRECISION, allowing assessment of aprocitentan’s efficacy (part 1) and the sustainability of its efficacy (parts 2 and 3). The similar results across the different subgroups with kidney impairment underpin aprocitentan’s efficacy in lowering BP and UACR, suggesting a pronounced cardiovascular and kidney-protective benefit.
