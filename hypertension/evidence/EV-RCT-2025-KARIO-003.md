## English Abstract

Nocturnal blood pressure (BP) is superior to daytime BP in predicting cardiovascular mortality. It is of clinical relevance to evaluate the effect of esaxerenone combination therapy on nighttime BP in patients with uncontrolled hypertension.

A post hoc analysis of a multicenter, open-label, phase 3 study evaluated the nighttime BP-lowering effects of esaxerenone as monotherapy or in combination with a calcium channel blocker (CCB) or renin–angiotensin system inhibitor (RASi) in Japanese patients with uncontrolled hypertension. In addition, the effect on N-terminal pro B-type natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP), a known biomarker of cardiovascular disease prognosis, was evaluated.

In total, 270 patients were included in this analysis (172, 49, and 49 patients in the monotherapy, CCB, and RASi groups, respectively). The 24-hour ambulatory systolic BP reduction from baseline was − 10.0, − 6.0, and − 17.0 mm Hg in the monotherapy, CCB, and RASi groups, respectively. Nighttime systolic BP decreased significantly from baseline to week 28 in all groups (P < 0.001 each); the RASi group showed the greatest change (− 20.6 mm Hg). NT-proBNP decreased significantly in all three treatment groups.

Esaxerenone when used in combination with a CCB or RASi, but especially when used in combination with a RASi, may be a useful treatment option for patients with uncontrolled hypertension to control nocturnal BP.

## 方法 / Methods

This was a post hoc analysis of a multicenter, open-label, phase 3 study of esaxerenone in patients with essential hypertension.15 The study protocol was approved by the institutional review boards of all participating centers. The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and Good Clinical Practice. All participants provided written informed consent. The study was registered at ClinicalTrials.gov under the identifier NCT02722265.

The study was conducted over a 4-week observation period, followed by two treatment periods (from baseline to week 12 and from week 12 until week 28 or 52). During the first treatment period, eligible patients were allocated to three groups and received treatment accordingly: esaxerenone monotherapy, esaxerenone plus a RASi, or esaxerenone plus a CCB. Patients received esaxerenone with a starting dose of 2.5 mg/day, which was increased to 5 mg/day if required to achieve BP targets as monotherapy or in combination with a CCB or RASi. In the second treatment period (after 12 weeks of treatment), dose adjustments were made, or an additional antihypertensive agent was added as necessary to achieve the target BP. The total treatment period was 28 or 52 weeks. Further details of the study treatment have been published previously.15

Key inclusion criteria were age ≥ 20 years, previously untreated essential hypertension or previous treatment with only one RASi or CCB at the start of the observation period, sitting systolic BP (SBP) of 140 to < 180 mmHg and a sitting diastolic BP (DBP) of 90 to < 110 mmHg, 24-hour ambulatory BP of ≥ 130/80 mmHg, and estimated glomerular filtration rate of ≥ 60 mL/min/1.73 m2. BP criteria were set per the 2014 Japanese Society of Hypertension (JSH) guidelines which were the latest Japanese guidelines at the time of study planning. In the guidelines, mild hypertension corresponds to grade I hypertension, with a lower BP limit of 140 mmHg for SBP and 90 mmHg for DBP, while moderate hypertension corresponds to grade II hypertension, with an upper BP limit of 179 mmHg for SBP and 109 mmHg for DBP. Furthermore, hypertension was defined using a 24-hour ABPM mean SBP ≥ 130 mmHg and DBP ≥ 80 mmHg, and this criterion was used as a reference for the selection process. Of note, the JSH 2014 guidelines do not provide grade classifications based on ABPM measurements.

Key exclusion criteria were secondary hypertension, orthostatic hypotension, or cardiovascular disease or intervention within the previous 6 months; cerebrovascular disease within the previous year; serum potassium level < 3.5 or ≥ 5.1 mEq/L; diabetic nephropathy, type 1 diabetes, or type 2 diabetes with albuminuria; glycated hemoglobin ≥ 8.4%; or estimated glomerular filtration rate < 60 mL/min/1.72 m2 on clinical examination during the observation period.

For this post hoc analysis, all three treatment groups from the primary study were integrated into a single group and data for up to 28 weeks were used for analysis.

The outcomes evaluated in this post hoc analysis were sitting SBP (nighttime/daytime) at week 28 and change from baseline, 24-hour average SBP at week 28 and change from baseline, change in N-terminal pro B-type natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP) from baseline to week 28, and change in dipping pattern distribution from baseline to week 28.

Sitting BP was measured at week 3 and week 4 (at the end of the observation period). During the treatment period, sitting BP was measured every 2 weeks until week 12 and then every 4 weeks until week 28 using an automatic BP monitor (HEM-759P Fuzzy device, Omron Healthcare Co., Ltd., Kyoto, Japan). The 24-hour BP measurements were taken at week 3 during the observation period, and at weeks 12, 28, and 52 during the treatment period using an ambulatory BP monitor (TM-2433, A&D Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan). BP measurements were taken over a period of 25 hours (minimum) at 30-minute intervals.

Each patient’s dipping pattern was based on their 24-hour ABPM recording. Nighttime SBP dipping (%) was calculated as (1 − average nighttime SBP/average daytime SBP) × 100. Each patient was classified into one of four nighttime BP dipping patterns based on their nighttime SBP dipping (%) as follows: extreme dippers were those with a BP decrease ≥ 20%; dippers, < 20% to ≥ 10%; non-dippers, < 10% to ≥ 0%; and risers, < 0%.16,17

Blood samples were collected using ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid disodium vacuum collection tubes to evaluate NT-proBNP levels. Blood samples were inverted, mixed, and centrifuged at 3,000 rpm for 10 min at 4 °C. The resulting plasma was frozen at ≤−20 °C in a sample container for storage, with a minimum volume of 0.8 mL for biomarker measurement. Stored samples were analyzed by an independent laboratory (LSI Medience Corporation, Tokyo, Japan). NT-proBNP levels were measured using an electro-chemiluminescence immunoassay, with a standard value of ≤ 125 pg/mL (minimum amount in plasma that can be measured).

Baseline characteristics are summarized using frequency and percentages for categorical variables and mean ± standard deviation for continuous variables. For changes in nighttime BP and NT-proBNP levels and for differences between the dipping patterns, least squares mean (along with 95% confidence intervals (CI)) and P values were calculated using analysis of covariance models with baseline value as a covariate. Log-transformed values of NT-proBNP levels were used to calculate the geometric mean and geometric mean ratio. Differences in proportions between dipping patterns were compared using the chi-square test. For paired categorical variables, McNemar’s symmetry test was used to compare changes in dipping pattern distribution from baseline to week 28. Statistical significance was set at 0.05 (two-sided). No adjustments for multiplicity were made as this was an explanatory study. All statistical analyses were performed using SAS System Release 9.4 (SAS Institute Japan Ltd., Tokyo, Japan).

## 结果 / Results

Of 368 patients enrolled in the study,15 270 patients who received either esaxerenone alone or in combination with a CCB or RASi were included in this post hoc analysis (full analysis set); 172 patients received esaxerenone monotherapy (monotherapy group), 49 patients received combination therapy with a CCB (CCB group), and 49 patients received combination therapy with a RASi (RASi group). The 98 patients were not included in the efficacy analysis of the current paper because they received an additional antihypertensive agent at 28 weeks.

There were no obvious differences in sitting BP or ABPM between the groups. The percentage of patients with Grade I hypertension was numerically higher in the monotherapy group than in the RASi and CCB groups. NT-proBNP levels were numerically slightly lower in the CCB group than in the other two groups.

The 24-hour ambulatory SBP reduction from baseline was − 10.0 mm Hg (P < 0.001) in the monotherapy group, − 6.0 mmHg (P < 0.01) in the CCB group, and − 17.0 mmHg (P < 0.001) in the RASi group, with the RASi group showing the numerically greatest change (Figure 1). ## 讨论 / Discussion

In this post hoc analysis, we confirmed the antihypertensive effect of esaxerenone in combination with a CCB or RASi and its effect on NT-proBNP levels in Japanese patients with uncontrolled hypertension. Esaxerenone showed a BP-lowering effect on nighttime hypertension, especially when administered in combination with a RASi, with a trend for an enhanced effect. NT-proBNP levels decreased regardless of whether esaxerenone was administered as monotherapy or in combination with a CCB or RASi. In particular, the combination with a RASi resulted in the numerically largest reduction.

In our previous post hoc analysis of the ESAX-HTN study, the decrease in nighttime SBP at 12 weeks was significantly greater with esaxerenone 2.5 and 5 mg/day than eplerenone (− 2.6 mm Hg (95% CI − 5.0, − 0.2) and − 6.4 mm Hg (95% CI − 8.8, − 4.0), respectively).12 Furthermore, in our previous long-term phase 3 study, we found that nighttime SBP decreased from baseline to week 28 in all dipping pattern groups, with the greatest change shown in the riser group (− 25.5 mm Hg).13 Our study demonstrated that the antihypertensive effect of esaxerenone remained consistent for up to 28 weeks, regardless of concomitant medications. In addition, the combination of esaxerenone and RASi yielded the strongest antihypertensive effect on nighttime BP (− 20.6 mm Hg). In addition to the strong antihypertensive effect, as shown in Table 1, there was a tendency for the esaxerenone dose at weeks 12 and 28 to be lower in the RASi groups compared to the monotherapy and CCB group. Less than 50% of patients in the RASi groups were dosed at 5 mg, while more than 60% in the CCB group at week 28. This also suggests the potential benefit of combining esaxerenone and RASi in terms of safety, as a lower dose of esaxerenone is associated with a lower risk of adverse effects. We speculate that the correction of aldosterone breakthrough by esaxerenone may have contributed to the observed antihypertensive effect on nocturnal hypertension. Esaxerenone would be a useful treatment option for patients with uncontrolled hypertension to control nighttime BP in combination with either CCB or RASi. In particular, its effects tended to be enhanced when used in combination with a RASi.

In the present study, a significant shift in dipping pattern restoration was shown in the monotherapy and CCB groups, but the change did not reach statistical significance in the RASi group. This may be because the sample size was small and the percentage of dippers at baseline was higher in the RASi group compared with the other groups, which may have reduced the level of improvement after the administration of the drug.

We previously evaluated changes in the dipping pattern distribution from baseline in hypertensive patients treated with esaxerenone and found that the proportions of patients with riser or non-dipper patterns decreased at week 28.12 Similar results were observed in this study, with the distribution of dipping patterns from baseline showing a decrease in the proportion of non-dippers at week 28. Moreover, the proportion of patients with non-dipper patterns decreased in all groups. These results suggest that esaxerenone, administered as monotherapy or in combination with a CCB or RASi, contributes to the normalization of the dipping pattern. Considering the higher risk of organ damage and cardiovascular death in patients with non-dipper patterns,3–6 we believe that esaxerenone treatment in these patients has clinical relevance. The association between a riser pattern and heart failure has not been fully elucidated, but it is independent of 24-hour BP, diurnal BP, and nocturnal BP, suggesting the existence of a mechanism “beyond BP.”11 A possible mechanism that could be explained is “circulating volume.” The non-dipping pattern is known to be associated with an increase in circulating volume, which is determined primarily by salt sensitivity and salt intake.18,19 Decreased salt intake and diuretics may cause a shift from a non-dipping pattern to a dipper pattern, with nocturnal BP decreasing to a greater extent than diurnal BP. The combination of esaxerenone with a RASi may provide greater protection against cardiovascular disease in salt-sensitive hypertensive patients. In the present study, esaxerenone restored the disrupted circadian BP rhythm from a non-dipper pattern to a dipper pattern and reduced plasma NT-proBNP levels.

Our study results revealed that NT-proBNP levels were significantly reduced after 28 weeks of treatment with esaxerenone in all groups, suggesting that cardiac overload may have decreased as a result of the reduction in nighttime BP caused by mineralocorticoid receptor inhibition. Elevated NT-proBNP levels have been linked to nocturnal hypertension and subclinical damage in the heart and kidneys of patients with hypertension, and NT-proBNP levels have been shown to decrease with antihypertensive medication.20–23 Furthermore, while BNP values were typically within the normal range, research from the Framingham study has shown that even slight increases in BNP levels were associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular events.24

In terms of the factors that contribute to hypertension, Asians tend to exhibit higher levels of salt sensitivity and salt intake compared with Western populations.25 In addition, Asians may be genetically predisposed to factors related to salt-sensitive gene polymorphisms of the renin–angiotensin system.25 Research has suggested that nocturnal hypertension is associated with high sodium intake and salt sensitivity, which may be specific characteristics of hypertension among Asian populations.18 In addition, nocturnal hypertension is more common in Asian populations than in Western populations.26,27 Therefore, antihypertensive strategies targeting nocturnal hypertension are particularly important for Asian patients with hypertension. Future studies are warranted to determine whether the results of this study are applicable in Western populations.

This study was a post hoc analysis with no prespecified protocol, and the results should be interpreted with caution. No control group was established in this study, and comparisons were made between the baseline and the end of treatment. Therefore, these results should only be used for hypothesis generation. In the future, a comparative design will be required to test the hypothesis. Because this study was conducted in Japanese patients, the generalizability of the results may be limited. In addition, the highly selective nature of the patient population, tailored to meet the requirements of a clinical trial, may have further limited the generalizability of the findings. Consequently, potential changes in NT-proBNP levels might have been overlooked. The stratification into three groups in this study resulted in a smaller sample size for each group, so the statistical power may have been inadequate.

## 结论 / Conclusion

When administered in combination with a CCB or RASi, esaxerenone may be a useful treatment option to control nocturnal BP in patients with uncontrolled hypertension, with a significant nighttime BP-lowering effect, especially when used in combination with a RASi. Esaxerenone administered as monotherapy or in combination therapy with a CCB or RASi may contribute to reducing the risk of cardiovascular disease (including heart failure) in patients with uncontrolled hypertension.
