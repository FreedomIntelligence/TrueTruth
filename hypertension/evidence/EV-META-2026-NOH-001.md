## English Abstract

High blood pressure (BP) in obese populations poses significant cardiovascular risks, yet the comparative effectiveness of various weight loss interventions on BP remains unclear. This systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA) aims to assess the comparative effectiveness of weight loss interventions in overweight/obese adults with prehypertension/hypertension on BP change and adverse events (AEs).

A systematic review and Bayesian NMA of randomised controlled trials of weight loss interventions in overweight/obese patients with prehypertension/hypertension will be conducted. PubMed, EMBASE and the Cochrane library (CENTRAL) and relevant references will be searched up to June 2025. Primary outcomes are changes in systolic and diastolic BP; secondary outcomes include AEs, body weight reduction (kg) and quality of life. Study selection, data extraction and methodological quality assessment using Cochrane risk of bias (RoB) 2.0 will be performed by independent two authors. A Bayesian NMA will be conducted using BUGSnet, with surface under the cumulative ranking curve to rank interventions. Subgroup analyses will explore heterogeneity by baseline BP severity, intervention duration and comorbidities, and sensitivity analyses will be performed for robustness of the results by RoB and sample size.

Ethical approval is not required for this systematic review as it will involve analysis of data only from previously published studies. The results will be disseminated through presentations at international conferences and publication in peer-reviewed journals.

## 背景 / Background

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the leading global cause of death and disability, highlighting the need to manage modifiable risk factors.1 Obesity and hypertension are two of the most important modifiable determinants of CVD.2 Global Burden of Disease (GBD) 2021 estimates indicate that high body mass index (BMI) caused about 1.9 million CVD deaths, with disability-adjusted life years more than doubling since 1990.2 GBD 2023 identified high systolic blood pressure (SBP) as the leading level 3 CVD risk factor.3

Obesity and hypertension are tightly interconnected. Excess adiposity increases sympathetic nervous system activity, renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system activation and renal sodium retention, all of which elevate blood pressure (BP) and contribute to the development of hypertension.4 Several cohort studies and a dose–response meta-analysis have demonstrated graded and often nonlinear associations between adiposity measures and incident hypertension, with the lowest risk generally observed within the normal BMI range (approximately 18.5–24 kg/m²).57 Clinically, obesity and hypertension frequently coexist, and the rising prevalence of obesity810 is contributing to higher rates of uncontrolled BP even among those receiving antihypertensive therapy.11

Given these interdependencies, intentional weight loss is recognised as a central non-pharmacologic strategy for BP reduction. Major international hypertension guidelines consistently recognise intentional weight loss as an effective non-pharmacologic strategy to lower BP.1217 The 2013 American Heart Association/American College of Cardiology/The Obesity Society (AHA/ACC) guideline for the management of overweight and obesity in adults recommends achieving an initial weight-loss goal of 5%–10% of baseline body weight within 6 months through a comprehensive lifestyle intervention that includes a reduced-calorie diet, increased physical activity and behavioural strategies.12 The guideline further notes that greater amounts of weight loss lead to reductions in BP and other CVD risk factors, along with decreased reliance on antihypertensive and metabolic medications. The most recent update of the Cochrane review on weight-reducing drugs suggested that body weight reduction in adults with essential hypertension was associated with SBP reduction by mean difference (MD) −2.6 mm Hg (95% CI −3.8 to −1.4 mmHg; 4 studies, 2058 participants) and diastolic BP (DBP) by MD −2.0 mm Hg (95% CI −2.7 to −1.2 mm Hg; 4 trials, 2058 participants).18 In addition, a meta-analysis of diverse weight loss interventions such as energy restriction, exercise or both in an obese population (about 74% of whom met the hypertension criteria) showed reductions in BP of 1 mm Hg per every kg of weight loss.19 However, comparative effects of various weight loss interventions on BP in overweight/obese patients with hypertension have not been established.

In this context, the purpose of this systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA) is to evaluate the comparative effectiveness of weight loss interventions on BP changes and adverse events (AEs) including discontinuation due to AEs in overweight/obese adults with prehypertension/hypertension.

## 方法 / Methods

The protocol for this systematic review and NMA was submitted to the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO, https://www.crd.york.ac.uk/prospero/, Registration ID: CRD42022376688) and the reporting will adhere to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis Protocols (PRISMA-P)20 (see online supplemental appendix 1 for PRISMA-P checklist).

An electronic search of PubMed, EMBASE and the Cochrane Library (CENTRAL) will be conducted to identify studies that evaluated BP changes by weight management interventions through 30 June 2025. Additional hand searches for relevant articles will also be conducted from the reference lists of the included articles. An extensive list of search terms which were mostly in two categories was used: (1) various synonyms of obesity and hypertension and (2) variety of weight loss interventions such as exercise, diet, antiobesity medications, behaviour change and complementary, alternative or traditional medicine (see online supplemental appendix 2 for full search strategies). No restrictions will be imposed on language or publication date.

Only parallel randomised controlled trials (RCTs) meeting the following criteria will be considered. Patients should be overweight or obese adults (aged ≥18 years) with prehypertension or hypertension, with or without obesity-associated comorbidities (eg, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidaemia, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease and obstructive sleep apnoea). For hypertension, the European Society of Hypertension (ESH) and the European Society of Cardiology (ESC) have defined hypertension as a mean SBP of more than 140 mm Hg or DBP more than 90 mmHg, and prehypertension as 130–139 mm Hg for SBP or 85–89 mm Hg for DBP.21 The 2017 ACC/AHA guidelines made the new threshold for definition of hypertension, 130 mm Hg for SBP or 80 mm Hg for DBP.14 Although there are differences with the BP cut-off for diagnosing hypertension in these two influential guidelines, there is also considerable agreement on the threshold for the initiation of antihypertensive medication in addition to lifestyle intervention, that is, SBP ≥140 mm Hg or DBP ≥90 mm Hg, SBP ≥130 mm Hg or DBP ≥80 (for ACC/AHA) or 85 mm Hg (for ESC/ESH) with CVDs.15 Therefore, this review will include parallel RCTs involving patients with SBP ≥130 mm Hg and/or DBP ≥80 mm Hg, diagnosed with hypertension or treated with antihypertensive medications. Patients diagnosed with prehypertension based on the same cut-off BP as the ESC/ESH guideline will also be eligible.

For overweight or obesity, patients with BMI ≥25 kg/m2 or BMI ≥23 kg/m2 (for Asia-Pacific region), or more than 110% of their ideal weight will be considered.2224 The studies involving the following patients will be excluded: pregnant or breastfeeding women; children and adolescents (aged <18 years); with severe CVD, cerebrovascular disease, other chronic conditions such as cancer, severe mental or physical handicap, or other conditions potentially causing hypertension, such as renal failure.

Any interventions for weight loss including pharmacotherapy, bariatric surgery, diet, exercise, behavioural therapy and complementary and integrative medicine will be included. Dietary strategies for hypertension, such as Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension,25 reducing sodium26 or moderation in alcohol intake27 will be considered as long as a decrease in body weight was reported in the study. Eligible controls will be categorised as different types of weight loss treatment, no treatment, wait list, usual care (eg, general advice for health such as brochures, dietary or physical guidelines, or normal visits to clinic), or placebo. If the control intervention is antihypertensive therapy only, this trial will be excluded. However, if antihypertensive therapy is applied as a cointervention, this trial will be included. In multiarm trials, interventions other than those referred to above will not be eligible.

The primary outcome will be the difference in mean change from baseline SBP and DBP between groups, at the end of treatment, measured in mm Hg. If the measured time points are not available, the nearest time point will be taken. Secondary outcomes will be drop-outs due to any AEs, body weight reduction (kg) and quality of life.

First, titles and abstracts will be screened; then, full texts will be evaluated based on the inclusion/exclusion criteria. This process will be performed independently by two authors (EN and IB). If any disagreements arise, the authors will discuss with the corresponding author (HL) to resolve the discrepancies. The selection process will be represented in a PRISMA flow chart (see figure 1).20 The two authors will work independently to extract the information according to the predefined extraction forms to collect relevant information and data with a Microsoft Excel programme (Microsoft Excel 2016 MSO): year of publication, first author, country, sample size, body weight, BMI, SBP and DBP, mean age, % female, treatment duration, measurement time, intervention, cointervention, control, drop-outs (%), presence of comorbidities, concomitant antihypertensive agents and quality of life. AEs will be extracted separately. In this review, AEs were defined as any unfavourable clinical outcomes reported in the included studies, including total AEs, treatment withdrawals due to AEs, episodes of uncontrolled BP and serious AEs requiring or prolonging hospitalisation.

The Cochrane risk of bias assessment tool 2.0 will be used to evaluate and present the risks of bias arising from the following: randomisation process; deviations from the intended interventions (effect of assignment to intervention); missing outcome data; and measurement of the outcome.28 We will assess the risk of bias for each domain as ‘low’, ‘high’ or ‘some concerns’. This process will be conducted by two authors independently (EN and IB) and any disagreements will be resolved by discussion with the corresponding author (HL).

This review will be conducted in compliance with the PRISMA NMA extension29 30 and adopt a Bayesian NMA framework.

We will conduct standard pairwise meta-analyses using a meta package V.6.5.0 in an R V.4.3.2 environment (R Core Team, Vienna, Austria). For continuous variables (eg, changes in BP), MD will be calculated with a 95% CI. If SDs are missing, they will be calculated according to the methods guided by the Cochrane Handbook of Systematic Reviews of Interventions.31 For binary outcomes (eg, drop-outs due to any AEs), OR with a 95% CI will be estimated. Heterogeneity among the included studies will be evaluated by visual inspection of the forest plots and the I2 statistic.32 If significant statistical and clinical heterogeneity is found in a particular comparison, instead of pooling the outcomes, we will summarise the results descriptively.

There may be no unit of analysis issues in the included studies due to an eligible type of study (ie, a simple parallel group design). However, in the event that repeated observations on participants or multiple intervention groups are found in the included trials, we will follow the guidance in Chapters 6 and 23 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions.31

The treatment network of the direct and indirect comparisons between different interventions will be described and graphically presented in the network geometry to determine if an NMA is feasible. The network geometry presents the shape of treatment comparisons; each node in the network indicates a weight loss intervention included and the node size represents the number of randomly assigned patients; and each line (edge) represents a direct comparison, and the width of the lines is proportional to the number of RCTs that compare every pair of interventions. Some intervention nodes will be merged to form single comparators (ie, lumping of interventions) to combine similar treatment effects such as different doses or alternative forms of the identical medication, heterogeneous durations of administration or different controls.33 Control interventions will be classified as active or inactive33; inactive controls are those in which no intervention is applied (ie, waitlist or no treatment), whereas active controls involve some degree of intervention (eg, usual care or placebo). A hierarchical model in which the intervention classes form one level of a hierarchy, and individual interventions form sublevels, will be adopted to merge different but related interventions into the identical class.34 This allows active and inactive controls to be treated as one reference control.

If the assumptions of similarity and heterogeneity are considered acceptable by reviewing the study characteristics in the network of treatments, we will move forward with NMAs for each outcome. In our review, a Bayesian NMA will be conducted using BUGSnet (Bayesian inference Using Gibbs Sampling to conduct a NMA, V.1.1.0) and Just Another Gibbs Sampler, V.4.3.1 in an R V.4.3.2 environment (R Core Team, Vienna, Austria).35 The Bayesian approach was chosen based on the following; the Bayesian method is free from the large-sample assumption to satisfy the normality assumption of the frequentist approach36; it can better handle the multi-arm trials and the heterogeneity between studies35; and it allows better establishment of hierarchies between competitive interventions and better decision-making for practice implication through presenting outcomes as probabilities.35 The posterior distributions will be estimated with non-informative prior distributions to limit deduction based on collected data only and with Markov Chain Monte Carlo simulation. After the NMA model has been run, we will evaluate the convergence and fit of the model on the basis of inspection of the trace plots and Monte Carlo errors.

A random-effects model will be used to consider both within-study and between-study variance.36 Comparative effect estimates will be presented as MD or OR with 95% credible intervals depending on the type of variable. The relative rankings of different weight loss interventions will be presented in network league tables and Surface Under the Cumulative RAnking curve (SUCRA) based on intervention’s efficacy and safety.37 SUCRA values are expressed as percentages where 100% indicates the best treatment option and 0% the worst in terms of the outcome of interest.

NMAs rely on the fundamental assumptions of transitivity and consistency across different comparisons within a network of trials. Transitivity refers to effect modifiers being distributed similarly between studies to allow for indirect comparisons to be valid.38 The assumption of transitivity will be assessed by comparing the main descriptive characteristics of the included studies (if data are available): hypertension status, baseline body weight or BMI, medication, concomitant diseases, mean age, female%, risk of bias category, duration of follow-up, number of participants and year of publication.

The assumption of consistency is the statistical manifestation of transitivity, ie, the direct estimates are in agreement with indirect estimates.38 BUGSnet has the options to evaluate consistency at both the global and local level. At the global level, the inconsistency and the consistency model will be compared with the deviance information criterion. At the local level, consistency can be explored using the leverage plots, which compare the posterior mean deviance of each data point between the models.35

The possible sources for heterogeneity will be investigated through subgroup analyses and meta-regression. Subgroup analyses will be performed on heterogeneity related variables: baseline hypertension status (prehypertension, stage 1, 2 hypertension), concomitant antihypertensive agent; cardiovascular risk factors such as mean age (65 vs <65), female (50% vs <50%), baseline BMI status (overweight, stage 1, 2 obesity), concomitant diseases (type 2 diabetes and/or dyslipidaemia).13 Additionally, meta-regressions will be run by participant and study characteristics for SBP and DBP with MD in weight change (by comparing study arms within each trial).

A sensitivity analysis will be conducted for the robustness of the results by excluding studies at a high/unclear risk of bias for randomisation and/or allocation concealment, studies with a sample size of less than 100 and studies not reporting BP changes as their primary outcome.

Ethical approval is not required for this systematic review as it will involve analysis of data from previously published studies. The results will be disseminated through presentations at international conferences and publication in peer-reviewed journals.
