## English Abstract

Effects of blood pressure (BP) control on the relationships between arterial stiffness and subsequent cognitive impairment remain unclear.

The Systolic Blood Pressure Intervention Trial Pulse Wave Velocity (PWV) substudy evaluated the effect BP control on total (T‐PWV, in m/s), structural (S‐PWV), and load‐dependent (LD‐PWV) arterial stiffness over 3 years as they related to time to cognitive impairment over 10 years modeled using multivariable proportional hazard models for baseline stiffness and joint longitudinal survival models for changes in stiffness.

Six hundred fourteen participants with stiffness measures had 90 cognitive impairment events over follow‐up. Reductions in LD‐PWV under intensive BP control were associated with 21% risk reduction for cognitive impairment. T‐PWV and S‐PWV continued to increase regardless of BP control with each m/s increase in stiffness associated with 11% to 12% increase in risk for cognitive impairment.

Intensive SBP control reduced LD‐PWV over 3 years, which reduced the risk of cognitive impairment over 10 years of follow‐up.

## 背景 / Background

Arterial stiffness increases with age
1
, 
2
 and associates with elevated risks for cardiovascular disease,
3
 stroke,
4
 cognitive impairment, and dementia.
5
, 
6
 Elastic arteries stiffen through two mechanisms: a structural mechanism mediated by age‐related changes in the vessel wall (tunica media thickening and vascular wall remodeling) and a load‐dependent mechanism mediated by blood pressure (BP). A novel strategy was recently developed to uncouple these two components to better understand the complex relationships among arterial stiffness, aging, and BP.
2
, 
7

Carotid–femoral pulse wave velocity (PWV) is considered the gold standard measurement of arterial stiffness.
8
 A growing body of literature relates arterial stiffness measured by PWV to cognition, cognitive decline, and the structural brain changes and biomarkers underlying dementia.
6
, 
9
, 
10
, 
11
, 
12
 Associations between higher arterial stiffness and longitudinal cognitive outcomes have been repeatedly shown in the literature.
5
 The specific impact of load‐dependent and structural stiffness on cognitive outcomes remains unclear. Further, few studies measure temporal changes in the mechanisms underlying arterial stiffness and how they relate to changes in cognition over time.

The Systolic Blood Pressure Intervention Trial (SPRINT) demonstrated the long‐term beneficial effects of intensive versus standard BP control over 3 years on the reduction in the hazards of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and dementia over 7 years.
13
, 
14
 Secondary analysis of SPRINT found higher estimated PWV was independently associated with an increased risk of MCI and dementia.
15
 It remains unclear if the observed relationships between arterial stiffness with cognitive impairment are apparent for direct measures of PWV and are attributable to innate structural changes in the arteries or BP‐dependent aspects underlying arterial stiffness.

By deriving load‐dependent PWV (LD‐PWV) and structural PWV (S‐PWV) from total PWV (T‐PWV), we recently showed that intensive versus standard BP control lowered the BP‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness (LD‐PWV) but not the age‐related structural stiffness (S‐PWV) over 3 years of BP control in SPRINT.
16
 Data from the Multi‐Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis (MESA) showed that total carotid PWV (T‐PWV) was associated with an increased risk of incident dementia, though there was no significant association with BP‐dependent (LD‐PWV, P = 0.19) or structural (S‐PWV, P = 0.06) arterial stiffness.
17
 Further, S‐PWV appears to drive the associations observed with arterial stiffness and global cognitive decline over time and brain imaging abnormalities associated with dementia.
18
 It is unclear how BP intervention‐related changes in arterial stiffness affect the associations between arterial stiffness and incident cognitive impairment.

This study seeks to further investigate the associations between changes in arterial stiffness in SPRINT during 3 years of BP control as they relate to the risk for developing cognitive impairment. We hypothesize that patients with lower arterial stiffness measures at baseline and those with observed reductions in LD‐PWV over BP intervention will have a lower risk of cognitive impairment.

## 方法 / Methods

The design of the SPRINT have been previously described.
16
, 
19
 Briefly, 9361 participants age ≥ 50 with hypertension and high cardiovascular disease risk without diabetes were randomized to either a standard systolic BP (SBP) target of <140 mm Hg or a more intensive SBP target of <120 mm Hg.
19
 Participants enrolled in the SPRINT‐PWV ancillary study
16
 were recruited from 11 of its 102 clinical sites—10 from a single clinical center network (University of Chicago, George Washington University, University of Texas Southwestern, University of Colorado, University of Utah, University of Pittsburgh, Tufts Medical Center, Vanderbilt University, Stanford University, and University of California‐San Diego) plus the Houston Veterans Administration (VA) Medical Center between 2012 and 2016. The only additional exclusion criterion for entry into the PWV ancillary study was the presence of atrial fibrillation, as arrhythmia precludes obtaining a valid carotid–femoral PWV (Figure S1 in supporting information).

The study's manual of procedures that each clinical site adhered to stipulated that upper arm brachial BP be measured after 10 minutes of supine rest, using a calibrated, oscillometric, automated BP measurement device to obtain SBP and diastolic BP (DBP) measures and used to calculate mean arterial pressure (MAP).
19
 The SPRINT‐PWV study protocol using SphygmoCor CPV system coincided with the SPRINT randomization study visit and annually through 3 years of follow‐up (±1 month of the baseline study date). The study's standardized procedures adhered to the recommendations published by the Consensus Conference on the Clinical Applications of Arterial Stiffness.
8

Four carotid–femoral PWV protocols were conducted with the participant in a supine position and the technically acceptable measurements were averaged. Details of the arterial stiffness modeling used to derive age‐related S‐PWV and BP‐dependent LD‐PWV from total arterial stiffness (T‐PWV) have been published for SPRINT‐PWV.
16
 Briefly, carotid–femoral PWV is used to represent T‐PWV across the participant's DBP–SBP dynamic range. Arterial mechanics were modeled using an exponential pressure–area relationship,
20
 using the participant's seated BP. S‐PWV was then calculated at the same 120/80 reference BP range for all participants.
2
, 
7
, 
17
 LD‐PWV was calculated from the difference between total arterial stiffness and structural arterial stiffness (LD‐PWV = T‐PWV – S‐PWV).
2
, 
7
, 
17
 If the participant's BP was > 120/80, load‐dependent stiffness was positive and if the participant's BP was < 120/80, load‐dependent stiffness was negative.

Procedures for neuropsychological evaluation and adjudication of cognitive impairment in the trial have been previously described.
13
, 
14
 The entire period of follow‐up used expert adjudication to classify participants as either no impairment, MCI, probable dementia, or unable to classify. For the on‐treatment phase of the trial, adjudicators relied on data from clinic‐based in‐person neuropsychological assessments, interviews with a trusted contact (family member or friend familiar with their day‐to‐day functioning), medical information including comorbidities and medications, and assessment of depression. Assessments for participants no longer able to complete face‐to‐face visits were performed by telephone using a standard protocol or with their trusted contacts. After the conclusion of the treatment phase, the long‐term follow‐up data were collected using centralized telephone follow‐up assessments following standardized and validated procedures used in other studies with the same outcomes.
14
, 
21
 Adjudication of cognitive status was completed by a panel of experts with clinical expertise diagnosing cognitive impairment using the same procedures throughout all phases of the trial and follow‐up using standardized diagnostic criteria for probable dementia and MCI.
22
,

The main trial included planned cognitive assessments at baseline, 2 and 4 years of follow‐up, and study closeout if it was > 1 year after the 4‐year follow‐up visit. Neuropsychological assessment was extended by in‐person and telephone protocols to increase the median follow‐up in the cohort.
14

Continuous variables are presented as median, 25th, and 75th percentiles. Categorical variables are presented as number and percent. The primary exposures were T‐PWV, S‐PWV, and LD‐PWV expressed in meters per second (m/s) at baseline and changes over 3 years of follow‐up. All participants with at least one follow‐up PWV measure were included in this analysis. The primary treatment condition was BP intervention as standard (SBP < 140 mmHg) versus intensive (SBP < 120 mmHg) treatment arms. All statistical analyses were performed in R. The main analyses focus on associations between baseline arterial stiffness measures and time to events of composite outcome of cognitive impairment, combining MCI and probable dementia using Cox proportional hazards models. Next, time‐varying effects of BP treatment on changes in arterial stiffness over the initial 3‐year intervention period and the effect of these changes in arterial stiffness had on time to events of cognitive impairment over a total of 10 years of follow‐up was evaluated using joint longitudinal survival models. Longitudinal models were fitted to each component of arterial stiffness, separately, to estimate participant‐specific longitudinal trajectories with multivariable adjustment for randomized treatment arm, time, and their interaction. The resulting change models were combined with Cox proportional hazards models for time to cognitive impairment, and a joint longitudinal survival model was estimated (using the JMBayes2 R package).

Each multivariable model sequentially adjusted first for treatment arm (Model 1), then adjustment for Model 1 + age, race, sex, and education (Model 2), and then Model 2 + baseline MAP (Model 3). Results from Cox proportional hazards modeling were reported as hazard ratio (HR) and 95% confidence interval (CI). We did not adjust for multiple comparisons and have reported all findings, whether positive or negative. We proposed three distinct hypotheses for each of the three components of arterial stiffness based on our prior findings in SPRINT.
16
 Hypothesis 1: because S‐PWV measures age‐related structural stiffness unaffected by BP intervention arm, we hypothesized that the benefits of BP lowering will not impact relationships between S‐PWV and cognitive impairment; Hypothesis 2: because LD‐PWV measures the BP‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness, we hypothesize that BP lowering produces large effects on LD‐PWV that in turn are associated with the risk for cognitive impairment; and Hypothesis 3: T‐PWV is a measure of total arterial stiffness and affected by changes in each component but unaffected by BP lowering. Accordingly, given that S‐PWV measures of age‐related structural aspects of arterial stiffness underlying T‐PWV, adjustment for age was expected to attenuate models of S‐PWV and T‐PWV. Therefore, we have specific interest in interpreting the treatment arm effects on S‐PWV and T‐PWV without age adjustment (Model 1) as well as with covariates (Models 2 and 3). In contrast, LD‐PWV represents the complex BP‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness that are highly influenced by covariates associated with hypertension; therefore, we primarily interpret for LD‐PWV the results from the covariate‐adjusted models (Model 2 and Model 3). We tested heterogeneity of treatment effect for T‐PWV, S‐PWV, and LD‐PWV by demographics: age, sex, and self‐reported race. Finally, we conducted dynamic path analysis (dpasurv R package) to estimate mediation effects of changes in arterial stiffness components under BP control on time to cognitive impairment in unadjusted and Model 2 covariate‐adjusted models to provide cumulative effect estimates for direct (treatment arm) effects, indirect (changes in arterial stiffness) effects, and total (direct plus indirect) effects on time to cognitive impairment with bootstrapped 95% CI from 1000 replicates.
24

## 结果 / Results

A subset of 614 SPRINT‐PWV participants randomized to intensive (n = 298) or standard BP treatment (n = 316) in SPRINT also had measures of T‐PWV and longitudinal cognitive measures to enable time‐to‐event analyses. These participants had a mean age of 74 ± 9 years; 40% were women; 70% and 21% self‐reported White and Black race, respectively; and 54% had obtained a college degree. BP and PWV metrics were the same between groups at baseline (Table 1). Extended and adjudicated time‐to‐event data resulted in 90 composite events of MCI (n = 62) and probable dementia (n = 28) events over a median follow‐up of 6.2 years in this cohort.

## 讨论 / Discussion

The SPRINT trial, a randomized clinical trial of intensive versus standard BP treatment also included longitudinal ancillary studies of arterial stiffness measured by PWV over 3 years and cognitive impairment over 10 years of follow‐up. SPRINT provides a unique opportunity to assess the effects of varying degrees of BP control on changes in arterial stiffness over time including the impact on subsequent development of cognitive impairment over time. As expected, higher baseline arterial stiffness measured by T‐PWV and S‐PWV were associated with time to cognitive impairment. These relationships were seen regardless of study intervention arm. In contrast, measures of LD‐PWV at baseline and the longitudinal changes over the trial were only associated with time to cognitive impairment after adjustment for demographic factors. Intensive BP treatment only produced significant declines in LD‐PWV over the course of the study in pace with the decline in BP. The LD‐PWV declines with intensive treatment were then associated with a > 20% reduced risk for cognitive impairments sustained over time. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first randomized clinical trial to show the effect of BP lowering on changing arterial stiffness parameters and their relationships with incident cognitive impairment over time. The intensive BP intervention produced marked declines in LD‐PWV, which tracked with the observed reductions in SBP induced by the intensive intervention. These declines corresponded to reduced risk for cognitive impairment over the course of the trial. While longitudinal mediation analysis did not detect a direct effect of treatment arm assignment on risk for cognitive impairment, treatment‐related reductions in LD‐PWV were associated with reduced risk for cognitive impairment. In this SPRINT PWV subset, this mediation effect did not reach statistical significance over follow‐up. In contrast to the marked reduction in LD‐PWV, neither standard nor intensive BP intervention arm affected total and structural measures of arterial stiffness, estimated by T‐PWV and S‐PWV. Instead, S‐PWV continued to increase over time regardless of BP‐lowering strategy. Accordingly, baseline T‐PWV and S‐PWV and their observed increases over time continued to be associated with time to cognitive impairment in SPRINT regardless of intensive and standard BP intervention.

The lack of effect of BP lowering on total and structural stiffness is expected as the calculation of S‐PWV was designed to exclude effects of BP and intended to represent the physiologic processes of vascular remodeling that occur in aging, namely disruption of elastin fibers and collagen cross‐linking.
25
 These structural aspects of arterial remodeling are generally not affected by BP treatment intensity.
16
 It is remarkable that both baseline structural stiffness and its longitudinal increases over the study duration were associated with time to cognitive impairment regardless of treatment effects, suggesting that structural stiffness calculations like S‐PWV are independent predictors of incident cognitive impairment. It is also not surprising that the inclusion of age dramatically attenuates the relationships between structural stiffness and time to cognitive impairment given that structural stiffness is a biomarker of arterial aging.
2
 Future work in larger cohorts with longer follow‐up will be needed to develop models in which S‐PWV may be expressed as an aging biomarker to represent accelerated and slower arterial aging. As T‐PWV and S‐PWV are highly correlated with age, the attenuation of their longitudinal effects in multivariable adjusted models are mostly attributable to inclusion of age. Other covariates provided little additional risk for the development of cognitive impairment. Conversely, it is intriguing that baseline and changes in LD‐PWV only become significant predictors of cognitive impairment when adjusted for age and other demographics. These relationships between changes in LD‐PWV and cognition were attenuated by adjustment for baseline MAP underscoring that reductions in LD‐PWV are linked to the baseline levels of arterial pressure necessitating BP intervention.

Prior work has established the relationships between arterial stiffness and dementia risk factors.

 Here, we show that BP treatment reduces LD‐PWV aspects of arterial stiffness and this decrease in LD‐PWV reduces the risk for developing cognitive impairment, even as S‐PWV and T‐PWV continue to increase over time. We can conclude that antihypertensive therapies used in SPRINT do not target structural aspects of arterial stiffness. Yet, structural arterial stiffness appears to be a more important risk factor for cognitive impairment and dementia pathology and may provide preventative benefits for dementia beyond BP lowering.

 It is unknown if S‐PWV or LD‐PWV and their changes over time are associated with abnormalities in Alzheimer's disease (AD) biomarkers beyond vascular disease and atrophy. The observed relationships between T‐PWV and AD biomarkers appear to only be evident in older adults in the eighth and ninth decades of life.

 Here we provided evidence that age‐dependent increases in structural stiffness underlie greater total arterial stiffness at older ages that may represent the accelerated aging trajectories for arterial structure observed later in life.
28
 In turn, higher arterial stiffness in older adults is associated with cognitive impairment and evidence of vascular and AD biomarker abnormalities in brain health. It is possible the age‐related risks for arterial stiffness and AD biomarker abnormalities may simply converge later in life. Yet, emerging mechanistic data in humans suggests greater peripheral arterial stiffness transmits excess pulsatility to the cerebral vasculature
29
 and this increases the risk for cerebral small vessel disease, disruption of the blood–brain barrier, and may impact protein clearance from the brain. Murine data provide conflicting evidence that arterial pulsatile flow does
30
 or does not
31
 drive the clearance of protein waste via the glymphatic system leading to AD‐related protein aggregation in the brain. Initial human data suggests the magnitude of intracranial arterial pulsatility relates to glymphatic flow imaging parameters.
32
 However, there is currently no clear evidence to suggest direct or temporal associations between the development of arterial stiffness and the development of AD pathology. The effects of treatment on arterial stiffness are seldom measured in clinical trials, apart from treatment of primary hypertension. Ongoing research is needed to directly target arterial stiffness, including: vasoactive compounds and endothelial dysfunction (e.g., estrogen receptor modulation, nitric oxide bioavailability), extracellular maintenance stiffening, vascular smooth muscle cell transference, inflammation, and oxidative stress.
33

There are many inherent strengths to this study. The randomized clinical trial design offering both intensive versus standard BP lowering directly affects arterial pressure in the context of detailed cognitive follow‐up with adjudicated cognitive outcomes. By design, the effect of BP intervention exclusively improved the BP‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness (measured by LD‐PWV) and does not impact structural arterial stiffness measured by S‐PWV. In SPRINT, these effects were measured longitudinally at four time points over 3 years of follow‐up. SPRINT MIND clearly showed intensive BP lowering reduced incident cognitive impairment measured by the composite events of MCI and probable dementia. Further, the decomposition of structural and BP‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness clarify the differential effects the intervention may have on components of arterial stiffness. This study provides proof of concept that standard and intensive BP protocols lower BP‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness as they lower BP; yet, neither standard nor intensive BP protocols reduced the total or underlying structural aspects of arterial stiffness. We show that total and structural arterial stiffness increased over time regardless of standard or intensive BP lowering. This also suggests additional therapies directed at improving structural aspects of stiffness could further mitigate the risk for cognitive impairment.
33

This study is also not without limitations. SPRINT was not designed to directly target total arterial stiffness as a dementia prevention strategy; therefore, inferences as to the effects of BP control on arterial stiffness need to be directly tested in a clinical trial. The relationships between arterial stiffness and BP intervention relative to MRI biomarkers of small vessel disease and AD biomarkers in SPRINT were not evaluated. SPRINT included various ancillary studies within subsets of its participants. Unfortunately, there is limited overlap between the PWV and brain MRI ancillary studies in SPRINT. Ideally, future trials will include measures of cerebral small vessel disease, brain structure, and perfusion to track the effects treatment‐induced lowering of arterial stiffness may have on brain structure and function. No selection was made in SPRINT relative to the innate baseline arterial stiffness of its participants. Therefore, individuals enrolled in SPRINT did not necessarily have elevated arterial stiffness compared to other more representative cohorts.

The SPRINT PWV substudy shows clear evidence that structural and load‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness are differently associated with incident cognitive impairment independent of BP control. Load‐dependent aspects of arterial stiffness decline dramatically and early with intensive BP lowering and associate with a reduction in the risk for developing cognitive impairment in covariate‐adjusted models of time to events. Load‐dependent arterial stiffness represents the BP‐dependent aspects of arterial pressure and stiffness. As anticipated, the load‐dependent aspects of stiffness decrease with intensive BP lowering and relate to a reduced risk of developing cognitive impairment over 3 years similar to BP. In contrast, the age‐related structural aspects of arterial stiffness are not impacted by magnitude of BP lowering and continued to increase over the course of follow‐up in SPRINT. Structural stiffness appears to be an age‐related risk marker for cognitive impairment, previously associated with cognitive decline over time, lower gray matter volume, and cerebral small vessel disease.
18
 Structural arterial stiffness remains an unaddressed risk factor for cognitive impairment and potential target for dementia prevention.
