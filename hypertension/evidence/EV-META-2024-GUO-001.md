## English Abstract

Traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) demonstrates significant effects on renal function, yet it remains unclear which TCM treatment yields the best clinical outcomes for renal hypertension (RH) management. This study aims to evaluate the safety and effectiveness of various TCM treatments for RH compared to conventional Western medicine (CWM).

Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) of TCM for RH were screened from 9 databases, covering the period up to July 2023. The Cochrane Risk of Bias tool was utilized to assess the risk of bias in the included RCTs. Network meta-analysis was conducted using RevMan 5.3 and Stata 14.0 software. Sensitivity analyses and meta-regression were performed to identify sources of heterogeneity and assess result robustness.

This study encompassed 3879 patients from 44 investigations involving 29 TCM interventions. The quality of the studies ranged from moderate to excellent, with well-defined inclusion criteria and generally low potential for biases related to outcome data and selective reporting. Tianma Gouteng Yin combined with Qiju Dihuang Wan + CWM demonstrated the highest total effective rate (relative risk = 1.31, 95% confidence interval (CI): 1.03, 1.66) compared to CWM alone. Bailing capsule + CWM was optimal for improving serum creatinine (mean difference (MD) = −53.78, 95% CI: −96.59, −10.98). Ziyin Pinggan Huayu decoction + CWM had the highest likelihood (surface under the cumulative ranking curves = 56.2%) of improving blood urea nitrogen. Zishen Pinggan prescription + CWM showed the greatest improvement in systolic blood pressure (MD = −46.29, 95% CI: −73.19, −19.38), while Ye Ren Gao decoction + CWM demonstrated the best improvement in diastolic blood pressure (MD = −13.13, 95% CI: −20.12, −6.13). Xifeng Qingnao decoction + CWM achieved the best improvement in 24-hour urinary protein (MD = −2.00, 95% CI: −3.26, −0.74). The combination of TCM and CWM was associated with a lower incidence of adverse effects.

Combination therapy of TCM with CWM surpasses CWM alone in treating renal hypertension. This study identifies the most effective combination treatment for RH, as well as optimal treatments for lowering blood pressure (Zishen Pinggan prescription + CWM) and improving renal function (Bailing capsule + CWM).

## 背景 / Background

Renal hypertension (RH) is a prevalent form of secondary hypertension and a distinct subset of refractory hypertension, which is attributed to significant renal lesions and lesions in the renal arteries.[1] Changes in blood pressure can result in a progressive decline in renal function, whereas declining renal function poses challenges for effectively managing blood pressure.[2] This damaging cycle results in an unfavorable prognosis characterized by escalating rates of impairment and mortality among individuals.[3] Currently, the incidence of RH is approximately 25%, with a higher frequency observed in elderly individuals.[4] Furthermore, hypertension occurs in as many as 84% of adult patients diagnosed with chronic kidney disease.[5] The close relationship between hypertension and chronic kidney disease amplifies the likelihood of unfavorable cardiovascular and cerebrovascular occurrences, with refractory RH being particularly frequent in patients with chronic kidney disease.[6]

A large number of studies have proved that there is a close relationship between atherosclerotic renal artery stenosis and cardiovascular risk. The increased incidence of cardiovascular disease in these patients has been attributed to the presence of atherosclerosis in other vascular beds, significant RAAS and sympathetic nervous system stimulation, and the coexistence of chronic kidney disease.[7] Considering the risk, and the potential of RH is secondary hypertension can correct the reason, the exploration of RH treatment on patients with more has the vital clinical significance.

The etiology of RH encompasses various pathogenic pathways and typically necessitates treatment with a combination of 2 or more antihypertensive medications.[8] Common dual-drug antihypertensive combinations employ either an angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor (ACEI) or angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB) in conjunction with a dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker (CCB), an ACEI or ARB in combination with a thiazide diuretic, or a dihydropyridine CCB alongside a thiazide diuretic. To manage individuals with inadequate blood pressure control, therapy may involve the administration of a three-drug combination regimen comprising an ACEI or ARB, a CCB, and a thiazide diuretic.[9]

However, it is challenging to obtain the anticipated outcomes when applying these treatments in real-world clinical settings. Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers, or renin inhibitors, which are usually avoided in patients with bilateral atherosclerotic renal arterial stenosis because of the inherent risk of precipitating acute renal failure.[10] Furthermore, most major randomized clinical trials (RCTs), including Angioplasty and Stenting for Renal Artery Lesions and so on, failed to find significant benefit from endovascular revas cularization in terms of blood pressure control, renal function improvement, or risk for adverse cardiovascular events, when compared with medical therapy.[11] ACEI combined with ARB has a significant antihypertensive effect, but there are risks of hypotension, increased serum creatinine, hyperkalemia and renal injury, so it is not recommended to use ACEI and ARB together in clinical practice.[12]

In contrast to conventional Western medicine (CWM), traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) has a less immediate and pronounced antihypertensive impact, but offer a smoother and safer antihypertensive response.[13] For example, TCM has distinct advantages for mitigating the toxic side effects and significant swings in blood pressure associated with CWM, making it a viable alternative or complementary approach. As such, researchers are recognizing the overall effectiveness of TCM for the prevention and treatment of RH, as well as its unique efficacy in residual renal function.[14] At the same time, the modern pharmacological mechanism of traditional Chinese medicine in the treatment of RH has been recognized by many scholars, which is mainly reflected in improving vascular endothelial function, improving water and sodium retention in the body, and blocking RAAS, inhibiting sympathetic activity, etc.[15]

However, the comparative clinical efficacy of various TCM treatments remains unclear. Indeed, the task of selecting treatment options within the realm of TCM frequently induces confusion among medical practitioners, which may inadvertently deprive patients of the optimal treatment option. Unfortunately, existing literature and systematic reviews of TCM preparations for RH are relatively limited, with a distinct lack of RCTs. Network meta-analysis is a statistical technique that can compare and evaluate the safety and effectiveness of various Chinese herbal medicines for the treatment of RH. Specifically, it utilizes both direct and indirect comparisons to identify the optimal treatment option.

In this study, we will explore the effects of TCM treatment compared to CWM treatment on blood pressure control and renal function improvement in patients with RH through a systematic review of randomized controlled trials and network meta-analysis.

## 方法 / Methods

This study was performed according to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Evaluation and Meta-Analysis Procedures (PRISMA-P).[16] To ensure the comprehensiveness and transparency of our reporting procedures and outcomes, we adhered to the PRISMA Extended Statement.[17] We registered on the PROSPERO and the registration number was CRD42023452172.

The Population-Intervention-Comparison-Outcome-Study Design framework was employed as the criteria for determining eligibility in this study, as outlined below.

The study included RCTs investigating the efficacy of TCM in the treatment of RH. The interventions employed in these studies comprised the administration of formulae, self-formulated formulae, or compounded herbal medicines. Furthermore, the trials were conducted in a blinded manner and the resulting articles were published in various languages.

The control group participants were administered standard therapies for RH, including RAAS blockers, CCBs, diuretics, beta-blockers, alpha-blockers, and a combination of antihypertensive medications. The study participants did not exhibit any other significant underlying medical conditions, such as acute myocardial infarction, severe heart failure, coagulation problems, liver diseases, or malignant tumors. Furthermore, the diagnostic criteria utilized in each study were required to align with the diagnostic criteria for RH acknowledged in Western or Chinese medicine. Furthermore, we did not impose any limitations on age, gender, color, geographic region, disease duration, or ethnicity for the inclusion of patients.

In this study, we identified the 29 most frequently utilized Traditional Chinese Medicines (TCMs) based on prior research and direct clinical observation. The selection procedure involved a comprehensive literature search and careful examination of clinical practice from 44 investigations. The list of TCMs included Bailing capsule (BL), Jinkui Shenqi decoction (JGSQD), Jinkui Shenqi Wan (JGSQW), Pinggan Yangyin Huoxue prescription, Qiangshen Gujing prescription, Qingjiang mixture (QJ), Zishen Yanggan decoction, Tianma Gouteng Yin decoction (TMGTY), Yanghuo Sanqi capsules, Ye Ren Gao decoction (YRG), Yishen Huoxue Huazhuo Jiedu decoction (YSHXHZJD), Zhenwu decoction (ZWD), Xiezhuo Huoxue Pinggan decoction, Zishen Pinggan prescription (ZSPG), Ziyin Pinggan Huayu decoction (ZYPGHY), Huanshuai oral liquid (HS), Maijunan tablet, Fangqi Dihuang (FQDHD), Huangkui capsule (HK), Niaoduqing Keli (NDQ), Tianma Gouteng Yin combined with Qi Ju Di Huang Wan (TMGYQJDH), Xifeng Qingnao decoction (XFQND), Xuefu Zhuyu decoction (XFZYD), Shengao prescription (SG), self-designed Baoshen Jiangya decoction, Zini prescription, Longmu decoction (LMD), Senku Gouteng decoction (SKGTD), and self-diagnosed Jiangya decoction. To facilitate data analysis, the term “regular treatment” was operationally defined as the administration of standardized and symptomatic treatment in accordance with the criteria that were approved during the period in which the study was conducted. Patients who met the criteria for inclusion in the control group were administered CWM treatment for RH. This treatment was provided without any nontraditional Chinese medicine interventions, such as herbal injections. However, patients were allowed to receive appropriate therapeutic and symptomatic treatments for their underlying disease. The duration and dosage of CWM treatment were not limited. In contrast, the treatment group was administered one of the 29 TCMs in conjunction with the matching medicine used in the control group. The dosage and duration of therapy were required to adhere to the prescribed criteria for the respective medicine.

Duplicate publications were removed, and only the most recent or comprehensive papers were retained. Additionally, we omitted the inclusion of meta-analyses, systematic evaluations, reviews, retrospective studies, case reports, animal experiments, cellular experiments, conference summaries, guidelines, indexes, non-randomized controlled trials, non-Chinese/English papers, correlation analyses between evidence and biochemical physicochemical correlations, and investigations pertaining to quality standards of Chinese medicines. Furthermore, nontraditional interventions of TCM, such as TCM injections, acupuncture, massage, and nursing care, were also eliminated from our study if they did not align with the specific disease being investigated. Literature with inaccurate or insufficient data was also omitted from the analysis.

The primary measures of interest encompassed the total effective rate, serum creatinine (SCR), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP), and 24-h urine protein (UTP). The antihypertensive impact was examined by the applicable requirements outlined in the Ministry of Health of the PRC’s Guidelines for Clinical Research on Drugs. The total effective rate was defined as the proportion of patients who achieved “significant effect” and “effective” effect in the total number of patients in the group (TCM or CWM). The term “significant effect” was operationally defined as the patient’s DBP returning to normal or decreasing by 20 mm Hg or more after receiving treatment. The term “effective” was operationally defined as a decrease in DBP of less than 10 mm Hg, but still falling within the normal range, a decrease in DBP of 10 to 20 mm Hg, or a decrease in SBP of 30 mm Hg or more. The term “ineffective” was operationally defined as a decrease in DBP of less than 10 mm Hg, without returning to normal, or a decrease in SBP of less than 30 mm Hg. We exclusively incorporated RCTs that encompassed at least one of these outcome measures to guarantee the credibility and precision of the results.

Computerized searches were conducted across various databases, including the China Knowledge Network, Wanfang database, China Scientific and Technical Journal Library (VIP), China Biomedical Literature Database, PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and Cochrane Library databases. The China Clinical Trial Registry and Clinical Trials were also included in the search. A search was conducted using each of these databases, which was queried until July 2023. To enhance the comprehensiveness of the literature, we additionally incorporated the retrospective inclusion of pertinent references. The search technique employed a combination of subject-specific and unrestricted phrases, encompassing a range of English search terms including “traditional Chinese medicine,” “Zhong Yi Xue,” “Chinese medicine,” “hypertension, renal,” “RH,” “blind,” “controlled clinical trial,” “randomized controlled trial,” and others. The search terms and tactics employed are shown in Table 1, using PubMed as a case study, with adjustments made to the search strategies of other databases in accordance with their respective guidelines. Pubmed database search strategy.

Two researchers conducted a comprehensive review of relevant academic literature and extracted pertinent data from the selected sources. Following the removal of duplicate entries, the titles and abstracts of the remaining articles were carefully reviewed to exclude those that were evidently non-compliant. Subsequently, the remaining literature was thoroughly examined in its entirety to determine its adherence to the predefined inclusion criteria. In cases where inconsistencies arose, these were resolved through collaborative discussion or by seeking input from a third researcher. Data extraction was performed using Microsoft Excel 2019 to gather pertinent information. The process of data extraction encompassed the retrieval of fundamental information, such as the article’s title, primary author, and publication date, among other relevant details. The study characteristics included the interventions employed, the number of people included in the study, the age range of the participants, the duration of the intervention, and any adverse effects observed in either trial and control groups. Additionally, crucial information necessary for assessing the risk of bias in the literature was identified. Lastly, the outcome indicators used in both the trial and control groups were specified.

The research quality of RCTs was evaluated by 2 investigators using the risk of bias assessment tool as specified in the Cochrane Systematic Reviewer’s handbook 5.1.[18] The analysis encompassed 7 distinct domains: random sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of participants and staff, blinding of outcome assessment, incomplete outcome data, selective reporting, and bias of others. These domains were chosen to assess potential sources of bias in the study. Each factor can be classified into 3 categories: “low risk of bias,” “unclear risk of bias,” or “high risk of bias.” In cases of disagreement, resolution was achieved through mutual deliberation or by consultation with an impartial third-party researcher.

Statistical analysis was conducted using the STATA 14.0 program. The statistical measure employed to analyze the effect of each treatment was the mean difference (MD), whereas the risk ratio (RR) was utilized to analyze the influence of dichotomous variables. Each effect was accompanied by its corresponding 95% confidence interval (CI). The network meta-analysis (NMA) technique was employed with STATA 14.0 software for the purpose of data preprocessing. To visually represent the network relationships among various interventions, evidence network diagrams were generated. In these diagrams, the size of the points corresponds to the number of patients associated with each intervention, with larger points indicating a larger patient population. The thickness of the lines connecting the 2 interventions represents the number of included studies.[19] The inconsistency factor (IF), 95% CI and P value of Z test were calculated when there was a closed loop. When P > .05 and the lower limit of 95% CI of IF value was 0, the results of direct comparison and indirect comparison were considered to be consistent, otherwise the closed-loop was considered to be significantly inconsistent. However, we did not incorporate a closed-loop system, which required the use of a consistency model. The ranking of efficacy was determined by calculating the area under the cumulative regression curve, also known as the surface under the cumulative ranking curves (SUCRA).[20] The final reticulated NMA yielded tabulated results that were used to determine the comparatively optimal intervention. A calibration-comparison funnel plot was devised to detect the existence of small-sample effects, and Begg’s and Egger’s tests were conducted to objectively evaluate publication bias. Clustering techniques and two-dimensional plots were employed to construct treatment clusters.[21] Clustering ranking graphs were obtained using the “Clustering” command in the Stata program. Sensitivity analysis was employed to evaluate the robustness and reliability of the pooled results of meta-analysis,[22] and Meta regression analysis was employed to screen out the influencing factors leading to heterogeneity.

## 结果 / Results

A comprehensive search of relevant databases yielded a total of 847 literature sources. After conducting the screening process, a total of 131 publications were identified as duplicates and subsequently excluded from the study. The remaining 716 papers were maintained for further analysis. A total of 323 studies were included in the analysis following the exclusion of meta-analyses, systematic evaluations, reviews, retrospective studies, case reports, animal experiments, cellular experiments, conference summaries, non-randomized controlled trials, analyses of correlation between evidence and biochemistry and physicochemical correlations, and studies on TCM quality standards. This exclusion was performed by screening the titles and abstracts of all studies. Upon further examination of the titles and abstracts of 393 studies, a total of 291 studies were deemed unsuitable because of their focus on diseases that were not relevant to this research. Additionally, 46 studies were excluded because their interventions were unsuitable. These interventions included herbal injections, acupuncture, massage, nursing interventions, non-Chinese medicine interventions, and interventions that were incorrectly implemented. As a result, 52 studies remained for further analysis. According to a comprehensive review of available literature, we identified 5 studies for which the original source material could not be located. Additionally, 3 studies were found to have incomplete or missing data. Ultimately, we identified 44 studies[23–66] conducted between the years 2001 and 2023 as suitable for inclusion in this study. The analysis incorporated a comprehensive set of 44 studies, which encompassed a total sample size of 3939 cases. The interventions employed in these studies primarily involved the administration of TCM, which took the form of scriptures, self-developed formulas, compounded Chinese herbal preparations, or herbal stimulants, in conjunction with CWM. The latter primarily consisted of CCB, ACEI, ARB, or a combination thereof. The treatment regimens encompassed 32 different scriptures, proprietary Chinese medicines, or self-developed formulas. The treatment period varied from 2 weeks to 6 months. The majority of studies demonstrated clinical effectiveness and improvements in renal function-related indicators such as UTP, SCR, BUN, and in-office blood pressure measurements. We critically appraised the methodological quality of the included RCTs in accordance with the Cochrane risk of bias tool. Among the 44 studies assessed for selective bias, 17 explicitly employed random number tables or computer randomization, indicating a low risk of bias. However, the remaining trials relied solely on randomization principles, lacking alternative assignment methods, resulting in unclear assessment of selective bias risk. Descriptions of random allocation concealment were insufficient across all studies. Regarding performance bias, one study used a double-blind design, and another used single-blind, both with low bias risk. However, lack of blinding information in other studies led to an indeterminate assessment. All 44 studies had well-defined inclusion/exclusion criteria, ensuring comparability of trial and control groups. Bias potential related to insufficient outcome data and selective reporting was low, but uncertainties were noted in blinding and random allocation concealment. Overall study quality ranged from moderate to excellent. Clinical efficiency (significantly effective + effective); SCR; BUN; SBP; DBP; and UTP were included as the evaluation metrics, and comparative corrected funnel plots were generated for studies assessing small-sample effects, as shown in Figure 12. The comparison-corrected funnel plots for BUN (Egger test: t = 2.99, P = .007 < .05; Begg test: Z = 0.45, P = .651 > .05) (Fig. 12C), DBP (Egger test: t = −1.39, P = .177 > .05; Begg test: Z = 1.70, P = .088 > .05) (Fig. 12E), UTP(Egger test: t = −0.21, P = .835 > .05; Begg test: Z = 0.43, P = .669 > .05) (Fig. 12F) showed basic symmetry, with the studies distributed approximately symmetrically on both sides of the midline, indicating that a small sample effect was less likely. Conversely, the comparison-corrected funnel plots for the total effective rate (Egger test: t = 6.94, P = .000 < .05; Begg test: Z = 4.17, P = .000 < .05)(Fig. 12A), SCR (Egger test: t = −2.80, P = .011 < .05; Begg test: Z = 2.95, P = .003 < .05)(Fig. 12B), and SBP (Egger test: t = −4.19, P = .000 < .05; Begg test: Z = 3.09, P = .002 < .05)(Fig. 12D) metrics exhibited poor symmetry, suggesting a potential small-sample effect. The reasons for this may be related to differences in the quality of the included studies, small sample sizes, inconsistent treatment regimens, and the different TCM dosage forms and intervention protocols used.

In this meta-analysis, sensitivity analyses were performed for the indicators of greater heterogeneity, SCR, BUN, SBP, DBP, and UTP, to assess the reliability and robustness of the results (Fig. 13A–D). For SCR, BUN, SBP, and DBP, the results were relatively robust when each randomized controlled trial was individually excluded from the present study for analysis. However, excluding one study,[45] the results for UTP were not statistically significant (Fig. 13E). The quality of the literature, type of intervention, study design and data collection methods, and geographic location of the study can all influence the results, which may lead to greater heterogeneity. Nevertheless, we believe that the conclusions of this meta-analysis are still reliable and robust.

## 讨论 / Discussion

Network meta-analysis has significant advantages in evaluating the relative effect of multiple treatment regimens. It can integrate multiple studies, improve statistical power, and provide more comprehensive information, which is helpful to explore the diversity of treatment regimens.[67]

In terms of overall efficacy, SCR, BUN, SBP, DBP, and UTP, the majority of TCM treatments combined with CWM were more effective than CWM alone. In terms of total effective rate, TMGYQJDH was preferable, whereas BL was the most effective at improving SCR. ZYPGHY was the most effective treatment for reducing BUN, but the confidence intervals at 95% covered the entire range of BUN values (MD = −4.40, 95% CI: −64.80, 56.00). As the 95% confidence interval spans a wide range, the precision of this effect is subject to greater uncertainty; thus, future research may need to examine the patient population, treatment regimen, interventions, and other variables in greater depth to determine whether this effect is clinically significant. In terms of lowering SBP, ZSPG in combination with CWM was the most effective, whereas YRG was most effective at lowering DBP. In terms of improving UTP, XFQND was more beneficial when combined with CWM.

Regarding the safety evaluation, the incidence of adverse reactions was lower when TCM was combined with CWM than when CWM was used alone. For the treatment of RH, the combination of TCM and CWM was not associated with any significant adverse effects. Moreover, reducing the dose, discontinuing the drug, or administering symptomatic treatment can effectively alleviate the symptoms of these adverse effects. However, clinicians must still be cognizant of the potential for adverse reactions when combining TCM with CWM.

The choice of antihypertensive drugs is limited by various factors that complicate treatment. For example, the protection of target organs is an important goal of treatment; however, some drugs can cause damage to target organs and must be taken for an extended period of time, with a high risk of recurrence after cessation.[68]

This research indicated that a variety of TCMs both directly and indirectly enhance the renal structure, thereby slowing the progression of hypertension and the decline of renal function.

Specifically, the application of Tianma Gouteng decoction maintains the kidney secretion function to a certain extent, resulting in an increase in the secretion of prostacyclin and a decrease in the production of plasma endothelin in patients with RH, which significantly reduces blood pressure and simultaneously inhibits oxidative stress and inflammation, enhancing the structure and function of the heart and reversing the phenomenon of vascular remodeling.[69]

As a common Chinese patent medicine in nephrology, Bailing capsule is identical to other Chinese patent medicines, such as urethane cleansing granules, renal failure, and Jinshuibao capsule, that have the same effect of reducing 24-h proteinuria and enhancing renal function.[70] Bailing capsule primarily reduces vacuolar degeneration and necrosis of renal tubular epithelial cells by inhibiting the formation of glomerular immune complexes and attenuating the infiltration of interstitial inflammatory cells, promoting the repair of renal tubules to decrease the degree of proteinuria and delay the onset of renal function decline.[71] Bailing capsule is a TCM refined by low temperature fermentation of Cordyceps sinensis strain. Cordyceps sinensis may through targeted animal model of diabetic nephropathy TGF-β1/Smad and TLR4/NF-κB signaling pathway to alleviate kidney injury and associated inflammation.[72]

The protective effect of Cordyceps sinensis is further confirmed by its ability to regulate autophagy, apoptosis, and oxidative stress processes.[73–75]

Moreover, by regulating the expression of cytokines, reducing the level of inflammatory factors, maintaining the expression of protein molecules in the podocytes, and downregulating the expression of kidney injury molecules and protein molecules, Zhenwu decoction can improve the mechanism of chronic kidney disease. In clinical studies, Zhenwu Tang achieved a significant improvement in the clinical symptoms of patients with chronic kidney disease.[76] The preventive/protective mechanism of ZWD can be explained on the basis of the anti-inflammatory potential of important pharmacodynamic components, of which an important class of compounds are flavonoids, which have good antioxidant activity, such as quercetin. Lu et al, according to a study of quercetin was proved through the NF-κB and IRF5 signaling inhibition of M1 macrophages polarization, which helps the upstream signal transduction TLR4/ Myd88 inactivation.[77]

Furthermore, Zishen Pinggan prescription has a significant impact on boosting the body’s immunity and resistance to disease, which can facilitate urination, enhance hematopoietic capacity, combat free radical oxidation, prevent aging, and reduce and lower blood pressure. Additionally, Nourishing Kidney and Liver Formula can modulate the biological effects of estrogen and stimulate the release of nitric oxide to dilate blood vessels, thereby reducing blood pressure.[78]

Ye Ren Gao prescription contains a number of medicinal ingredients, including Radix Rehmanniae Praeparata, Radix Ginseng, Flos Chrysanthemi, Fructus Oyster, and Radix Achyranthis Bidentatae, The antihypertensive effect of this medication can be strengthened by adding Xia Gu Cao, Pueraria Mirifica, Hooker’s Tree, and Sheng Da Huang.[79] Raw radix rehmanniae and cultivated land Huang Junke significantly reduce the high fat feedstuff and diabetic mice induced by STZ TLR4 in pancreas, kidneys, liver, the NF-κB, NLRP3, IL-1β expression level, improve the inflammatory response.[80]

The main ingredient of Xifeng Qingnao decoction, tianma, has the effect of lowering peripheral vascular resistance, dilating small arteries and microvessels, and increasing cerebrovascular blood flow.[81] Tianma-gouteng Decoction can delay vascular aging in spontaneous hypertension model rats, and the possible mechanism is to enhance the expression of MFN2, improve intracellular oxidative stress, and regulate the expression of sening-related proteins, so as to realize the effect of delaying vascular aging.[82]

Moreover, the components of Crocus sativus, Rhizoma linguistic Chuanxiong, and Semen cassiae can inhibit platelet aggregation, enhance the deformation ability of red blood cells, and simultaneously dilate blood vessels.[83] Crocus sativus also exhibits antihypertensive, sedative, and anticonvulsant properties. Additionally, some of the Chinese herbs in the composition of Xifeng Qingnao decoction exert significant antihypertensive effects, which also serves to protect cardio-cerebral function and reduce cardiovascular damage.[84]

By addition and subtraction, Yishen Huoxue Huazhuo Jiedu decoction (YSHXHZJD) is derived from Liu Wei Di Huang Wan. The resulting formula has a nephroprotective effect that can reduce the inflammatory damage in diabetic nephropathy,[85] lower the malondialdehyde content, and inhibit the activity of superoxide dismutase, thereby protecting the renal tissues of the body from damage caused by free radicals.[86]

In this study, we explore the therapeutic mechanism of various Chinese herbal remedies for RH. By modulating inflammatory factors, anti-oxidative stress, and other pathways, these herbal remedies exert beneficial effects on improving chronic kidney disease. Cluster analysis revealed that NDQ + CWM and HS + CWM performed better with respect to the total effective rate and SCR, ZWD + CWM and BL + CWM performed better with respect to SCR and BUN, and ZSPG + CWM and YRG + CWM performed better with respect to SBP and DBP. Several formulations that employ the method of “benefiting the kidneys and calming the liver, activating blood, and resolving phlegm” were more effective at reducing blood pressure and enhancing renal function. Whether this result is attributable to TCM evidence-based treatment or the more mature and standardized clinical application of TCM formulations should be investigated through future research.

However, network meta-analysis also faces some potential problems, including the reliance on hypothetical premise, the influence of heterogeneity and consistency, and quality and availability of data and release the likely impact of bias and preference.[67] Despite substantial heterogeneity in some of the indicators, sensitivity analysis revealed that the results were robust. The heterogeneity of these indicators may be attributable to study data variability, including distinct patient groups, differences in interventions, and variety in study design. Despite our efforts to eradicate potential sources of heterogeneity in the sensitivity analysis, the diversity of the studies may still contribute to heterogeneity. Heterogeneity may be affected by variables like study location, sample size, and study quality. Although we attempted to exclude these variables, they may still have had some impact on the results.

To examine the heterogeneity of SCR, BUN, SBP, and UTP indicators, we conducted a comprehensive qualitative analysis. Methods and measurements may have differed between studies, such as sample collection and processing techniques and the laboratory techniques used to calculate the indices, which may have contributed to the heterogeneity to some extent. The diversity of the patient population may also influence the outcomes; that is, variations in age, gender, and disease severity may contribute to increased heterogeneity. To better understand this phenomenon, future research should place greater emphasis on the standardization of methods and the reporting of more exhaustive data. Our study raises several key questions: do different types of TCM, such as capsules, tablets, and decoctions, differ in their therapeutic effects, and how can the best dosage form be determined? Our results provide important clues for future research. In addition, we need to understand whether TCM causes adverse reactions and whether these reactions are related to specific ingredients, doses, or other factors. Further clinical studies will help to solve these problems. In addition, we also need to investigate whether patients with different disease severity have different treatment responses to different types of TCM and explore whether individualized treatment regimens can be developed based on disease characteristics. In addition, we need to understand whether these TCM are suitable for patients with other systemic diseases and find more accurate evaluation methods to evaluate the treatment effect. Future research should focus on addressing these issues to strengthen the credibility of the efficacy of TCM. We expect that in the future, more high-quality clinical studies will adopt advanced methods to promote the progress of TCM research and provide more reliable basis for clinical practice.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Overall, the quality of the studies ranged from moderate to excellent, with well-defined inclusion criteria and generally low potential for biases related to outcome data and selective reporting. According to all commonly measured outcomes, the combined treatment of RH with CWM and TCM is superior to that with CWM alone. TMGYQJDH + CWM exhibits the best total efficacy, ZSPG + CWM and YRG + CWM have the best effect on blood pressure, and ZWD + CWM and BL + CWM have the best effect on renal function. Combined TCM and CWM treatment for RH is also associated with a reduced incidence of adverse effects. Combined TCM and CWM can effectively reduce blood pressure and enhance 24-h urine protein, SCR, and BUN indices, resulting in better protection of renal target organs, which has positive implications for preventing and reducing the incidence of end-stage renal disease. Our study identifies optimal TCM–CWM combination treatments, providing clinical application guidance and establishing a solid clinical foundation for the use of TCM in RH therapy. Nonetheless, the results of this research should be confirmed through additional large-sample, double-blind, multi-center randomized controlled trials.
