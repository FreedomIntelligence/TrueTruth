## English Abstract

Uncontrolled hypertension presents a substantial challenge to healthcare systems. This study was designed to investigate whether pharmacogenomics-guided personalized medication regimens could enhance blood pressure control and medication optimization in patients with hypertension compared to conventional management approaches.

In this multicenter, single-blind, prospective, randomized controlled trial, 776 hypertensive outpatients were enrolled and allocated to either a personalized medication group or a control group across eight participating hospitals. All patients received 4 weeks of standardized hypertension management, followed by a first follow-up. Subsequently, the personalized medication group adjusted antihypertensive therapy according to seven predefined pharmacogenetic polymorphisms, while the control group continued standard treatment. After an additional 4 weeks, a second follow-up was performed to reassess blood pressure, medication utilization, and adverse events.

The personalized medication group achieved a notably higher blood pressure control rate compared to the control group, with female patients demonstrating particularly pronounced benefits. Additionally, the personalized medication group required fewer antihypertensive medications. The incidence of adverse events, including reactions to angiotensin II receptor blockers, angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, β-blockers, calcium channel blockers, and diuretics, did not differ significantly between groups. Multifactorial analysis indicated that personalized medication significantly lowered the risk of uncontrolled blood pressure. Furthermore, the CYP2C9*3 gene variant was associated with uncontrolled blood pressure after standardized treatment.

Pharmacogenomics-guided personalized medication regimens significantly improved blood pressure control and optimized medication usage without increasing adverse events. These findings highlight the potential of pharmacogenomics to guide precision therapy in hypertension management.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension is one of the most prevalent chronic diseases worldwide, affecting over one billion adults and posing a significant threat to global public health [1]. Its prevalence continues to rise, especially in Asia, where the percentage of adults with hypertension has increased from 24 to 28% in recent years [2]. According to the WHO, uncontrolled hypertension markedly elevates cardiovascular risk and mortality, placing a substantial economic and healthcare burden on society [3]. Despite the widespread use of antihypertensive medications, lifestyle interventions, and health management strategies, global blood pressure (BP) control rates remain suboptimal, ranging from only 7 to 50% in different populations [4–6]. Such limited success is often attributed to factors like poor medication adherence, drug-related adverse effects, and the lack of a truly personalized therapeutic approach that accounts for each patient's unique biological variability.

Recent advances in pharmacogenomics offer promising avenues to overcome these challenges. By leveraging genetic information, clinicians can move toward personalized antihypertensive treatment strategies that optimize drug choice and dosing for individual patients [7]. Currently, pharmacogenomics brings new opportunities for personalized treatment of hypertension. Through high-throughput genetic analysis, several genetic polymorphisms have been identified that affect the efficacy and adverse reactions of antihypertensive drugs, particularly in Asian populations. For instance, the I/D polymorphism of the ACE gene significantly influences the efficacy of angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEI) [8], while the CYP2C9*3 variant reduces the catalytic activity toward losartan, thus diminishing its BP-lowering effect [9]. Variations in the ADRB1 gene alter β-blocker receptor signaling [10]. and the CYP2D6*10 mutant allele impairs β-blocker clearance, impacting both effectiveness and safety [11,12]. Mutations in CYP3A5 slow the metabolism of calcium channel blockers (CCBs), increasing systemic exposure and adverse event risks [13], and CACNA1C polymorphisms can directly reduce CCB efficacy [14]. Additionally, ADD1 gene polymorphisms influence BP regulation and the responsiveness to diuretics [15]. By incorporating these pharmacogenomic insights into clinical decision-making, we can tailor drug selection and dosing more accurately, potentially improving treatment outcomes and minimizing adverse effects.

However, despite these promising findings, most clinical evidence in this area is derived from single-center studies with limited sample sizes and heterogeneous populations. Moreover, much of the existing data do not adequately account for standardized management measures prior to personalized medication adjustments. To address these gaps, our study aimed to conduct a multicenter, single-blind, prospective, randomized controlled trial in an Asian population. We integrated seven key genetic loci related to antihypertensive drug response into personalized medication regimens, building upon a standardized hypertension management foundation. Through this approach, we sought to determine whether a pharmacogenomics-guided personalized treatment strategy could improve BP control, optimize medication utilization, and maintain safety, ultimately guiding more effective and precise hypertension management strategies.

## 方法 / Methods

This study focuses on outpatient hypertensive populations, aiming to evaluate whether pharmacogenomics-based individualized therapy improves BP control compared to conventional management. This multicenter, single-blind, prospective, randomized controlled trial was conducted across eight hospitals in Fujian Province, China, between March 1, 2022, and July 31, 2024. The study adhered to the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and received approval from the Ethics Committee of the First Affiliated Hospital of Fujian Medical University ([2021]213). All participants provided written informed consent. The trial was registered with the Chinese Clinical Trial Registry (ChiCTR2200057507) (https://www.chictr.org.cn/). The flowchart of the study design is presented in Fig. 1.

Flowchart of the study design.

Based on preliminary data and clinically relevant differences, we assumed a 10% difference in BP control rates between the personalized medication group and the control group at the second follow-up. Assuming a control group BP control rate of approximately 60%, a two-sided alpha of 0.05, and a power of 80%, we estimated that approximately 700 patients (350 per group) would be required. Considering a 10–15% attrition rate, 776 patients were initially enrolled to maintain adequate power after potential exclusions.

Eligible participants were adults (≥18 years) with primary hypertension (stage 1–2), defined as SBP of 140–179 mmHg and/or DBP of 90–109 mmHg. Exclusion criteria included secondary hypertension, use of three or more antihypertensive drugs at enrollment, concurrent medications affecting BP or interacting with study drugs, a heart rate of 55 bpm or less, malignancy, severe hepatic or renal insufficiency, severe heart failure (ejection fraction <40%), recent stroke, severe trauma or major surgery, and pregnancy or lactation.

A total of 978 patients were screened for eligibility at the outset of the study. During the screening phase, 202 patients were excluded due to screening failure. Specifically, 164 patients did not meet the inclusion criteria for the following reasons: secondary hypertension (n = 32), heart failure (n = 36), severe renal insufficiency (n = 32), severe hepatic insufficiency (n = 13), heart rate of 55 bpm or less (n = 28), and other comorbidities (e.g., malignancies, n = 23). Additionally, 38 patients withdrew consent prior to randomization. Consequently, 776 patients were randomized into two groups: the personalized medication group and the control group. All participants initially underwent a 4-week standardized antihypertensive management. Following this, the personalized medication group had their treatment regimens adjusted based on genetic data, whereas the control group received medication adjustments in accordance with the hypertension management guidelines. During the study, 55 patients in the personalized medication group and 32 in the control group dropped out. The reasons for dropout included loss to follow-up (n = 47), withdrawal of informed consent (n = 12), incomplete genotype data (n = 20), and missing baseline BP or clinical data (n = 8) (Supplementary Table 2). For the final analysis, 689 patients (333 in the personalized medication group and 356 in the control group) were included per protocol, and all 776 randomized patients were analyzed by intention-to-treat.

Participants were randomly assigned in a 1 : 1 ratio to either a personalized medication group or a control group. A computer-generated randomization sequence was created by an independent statistician not involved in patient recruitment or data collection. This was a single-blind trial in which patients were not informed of their group allocation. The clinicians and investigators managing treatments knew the assignments, but patients received standard counseling and instructions to minimize bias.

All participants underwent 4 weeks of standardized hypertension management according to the “National Guideline for Hypertension Management in China” before starting the assigned intervention [16]. This standardized phase included lifestyle counseling, standardized BP measurement training, and, if indicated, guideline-based adjustments to antihypertensive therapy. The purpose was to reduce inter-center variability and establish a comparable baseline.

After the first follow-up, the personalized medication group received antihypertensive treatments guided by seven predefined genetic polymorphisms (ACE, CYP2C9*3, ADRB1, CYP2D6*10, CYP3A5, CACNA1C, ADD1). Based on the pharmacogenetic table, medications that do not meet the phenotype of ’normal sensitivity’ or ’normal metabolism’ should be discontinued and replaced with different class of antihypertensive drugs that fulfill these phenotypes (Supplemental Table 1). All antihypertensive drugs were administered at standard doses. The control group continued receiving standard antihypertensive therapy per guidelines, without genetic guidance. Both groups were followed for an additional 4 weeks, after which a second follow-up visit was conducted to reassess BP, adverse events, and medication utilization.

The primary endpoint was the BP control rate at the second follow-up, defined as SBP less than 140 mmHg and DBP less than 90 mmHg. Patients not meeting these criteria were considered to have uncontrolled BP. Secondary endpoints included the number of antihypertensive medications used and the incidence of adverse events, such as cough (caused by ACEIs), hyperkalemia [caused by ACEIs or angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs)], hypokalemia, renal function abnormalities (caused by diuretics), edema, gum hyperplasia (caused by CCBs), bradycardia, and orthostatic hypotension (caused by β-blockers).

BP was measured using a validated automatic electronic sphygmomanometer (HBP-1300; Omron Healthcare, Liaoning, China). Patients rested quietly for at least 5 min in a seated position, with back support, arms relaxed, and feet flat on the ground. They were instructed to avoid caffeine, smoking, and strenuous exercise before measurement. The BP cuff was positioned on the upper arm at heart level, and three consecutive readings were averaged to obtain the final SBP and DBP values.

Genotyping of the seven selected single nucleotide polymorphisms (ACE: rs1799752, CYP2C9*3: rs1057910, ADRB1: rs1801253, CYP2D6*10: rs1065852, CYP3A5: rs776746, CACNA1C: rs2238032, ADD1: rs4961) was performed using a commercial fluorescence-based PCR assay kit (Tianlong, Xi’an, China). To prepare the samples, 10 μl of blood was treated with a specialized extraction solution to deplete erythrocytes, and 1 μl of the leukocyte fraction was added to the kit. PCR amplification was conducted using a fluorescent quantitative PCR detection system (Fascan 48E, Xi’an, China) following the manufacturer's instructions. Genotype calls were made based on the fluorescence signals and amplification curves.

Data were analyzed using SPSS version 25.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, New York, USA). Continuous variables were expressed as mean ± standard deviation or median (interquartile range) and compared using Student's t-tests or Mann-Whitney U tests as appropriate. Categorical variables were reported as frequencies and percentages and analyzed using Pearson's chi-square or Fisher's exact tests. Holm-Bonferroni adjustments were applied for multiple comparisons of secondary endpoints. Multivariable logistic regression models were constructed to assess the association between personalized medication and uncontrolled BP. A P value less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## 结果 / Results

Of the 776 randomly assigned hypertensive patients, all were included in the intention-to-treat analyses. There were 87 dropouts (32 in the control group and 55 in the personalized medication group). Comparison of the reasons for dropout between the two groups revealed no significant differences. Additionally, baseline data analysis showed no significant differences between the two groups in terms of age, sex, height, weight, waist circumference, lack of exercise, high-salt diet, smoking, alcohol consumption, psychological stress, heart rate, SBP, and DBP (Supplemental Table 2).

A total of 689 patients completed the study per protocol and were included in the subsequent analysis, with 333 in the personalized medication group and 356 in the control group. At baseline, there were no statistically significant differences between the two groups in terms of age, sex, height, weight, waist circumference, physical inactivity, high-salt diet, smoking, alcohol consumption, psychological stress, heart rate, SBP, DBP, or the usage rates of ACEI, β-blockers, and CCB. However, the personalized medication group exhibited a lower initial BP control rate (34.53 vs. 43.54%, P = 0.016) and lower utilization of angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARB; 42.04 vs. 52.25%, P = 0.007) and diuretics (6.01 vs. 14.04%, P < 0.001) compared to the control group (Table 1).

Following an additional 4 weeks of intervention (second follow-up), the personalized medication group achieved a higher BP control rate than the control group (81.68 vs. 72.75%, P = 0.005, Holm-Bonferroni adjusted P = 0.015). The personalized medication group also had a higher BP control rate at the second follow-up compared with the first follow-up (81.68 vs. 70.57%, P < 0.001) (Fig. 2A). In terms of medication usage, there were no significant differences at the first follow-up. By the second follow-up, the personalized medication group used fewer types of antihypertensive drugs than the control group (P = 0.005, adjusted P = 0.015) (Fig. 2B), and fewer patients required three or more drugs (10.51 vs. 16.01%, P = 0.044). Throughout the study, no significant differences were observed in adverse reaction rates (including ARB, ACEI, β-blockers, CCB, and diuretics) between the two groups (P > 0.05, adjusted P > 0.05) (Fig. 2C, Supplemental Table 4).

Subgroup analyses by sex revealed that, among men, the personalized medication group had a higher BP control rate at the second follow-up compared to the control group (78.13 vs. 70.95%), although this did not reach statistical significance (P = 0.100). Nevertheless, men in the personalized medication group demonstrated a significant improvement from the first to the second follow-up (69.27 vs. 78.13%, P = 0.027). In contrast, women benefited more substantially from personalized medication. At the second follow-up, female patients in the personalized medication group had significantly higher control rates than both the control group (86.52 vs. 75.34%, P = 0.016) and their own first follow-up results (86.52 vs. 72.34%, P = 0.003) (Fig. 2D).

To further confirm these findings, an interaction analysis was conducted using logistic regression models that included treatment group, sex, and their interaction term. The interaction was statistically significant (P = 0.003), indicating that the effect of personalized medication differed by gender. For female patients, personalized medication significantly improved BP control [odds ratio (OR) = 2.101, 95% confidence interval (95% CI) = 1.139–3.878, P = 0.018], whereas in male patients, the association did not reach statistical significance (OR = 1.462, 95% CI = 0.929–2.302, P = 0.101).

Subsequently, we analyzed 419 patients with uncontrolled hypertension at enrollment, and the baseline characteristics were shown in Supplemental Table 5 in Supplemental Digital Content. Both groups improved BP control after the standardized management phase (personalized medication: 61.47%; control: 57.21%, P = 0.376). However, at the second follow-up, the personalized medication group achieved a significantly higher BP control rate than the control group (77.98 vs. 63.68%, P = 0.001, adjusted P = 0.003). The personalized medication group's BP control rate at the second follow-up also exceeded its own first follow-up level (77.98 vs. 61.47%, P < 0.001) (Supplemental Figure 1A). Additionally, this group used fewer antihypertensive medication classes (P = 0.024, adjusted P = 0.048) (Supplemental Figure 1B), and fewer patients required three or more drugs (11.01 vs. 19.40%, P = 0.023). Adverse reaction rates remained comparable between groups (P > 0.05, adjusted P > 0.05) (Supplemental Figure 1C).

In the analysis by sex, both male and female patients in the personalized medication group had higher BP control rates at the second follow-up compared to the control group (men: 75.38 vs. 61.34%, P = 0.017; women: 81.82 vs. 67.07%, P = 0.027), and also higher than the control rate at the first follow-up (men: 75.38 vs. 58.23%, P = 0.002; women: 81.82 vs. 64.77%, P = 0.003) (Supplemental Figure 1D).

In the overall population, the personalized medication group exhibited a significant reduction in SBP from the first to the second follow-up (130.12 ± 12.20 to 127.98 ± 11.05 mmHg, P = 0.004), whereas no significant change was observed in the control group (129.48 ± 12.88 vs. 129.51 ± 12.55 mmHg). The change in SBP (ΔSBP) was significantly greater in the personalized medication group than in the control group (-2.14 ± 13.41 vs. 0.03 ± 9.65 mmHg, P = 0.011). Additionally, mean arterial pressure (MAP) and pulse pressure (PP) in the personalized medication group were both significantly reduced at the second follow-up compared to their own baseline values (MAP: 95.98 ± 8.57 vs. 97.10 ± 9.37 mmHg, P < 0.05; PP: 48.01 ± 9.83 vs. 49.53 ± 11.28 mmHg, P < 0.05), while no significant changes were observed in the control group (Supplemental Table 6). Similarly, consistent results were obtained with intention-to-treat analysis (Supplementary Table 7).

In patients with uncontrolled hypertension at enrollment, the personalized medication group also demonstrated significant improvements. SBP in the second follow-up decreased compared to its first follow-up (129.91 ± 10.72 vs. 132.92 ± 11.37 mmHg, P = 0.004), and also decreased compared to the control group's second follow-up (129.91 ± 10.72 vs. 132.30 ± 12.83 mmHg, P = 0.04). Moreover, MAP and PP were both significantly reduced at the second follow-up compared to the first follow-up in the personalized medication group (97.47 ± 8.24 vs. 99.33 ± 10.36 mmHg, P = 0.021, 48.65 ± 10.22 vs. 50.38 ± 11.87 mmHg, P = 0.008). For patients whose BP was already controlled at enrollment, no significant intergroup differences or notable improvements were observed, indicating that the most pronounced benefits of personalized medication occurred in patients with initially uncontrolled hypertension (Supplemental Table 8).

Logistic regression analysis demonstrated that personalized medication reduced the risk of uncontrolled BP (unadjusted OR = 0.599, 95% CI = 0.417–0.861, P = 0.006). This association persisted after adjusting for age, sex, height, weight, waist circumference, smoking, alcohol consumption, physical inactivity, high-salt diet, psychological stress, hypertensive medications and genotypes (multivariable-adjusted OR3 = 0.623, 95% CI = 0.419–0.928, P = 0.020). ## 讨论 / Discussion

Our study provides robust evidence that pharmacogenomics-guided personalized medication, when implemented alongside standardized hypertension management, can significantly improve BP control without increasing adverse events. Notably, these benefits were most pronounced in patients who entered the study with uncontrolled hypertension, an especially challenging subgroup due to their heightened risk of future cardiovascular morbidity and mortality [17,18]. By tailoring drug selection and dosing to individual genetic polymorphisms, we not only achieved more rapid and stable BP reduction in these difficult-to-treat patients but also potentially altered their long-term clinical trajectory. Achieving prompt control may therefore translate into substantial reductions in future cardiovascular events and a consequent decrease in healthcare burdens, underscoring the value of personalized therapeutic strategies in clinical practice.

A particularly noteworthy aspect of our findings is the enhanced efficacy of personalized medication strategies in female patients. Although both men and women benefitted from the pharmacogenomics-driven approach, the magnitude of improvement was greater in women. This observation aligns with a growing body of literature suggesting that gender differences are integral to understanding antihypertensive drug responses [19–21]. From a pharmacokinetic perspective, women may exhibit distinct patterns of drug absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion, often influenced by variations in body composition, plasma protein binding, and enzyme activity [19]. Hormonal fluctuations, including differences in estrogen and progesterone levels, can modulate vascular tone, endothelial function, and sympathetic activity, thereby impacting drug responsiveness [22]. In addition, renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system regulation may differ by sex, potentially altering the sensitivity to certain antihypertensive agents [20]. Furthermore, emerging evidence indicates that genetic variants affecting drug-metabolizing enzymes, transporters, and receptors may have differential penetrance or expression in women, altering both the efficacy and safety profiles of commonly used antihypertensives like valsartan, olmesartan, and hydrochlorothiazide [23]. Women have also been reported to experience different patterns of adverse drug events compared to men [24], suggesting that a one-size-fits-all approach to antihypertensive therapy may not be optimal. Taken together, these multifaceted sex-related influences underscore the importance of considering sex-specific factors when implementing precision medicine, and our results highlight the particular value that pharmacogenomics may offer to female patients who have historically been underrepresented in tailored treatment paradigms.

It is also crucial to acknowledge the foundational importance of standardized hypertension management. Both the personalized medication and control groups showed significant BP improvements after standardized management alone, underscoring its indispensable role as a cornerstone of hypertension care [25–27]. While adding pharmacogenomic insights offers an additional layer of refinement, not all patients may require this step. For those who have already achieved adequate BP control through conventional approaches, further genetic testing and medication adjustments might not yield meaningful benefits, thus conserving healthcare resources and avoiding unnecessary complexity.

Our genetic analyses identified that only variations in ARB-related genes were associated with uncontrolled BP in a Chinese population. Although single genetic markers may not fully predict treatment outcomes, the high prevalence of multiple mutations among participants highlights the polygenic nature of antihypertensive response [28–30]. As genotyping technologies advance and costs decline, future research should integrate multiple single nucleotide polymorphisms into polygenic risk scores and use machine learning algorithms to refine predictive accuracy. Such models could facilitate even more precise antihypertensive regimens, moving us closer to a fully individualized standard of care.

Despite these promising findings, several challenges remain. We did not quantitatively measure medication adherence, instead relying on standardized counseling and written instructions. Additionally, although we implemented a run-in phase to standardize antihypertensive regimens, baseline imbalances in ARB and diuretic usage could introduce residual confounding. Stratified randomization or other advanced allocation methods may help ensure more balanced comparisons and a clearer attribution of effects to the pharmacogenomic intervention. It is important to acknowledge that our cohort was drawn from an Asian population, and genetic determinants of antihypertensive drug response vary across ethnicities and regions. Larger, multicenter trials are needed to validate our findings in more diverse populations. Moreover, the modest sample size and relatively brief follow-up duration limit our capacity to assess the long-term sustainability of BP control and its eventual effects on cardiovascular outcomes. Future studies with larger cohorts, prolonged observation, and dynamic predictive modeling may clarify whether these early gains lead to sustained clinical benefits.

## 结论 / Conclusion

This study supports the integration of pharmacogenomics into hypertension management, particularly for patients with uncontrolled BP and female patients who may derive disproportionate benefit from individualized regimens. While obstacles such as cost, logistics, adherence monitoring, and the need for broader external validation persist, ongoing advances in genetic testing technologies and predictive analytics are likely to accelerate the routine clinical use of pharmacogenomics. Over time, these efforts may drive the field toward a future in which precision antihypertensive therapy becomes both an attainable and sustainable cornerstone of patient-centered cardiovascular care.
