## English Abstract

Renal denervation (RDN) is an emerging therapy for resistant hypertension. The therapeutic intravascular ultrasound (TIVUS) system offers a nonocclusive, multidirectional approach, but its long-term efficacy and safety remain unknown.

We prospectively followed 12 patients with severe resistant hypertension who underwent bilateral ultrasound RDN as part of the TIVUS trial. Office BP was measured at baseline, 1, 3, 6, and 12 months; 24-h ambulatory BP at years 1, 2, 5, and 10. Estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) and adverse events were tracked. BP changes were analyzed using mixed-effects models adjusted for baseline covariates.

Mean age was 65 ± 8 years; 75% had diabetes. Baseline office BP averaged 176/84 mm Hg. The procedure was completed in all patients without complications. Systolic office BP decreased by 22.1 mm Hg at 1 month and 36.2 mm Hg at 12 months (both p < 0.001). At 10 years, office BP reductions were −38.6/−13.3 mm Hg (p < 0.001). Ambulatory BP declined from 149.4 ± 9.8 to 128.4 ± 10.0 mm Hg (ASBP) and from 85.0 ± 8.5 to 74.4 ± 9.2 mm Hg (ADBP). The 10-year change in antihypertensive defined daily doses was −1.70 (95% CI −2.75 to −0.38; p = 0.002). eGFR declined modestly (68.6 to 60.2 mL/min/1.73 m2), consistent with aging. No procedure-related adverse events occurred.

TIVUS ultrasound RDN achieved durable BP reduction with preserved renal function over 10 years.

## 背景 / Background

Resistant hypertension remains a challenging clinical problem associated with elevated risks of stroke, myocardial infarction, and kidney failure [1, 2]. Overactivity of the renal sympathetic nervous system is a key contributor to resistant hypertension, driving vasoconstriction, sodium retention, and activation of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. Catheter-based renal denervation (RDN) emerged over a decade ago as a novel approach to modulate this pathway by ablating renal sympathetic nerves [1]. Early open-label trials of radiofrequency (RF) RDN demonstrated substantial office BP reductions (e.g., −32/−12 mm Hg at 6 months in SYMPLICITY HTN-2) [3]. However, the field faced a setback when the sham-controlled SYMPLICITY HTN-3 trial found no significant BP difference between RDN and sham at 6 months [1], attributed in part to procedural and design limitations, including incomplete circumferential ablation, operator inexperience, and variability in medication adherence, as highlighted in subsequent post hoc analyses [4].

In recent years, there has been a resurgence of evidence supporting RDN efficacy when performed with optimized techniques and trial designs [1]. Multiple randomized trials using both RF and ultrasound-based systems have confirmed modest but significant BP reductions in hypertensive patients off and on medications [5–7]. Notably, in 2023, the US Food and Drug Administration approved two RDN devices (a RF catheter and an ultrasound balloon system) for clinical use in hypertension, reflecting growing recognition of RDN as a legitimate adjunct therapy. Most RDN trials have followed patients for 6–36 months, and questions remain about the long-term durability and safety of the BP-lowering effect. Sympathetic nerve fibers can regenerate over time, potentially attenuating initially positive results [6, 8–10]. Furthermore, establishing long-term impacts on renal artery integrity and kidney function is important.

Ultrasound energy has emerged as an alternative to RF for RDN, offering the ability to circumferentially ablate periarterial nerves without continuous catheter-wall contact. The therapeutic intravascular ultrasound (TIVUS) catheter system (SoniVie, Israel) emits high-intensity, non-focused ultrasound in a radially symmetric fashion, allowing “free-floating” positioning in the artery with effective nerve ablation to a depth of up to 10 mm. Unlike balloon-based ultrasound catheters that occlude the artery during energy delivery, TIVUS does not require arterial occlusion or fluid cooling [11–15]. A first-in-man multicenter study conducted between 2013 and 2015 demonstrated the feasibility and short-term efficacy of this technology in 39 patients with resistant hypertension. RDN with TIVUS achieved significant 6-month office BP reductions (−30/−14 mm Hg) with a favorable safety profile [16]. These initial results were promising, but only a short-term follow-up was reported. Given the need to establish whether RDN using ultrasound energy provides sustained benefit, we conducted a 10-year follow-up study of patients who underwent RDN with the TIVUS system. We report the decade-long outcomes in terms of office and ambulatory BP, renal function, and clinical safety. We also compare these long-term findings to the initial trial and other available long-term RDN data.

## 方法 / Methods

This was a prospective, two-center, single-arm longitudinal study conducted at university hospitals, designed to evaluate the 10-year outcomes following ultrasound RDN. The study included 12 consecutive patients with severe resistant hypertension who were enrolled and treated at our institution as part of the trial, with all procedures performed by the same operator. Technical success of the RDN procedure was defined pragmatically as successful catheter deployment and completion of the intended bilateral ultrasound ablations without procedural complications, consistent with early-generation RDN studies such as the SYMPLICITY HTN-1 trial. At the time these procedures were performed, no validated intra-procedural markers of denervation completeness were available, and treatment delivery was guided by operator judgment [17]. Patients underwent a multistep screening process that included eligibility assessment, home BP monitoring, 24-h ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM), and renal angiography. Patient screening, exclusions, and follow-up are summarized in Figure 1. Of the 18 initially consented patients, six failed screening due to predefined exclusion criteria, including unsuitable renal artery anatomy identified on angiography (n = 4; accessory renal arteries, insufficient vessel length or diameter) and impaired renal function below protocol thresholds (n = 2).

Study flow diagram. ABPM, ambulatory blood pressure monitoring; SOC, standard of care; OSBP, office systolic blood pressure; ODBP, office diastolic blood pressure; ASBP, ambulatory systolic blood pressure; ADBP, ambulatory diastolic blood pressure.

Resistant hypertension was defined at the time of the treatment as an office systolic BP ≥160 mm Hg despite adherence to at least three antihypertensive medications of different classes (including a diuretic) at maximally tolerated doses [3]. This systolic BP threshold reflects the historical inclusion criteria of early RDN trials conducted during the study enrollment period (2011–2013), which preferentially targeted patients with severe, uncontrolled hypertension [18, 19]. Consequently, the present cohort represents a population with particularly advanced and treatment-refractory disease, as reflected by the markedly elevated baseline office systolic blood pressure (SBP). Although resistant hypertension was defined by prior treatment with three or more antihypertensive drug classes, including a diuretic, baseline medication use reflects contemporaneous prescribing at study entry. In a subset of patients, diuretics had been temporarily withheld or discontinued due to clinical considerations such as impaired renal function, electrolyte abnormalities, or intolerance, despite prior documented use consistent with the definition of resistant hypertension, a practice that has been reported in other resistant hypertension cohorts and real-world studies [20].

Patients were screened between 2011 and 2013 and were eligible if they had essential (primary) hypertension and met the BP criteria on two separate visits. Key exclusion criteria were secondary causes of hypertension, significant renal artery abnormalities (e.g., stenosis, dual renal arteries), and advanced chronic kidney disease (baseline estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] <45 mL/min/1.73 m2). Patients with eGFR in the moderate CKD range were not automatically excluded if considered clinically stable, as long as eGFR was ≥45 at screening; 5 (42%) patients had CKD stage 2–3a at baseline. All patients provided written informed consent. The study was approved by the local institutional review board and conducted by the Declaration of Helsinki.

Patients underwent RDN using the TIVUS ultrasound catheter (Cardiosonic/SoniVie) in a catheterization laboratory setting. Procedures were performed by an experienced interventional specialist under conscious sedation. After femoral arterial access (8-Fr sheath), bilateral renal angiography was performed to confirm suitable anatomy (renal artery diameter ≥4 mm and length ≥20 mm, with no significant stenoses). The TIVUS catheter was then advanced to each renal artery over a guidewire. The device’s ultrasonic transducers were activated to deliver high-frequency, non-focused ultrasound energy to the arterial wall in a circumferential pattern. Unlike balloon-based systems, the TIVUS catheter does not anchor to the vessel wall, allowing blood flow during energy delivery [1]. A series of ultrasound emissions was applied at multiple longitudinal levels of each renal artery to achieve full-length coverage of the periarterial space. The energy delivery protocol (number and duration of sonifications) was as per manufacturer guidelines and operator discretion to ensure thorough nerve ablation; typically, 3–5 ultrasound bursts, 40 s each, were delivered in each renal artery, with slight catheter repositioning between emissions to treat different segments [16]. Intra-arterial nitroglycerin was administered after RDN if any spasm was observed. Patients received hydration and were monitored overnight. Procedural complications (such as renal artery dissection, access-site hematoma, or hypotensive episodes) were recorded.

Follow-up assessments were scheduled at 1, 3, 6-, and 12-months post-procedure, and annually thereafter. Office BP was measured at each clinic visit using an automated oscillometric sphygmomanometer, with patients seated quietly for ≥5 min, and the average of three readings was recorded according to guideline recommendations [4]. For long-term follow-up beyond the first year, 24-h ABPM was performed annually to provide a more robust assessment of BP control devoid of any white-coat effect. ABPM was conducted with a validated oscillometric device (spaced measurements every 30 min during daytime, every 60 min at night). Renal function was assessed by measuring serum creatinine and eGFR at baseline and during follow-up visits. The core lab-calculated eGFR (MDRD formula) was recorded at baseline, 30 days, one, two, five, and ten years. An acute kidney injury was defined as a rise in serum creatinine ≥0.3 mg/dL or ≥50% from baseline within 30 days of the procedure, per KDIGO criteria. All adverse events and hospitalizations were documented at follow-up visits or by telephone interviews at yearly intervals. Particular attention was paid to any hypertensive crises or deaths.

To account for changes in antihypertensive therapy over time, we included two pharmacological covariates in the models. Delta DDD was defined as the difference between the total defined daily dose (DDD) of all prescribed antihypertensive medications at baseline and at 10-year follow-up. The DDD for each drug was calculated according to the World Health Organization (WHO) ATC/DDD system and summed across all antihypertensive medications. Number of classes was defined as the total count of distinct antihypertensive drug classes prescribed at each time point (e.g., renin-angiotensin system inhibitors, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, diuretics). These variables were considered as potential pharmacological confounders in the mixed-effects models.

The primary efficacy endpoints were the changes in systolic and diastolic BP from baseline through each follow-up time point. This included both office systolic/diastolic BP (OSBP/ODBP) and 24-h ambulatory systolic/diastolic BP (ASBP/ADBP). The primary safety endpoints were any procedure-related complications (vascular injury, hematoma, etc.), incidence of acute kidney injury post-procedure, significant decline in renal function (≥30% reduction in eGFR from baseline), and all-cause mortality during follow-up.

Continuous data are presented as mean ± standard deviation. Categorical data are given as counts and percentages. Longitudinal changes in blood pressure were evaluated using linear mixed-effects models to account for repeated measures within patients. Each model included a random intercept for each patient to model between-subject variability. Time was treated as a categorical fixed effect (baseline, 1 month, 3 months, 6 months, 12 months, 2 years, 5 years, and 10 years). The primary inference regarding long-term blood pressure reduction was based on estimated changes at each follow-up time point relative to baseline from the mixed-effects models, without assuming a linear decline over time.

Unadjusted (crude) models were first fitted for systolic and diastolic blood pressure (DBP). Adjusted models were then constructed to control for baseline covariates that might influence blood pressure trajectories, including age, sex, body mass index, current smoking, and change in total DDDs (ΔDDD = DDD at baseline − DDD at 10 years). These covariates were entered as fixed effects.

From the mixed-effects models, estimated mean blood pressure changes at each follow-up time point relative to baseline were derived with 95% confidence intervals and corresponding p values. Individual patient blood pressure trajectories were also visualized to illustrate interindividual variability in long-term response and to allow each patient to serve as their own control. For descriptive purposes only, patient-level long-term blood pressure trends were summarized using simple linear regression slopes and are presented in the online supplementary material (for all online suppl. material, see https://doi.org/10.1159/000551407); this analysis was not intended to represent a continuous linear physiological decline in blood pressure over time. Given the small sample size, p values are interpreted descriptively; a two-sided α of 0.05 was considered significant for the endpoint. All analyses were conducted with STATA software, version 17.

In adjusted models incorporating key demographic and pharmacological covariates (age, sex, body mass index, smoking status, and change in total DDDs [ΔDDD]), substantial reductions in OSBP were observed early and were sustained throughout long-term follow-up. Compared with baseline, OSBP declined by −22.12 mm Hg at 1 month and −33.83 mm Hg at 3 months (both p < 0.001), with durable reductions maintained through 10 years (−38.57 mm Hg; 95% CI −44.15 to −33.11; p < 0.001). These estimates were consistent with those observed in unadjusted models, indicating robustness of the time-related BP effect.

Similarly, ODBP demonstrated significant early reductions that persisted over long-term follow-up. Relative to baseline, ODBP decreased by −5.41 mm Hg at 1 month (p = 0.027) and −10.02 mm Hg at 3 months (p = 0.001), with sustained reductions observed through 10 years (−13.32 mm Hg; 95% CI −17.97 to −8.67; p < 0.001).

In these parsimoniously adjusted models, ΔDDD was significantly associated with OSBP (−1.70 mm Hg per unit ΔDDD; 95% CI −2.75 to −0.38; p = 0.002), whereas no statistically significant association between ΔDDD and ODBP was observed (p = 0.709). No other covariates demonstrated consistent or robust associations with longitudinal blood pressure change. These results demonstrate that RDN provides a sustained and clinically meaningful reduction in SBP and DBP independent of potential clinical and therapeutic confounders. ## 讨论 / Discussion

In this single-center study, we demonstrate that an ultrasound-based RDN procedure (TIVUS system) resulted in substantial and sustained BP reductions over a 10-year follow-up period, in a high-risk, resistant hypertensive population. OSBP was reduced by approximately 30 mm Hg at 6 months, and this reduction persisted at 10 years post-procedure. ODBP was lowered by ∼10–14 mm Hg throughout follow-up. Importantly, ambulatory measurements confirmed these findings: at 10 years, 24-h ASBP and ADBP remained ∼20 and ∼10.6 mm Hg lower than baseline, respectively. Notably, these long-term BP reductions were achieved without procedure-related complications and with no deleterious impact on renal function. Interpretation of DBP changes should therefore be made cautiously, as oscillometric devices are known to measure DBP with greater variability and potential error, and the narrower range of diastolic BP reduction reduces sensitivity to detect treatment effects compared with systolic BP.

This is the first report of decade-long outcomes following ultrasonic (non-balloon occlusive) RDN, and it affirms the durability of the BP-lowering effect of RDN with the TIVUS catheter in patients with resistant hypertension. Our findings extend the initial results of the first-in-man TIVUS trial, which documented significant blood pressure drops at 6 months [16].

The maintenance of BP control over 10 years suggests that the physiological effects of RDN – disruption of renal afferent and efferent sympathetic pathways – are durable. This long-lasting benefit addresses a key question in the field: whether nerve regrowth or reinnervation could lead to BP increase years after the procedure. It should be noted that most preclinical studies investigating renal nerve reinnervation have been conducted using RF-based RDN. In contrast, dedicated animal data evaluating nerve regrowth following ultrasound-based RDN remain limited, representing an important area for future translational research [18]. Our data suggest that any reinnervation that may occur, particularly with US-based TIVUS RDN, is either incomplete or functionally insignificant over 10 years, in the context of sustained medical therapy. This aligns with prior smaller-scale observations that BP reductions can persist at 3–5 years after RDN [3, 12, 14, 21, 22]. These findings are concordant with additional long-term series reporting durable BP lowering after catheter-based RDN up to 9–10 years, with acceptable safety profiles [11, 23].

Recently, a single-center report from Tianjin, China described 10-year outcomes of 60 patients after RF RDN, similarly noting a sustained office BP reduction of ∼ −36/−15 mm Hg at decade follow-up. Despite differences in RDN energy modality (RF vs. ultrasound) and population, the concordance between that study and ours (−36/−15 mm Hg vs. −39/−14 mm Hg) at 10 years reinforces the concept that effective RDN can induce long-lasting blood pressure lowering [5]. The magnitude of BP reduction observed is clinically meaningful. Resistant hypertension patients often have baseline BPs in the 160–180/90–100 range, as in our cohort. Achieving an average SBP drop of ∼30 mm Hg could be transformative, potentially shifting patients from uncontrolled to well-controlled ranges.

The TIVUS system has been specifically shown to be safe and effective in both the short and long term. This catheter uses non-focused ultrasound emitted radially, which differentiates it from other ultrasound RDN devices like the Paradise system (which uses a balloon to center a focused ultrasound transducer). The free-floating design of TIVUS, combined with the penetration depth of ultrasound, allows efficient circumferential ablation without vessel occlusion [1].

In our study, this translated to a short and straightforward procedure with no vascular complications. We observed no instances of renal artery stenosis or aneurysm formation in follow-up, which is consistent with the expectation that ultrasound energy, delivered in a controlled fashion, causes minimal arterial wall injury beyond the intended nerve modulation. The long-term preservation of kidney function in our cohort addresses concerns that RDN might impair renal perfusion or cause chronic kidney damage [3].

Our patients’ eGFR decline (∼8 mL/min in 10 years) mirrors what one would anticipate from aging and long-standing hypertension alone, and there was no early post-procedure dip in GFR. The modest decline in eGFR observed over 10 years is consistent with expected age- and hypertension-related renal function decline in patients with long-standing hypertension and diabetes, as reported in contemporary longitudinal cohort studies [24]. These findings align with prior reports that RDN does not accelerate renal deterioration, and they add to this body of evidence by providing a full decade of follow-up data. It is important to compare our results with those of other device-based approaches and trials to put the efficacy into perspective [1]. Recent sham-controlled trials of RDN (both RF and ultrasound) have reported more modest BP reductions at 6 months or less (often on the order of 5–10 mm Hg in ambulatory SBP) [25]. The RADIANCE-HTN SOLO trial of ultrasound RDN (Paradise) in an off-medication setting showed a −8.5 mm Hg greater daytime SBP reduction than sham at 2 months [26]. Medication-background trials, such as RADIANCE-HTN TRIO (in patients on triple therapy), found an additional 4–5 mm Hg drop in ambulatory SBP at 2 months compared to sham. These are smaller than the of blood pressure drops we report [26]. A recent systematic review and meta-analysis of long-term RDN cohorts corroborates the persistence of BP lowering beyond the early months, supporting the durability signal across modalities and study designs [27]. Ongoing randomized, sham-controlled evaluation of the latest-generation TIVUS catheter in the THRIVE global pivotal trial will provide a more rigorous assessment in a larger, multicenter population [28]. In addition, a 4-Fr, 130-cm TIVUS catheter enabling radial-access RDN has now been reported in a first-in-man case, supporting the feasibility of lower profile access strategies that could broaden adoption [26].

There are important differences in study design to consider: our study did not have a sham control, and patients remained on medications throughout, so the observed reduction is the total BP change from baseline (which could include medication effects or regression to the mean). Notably, our cohort was characterized by an exceptionally high baseline office SBP (∼176 mm Hg), resulting from the use of a ≥160 mm Hg inclusion threshold for resistant hypertension. Baseline blood pressure has been consistently shown to be a strong and independent predictor of the magnitude of blood pressure reduction following RDN, with patients starting at higher pressures experiencing larger absolute declines [4, 29, 30]. Contemporary analyses from randomized RDN trials and pooled datasets have confirmed that baseline systolic BP is among the most robust determinants of subsequent BP response [31]. Accordingly, the pronounced and durable BP reductions observed in the present study should be interpreted in the context of this severe baseline hypertensive burden. When baseline BP severity is taken into account, the magnitude of long-term blood pressure lowering observed in our cohort is directionally consistent with prior RDN studies and does not represent an outlier response [12, 30, 32]. Beyond blood pressure reduction alone, RDN targets the underlying pathophysiology of resistant hypertension through durable modulation of renal sympathetic afferent and efferent signaling [19]. Importantly, accumulating long-term evidence from randomized trials, pooled analyses, and international registries supports the safety of RDN, with preserved renal artery integrity and stable renal function over extended follow-up [21, 30, 33].

Indeed, our baseline BPs were measured on medications, and patients may have had some degree of Hawthorne effect or improved adherence after enrollment. The mixed-effects model adjustment for medication uses and patient factors in our analysis attempts to isolate the RDN effect, and still finds ∼ −39/−14 mm Hg reduction at 10 years. This suggests a truly large effect; however, we must be cautious when comparing uncontrolled and controlled data. It is plausible that the patients enrolled in our study were very severe and truly resistant, so when they received RDN (plus continued medical management), they experienced a dramatic improvement.

Notably, our results show no late “escape” of BP – as might have been speculated if nerve regrowth was significant. The sustained nature of the response may reflect on ultrasonic TIVUS RDN as a one-and-done therapy. An interesting observation was the heterogeneity in individual responses. While every patient’s BP decreased, some had much larger reductions than others (as evidenced by the range of individual slopes and the wide SD of BP changes). This heterogeneity has been noted in all RDN studies and underscores that factors beyond the procedure itself (such as patient anatomy, sympathetic tone, genetics, or concurrent lifestyle changes and medication compliance) influence outcomes. Future research is needed to identify predictors of a robust response to RDN. Some clues have been suggested, for example, higher baseline renin levels or a higher baseline heart rate could indicate a greater sympathetic drive and thus a greater potential benefit. Our sample is too small to draw such conclusions, but we did see that patients with very high starting BP tended to have larger absolute drops – essentially, those with more to lose lost more, which is intuitive and aligns with observations that baseline BP is a determinant of BP reduction extent. Regardless, even the more “modest” responders in our cohort (e.g., someone whose SBP dropped ∼15–20 mm Hg) still gained a meaningful benefit in terms of BP control.

From a clinical standpoint, the long-term safety demonstrated here is highly relevant. Early RDN enthusiasts were met with questions about renal artery damage and adverse events. Our study contributes to the reassuring safety profile, with no significant vascular complications acutely and no evident harms even 10 years later.

During the 10-year follow-up period, our cohort experienced two cerebrovascular events, two myocardial infarctions, and 1 case of progression to end-stage renal failure. Although this number of major adverse cardiovascular events may appear higher than that reported in some other long-term RDN series, it should be interpreted in the context of the exceptionally high baseline cardiovascular risk of our population. Patients in the present study were characterized by advanced age, long-standing and severe resistant hypertension, and a high prevalence of diabetes mellitus, chronic kidney disease, and established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease.

For comparison, Sesa-Ashton et al. [26] reported three cerebrovascular events and one myocardial infarction among 66 patients during long-term follow-up after RDN, corresponding to a lower absolute event count but within a substantially larger and less severely affected cohort. Differences in event rates across studies likely reflect heterogeneity in baseline risk profiles, sample size, and duration of observation rather than procedure-related harm [34]. Importantly, in our study, none of the reported cardiovascular events or the case of renal failure were adjudicated as related to the RDN procedure, and no patient required repeat renal artery intervention or demonstrated evidence of renal artery injury on long-term follow-up. Taken together, these findings underscore that while patients undergoing RDN often represent a population at inherently high cardiovascular risk, the procedure itself does not appear to confer excess long-term cardiovascular or renal hazard, even over a decade of observation.

The observed modest but significant correlation between net medication dose changes (ΔDDD) and BP reduction (approximately −1.60 mm Hg OSBP and −0.85 mm Hg ODBP per ΔDDD) aligns with and contextualizes findings from previous RDN trials, which have highlighted the interplay between antihypertensive medication burden and the efficacy of RDN. In the SPYRAL HTN-ON MED trial, for example, adjunctive medication adjustments clearly influenced outcomes – patients in the sham arm (particularly in the USA cohort) required greater intensification of drug therapy, achieving blood pressure reductions that narrowed the gap versus RDN, whereas outside the USA (where medication changes were minimal) RDN produced significantly larger of blood pressure drops than sham [35]. By contrast, the SPYRAL HTN-OFF MED studies eliminated antihypertensive therapy entirely, demonstrating a definitive BP-lowering effect of RDN in the absence of any medications [35, 36]. Similarly, the DENERHTN trial – which applied a standardized stepped-care regimen in both arms – showed that adding RDN yielded greater ambulatory BP reduction than medications alone (about a 6 mm Hg larger drop in daytime SBP), even though the number of drugs and doses remained comparable between groups at 6 months [37, 38]. Furthermore, real-world data from the Global SYMPLICITY Registry indicate that RDNs’ blood pressure benefits can be sustained over multiple years without an increase in concurrent medication burden [36, 39]. Taken together, these findings echo the current results by underscoring that while intensification or reduction of pharmacotherapy after RDN can modestly affect blood pressure, the overall BP improvements from RDN are largely achieved in parallel with stable or even reduced medication use – highlighting RDN’s potential to alleviate medication burden without compromising blood pressure control.

In routine stepped antihypertensive therapy, long-term BP trajectories commonly reflect iterative medication escalation, adherence variability, and tolerability limitations, and BP control may fluctuate over time as treatment is adjusted. In contrast, the long-term BP reductions observed after RDN in this cohort were maintained over a decade with only modest net changes in medication burden (ΔDDD), suggesting durable BP control not solely attributable to progressive pharmacologic intensification. The supplementary slope summaries are therefore presented as descriptive metrics to complement the primary time point-based mixed-effects analyses by illustrating interindividual variability in long-term response.

Long-term changes in antihypertensive medication burden following RDN have varied across published cohorts. While some studies have reported stable medication use despite sustained blood pressure reductions, others have demonstrated meaningful reductions in DDDs over extended follow-up. Some long-term studies have shown no significant change in antihypertensive DDDs following RDN [32, 40], whereas others have observed a significant decline in medication burden during long-term follow-up [34]. The reduction in DDDs observed in the present study aligns more closely with the latter findings and may reflect the particularly severe baseline hypertension and high cardiovascular risk profile of our cohort, in whom sustained blood pressure control enabled gradual medication de-escalation in routine clinical practice. In routine stepped antihypertensive therapy, long-term blood pressure trajectories commonly reflect iterative medication escalation, adherence variability, and tolerability limitations, and blood pressure control may fluctuate over time as treatment is adjusted. In contrast, the durable blood pressure reductions observed after RDN in this cohort were maintained over a decade with only modest net changes in medication burden (ΔDDD), suggesting sustained blood pressure control not solely attributable to progressive pharmacologic intensification. Differences in baseline disease severity, follow-up duration, and medication management strategies likely contribute to the heterogeneity of medication trajectories reported across long-term RDN studies.

Our study has limitations. First, the sample size is small (n = 12), and all patients were treated at a single center. This limits generalizability; there may have been site-specific factors (e.g., operator technique, patient characteristics) that contributed to the excellent outcomes. In addition, the small sample size constrained the number of covariates that could be reliably included in multivariable models without increasing the risk of overfitting. Accordingly, adjusted analyses were intentionally parsimonious and should be interpreted as exploratory rather than definitive assessments of independent predictors. Second, the study lacked a control or sham group. Therefore, we cannot definitively evaluate how much of the BP reduction was due to the RDN procedure versus other factors such as medication optimization, lifestyle modifications, or regression to the mean. Similar considerations regarding procedural execution and standardization have been highlighted in prior RDN trials, where post hoc analyses identified procedural and trial design limitations, including incomplete circumferential ablation and operator inexperience. An important limitation of the present study relates to procedural heterogeneity. RDN during the study period was not fully standardized, and objective intraoperative indicators of denervation completeness were not available. Technical factors such as the number of ablations per artery and the longitudinal distribution of energy delivery have since been recognized as important determinants of blood pressure response and may contribute to interindividual variability. Because detailed procedural metrics were not systematically recorded in this early-generation TIVUS cohort, formal analyses of efficacy according to specific technical parameters were not feasible.

Nevertheless, interindividual variability in long-term blood pressure response was explicitly addressed at the analytical level. Individual blood pressure trajectories were visualized, and longitudinal changes were modeled using mixed-effects regression with patient-specific random intercepts, allowing each patient to serve as their own control and accounting for between-patient heterogeneity that likely reflects a combination of procedural, biological, and clinical factors. While this approach cannot disentangle the relative contribution of specific technical variables, it ensures that such variability is incorporated into the analysis rather than ignored. These limitations underscore the importance of standardized procedural protocols and detailed technical reporting in contemporary and future RDN trials, many of which now incorporate predefined ablation strategies and systematic procedural documentation to better link technical execution with clinical efficacy.

We attempted to mitigate this by adjusting for changes in medication and other covariates. The sustained nature and magnitude of the BP reduction (persisting for 10 years) strongly suggest a true therapeutic effect rather than a transient placebo response. Nonetheless, the results should be interpreted in light of this inherent limitation. Ambulatory BP is generally lower than office BP in resistant hypertension due to white-coat effects, which could mean the long-term values (ambulatory) are not directly apples-to-apples with the baseline (office) values. However, baseline ambulatory BP was also elevated in these patients, so we believe the comparison remains valid. Despite these limitations, our study offers real-world evidence for the decade-long performance and safety of ultrasound RDN, and the durability we observed is directionally consistent with other long-term RDN series, including RF-based cohorts [41].

## 结论 / Conclusion

The use of a TIVUS in patients with severe resistant hypertension resulted in a significant and sustained reduction in blood pressure without evidence of loss of efficacy over time. The intervention demonstrated an excellent safety profile, with no procedure-related complications, preservation of renal function, and no long-term adverse events observed. This study provides the first evidence of decade-long durability for ultrasound-based, non-balloon occlusive, RDN. The results support the concept that targeting the renal sympathetic nerves with the US-based TIVUS catheter may offer enduring blood pressure control in treatment-resistant hypertension. These findings, if confirmed in larger and controlled trials, suggest that ultrasonic TIVUS RDN is a viable and lasting therapeutic option for patients with resistant hypertension.
