## English Abstract

Hypertension is a common health issue among elderly populations, substantially increasing morbidity and mortality risks. This meta-analysis aimed to determine optimal systolic blood pressure (SBP) targets in elderly hypertensive patients and their effects on clinical outcomes.

We conducted a systematic search of PubMed, Embase, and the Cochrane Library to identify randomized controlled trials involving antihypertensive therapy in participants aged 60 years and older. Mortality, cardiovascular events, and significant adverse events data were extracted and analyzed using random-effects models.

The analysis included 24 studies, with 9 specifically examining elderly participants aged 60 and older. Targeting a lower SBP of less than 140 mmHg was associated with significant reductions in primary outcome events (relative risk [RR], 0.69; 95% confidence interval [CI], 0.56–0.86), all-cause mortality (RR, 0.64; 95% CI, 0.49–0.83), cardiovascular mortality (RR, 0.59; 95% CI, 0.39–0.87), and stroke (RR, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.47–0.98; I2 = 0%). Achieving an intensive SBP target in the pooled range less than 130 mmHg reduced the risks of primary outcome events (RR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.62–0.85), heart failure (RR, 0.57; 95% CI, 0.38–0.84), and stroke (RR, 0.72; 95% CI, 0.53–0.96), though it also led to an elevated risk of hypotension (RR, 1.43; 95% CI, 1.18–1.73).

In elderly hypertensive patients, lower SBP targets correlate with improved clinical outcomes, including reduced mortality and cardiovascular events. Nonetheless, the heightened risk of adverse effects underscores the need for careful, individualized treatment strategies. Additional research is warranted to refine these targets and achieve a balance between therapeutic efficacy and safety.

## 背景 / Background

According to the Korean statistics in 2022, the population aged 65 years or older was 9,267 thousand persons, accounting for 18% of the total population, a 0.9% increase compared to the previous year [1]. According to Korea hypertension fact sheet 2021, As of 2019, in South Korea, about 64% of individuals aged 65 and older had hypertension, with rates of 59% for males and 68% for females [2]. This chronic condition affects an estimated 4.95 million people in total, comprising 1.96 million males and 2.99 million females, making it a prevalent health issue among the elderly [2]. Hypertension, also known as high blood pressure (BP), is a health condition that substantially elevates the chances of developing heart, brain, kidney, and various other ailments. It is a primary contributor to premature mortality on a global scale [34]. Despite advancements in hypertension management, there remains significant uncertainty regarding the optimal systolic blood pressure (SBP) targets in elderly patients, particularly in balancing cardiovascular benefits against potential harms such as hypotension and renal impairment.

Especially in older age, studies have reported an increased risk of cardiovascular events and death in older patients with lower BP compared with those with higher BP [56]. In recent two long-term outcome trials, the SPRINT trial and the STEP trial, have demonstrated the efficacy of intensive BP-lowering treatment in elderly individuals with hypertension [78]. However, the optimal SBP treatment target in the elderly hypertensive patients is full of challenges because of a highly complex status such as comorbidity, poly-pharmacy, frailty [910]. The 2023 European Society of Hypertension (ESH) guideline recommended the main objective of treatment is to reduce BP to levels below 140/80mmHg (Class I, level of evidence [LOE] A), and lowering it further to below 130/80 mmHg may be considered if the treatment is well tolerated (Class I, LOE B) [10]. Current Korean Society of Hypertension (KSH) guideline in 2022 suggests SBP goal below 140mmHg is recommended for noninstitutionalized ambulatory community-dwelling adults (≥ 65 years of age) as Class I, LOE A [11]. Therefore, we conducted a meta-analysis of cohort studies focusing on the correlation between hypertension and clinical outcomes in elderly populations.

## 方法 / Methods

The protocol for this systematic review was developed following the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA) Protocols, and the PRISMA guidelines were adhered to in the reporting process [12].

A systematic search of the literature was performed using PubMed, Embase, and the Cochrane Library of Controlled Trials until January, 2023. The search criteria encompassed a variety of terms, utilizing free text by Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) and Pharmacologic Action (PA) such as (“Hypertension/drug effects” [MeSH] OR “Hypertension/drug therapy” [MeSH] OR “Blood Pressure/drug effects” [MeSH]) AND (“Antihypertensive Agents” [MeSH] OR “Antihypertensive Agents” [PA]). Studies mentioning randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in reviews or meta-analyses were excluded from the search strategy. Embase was restricted to articles and e-publications to avoid conference abstracts and meeting materials. The Cochrane database, which already categorizes RCTs under “Trials,” was not searched separately. Additional studies were identified by reviewing systematic reviews and study bibliographies. The comprehensive search strategy is outlined in Supplementary Table 1.

Two researchers (ES Shin and SH Ihm) independently extracted the following data from each included study: author names, publication year, study design, sample size, participant demographics, intervention details, control conditions, outcomes, follow-up duration, and results. Any discrepancies between the 2 researchers’ extractions were resolved through discussion. The studies compared lower BP targets versus standard BP targets (or a reference group), and more intensive BP targets versus less intensive BP targets. Outcomes were All-cause mortality and cardiovascular death and cardiovascular outcomes such as myocardial infarction (MI), heart failure, coronary heart disease (CHD), and stroke. Serious adverse events were defined as hypotension, renal failure, and falls. The analysis focused on these SBP categories, with particular attention to thresholds of 150, 140, 130 mmHg for detailed investigation. The systematic review was restricted to randomized controlled trials that involved antihypertensive treatments lasting a minimum of one year. Moreover, only studies involving participants aged 60 years and older were considered. The data extraction was standardized, with all researchers utilizing a uniform form tailored to meet the specific needs of the review, ensuring accuracy and consistency throughout the process. For the meta-analysis, a total of nine RCTs were selected, focusing on elderly patients and comparing the clinical effects of intensive versus standard BP management [7813141516171819]. These studies provide crucial evidence for determining optimal SBP targets in older adults.

Meta-analyses were performed based on two primary factors: 1) age groups, divided into mid-life and late-life with 65 years as the cutoff; and 2) BP metrics, including hypertension, SBP. The effect sizes were represented as relative risks with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). For statistical testing, the Z-statistic was calculated to evaluate the null hypothesis that there is no effect. The Z-statistic is derived by dividing the estimated effect size by its standard error and follows a standard normal distribution under the null hypothesis. The pooled effect sizes from individual studies were synthesized to provide a comprehensive summary of the treatment effects. Meta-analyses using the Mantel-Haenszel fixed-effects model were conducted to estimate relative risks in studies where heterogeneity was minimal, assuming a common true effect across all studies. This model provided weighted averages of the relative risks, offering precise and interpretable results when the variability among studies was due only to random sampling error. All statistical analyses were performed using Review Manager (RevMan Version 5.3, 2014; The Nordic Cochrane Centre, The Cochrane Collaboration, Copenhagen, Denmark) [20]. The mean differences from baseline to follow-up were calculated for both the control and experimental groups to assess the outcomes of the interventions. The mean differences and sensitivity analysis were inputted into RevMan Cochrane online resource. To assess statistical heterogeneity, the I2 statistic was used, with an I2 value of 50% or higher indicating significant heterogeneity. A random effects model was applied to calculate the effect sizes. Forest plots were utilized to display between-group mean differences and 95% CIs for ambulatory SBP and diastolic blood pressure (DBP), providing a visual representation of the individual study outcomes and the overall pooled results.

## 结果 / Results

The literature search yielded 11,393 articles on hypertension management in elderly populations (Fig. 1). After removing 3,059 duplicate records, 8,334 articles were reviewed by 2 independent assessors. Following the screening of titles and abstracts, 8,100 studies were excluded. Subsequently, 234 full-text articles were evaluated for eligibility, resulting in 24 studies being included in the final qualitative and quantitative analyses. The reviewed studies were geographically diverse, with 4 originating from Asia, two international studies, and three from North America (Table 1) [7813141516171819]. We evaluated 5 SBP target ranges based on data from 9 randomized controlled trials involving older adults aged 60 years and above, assessing the effects of intensive versus standard BP management on outcomes (Supplementary Tables 2 and 3). Across all SBP ranges, intensive BP control consistently reduced primary outcome events, including all-cause mortality, cardiovascular events, and adverse events, compared to standard treatment. For SBP targets of 140–159 mmHg, a significant reduction in primary events was observed (relative risk [RR], 0.72; 95% CI, 0.66–0.80; P < 0.001; I2 = 0%). Similarly, for SBP targets ≤ 150 mmHg, intensive treatment led to a significant benefit (RR, 0.75; 95% CI, 0.68–0.83; P < 0.001; I2 = 38%). Lowering SBP to below 140 mmHg further reduced primary events but high heterogeneity (RR, 0.69; 95% CI, 0.56–0.86; P < 0.001; I2 = 64%). At an SBP range less than 130 mmHg, the intensive treatment group also showed a significant benefit (RR, 0.73; 95% CI, 0.62–0.85; P < 0.001, I2 = 0%) (Fig. 2).

The meta-analysis revealed that achieving lower SBP targets (< 140 mmHg) was significantly associated with a reduction in all-cause mortality when compared to higher SBP targets. The RR of all-cause mortality was reduced by 36% in the lower SBP target group (Supplementary Fig. 1). For targets of less than 150 mmHg, the meta-analysis revealed a significant reduction in both all-cause mortality (RR, 0.84; 95% CI, 0.73–0.97; I2 = 0%) and cardiovascular mortality (RR, 0.71; 95% CI, 0.60–0.85; I2 = 0%) (Supplementary Figs. 1 and 2). The data also suggest that intensive reduction, achieving SBP targets of less than 130 mmHg offers further decreased the all-cause mortality, and lowering SBP to below 140 and 150 mmHg was associated with significant reductions in risk of death. Specifically, at SBP targets below 150 mmHg, cardiovascular mortality was reduced by 29% (RR, 0.71; 95% CI, 0.60–0.85; I2 = 0%). However, further lowering SBP to below 130 mmHg did not show additional benefit in cardiovascular mortality (Supplementary Figs. 1 and 2).

Lower SBP targets were associated with a lower risk of heart failure and stroke (Fig. 3, Supplementary Fig. 3). The risk of HF was reduced by 43% (RR, 0.57; 95% CI, 0.38–0.84; I2 = 31%) in the lower SBP target group (less than 130 mmHg) (Fig. 3). The analysis showed notable benefits of lower SBP targets in reducing the risk of stroke in elderly populations. At SBP targets of 140–159 mmHg, the relative risk of stroke decreased by 35% (RR, 0.65; 95% CI, 0.50–0.83; P < 0.001). This effect persisted for SBP targets below 140 mmHg, with a 32% lower risk (RR, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.47–0.98; I2 = 0%), showing consistent benefit across studies with minimal heterogeneity. Moreover, for more intensive SBP targets below 130 mmHg, the impact on stroke risk was statistically significant (RR, 0.72; 95% CI, 0.53–0.96; I2 = 0%) (Supplementary Fig. 3). For MI, the SBP target of 140–159 mmHg showed a reduced risk compared to the 160–179 mmHg range (RR, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.48–0.97; P = 0.03). However, further lowering SBP did not consistently demonstrate added beneficial effect (Supplementary Fig. 4).

## 讨论 / Discussion

The meta-analysis aimed to evaluate optimal SBP targets in elderly hypertensive patients, with findings highlighting both benefits and risks. Key conclusions include: 1) Achieving SBP targets below 140 mmHg is significantly associated with reduced all-cause and cardiovascular mortality, indicating a strong survival benefit for this group. 2) Intensive SBP lowering consistently reduced primary events, including heart failure and stroke, across a range of SBP targets. Notably, the pooled analysis of trials targeting SBP < 130 mmHg (including SPRINT and STEP trials) demonstrated a significant reduction in cardiovascular events, suggesting that benefits may extend below the 130 mmHg threshold when appropriately selected patients are treated. 3) However, this intensive range was also associated with an increased risk of adverse events, particularly hypotension, though the heterogeneity was modest, indicating acceptable consistency across studies.

These findings emphasize the importance of a patient-centered approach in the management of hypertension in older adults. Clinicians should consider frailty scores and comorbidities when setting SBP targets, as intensive treatment may be unsuitable for highly frail patients. For example, frail patients or those with multiple comorbidities may face higher risks of adverse outcomes such as hypotension and renal failure, outweighing the cardiovascular benefits of aggressive BP lowering. Tailored treatment strategies, incorporating comprehensive geriatric assessments, are essential to ensure that the potential benefits of intensive BP reduction are balanced against the associated risks. It is important to note that the findings supporting SBP targets < 120 mmHg were largely derived from the SPRINT trial, which utilized unattended automated office BP measurements. This methodological distinction may limit the direct applicability of these findings to routine clinical settings using conventional office BP measurements.

Our findings align with the outcomes of landmark trials such as SPRINT and STEP, which demonstrated significant cardiovascular benefits of intensive BP-lowering strategies in elderly hypertensive populations [821]. Both trials provided compelling evidence supporting more aggressive BP control to reduce cardiovascular events. Similarly, the SHEP trial showed that lowering SBP to 140–159 mmHg markedly reduced the risk of stroke and cardiovascular events in older adults, corroborating our analysis [16]. The HYVET trial, which focused on individuals aged 80 and older, also demonstrated reductions in all-cause mortality and stroke risk with SBP targets ≤ 150 mmHg, findings that further validate our results [17]. Conversely, trials such as JATOS and VALISH, which targeted SBP below 140 mmHg, yielded mixed results, showing no significant differences in outcomes between intensive and standard treatment within individual studies [1415]. Nonetheless, our pooled analysis indicates that more aggressive SBP reduction provides cardiovascular protection, with an associated significant reduction in all-cause mortality as well as stroke. This meta-analysis emphasizes the nuanced need to balance the cardiovascular advantages of lower SBP targets with the potential for adverse events. This perspective is especially relevant in older adults, who often face unique challenges such as frailty, polypharmacy, and comorbidities. Consequently, our study reinforces the importance of individualized BP management in elderly patients, aligning treatment strategies with each patient’s specific clinical profile and risk factors to optimize health outcomes.

In our meta-analysis, the primary cardiovascular outcomes did not differ significantly across SBP target categories. However, the subgroup analysis of adverse events revealed that both the < 130 mmHg groups were associated with increased risk of hypotension with moderate heterogeneity in elderly patients. This suggests that while intensive BP control may confer cardiovascular benefit, the risk of treatment-related adverse effects remains a concern even at moderate targets. Taken together, these results from our meta-analysis and the STEP and SPRINT trials support that a target SBP around 130 mmHg may represent a safer and more pragmatic approach in most elderly hypertensive patients, especially those with frailty or multiple comorbidities. Individualization of BP targets remains critical. This approach is consistent with recent hypertension guidelines. The 2023 European Society of Cardiology guideline recommends lowering SBP to < 140 mmHg in older adults (Class I, LOE A), and further down to < 130 mmHg if well tolerated (Class I, LOE B) [10]. Similarly, the 2022 KSH guideline endorses a target SBP < 140 mmHg for adults aged ≥ 65 years (Class I, LOE A), while acknowledging that further reduction toward 130 mmHg may be reasonable in well-selected patients [11]. These recommendations support our conclusion that a target near 130 mmHg offers a practical balance between efficacy and safety in elderly patients.

The findings of this meta-analysis have important implications for clinical practice. While current hypertension guidelines from the ESH and the KSH recommend initiating antihypertensive treatment in individuals aged 60 or 65 years and older when SBP reaches or exceeds 140 mmHg, independent of DBP levels [1011]. Our findings, however, suggest that reducing SBP to below 130 mmHg may confer additional benefits, provided the patient can tolerate treatment. A personalized approach remains crucial, as evidence indicates that while intensive BP control lowers the risk of major cardiovascular events, it also raises the risk for adverse outcomes such as renal impairment and hypotension [2223]. For older adults and patients with multiple comorbidities, clinicians should tailor BP management strategies to the individual’s health status, balancing the potential for cardiovascular benefit against the risk of harm. Given the increased likelihood of adverse events like hypotension and renal failure with intensive BP lowering, individualized treatment plans, coupled with close monitoring and frequent reassessment, are essential to optimize outcomes [2425].

This meta-analysis has several strengths, including a comprehensive search strategy, rigorous study selection, enhancing the generalizability of findings. However, there are several limitations. First, the analysis relied on only 9 RCTs, which may limit the robustness of the conclusions and necessitate caution in interpreting results, particularly for broader populations. Secondly, heterogeneity in study design, primary outcomes, population characteristics, and follow-up duration could impact pooled estimates, especially at SBP targets below 140 mmHg. Moreover, among the analyzed studies, there was considerable variation in BP measurement methods, with some studies using clinic-based measurements and others relying on home BP readings. Additionally, intervention approaches differed in terms of antihypertensive drug types, dosage adjustments, and administration frequency, potentially contributing to further heterogeneity in the meta-analysis. The inclusion of observational studies for adverse event data, such as hypotension and renal failure, may introduce bias. Observational studies lack the control over variables that RCTs offer, potentially affecting the accuracy of adverse event risk estimates. A notable limitation is the lack of studies focusing on DBP in elderly populations, despite the importance of pulse pressure in this group. The absence of DBP-focused research restricts the ability to fully evaluate optimal BP management in older adults, who often present with wide pulse pressures. Lastly, the lack of patient-level data limited our capacity to assess individual variations in response to BP targets, such as differences based on frailty or comorbidities. Furthermore, we excluded trials that focused exclusively on patients with diabetes mellitus, as this population has distinct cardiovascular risk profiles and is subject to diabetes-specific treatment guidelines. While this approach improved the homogeneity of the study population, it may limit the generalizability of our findings to elderly patients with coexisting diabetes.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Our findings reinforce the importance of achieving SBP targets below 140 mmHg in elderly hypertensive patients to reduce mortality and cardiovascular events, while emphasizing the need for individualized treatment strategies to minimize adverse effects. Pooled analysis of trials targeting SBP below 130 mmHg demonstrated additional benefits, particularly in reducing stroke and heart failure, although the risk of hypotension was increased. Clinicians should weigh the benefits and risks of intensive BP lowering and tailor treatment plans to the needs and health status of each elderly patient.
