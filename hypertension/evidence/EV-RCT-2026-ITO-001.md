## English Abstract

Sodium‐glucose co‐transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors have demonstrated blood pressure (BP)‐lowering effects; however, most studies included patients taking antihypertensive medications. Using real‐world Japanese data, we assessed factors associated with SGLT2 inhibitor‐mediated BP reduction in patients not taking antihypertensive therapy. After excluding those taking antihypertensive medications, we retrospectively analyzed 6,988 patients newly prescribed SGLT2 inhibitors (2014‒2023). BP was evaluated using health check‐up data before and after SGLT2 inhibitor prescription. The mean patient age was 61.6 years, and 66.8% were male. Systolic/diastolic BP were reduced by 2.3±7.6/2.3±8.4%, respectively, following SGLT2 inhibitor initiation. Multiple regression analysis identified baseline systolic BP as the strongest predictor (R2 = 8.83%), while BMI change was substantially associated with SBP reduction (R2 = 2.76%). Compared with a propensity score‐matched cohort of DPP‐4 inhibitor new users, greater BP reduction was observed in the SGLT2 inhibitor group, with differences of 2.3%/2.1% for systolic/diastolic BP. An ≈2% BP reduction may occur following SGLT2 inhibitor initiation.

## 背景 / Background

The prevalence of lifestyle‐related diseases, such as diabetes and hypertension, is growing annually, posing a considerable challenge [1, 2]. Sodium‐glucose co‐transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors are used clinically to treat type 2 diabetes, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease. Although SGLT2 inhibitors are not officially approved as antihypertensive agents, recent studies have highlighted their blood pressure (BP)‐lowering effects [3]. Furthermore, a recent meta‐analysis demonstrated reductions in both systolic BP (SBP) and diastolic BP (DBP) [4, 5, 6]. However, these meta‐analyses did not exclude the influence of antihypertensive medications and were based on clinical trials. The real‐world setting markedly differs from clinical trial settings and may result in an effect size that differs from previous clinical evidence. Accordingly, we assessed BP changes following SGLT2 inhibitor initiation in patients not taking antihypertensive medications using a Japanese real‐world database. We then examined factors associated with BP changes after SGLT2 inhibitor prescription and compared these changes with those in propensity score‐matched DPP‐4 inhibitor new users.

## 方法 / Methods

We conducted a retrospective cohort study using health insurance claims and health examination data collected by DeSC Healthcare Inc. (Tokyo, Japan) between July 2014 and August 2023. The DeSC dataset consisted of anonymized medical information provided by Japanese health insurers [1].

The study design scheme is presented in Figure S1. The earliest health check‐up data obtained 60 days or more after the initiation of the SGLT2 inhibitor prescription were used as post‐treatment health check‐up data, whereas the health check‐up data immediately prior to prescription initiation were used as pre‐treatment data. We included only patients with continuous SGLT2 inhibitor use, excluding those whose prescription period did not cover the post‐treatment examination date.

This study was approved by the Research Ethics Committee for Life Science and Medical Research of Tohoku Medical and Pharmaceutical University (approval ID: 2024‐2‐015). The requirement for informed consent was waived because all data were fully de‐identified before analysis [7].

We identified 154,625 individuals who were newly prescribed SGLT2 inhibitors. After applying the exclusion criteria indicated in Figure S2, data from 6,988 individuals without the use of antihypertensive medications were included in the analysis. Using the same approach, we identified 7,271 individuals who were newly prescribed DPP‐4 inhibitors (Figure S3).

Drug data were obtained from the DeSC claims database [1, 2, 8]. An observation period of 180 days was established from the start of data collection, and patients who were prescribed SGLT2 inhibitors without having received them during this period were classified as new users. The Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical (ATC) codes for the SGLT2 inhibitors used in this study were A10BK01, A10BK02, A10BK03, A10BK05, A10BK07, and A10BK99. Participants with the following antihypertensive medication codes were excluded from the study: C08CA, C09AA, C09CA, C03AA, C03BA, C07AA, C07AB, C07AG, C02CA, G04CA03, C03CA01, C03DA, C03DB, C08DB, C09XA, C09DX04, C02AA, C02AB, C02AC, and C02DB [1, 2, 8]. The definition of low dose is shown in Table S1.

Although two measurements are recommended during health check‐ups, the method of measurement is at the discretion of each health check‐up provider. Details of BP measurements have been provided elsewhere [1]. The rate of BP reduction was calculated using the following formula: (post‐treatment–pre‐treatment)/pre‐treatment × 100 (%).

Blood and urine samples, as well as questionnaires on lifestyle habits and medical history, were administered at each annual health check‐up. Health check‐up seasons were defined as summer (June–September), winter (December–March), or spring/autumn (April–May and October–November) [1, 2, 8]. The estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) was calculated using the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration equation with the Japanese coefficient of 0.813 [9].

The differences in characteristics between the groups were compared using standardized mean differences (SMDs), where SMD < |0.1| indicated no significant difference between the two groups [10]. Multiple regression analysis was performed to explore the characteristics associated with the SBP‐lowering effects, from which R2 values and regression coefficients were estimated. Missing data were observed for several variables, including smoking status (6.2%), alcohol consumption (14.4%), history of stroke (13.6%), history of heart disease (13.6%), history of kidney disease (13.7%), and eGFR (24.7%). Therefore, multiple imputations were performed to create 100 complete datasets for analysis. We compared the BP change after the initiation of the SGLT2 inhibitor prescription with that after the initiation of the DPP‐4 inhibitor prescription using a propensity score matched cohort design (the details are shown in the Supplementary material). The differences in BP between matched pairs were tested using a paired t‐test. Data analyses were performed using SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA).

## 结果 / Results

The current study included 6988 participants, with a mean age of 61.6 years, and males accounted for 66.8% of the total cohort. Compared with the pre‐treatment health check‐ups, SBP and DBP were reduced by −2.3 ± 7.6/−2.3 ± 8.4% (as amplitudes: −3.2 ± 9.8/−2.0 ± 6.4 mmHg), respectively, at the post‐treatment health check‐up following SGLT2 inhibitor prescription. The median interval between the pre‐ and the post‐treatment health check‐ups (interquartile) was 1.01 (0.97–1.13) years. Higher baseline SBP (SMD = 0.78) was the only baseline characteristic showing a difference among quartiles of SBP change while BMI change showed a substantial difference (Table S2). Multiple regression analysis identified baseline SBP as the predictor of SBP reduction after SGLT2 inhibitor treatment with R
2 of 8.66% (Table S3). When HbA1c change and BMI change were included in the model, BMI change was substantially associated with SBP reduction (R

## 讨论 / Discussion

In this study, we demonstrated that SBP decreased by approximately 2% (SBP reduction of 3.2 mmHg) after the initiation of SGLT2 inhibitor therapy in individuals not taking antihypertensive medications. In Empagliflozin Cardiovascular Outcome Event Trial in Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus Patients (EMPA‐REG OUTCOME), empagliflozin reduced SBP by 3–5 mmHg versus placebo [11]. In Dapagliflozin and Prevention of Adverse Outcomes in Heart Failure (DAPA‐HF), dapagliflozin reduced SBP by 2.5 mmHg versus placebo [12]. Our findings are largely consistent with these trials. However, previous studies including these trials did not sufficiently control the effect of antihypertensive medication use [4, 5, 6, 11, 12, 13]. Previous small‐scale clinical trials (single‐arm studies with n < 300) have reported BP‐lowering effects of SGLT2 inhibitors in patients not taking antihypertensive medications [14, 15]. However, a study targeting the Japanese population (single‐arm study with n ≈ 50) failed to demonstrate the BP‐lowering effects of SGLT2 inhibitors in antihypertensive treatment‐naïve patients [16]. In this context, the present large‐scale analysis demonstrated significant BP‐lowering effects of SGLT2 inhibitors based on real‐world data and explored the factors associated with these effects.

In the multivariable analysis, BMI reduction after the initiation of SGLT2 inhibitor prescription was substantially associated with BP lowering (R
2 = 2.76%), suggesting that weight loss is an important factor in the antihypertensive effect of SGLT2 inhibitors. However, this factor explained only approximately 3% of the variance, indicating that other mechanisms, such as natriuresis, may also contribute to the BP‐lowering effect, although our study does not provide direct evidence for these mechanisms. Furthermore, it should be noted that BP changes following SGLT2 inhibitor prescription showed substantial standard deviation, indicating considerable inter‐individual variability in treatment response.

This study has some limitations. First, patients taking antihypertensive treatment were excluded. This cohort represents a highly selected population with relatively well‐controlled baseline BP (mean SBP 128.1 mmHg), which may minimize the observed antihypertensive effect. Furthermore, excluding patients who initiated antihypertensive treatment may have led to an overestimation of the effects of SGLT2 inhibitors. Second, the greater antihypertensive effect observed in individuals with higher baseline BP levels may be largely attributed to regression to the mean. The previous placebo‐controlled trial has also demonstrated greater SGLT2 inhibitor‐mediated BP reduction in groups with higher baseline BP [11, 12, 16]. Third, the follow‐up timing varied among individuals. Furthermore, we did not account for lifestyle modifications during the follow‐up period. However, the comparison with DPP‐4 inhibitor users helps mitigate these concerns. Fourth, detailed information on BP measurement devices and protocols at individual health check‐up facilities was not available. Variations in measurement methods across facilities or temporal changes in protocols may have contributed to the observed BP variability.

In conclusion, in real‐world clinical practice, an approximate 2% BP reduction (SBP: 2.3 ± 7.6%, DBP: 2.3 ± 8.4%) was observed following SGLT2 inhibitor initiation in patients not receiving antihypertensive treatment. Greater BMI reduction was associated with more pronounced BP lowering, suggesting weight loss as an important contributing factor. However, substantial inter‐individual variability in treatment response warrants careful BP monitoring in clinical practice.
