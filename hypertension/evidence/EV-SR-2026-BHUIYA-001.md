## English Abstract

Aliskiren has been proposed as an alternative for renin‐angiotensin system blockage in chronic kidney disease (CKD); however, its efficacy and safety remain uncertain. The objective of this study was to evaluate the antihypertensive and antiproteinuric effects of aliskiren monotherapy in patients with CKD and hypertension. PRISMA 2020 guidelines were adopted for this systematic review and meta‐analysis (PROSPERO: CRD420251024983). Relevant studies published (January 2001–February 2026) were identified in PubMed and Web of Science that compared aliskiren monotherapy with placebo or other antihypertensives in adults with CKD. Random‐effects models were used to assess changes in systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP), and urinary protein excretion. Risk of bias was evaluated using the RoB 2 and ROBINS‐I tools. Nine trials (eight for quantitative analysis) were included. Overall, aliskiren did not significantly reduce SBP (MD −2.91 mmHg; 95% CI −8.74 to 2.91; p = 0.33) or DBP (n = 300; MD −1.35 mmHg; 95% CI −4.72 to 2.02; p = 0.43). In subgroup analysis, short‐term trials (<24 weeks) showed significant SBP reduction (MD −9.31 mmHg; 95% CI −17.72 to −0.89), whereas longer‐term studies (≥24 weeks) did not. Five trials (n = 209) reported a 28% reduction in urinary protein excretion with aliskiren (log response ratio −0.33; 95% CI −0.57 to −0.09; p = 0.007). Aliskiren provides a transient decrease in blood pressure and sustained antiproteinuric effect in patients with CKD, although heterogeneous and estimation‐dependent. It may be considered an option for individuals intolerant of ACE inhibitors or angiotensin receptor blockers, although evidence on long‐term efficacy and safety remains limited.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension is closely interrelated with chronic kidney disease (CKD) as the two worsen each other [1]. Hypertension is both a cause and a consequence of decreased kidney function. Data from large potential cohorts in the general population and in treated hypertensive patients suggest that hypertension is an independent risk factor for adverse renal outcomes, with a close relationship between the level of blood pressure and the incidence and progression of CKD [2, 3]. Conversely, CKD is an independent risk factor for the development of hypertension and cardiovascular disease. In turn, hypertension and CKD appear to have a bidirectional relationship [1, 4].

The renin‐angiotensin system (RAS) remains a crucial target for pharmacotherapy in this population [5, 6]. Due to their nephro‐ and cardio‐protective properties, angiotensin converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs) and angiotensin‐II receptor blockers (ARBs) remain first‐line agents in CKD with albuminuria [7, 8]. However, ACEI administration results in elevated bradykinin levels in the vascular system. This action is believed to enhance the vasodilatory properties of ACEIs while simultaneously inducing lung irritation, leading to frequent coughing [9].

Aliskiren, a long‐acting oral direct renin inhibitor (DRI) that reduces functional plasma renin activity (PRA) and blocks RAS, has been approved for the treatment of hypertension [10]. Aliskiren works differently from ACEIs/ARBs; as a renin inhibitor, it acts at the initial step in the RAS cascade [11]. Aliskiren uniquely reduces PRA, unlike most frequently used antihypertensive medications, except β‐adrenoceptor antagonists [12]. However, the ALTITUDE trial gave regulatory warnings that contraindicate combining aliskiren with ACEIs or ARBs for patients with diabetes or those with moderate to severe renal impairment, since duplicate RAS blockade may increase the risk of adverse reactions [13]. The Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) guidelines for hypertension management in CKD, along with the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), advise against combining renin–angiotensin system (RAS) blockers such as ACEIs, ARBs, or DRIs due to an increased risk of developing hyperkalemia, hypotension, and renal impairment [7].

Numerous prior systematic reviews and meta‐analyses have assessed the antihypertensive efficacy and safety of aliskiren in the general hypertension population, although none have focused on the CKD group. The meta‐analysis conducted by Zhao et al. (2020) indicates that aliskiren monotherapy reduces blood pressure to a similar extent as ARBs and ACEIs in individuals with mild to severe hypertension [14]. Musini et al. authored a review published in the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews in 2017 [15]. The review indicated aliskiren lowered blood pressure more effectively than a placebo. However, aliskiren exerted antihypertensive efficacy similar to that of ACEIs and ARBs, and a safety profile similar to or better than that of other antihypertensive drugs [15]. However, at this time, there is a lack of analysis evaluating the benefits of aliskiren on urinary protein excretion in patients with CKD.

Given these recommendations, the present meta‐analysis focuses exclusively on aliskiren monotherapy in patients with CKD and hypertension. By excluding studies involving combination therapy with ACEIs or ARBs, this analysis aims to provide clarity on the efficacy of aliskiren in lowering BP, urinary protein excretion and to characterize its safety profile, including risk of hyperkalemia, renal dysfunction, and hypotension, within the context of CKD.

## 方法 / Methods

The current study adheres to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta‐analyses (PRISMA) 2020 guidelines [16]. The detailed protocol of this systematic review and meta‐analysis was registered in the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO, CRD420251024983).

PubMed, Web of Science, and ClinicalTrials.gov were utilized to conduct a comprehensive literature search in identifying the effects of aliskiren in patients with hypertension and CKD from January 2001–February 2026. The search terms included both controlled vocabulary terms (MeSH) and free‐text keywords related to aliskiren and direct renin inhibition (e.g., “Aliskiren,” “Direct Renin Inhibitor,” and “Renin Inhibition”), with terms for kidney dysfunctions (e.g., “Kidney Diseases,” “Chronic Kidney Disease,” “CKD,” “Renal Impairment,” “Diabetic Nephropathy,” “Nephropathy,” “Proteinuria,” “Albuminuria,” “End‐Stage Renal Disease,” “ESRD”). Hypertension‐related studies were included to ensure capture of relevant blood pressure change results in CKD trials. However, hypertension was not specified as an explicit eligibility criterion for the systematic review and meta‐analysis of urinary protein excretion. All relevant trials of aliskiren in patients with CKD were identified, regardless of whether the patients were hypertensive. In addition, the reference lists of relevant trials and review articles were manually screened to ensure completeness. The full electronic search strings for each database are presented in the supplementary material.

Trials were included if they utilized parallel or crossover designs and provided adequate data for the computation of group‐level effect sizes with aliskiren intervention. In addition, studies were included if the sample population investigated adult participants (≥18 years) diagnosed with any form of CKD, including conditions such as diabetic nephropathy, proteinuria, albuminuria, or renal impairment. In accordance with previous meta‐analyses on renoprotection, we implemented outcome‐specific eligibility criteria for the blood pressure outcomes and urinary protein excretion meta‐analysis [17, 18]. Therefore, the effects on blood pressure change and urinary protein excretion were examined separately instead of presuming they occurred together in the same study populations. Urinary protein excretion analyses included CKD populations in which hypertension was prevalent but not always explicitly defined as an inclusion criterion. The comparator was not restricted and could include placebo, a RAS inhibitor (e.g., ACEIs or ARBs), or non‐RAS antihypertensive agents. Only clinical trials were included to ensure the incorporation of high‐quality evidence regarding the efficacy and safety of aliskiren in this population. Additionally, studies were limited to those published in English between January 2001 and February 2026.

Studies were excluded if aliskiren was administered in combination with other RAS inhibitors, such as ACEIs or ARBs, given the safety concerns and contraindications highlighted by regulatory agencies such as the U.S. FDA. Nonhuman studies, including animal and laboratory‐based investigations, were excluded to maintain clinical relevance. Observational studies, case reports, editorials, letters to the editor, and conference abstracts without full‐text availability were not considered, as they do not provide sufficient methodological rigor for quantitative synthesis. Studies enrolling pediatric populations (<18 years) were excluded to maintain focus on adult patients. In addition, trials involving patients with severe renal impairment (eGFR < 30 mL/min) receiving aliskiren in combination with ACEIs or ARBs were excluded, in line with U.S. FDA warnings regarding the increased risk of adverse outcomes in this subgroup. Finally, studies were omitted if they did not quantify urine protein or lacked a comparator arm.

Blood pressure: Mean change from baseline of SBP/DBP was captured as group means change and SD for aliskiren and comparator arms.

Urinary protein excretion: The primary outcome was defined as a change in urine protein excretion, which included either the measurements of total proteinuria (24‐h urinary protein excretion or protein–creatinine ratio) or the urinary albumin–creatinine ratio (UACR) [18]. These metrics were aggregated into a singular construct termed urinary protein excretion. The effect size for each study was calculated by the log response ratio (LRR), which is the natural logarithm of the ratio between the post‐treatment mean in the aliskiren group and the mean in the comparator group [17, 18]. When studies reported only the ratio of post‐ to pre‐treatment averages for each group (e.g., A/B ratios or percent change from baseline), the log ratio of change (LRC) was calculated as the logarithm of the ratio of the aliskiren and comparator change factors. Negative LRR or LRC readings suggest a decrease in urine protein excretion, indicating the efficacy of aliskiren treatment.

For the literature screening and study identification process, Rayyan was used to manage references, literature screening and identification process. Raayan is a web‐based platform used to facilitate reference importation, deduplication, screening, and data extraction for systematic reviews and meta‐analyses [19]. From each of the included studies, data were extracted independently by NMMAB and LVZ for the author and year of publication, sample size, and population (diabetic or nondiabetic CKD), dose, duration, and type of comparator, and primary outcome reported as the mean or geometric mean, SD, or SE for each treatment group reported in the study. If available from crossover studies, within‐subject estimates were used; if not, a conservative within‐subject correlation of ρ = 0.5 was assumed for the SE estimates. All reported estimates were compiled into a single dataset for quantitative meta‐analysis. The following equation was used to calculate the standard errors on a log scale from geometric mean ratios with 95% confidence.

SE=lnupperCI)−lnlowerCI2×1.96

Blood pressure change: All analyses were performed using the metafor package in R (version 4.3.2). Random‐effects meta‐analyses were calculated using the meta R package with the metacont function, where the effect size is the mean difference in change, and the DerSimonian‐Laird estimator was applied for between‐study variance.

Urinary protein excretion: Random‐effects models fit on the log scale using metafor. Studies with means by arm from posttreatment were modeled as LRRs. For trials that provided within‐arm change ratios, we calculated treatment effects as log ratios of change (arm‐level differences) and arm‐level variances of log‐transformed means and SDs, or arm‐level log‐confidence intervals, using delta‐method formulas and summing across each arm in parallel designs. We exponentiated pooled point estimates and confidence intervals so that values less than 1 would be consistent with aliskiren being superior. All statistical tests were conducted as two‐tailed, with a p‐value of less than 0.05 being statistically significant.

Heterogeneity and Small‐study Effects. Cochran Q and I
2 evaluated heterogeneity for all models. Influence diagnostics and funnel plots were inspected. Egger regression was applied for urinary protein models when k permitted stable inference.

Blood pressure change: Leave‐one‐out analyses for SBP and DBP were performed with metainf and random effects. Subgroup analyses stratified by the length of treatment assignment were prespecified at less than 24 weeks and 24 weeks or more.

Urinary protein excretion: Two prespecified sensitivity analyses were done. First, an LRR‐only model was conducted to include studies with posttreatment means by arm. The second model was a subgroup model by diabetes status and comparator class, to assess whether effect sizes varied according to the underlying population or control treatment. For a more straightforward interpretation, ratios were back‐transformed.

NMMAB and JC independently assessed the methodological quality of included studies using Cochrane‐recommended tools. Upon disagreement over the judgment, LVZ again evaluated the quality and finally decided on the study's quality. For randomized controlled trials, we applied the Risk of Bias 2 (RoB 2) tool, which evaluates five key domains and provides an overall risk‐of‐bias judgment [20]. For the non‐randomized study, we used the Risk of Bias in Non‐randomized Studies—of Interventions (ROBINS‐I) tool, which covers seven domains tailored to observational and quasi‐experimental designs [21]. All assessments were conducted independently by two reviewers, with disagreements resolved by consensus. Visualizations were generated to summarize study‐level and domain‐level judgments.

## 结果 / Results

The initial database search identified 931 records (PubMed: 371; Web of Science: 560; and ClinicalTrials.gov: 0). After removing duplicates, 375 records remained for screening. Following title and abstract screening, 36 full‐text articles were assessed for eligibility. Of these, nine studies met the inclusion criteria, and eight studies were included in the quantitative meta‐analysis of blood pressure change and urinary protein excretion (Figure 1). However, the number and identity of included trials differed between the blood pressure and urinary protein excretion analyses because not all studies reported both outcomes. Therefore, pooled estimates for these endpoints were derived from outcome‐specific subsets of eligible studies. Of the final 8 studies, 6 were included in the blood pressure change meta‐analysis and 5 in the urinary protein excretion meta‐analysis. Overall, 3 studies were common to both the blood pressure change and urinary protein excretion analyses. No additional studies were identified through reference list screening. One study by Spanos et al. was excluded because additional antihypertensive agents were administered to seven patients in each group to achieve blood pressure control [22]. Bomback et al. conducted a nine‐month prospective study to evaluate the renal and hormonal effects of aliskiren‐based therapy in patients with CKD. Although they reported qualitative evidence that aliskiren therapy was associated with reductions in urinary protein excretion, this study was excluded from the quantitative synthesis due to a lack of comparator data [23].

Flow diagram of the literature search and study selection process.

Of the eight included trials for meta‐analysis, six were randomized, open‐label, parallel‐group studies, one was a multicenter PROBE trial, and one was a double‐blind, placebo‐controlled crossover study (Table 1). The studies included could be divided into two groups based on treatment duration: short‐term (<24 weeks) and long‐term (≥24 weeks). Trials primarily included patients with CKD stages 1–4, with certain studies incorporating both diabetic and nondiabetic participants. A limited number of studies concentrated on special disease states, such as patients receiving hemodialysis or those with type 2 diabetes and albuminuria. Five studies were included for the meta‐analysis of urinary protein excretion. A total of 105 participants received aliskiren, whereas 104 received a comparator (ARB, placebo, or ACEI). Comparators included ARBs (e.g., valsartan and losartan), amlodipine, perindopril, or placebo/control arms. All studies assessed reductions in SBP and DBP. Renal outcomes included proteinuria, urinary albumin‐to‐creatinine ratio, eGFR, and ESRD progression. Safety endpoints (e.g., serum potassium, PRA, and adverse events) were reported in several trials, while one study also evaluated vascular function indices. Urinary protein excretion was reported as either UACR, UAER, or 24‐h protein excretion (Table 2).

Overall, most randomized controlled trials were judged to have “some concerns” of bias, largely due to open‐label designs and potential deviations from intended interventions, although outcome measurement and reporting were generally low risk (Supplementary Figure 1). One trial [24] was rated at high risk of bias in the deviations domain due to discretionary dose adjustments. The non‐randomized study [25] was evaluated with the ROBINS‐I tool and judged to be at serious overall risk of bias, primarily due to confounding and lack of randomization (Supplementary Figure 2).

Six randomized controlled trials, including a total of 300 participants (153 in the aliskiren groups and 147 in comparator groups), were analyzed. The pooled analysis under a random‐effects model showed that Aliskiren was associated with a nonsignificant reduction in SBP compared with comparators (MD −2.91 mmHg; 95% CI −8.74 to 2.91; p = 0.33) (Figure 2).

Subgroup analysis by treatment duration demonstrated a potential interaction. In shorter trials (<24 weeks, k = 3), Aliskiren was associated with a statistically significant reduction in SBP (MD −9.31 mmHg; 95% CI −17.72 to −0.89) though heterogeneity remained very high (I
2 = 97.2%). In contrast, in longer trials (≥24 weeks, k = 3), there was no evidence of benefit (MD + 3.37 mmHg; 95% CI −0.17 to 6.91) and heterogeneity was absent (I
2 = 0%). The test for subgroup differences was significant (Q = 7.41; p = 0.0065), suggesting treatment duration may explain some of the variability across studies (Figure 3).

Smaller studies, such as Siddiqi et al. and Kuriyama et al., reported relatively larger reductions in SBP than larger trials. Nevertheless, the studies' distribution continued to mainly focus on the combined effect estimate. Given the limited trials contributing to this outcome, formal statistical testing for publication bias was not performed, and these findings should be interpreted cautiously. The funnel plot and sensitivity analysis are presented in Supplementary Figures 3 and 5, respectively.

The random‐effects model showed aliskiren produced a nonsignificant reduction in DBP compared with comparators (MD −1.35 mmHg; 95% CI −4.72 to 2.02; p = 0.43) (Figure 4). There was substantial heterogeneity across studies (I
2 = 86.6). Sensitivity analysis revealed that removal of Siddiqi 2011, the study with the largest treatment effect (MD −16 mmHg), decreased heterogeneity (I

From the funnel plot of the DBP change, the distribution of effect estimates appeared generally symmetric around the pooled mean difference, with no clear evidence of small‐study effects. The funnel plot and sensitivity analysis are presented in Supplementary Figures 4 and 6, respectively.

However, subgroup analysis by treatment duration revealed that in shorter trials (<24 weeks, k = 3), aliskiren suggested a trend toward lower DBP (MD −4.65 mmHg; 95% CI −9.76 to 0.46; p = 0.07), though not statistically significant and with high heterogeneity (I
2 = 94.4%). In contrast, longer trials (≥24 weeks, k = 3) showed no reduction (MD 1.65 mmHg; 95% CI −0.70 to 4.00; I
2 = 0%). The difference between subgroups was statistically significant (Q = 4.81; p = 0.028), suggesting that treatment duration may influence the observed effect (Figure 5).

A total of five studies [24, 26, 27, 28, 29] were included in the quantitative synthesis evaluating the effect of aliskiren on urinary protein excretion among patients with CKD. Although Woo et al. reported proteinuria outcomes, extractable means or SDs for urinary protein were not available, which resulted in the exclusion of this study from the evaluation of urinary protein excretion. Using a random‐effects model, aliskiren treatment was associated with a statistically significant reduction in urinary protein excretion compared to control therapies (pooled LRR = −0.33 [95% CI −0.57 to −0.09]; p = 0.007). This corresponds to approximately 28% lower urinary protein levels in the aliskiren group. Between‐study heterogeneity was moderate (τ2 = 0.05; I
2 = 69.2%; p = 0.009), suggesting some variability in effect size across studies (Figure 6).

Forest Plot for the effect of aliskiren on urinary protein excretion.

Subgroup analysis by effect type indicated studies reporting (LRRs) showed a robust and statistically significant reduction in urinary protein excretion (LRR = −0.52 [95% CI −0.69 to −0.35]; p < 0.001), whereas those reporting log ratios of change (LRC) did not show a statistically significant effect (LRC = −0.09 [95% CI −0.29 to 0.12]; p = 0.40). The residual heterogeneity after accounting for effect type was minimal (I
2 = 1.8%), confirming the type of effect metric explained most of the between‐study variance (Table 3).

Subgroup analysis of aliskiren effect on urinary protein excretion by effect type.

When stratified by diabetes status, a consistent reduction in urinary protein excretion was observed across all subgroups, though none reached statistical significance due to limited study numbers. The estimated pooled effects were −0.42 (nondiabetic), −0.13 (unspecified), and −0.33 (diabetic) on the log scale, suggesting a similar direction of benefit across populations (Table 4). Influence diagnostics identified one potential outlier [27], but excluding this study did not materially alter the pooled estimate. Egger's regression test showed no evidence of small‐study or publication bias (z = 0.97, p = 0.33).

Subgroup analysis of aliskiren effect on urinary protein excretion by diabetes status.

The visual inspection of the funnel plot for urinary protein excretion suggested moderate asymmetry in the included studies. However, Lizakowski et al. reported comparatively larger reductions in urinary protein excretion. This pattern might indicate small‐study effects, clinical heterogeneity among CKD populations, differences in baseline proteinuria levels, or variability in study duration, rather than actual publication bias. As fewer than ten studies contributed to this analysis, the funnel plot findings should be interpreted with caution. The funnel plot is presented in Supplementary Figure 7. Overall, Heterogeneity in urinary protein excretion models was largely explained by effect‐type. When stratified, within‐stratum heterogeneity was minimal (I
2 = 1.8% in both LRR and LRC strata), and the test of moderators was significant (Table 3).

No significant increase in serum potassium level was observed in any of the studies included in the systematic review. Mild, nonsignificant increases in mean serum potassium have been reported in one study [30], which described three cases of hyperkalemia (K+ > 5.5 but < 6.0 mmol/L), all on aliskiren therapy but not severe enough to warrant withdrawal. Persson et al. [27] found a slight, dose‐dependent increase (0.2–0.3 mmol/L) in serum potassium up to the 600 mg dose, but none that met the definition of hyperkalemia. Uzu et al. [29] also found no meaningful average potassium changes (<0.1 mmol/L), nor high rates of hyperkalemia (1.3% with aliskiren vs. 0.9% with ARB), and all hyperkalemia episodes were mild and not clinically significant. Suzuki et al. [26] and Kuriyama et al. [31] reported stable potassium levels throughout the treatment period, and no hyperkalemia episodes were described in any patients. Overall, aliskiren monotherapy demonstrates small, clinically insignificant increases in serum potassium and appears to carry a low risk of mild hyperkalemia in CKD and hypertensive populations.

## 讨论 / Discussion

We conducted a systematic review and meta‐analysis that provides clinically significant information on the effect of aliskiren monotherapy on BP in patients with CKD and hypertension, as well as the effect on urinary protein excretion in patients with CKD. Aliskiren did not demonstrate a significant reduction in either SBP or DBP across all treatment durations. However, in the short term (less than 24 weeks), the mean SBP was significantly lowered by MD ‐9.31 mmHg (95% CI −17.72 to −0.89). More importantly, aliskiren led to an overall 28% reduction in urinary protein excretion (pooled LRR = −0.33 [95% CI −0.57 to −0.09]; p = 0.007). The effect of aliskiren on urinary protein excretion suggested the beneficial effects of this drug in CKD are more likely to be due to changes in urinary protein excretion rather than to lower blood pressure. Since previous studies like ALTITUDE and VA NEPHRON‐D, which identified hyperkalemia, hypotension, and acute kidney injury as the most relevant adverse effects related to dual RAS blockade, aliskiren monotherapy could be a safer alternative when direct renin inhibition is clinically justified [13, 32]. Importantly, blood pressure and urinary protein excretion outcomes were based on different, partially overlapping sets of trials. Consequently, these results should not be interpreted as effects observed in the same individuals, but rather as separate, outcome‐specific effects reported across distinct CKD study populations.

The shorter treatment duration for lowering blood pressure was found to be relevant to clinical practice. The present analysis found that the antihypertensive effects of aliskiren monotherapy were meaningful in short‐duration studies (<24 weeks) in patients with mild‐to‐moderate hypertension but were not significant in longer‐duration studies (≥24 weeks), suggesting the drug may lose its effect over time. The time‐dependent loss of antihypertensive effects may be related to the known increases in PRA following DRIs. This would have counterregulatory effects on the formation of angiotensin II and would partially offset the desired reduction in blood pressure [33]. Gradman and Kad noted that while PRA decreases following initial treatment with direct renin inhibition, this is followed by a paradoxical rise in circulating renin leading to angiotensin II breakthrough [34]. As a result, these findings suggest aliskiren monotherapy may not be sufficient for long‐term control of blood pressure in patients with CKD requiring long‐term antihypertensive therapy. The 2021 KDIGO Clinical Practice Guideline for the Management of Blood Pressure in CKD supports SBP targets of < 120 mmHg in patients with CKD when tolerated, using standardized office measurement [7]. Likewise, the SBP reductions with aliskiren monotherapy were modest and short‐term (mean SBP reduction of 9 mmHg at <24 weeks). Therefore, aliskiren monotherapy may not be expected to offer long‐term effects in hypertensive patients with CKD, since a large number of patients require combination therapy with two or more medications with different mechanisms of action. Significant heterogeneity (I
2 = 93.3% for SBP) was observed in the analysis, mainly driven by the Siddiqi et al. [25], which likely reflects the diversity in treatment effects anticipated in standard clinical practice. These results are consistent with the clinical experience that while aliskiren appears to have as decisive an action in producing rapid blood pressure lowering as other agents in the RAS, it should not be used as first‐line therapy in CKD because of the lack of prolonged treatment efficacy and outcome benefits over and above ACEIs and ARBs.

The most clinically compelling finding of the present meta‐analysis is the reduction in urinary protein excretion observed with aliskiren monotherapy (of approximately 28%). The decrease in urinary protein excretion observed in the whole cohort was meaningful because biomarkers, such as proteinuria, have been shown in several landmark studies to be surrogates and independent risk factors for both CKD progression and cardiovascular events. For example, in the RENAAL trial, a 50% reduction in proteinuria was associated with a 45% reduction in the risk of doubling of serum creatinine or end‐stage renal disease in patients with type 2 diabetic nephropathy [35]. A similar study by deZeeuw et al. stated that the initial decrease in proteinuria is predictive of good long‐term renal progression [36]. The most significant reduction in proteinuria was observed in participants with the smallest decline in glomerular filtration rate, suggesting the antiproteinuric effects are not entirely dependent on systemic blood pressure lowering. DRIs can lower the pressure inside the glomeruli by widening the efferent arterioles. Consequently, this effect lowers the hydraulic pressure of the capillaries inside the glomeruli and the amount of protein that passes through the glomerular basement membrane.

Complete RAS blockade reduces proteinuria in patients receiving the maximum dose of an ACEI or an ARB, suggesting RAS blockade aims at residual proteinuria by acting via complementary pathways [37]. However, whether the antiproteinuric effects are clinically relevant in the context of safety data from large outcome trials needs to be further studied. The ALTITUDE trial, in which aliskiren was added to either an ACEI or an ARB in patients with type 2 diabetes and CKD, was terminated early due to increased risk of non‐fatal stroke, hyperkalemia, hypotension, and renal impairment events, despite a reduction in albuminuria [13]. The discordance between the surrogate endpoint (reduced proteinuria), and clinical endpoints (reduced cardiovascular and renal risk progression) reflects the real‐world challenges of clinical medicine in the management of a chronic disease such as CKD. Our results suggest aliskiren monotherapy may have a short‐term benefit in patients with CKD for whom an ACEI or ARB is otherwise contraindicated or poorly tolerated due to related side effects, such as intractable cough with the ACEIs, angioedema with either class, or both, but whose albuminuria reduction remains desirable. Although a post hoc research study, titled Aliskiren Quantitative Atherosclerosis Regression Intravascular Ultrasound Study (AQAURIUS), suggested a higher benefit of aliskiren in diabetic patients, our findings indicate that the antiproteinuric effect may be generalizable to both diabetic and nondiabetic CKD populations and should be considered if a clinical indication for direct renin inhibition exists [38]. However, the possible advantages in relation to the insufficient long‐term outcome data for aliskiren monotherapy and the substantial evidence supporting ACEIs and ARBs as primary medicines for renoprotection in CKD should be weighed while in clinical practice.

The overall quality of the evidence was limited by methodological concerns. Most randomized trials were judged to have some concerns of bias, primarily due to open‐label designs and discretionary treatment adjustments, which may have led to deviations from intended interventions. However, outcome measurements (blood pressure and albuminuria) were objective and generally at low risk of detection bias. One trial [24] was rated at high risk of bias due to protocol deviations, underscoring challenges in maintaining intervention fidelity in pragmatic settings. The single non‐randomized study [25] (Siddiqi 2011) was assessed with ROBINS‐I and judged to be at serious risk of bias, mainly because of confounding and lack of randomization, limiting the reliability of its findings. Taken together, while the included studies provide valuable insights into the effects of aliskiren, these methodological limitations reduce confidence in the pooled estimates and highlight the need for additional high‐quality, blinded randomized controlled trials.

The result of the pooled blood pressure analysis should be interpreted carefully due to the substantial heterogeneity, particularly in blood pressure analysis. The observed heterogeneity was not uniform. It was concentrated in short‐duration studies, whereas trials with follow‐up of 24 weeks or longer showed no evidence of heterogeneity and no meaningful reduction in blood pressure. This suggests that direct renin inhibition may temporarily lower blood pressure, be influenced by context, or be amplified by small‐study effects, particularly in brief pilot trials such as Siddiqi et al. [25]. From a clinical standpoint, this is also understandable in CKD: transient hemodynamic abnormalities may not persist until compensatory mechanisms, antihypertensive medication, and volume status have attained equilibrium.

Differences in study populations and settings further explain the observed variability. The included trials cover a diverse range of CKD phenotypes, including non‐dialysis CKD and hemodialysis populations. These conditions differ fundamentally in blood pressure regulation, volume dependence, and responses to modulation of the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system, as reflected by the inclusion of hemodialysis patients [29] and non‐dialysis CKD cohorts [24, 26, 28, 30]. In addition, trials used different active comparators, background therapies, and blood types of pressure measurement approaches. All of these together might also influence the estimated treatment effects in CKD. These sources of clinical heterogeneity limit the interpretability of a single pooled blood pressure estimate and justify our stratified analyses instead of a uniform summary effect.

In contrast, heterogeneity in urinary protein excretion outcomes was largely reduced after accounting for differences in effect metrics. Trials reported urinary albumin excretion rate, urinary albumin‐to‐creatinine ratio, or 24‐h urinary protein, and some used geometric means due to skewed distributions [26, 27]. When studies were stratified by effect type, very little within‐group heterogeneity was observed. The apparent inconsistency in the overall model would be due to the mixing of logarithmic ratios and change‐based estimands. This finding indicates a uniform antiproteinuric effect of aliskiren across studies, even though blood pressure outcomes vary. However, pooled estimates should be interpreted with caution, emphasizing more on clinically coherent subgroups and sensitivity analyses that more accurately represent real‐world CKD heterogeneity.

## 结论 / Conclusion

This meta‐analysis of existing trials indicates that aliskiren monotherapy produces only modest reductions in blood pressure among hypertensive patients with CKD when treated for short‐term (<24 weeks), although heterogeneity was extremely high due to differences in study duration, small sample sizes, baseline blood pressure levels, CKD phenotypes, and variability in comparator therapies. There was no clear evidence of superior long‐term antihypertensive benefit compared with other agents. However, aliskiren‐treated patients exhibited a significant 28% reduction in urinary protein excretion in patients with CKD, a finding consistent across both diabetic and nondiabetic CKD populations. This suggests a potential renoprotective effect independent of blood pressure lowering. Given these findings, short‐term aliskiren monotherapy may be a reasonable option for selected patients who are intolerant of, or have contraindications to, ACEIs or ARBs. Nonetheless, because the current evidence base is heterogeneous, of moderate certainty, and limited by short follow‐up durations, further large‐scale and longer‐term clinical trials are warranted to better define the role of aliskiren in CKD management.
