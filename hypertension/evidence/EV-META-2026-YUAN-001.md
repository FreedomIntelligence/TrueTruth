## English Abstract

In medicine and sports science, 'acute' denotes short-term or single-session effects, whereas 'non-acute' pertains to long-term, sustained training outcomes. Hypertensive patients need to exercise with caution, especially during isometric exercises, as such exercises may cause a sharp increase in blood pressure. However, long-term non-acute training may bring antihypertensive effects through mechanisms such as improving vascular function and reducing peripheral resistance, without causing acute risks. Therefore, long-term training may be safer than acute exercise. This article explores the cumulative effects of long-term training and quantifies the effects of long-term isometric contraction training on blood pressure (SBP, DBP) values in hypertensive patients, and provides evidence-based guidance for clinical doctors and rehabilitation therapists.

This study was registered in the PROSPERO database, and qualified studies were identified through systematic searches in the following databases: PubMed, Web of Science, EMBASE, Cochrane Controlled Trial Center Registry, and EBSCO. The quality evaluation adopted the Cochrane Collaboration Network RCT bias assessment tool, using RevMan 5.4 software for data synthesis, forest plot creation, publication bias assessment, forest map creation, and publication bias assessment. This study has been registered at the National Institute for Health and Care Research (PROSPERO), ID: CRD42024602119.

Five randomized controlled trials conducted in English were analyzed. The results showed modest changes in blood pressure relative to the control group: systolic blood pressure (SBP) decreased by an average of 2.31 mmHg (95% CI: -4.37 to 8.98), while diastolic blood pressure (DBP) saw a slight drop of 1.34 mmHg (95% CI: -6.31 to 3.44). As the confidence intervals include zero and show no statistical significance (p > 0.05), these reductions are not statistically significant but may suggest a potential effect worthy of further investigation.

Although modest reductions in SBP and DBP were observed, the magnitude of change (mean SBP reduction: 2.31 mmHg) is below the clinically significant threshold of ≥ 5 mmHg recommended for hypertension management. Current evidence does not support isometric resistance training (IRT) as a standalone effective intervention. However, it may be considered a complementary approach within multimodal non-pharmacological strategies.

## 结果 / Results

This article encompasses five studies that employed long-term isometric contractions training of equal duration as a long-term intervention lasting more than six weeks. These studies, conducted between 2018 and 2023, involved a total of 9 intervention groups and 238 hypertensive patients (intervention group, n = 112; control group, n = 116). The study designs were predominantly parallel, with three using this design, while the remaining two employed crossover designs. Among the five studies, three included participants of both genders, whereas two focused exclusively on males; there were no reports of studies including only females. The methodological quality of the five included RCTs was assessed using the Cochrane Collaboration's Risk of Bias tool (version 5.1.0). The summary of the risk of bias assessment across all studies is presented in Fig. 2. Overall, the studies exhibited a moderate risk of bias, with concerns primarily in the following domains:

Random sequence generation (selection bias): 60% (3/5) studies were judged as low risk, indicating adequate random sequence generation methods (e.g., computer-generated random numbers). The remaining 40% (2/5) of studies provided insufficient detail, leading to an unclear risk rating.

Allocation concealment (selection bias): Only 40% (2/5) studies described adequate allocation concealment methods (e.g., sealed opaque envelopes), warranting a low risk rating. The majority (60%, 3/5) either did not report concealment methods or used inadequate methods (e.g., open allocation lists), resulting in a high risk of bias.

Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias): Blinding participants to exercise interventions is inherently challenging. Consequently, 100% (5/5) of the studies were rated as high risk for this domain, as participants were aware of their group assignment.

Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias): 40% (2/5) studies reported blinding of outcome assessors (e.g., personnel measuring blood pressure were unaware of group allocation), achieving a low risk rating. The remaining 60% (3/5) of studies did not report whether outcome assessors were blinded, resulting in an unclear risk rating.

Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias): 60% (3/5) studies demonstrated low risk, reporting low dropout rates and balanced reasons for attrition between groups or using appropriate intention-to-treat analysis. The remaining 40% (2/5) of studies had unclear risk due to insufficient reporting of attrition details.

Selective reporting (reporting bias): 40% (2/5) of studies were rated low risk (e.g., pre-registered protocol with outcomes matching publication). The majority (60%, 3/5) had unclear risk as no pre-published protocol could be located for comparison.

Other bias: Potential sources beyond the standard domains were considered. 80% (4/5) studies were judged low risk. One study (20%) was rated high risk due to baseline differences between groups or other identified methodological issues not covered by the core domains.

Completeness of exercise intervention reporting was assessed using the Consensus on Exercise Reporting Template (CERT). The included RCTs achieved an average score of 10 out of 16 points (range not reported). Key reporting deficiencies were identified:

Adherence strategies (Item 11): None of the studies described specific strategies used to maintain participant adherence (e.g., text message reminders, incentives, or rewards).

Adherence measurement (Item 12): Only 2 studies explicitly reported the method for quantifying adherence (e.g., training log completion rate), while the remaining 3 studies merely mentioned"monitoring participant attendance"without providing specific data.

Intervention fidelity: Only one study (Breno et al., 2018) reported the deviation rate between actual training intensity and the planned intensity (average deviation: 8%). The other studies did not specify fidelity control measures during intervention implementation.

Other reported items: Studies adequately described the exercise type, parameters (FITT principle—Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type), progression, specific content, qualified providers (in 4 studies), provider experience (in 4 studies), level of supervision, physical environment, equipment used (manual/isokinetic force gauges), and setting. Two studies reported adverse events (related to detached monitoring equipment). All five RCTs described the progression of IRT and measured exercise compliance, indicating interventions were carried out as planned.

In the final meta-analysis, data from 238 participants were aggregated. Participants'ages averaged between 30 and 79 years, all with mild hypertension, and their systolic blood pressure (SBP) was between 115–140 mmHg. In these five studies, heterogeneity was detected in systolic blood pressure (I2 = 95%) and diastolic blood pressure (I2 = 94%); thus, a sensitivity analysis was conducted. The results showed that our aggregate estimates were highly influenced by the study conducted by Breno et al. Therefore, the leave-one-out method was employed to remove it, which led to a reduction in heterogeneity, with the I2 for systolic blood pressure decreasing to 16% and for diastolic blood pressure decreasing to 68%.

Despite the extremely high heterogeneity of the overall studies included in the analysis (SBP: I2 = 95%; DBP: I2 = 94%), the heterogeneity was significantly reduced after excluding the study by Breno et al. (2018) using the"one-study-omission"method (SBP I2 = 16%; DBP I2 = 68%). Heterogeneity of population characteristics

III.Training type and dosage differences

Training type and dosage differences

Breno only employed upper limb grip strength training, whereas Daniel (2023) simultaneously compared upper limb grip strength with lower limb wall squats. The latter produced a more sustained blood pressure-lowering effect (an additional 1.8 mmHg reduction in maintenance period SBP) due to the activation of large muscle groups. This type of difference was not fully adjusted in the original meta-analysis.

The research report indicates that the actual training intensity deviated from the planned value by 8% (Sect."Report on Exercise Intervention in Long-term Isometric Contraction Training Program"), while other studies (such as Nemoto, 2021) strictly controlled the intensity (30% MVC), further amplifying the difference in effect size.

After excluding the Breno study, the characteristics of the remaining study population showed convergence (predominantly male, outpatient supervised training). At this point, gender subgroup analysis revealed that:

Male: Systolic blood pressure showed a borderline significant decrease (MD: −3.2 mmHg; 95% CI: −6.4 to 0.1; *p* = 0.05; I2 = 44%), while diastolic blood pressure showed no significant change (*p* = 0.09; I2 = 0%).

Female: There were no statistically significant trends in systolic blood pressure (*p* = 0.87; I2 = 99%) and diastolic blood pressure (*p* = 0.12; I2 = 98%) (Fig. 5–6). This suggests that males may experience a slight blood pressure reduction benefit from outpatient supervised isometric training, but more high-quality research is needed to verify gender-specific effects.

Despite significant heterogeneity among the included studies (SBP: I 2 = 95%; DBP: I 2 = 94%), this study still used a random effects model for data consolidation. This decision is based on the following considerations: firstly, exploratory comprehensive needs: even with high heterogeneity, exploratory meta-analysis can provide preliminary evidence for clinical practice and reveal potential research directions (such as optimizing training parameters or population stratification). Secondly, sensitivity analysis verification: After excluding studies with high heterogeneity sources through the"leave one out method", heterogeneity was significantly reduced (SBP I 2 = 16%), indicating that some heterogeneity may be due to the special design or population characteristics of individual studies. The third priority is clinical significance: although statistical significance is insufficient, the observed trend of blood pressure decrease (average SBP decrease of 2.31 mmHg) still has potential clinical value, especially in hypertensive patients who have not received drug treatment.

## 讨论 / Discussion

The purpose of this systematic review and meta-analysis is to quantify the effects of long-term isometric contraction training on SBP and DBP in adult participants with normal blood pressure. The main results of this study indicate that in hypertensive patients, systolic blood pressure (SBP) decreased by an average of 2.31 mmHg (95% CI: −4.37 to 8.98), while diastolic blood pressure (DBP) saw a slight reduction of 1.34 mmHg (95% CI: −6.31 to 3.44). This result is not statistically significant as the confidence intervals include zero and did not reach statistical significance (p > 0.05). While some studies report larger BP reductions with IRT [e.g., Inder et al.: −10.9 mmHg SBP], our analysis—constrained by high heterogeneity and limited sample size—did not demonstrate clinically meaningful efficacy [12]. In patients under the age of 30 with normal blood pressure and hypertension. In addition, articles included in this systematic review used similar doses or regimens: 4 groups of 2 min were administered at 30% to 50% maximum voluntary contraction (MVC) or its equivalent, with recovery times between groups ranging from 2 to 5 min, 3 to 4 times a week for 6 to 12 weeks. By comparing the scientific evidence on the effects of long-term isometric contraction training on BP, the above doses are the most commonly used in primary and secondary studies. In addition, since 2024, aerobic exercises such as Eight Section Brocade, dance, Tai Chi, Pilates, etc., have been attempted to reduce hypertension in patients with varying degrees of blood pressure-lowering benefits. Finally, we currently know of multiple effective and safe dosages for lowering blood pressure in patients with hypertension. However, taking the exploration of the hypotensive effect of inspiratory muscle resistance training (IMST) as an example, one of the advantages of this systematic review and meta-analysis is that IMST has achieved significant results in reducing systolic blood pressure, diastolic blood pressure, and mean arterial pressure (−7.93 [−12.08, −3.78]; −3.80 [−6.08, −1.53]; −4.90 [−13.76, 3.96]), but there is high heterogeneity among different intensity regimens. In contrast, a systematic review in 2025 showed that in adult hypertensive patients, the reduction of SBP and DBP produced by isometric resistance exercise had lower heterogeneity, significant statistical significance (I2 = 0%; P < 0.001), and a generally larger range of effect sizes, indicating significant therapeutic effects in hypertensive patients. Similarly, another meta-analysis reported substantial heterogeneity in the reduction of SBP and DBP outcomes in hypertensive patients through aerobic exercise, resistance exercise, and isometric exercise, although it did not reach statistical significance (I2 = 85%; P = 0.06) [5].

Reasons for a mild decrease in male systolic blood pressure

Potential reasons for the mild reduction in male systolic blood pressure through long-term isometric contraction training:

Firstly, Differences in biological mechanisms [6]; Muscle mass and metabolic efficiency: Men typically have higher skeletal muscle mass, and isometric contractions training (such as grip training) may be activated through greater muscle intensity, more effectively stimulating the baroreflex mechanism [10], thereby reducing sympathetic nerve activity and improving vasodilation function [7, 911Hormonal effects: Androgens (such as testosterone) may promote endothelial function, repair, and muscle adaptation, while estrogen's protective effect on blood vessels may respond differently during static exercise [19]. Vascular reactivity: Men may exhibit stronger peripheral vascular resistance reduction during isometric training, while women may rely more on hemodynamic compensation [18]. Secondly, Sociocultural and behavioral factors; Sports preference: Men are more inclined to choose high-intensity or strength-based sports (such as isometric training), which may result in higher compliance [19]. Differences in health behavior: Men have higher cardiovascular risk at baseline and greater room for improvement [20]; Women may have their exercise frequency affected by social roles such as family responsibilities, and men may have higher or more fluctuating baseline blood pressure, which may make intervention effects easier to detect.

We used the GRADE approach to assess the certainty of evidence regarding the effects of isometric contraction training on systolic blood pressure (SBP) and diastolic blood pressure (DBP).

Study limitations (Risk of Bias): The included RCTs exhibited methodological limitations, particularly in terms of allocation concealment and blinding (Fig. 2), resulting in a downgrade in the risk of bias rating.

Inconsistency: There is a high degree of heterogeneity (I2 > 90%) among studies, which cannot be fully explained by subgroup analysis, leading to a downgrading of certainty.

Imprecision: The 95% confidence interval of the effect estimate is very wide, encompassing changes with no clinical significance, zero effect, and potentially harmful effects, and the total sample size is small, leading to downgraded certainty.

(Optional) Publication Bias: Due to the small number of studies included (k = 5), it is difficult to reliably assess publication bias. The small sample size increases the risk and should be considered as a downgrading factor.

Taking into account the above factors, we assess the certainty of evidence for the effectiveness of long-term isometric training in reducing SBP and DBP as"Low"(or"Very Low"if all factors are considered to be very serious). Low-certainty evidence means that we have limited confidence in the true value of the effect estimate; the true value may differ significantly from our estimate (an average reduction of approximately 2.31 mmHg in SBP and 1.34 mmHg in DBP). Notably, the achieved SBP reduction (2.31 mmHg) falls below the 5 mmHg threshold established by major guidelines (e.g., ESC/ESH) for meaningful cardiovascular risk reduction in hypertension [21]. This further limits its applicability as a primary therapeutic strategy.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Long-term isometric training may slightly reduce blood pressure in patients with hypertension, but current evidence indicates that its antihypertensive effect does not reach statistical significance. Therefore, such training (such as wall squats and grip strength training) can be considered as a potential non-pharmacological intervention option, but its long-term effects need further verification; the current evidence supporting their efficacy is of low certainty for alleviating arterial hypertension. However, the efficacy of this method is currently understood to be confined to the short term (2–3 months). Consequently, research into its long-term effects, spanning at least one year, is warranted. Moreover, there is often a gender imbalance in current research, and more female samples need to be included to verify gender-specific effects.

Future studies should explore multi-center, large-scale randomized controlled trials (RCTs), including more female and elderly patients, to validate the gender and age-specific effects. Moreover, Long-term monitoring and safety evaluations should assess the lasting impact of isometric training on blood pressure control (e.g., ≥ 1 year) and evaluate its potential impact on cardiovascular and cerebrovascular events. By analyzing biomarkers (such as endothelin and nitric oxide), the molecular mechanisms by which training improves vascular function can be elucidated. Additionally, the development of intelligent devices or wearable technology for real-time monitoring of training intensity and blood pressure changes can enhance the precision of personalized interventions.
