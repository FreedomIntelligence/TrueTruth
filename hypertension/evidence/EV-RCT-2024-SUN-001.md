## English Abstract

The efficacy of sacubitril/valsartan versus olmesartan remains controversial for the control of hypertension. We conduct a systematic review and meta-analysis to explore the influence of sacubitril/valsartan versus olmesartan on the control of hypertension.

We have searched PubMed, Embase, Web of science, EBSCO, and Cochrane library databases through July 2023 for randomized controlled trials assessing the effect of sacubitril/valsartan versus olmesartan on the control of hypertension. This meta-analysis is performed using the random-effect model or fixed-effect model based on the heterogeneity.

Seven randomized controlled trials and 3677 patients were included in the meta-analysis. Overall, compared with olmesartan treatment for hypertension, sacubitril/valsartan treatment was associated with substantially decreased systolic blood pressure (mean difference [MD] = −4.58; 95% confidence interval [CI] = −7.90 to −1.25; P = .007), diastolic blood pressure (MD = −1.70; 95% CI = −3.24 to −0.17; P = .03), and pulse pressure (MD = −2.31; 95% CI = −4.41 to −0.21; P = .03), as well as improved systolic blood pressure control (odds ratio [OR] = 1.65; 95% CI = 1.15 to 2.38; P = .006), but had no influence on diastolic blood pressure control (OR = 1.33; 95% CI = 0.93 to 1.88; P = .11) or adverse events (OR = 1.06; 95% CI = 0.90 to 1.24; P = .51).

Sacubitril/valsartan is better than olmesartan for the reduction of blood pressure for patients with hypertension.

## 背景 / Background

The incidence of hypertension is rapidly increasing, and becomes one common disease among cardiovascular system.[1–3] Especially in elderly populations, the incidence of hypertension is extensively increased.[4–6] There are many treatment options for hypertension currently, but blood pressure (BP) control is still inadequate in many patients, especially in terms of systolic blood pressure (SBP) control.[7,8] It is urgent to develop new therapeutic options targeting the underlying pathophysiology of systolic hypertension.

Sacubitril/valsartan is known as a first-in-class angiotensin receptor neprilysin inhibitor, and is currently used for the treatment of chronic heart failure.[9–11] Recently, the indication for sacubitril/valsartan is expanded to treat patients with a left ventricular ejection fraction below normal.[12] Sacubitril/valsartan holds important promise in the reductions of systolic and pulse pressures in both Western and Asian patients with mild to moderate hypertension compared with valsartan and placebo.[9,13]

In addition, olmesartan is regarded as one of the most widely prescribed angiotensin II receptor blockers for the treatment of systolic hypertension in elderly patients, and its efficacy for controlling BP in patients was confirmed.[14–16] Several randomized controlled trials (RCTs) compared the efficacy of sacubitril/valsartan with olmesartan on the control of hypertension, but the results were not well established.[12,17,18] With accumulating evidence, we therefore performed this systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs to explore the efficacy of sacubitril/valsartan versus olmesartan for the control after hypertension.

## 方法 / Methods

Ethical approval and patient consent are not required because this is a systematic review and meta-analysis of previously published studies. The systematic review and meta-analysis are conducted and reported in adherence to Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses.[19]

Two investigators independently searched the following databases from inception to July 2023: PubMed, Embase, Web of science, EBSCO, and Cochrane library databases. The electronic search strategy was conducted using the following keywords: “hypertension” AND “sacubitril/valsartan” AND “olmesartan.” We also checked the reference lists of the screened full-text studies to identify other potentially eligible trials. The inclusive selection criteria were as follows: (i) study design was RCT; (ii) intervention treatments were sacubitril/valsartan versus olmesartan; (iii) patients were diagnosed with hypertension; (iv) the studies published in English were included. Patients with severe hypertension (mean sitting diastolic blood pressure [msDBP] ≥ 110 mm Hg or msSBP ≥ 180 mm Hg) or secondary forms of hypertension were excluded.

We extracted the following information: author, number of patients, age, female, body mass index, duration of hypertension, detail methods in each group, etc. Data were extracted independently by 2 investigators, and discrepancies were resolved by consensus. The primary outcomes were the reduction in SBP and DBP. Secondary outcomes included pulse pressure (PP), SBP control, DBP control, and adverse events. The proportion of patients achieved successful BP control based on the following criteria: SBP control (SBP < 140 mm Hg),and DBP control (DBP < 90 mm Hg). Adverse events mainly included hyperlipidemia, hyperuricemia, dizziness, increased blood glucose, cough, upper respiratory tract infection, headache, influenza, and nasopharyngitis.

Methodological quality of the included studies was independently evaluated using the modified Jadad scale.[20] There were 3 items for Jadad scale: randomization (0–2 points), blinding (0–2 points), and dropouts and withdrawals (0–1 points). The score of Jadad scale varied from 0 to 5 points. An article with Jadad score ≤2 was considered to be of low quality. If the Jadad score ≥3, the study was thought to be of high quality.[21]

We estimate the mean difference (MD) with 95% confidence interval (CI) for continuous outcomes and odd ratio (OR) with 95% CIs for dichotomous outcomes. Heterogeneity was reported using the I2 statistic, and I2 > 50% indicated significant heterogeneity.[22] The random-effect model was used for significant heterogeneity, and otherwise the fixed-effect model was applied. Whenever significant heterogeneity was present, we search for potential sources of heterogeneity via omitting one study in turn for the meta-analysis or performing subgroup analysis. All statistical analyses were performed using Review Manager Version 5.3 (The Cochrane Collaboration, Software Update, Oxford, UK).

## 结果 / Results

Finally, 7 RCTs[12,17,18,23–26] that meet our inclusion criteria were included in the meta-analysis. Table 1 demonstrated the baseline characteristics of 7 eligible RCTs in the meta-analysis. Among the 7 studies included here, 5 studies reported SBP and DBP,[12,17,24–26] 3 studies reported PP,[12,24,26] 4 studies reported SBP control and DBP control,[12,17,18,23] and 4 studies reported adverse events.[12,18,23,25] Jadad scores of the 7 included studies varied from 4 to 5, and all 7 studies were considered to have high quality according to quality assessment.

Five studies were included for the meta-analysis of SBP and DBP.[12,17,24–26] Compared to olmesartan treatment for hypertension, sacubitril/valsartan treatment resulted in significantly reduced SBP (MD = −4.58; 95% CI = −7.90 to −1.25; P = .007) with significant heterogeneity among the studies (I2 = 99%, heterogeneity P < .00001; Fig. 2) and DBP (MD = −1.70; 95% CI = −3.24 to −0.17; P = .03) with significant heterogeneity among the studies (I2 = 97%, heterogeneity P < .00001; Fig. 3).

## 讨论 / Discussion

The use of sacubitril/valsartan showed significant BP control in Asian patients with mild to moderate hypertension.[27] Previous studies documented the potential superior benefits of sacubitril/valsartan over olmesartan in reducing BP in patients with hypertension.[23,25] In order to compare the efficacy of sacubitril/valsartan versus olmesartan for patients with hypertension, our meta-analysis included 7 RCTs and 3677 patients with hypertension. The results revealed that compared to olmesartan treatment for hypertension, sacubitril/valsartan treatment was confirmed to significantly reduce SBP, DBP, and PP, as well as improve SBP control, but showed no obvious impact on DBP control. These also suggested that sacubitril/valsartan treatment can lead to the mean reduction of SBP by 4.58 mm Hg and mean reduction of DBP by 1.7 mm Hg compared with olmesartan treatment. The reduction of SBP was higher than the reduction of DBP after the treatment with sacubitril/valsartan than olmesartan treatment.

Regarding the sensitivity analysis, significant heterogeneity was seen among the included studies for the primary outcomes. Significant heterogeneity remained when performing the sensitivity analysis by omitting one study in turn or subgroup analysis based on doses of drugs. Several factors may account for it. First, sacubitril/valsartan was taken at the doses of 200 mg/d or 400 mg/d, which may affect the efficacy assessment. Second, the treatment duration ranged from 8 weeks to 14 weeks, which may produce some bias. Third, these patients had different duration and severity of hypertension.

Sacubitril/valsartan is able to provide simultaneous neprilysin inhibition and angiotensin II receptor-1 blockade, and is used for the treatment of heart failure with reduced ejection fraction in the PARADIGM-HF trial.[28] Neprilysin inhibition can improve the circulating levels of natriuretic peptides, which promote natriuresis, diuresis, vasodilation, and endothelial permeability and inhibit the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system, the sympathetic nervous system, and aldosterone secretion.[29,30] Sacubitril/valsartan possesses the activities of both angiotensin receptor blocking and neprilysin inhibition,[31] which may account for the enhanced antihypertensive effects compared to an angiotensin II receptor blocker olmesartan.

The potential adverse events of sacubitril/valsartan mainly included hyperlipidemia, hyperuricemia, dizziness, increased blood glucose, cough, upper respiratory tract infection, headache, influenza, and nasopharyngitis.[12,18,23] However, these adverse events were generally mild, and good recovery can be achieved after the corresponding treatments. In addition, our results found no increase in adverse events after the treatment with sacubitril/valsartan compared to olmesartan. This meta-analysis has several potential limitations. First, our analysis is based on 7 RCTs, and more RCTs with large patient samples are needed to confirm our findings. Second, there is significant heterogeneity, which may be caused by various doses and treatment durations of drugs. Third, it is not available to explore the ideal dose of sacubitril/valsartan for hypertension due to the limited eligible studies.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Sacubitril/valsartan is superior to olmesartan for the reduction of BP in patients with hypertension, as evidenced by the decreased SBP, DBP, PP, and improved SBP control.
