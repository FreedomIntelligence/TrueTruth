## English Abstract

The regulation of blood pressure is closely linked to sympathetic activity. This retrospective study analyzed 142 hypertensive patients undergoing computed tomography (CT)-guided O2–O3 neurolysis targeting intervertebral foramina or lumbar paravertebral ganglia for pain management. Results demonstrated significant reductions in both postoperative in-hospital blood pressure and office blood pressure during follow-up alongside decreased antihypertensive medication use. Notably, blood pressure reduction showed independence from pain relief but exhibited strong anatomical correlation with renal sympathetic innervation, specifically with the lower thoracic vertebral level (T9–T12) and the upper lumbar vertebral level (L1–L2). The findings suggest that modulation of thoracolumbar sympathetic pathways through minimally invasive gas neurolysis may represent a novel therapeutic strategy for hypertension management, potentially offering a targeted approach for renal sympathetic denervation. Further prospective studies are warranted to validate these observations.

## 背景 / Background

Despite lifestyle modifications and antihypertensive therapies, ~75% of hypertension (HTN) patients fail to achieve target blood pressure (BP), with ~10% progressing to resistant hypertension [1]. Although sympathetic nervous system overactivation is central to HTN pathogenesis, the therapeutic efficacy of catheter-based renal denervation (RDN) for resistant hypertension remains controversial [2]. Historically, the sympathetic ganglia served as anatomical targets during early surgical RDN techniques for refractory hypertension. In recent years, novel minimally invasive RDN approaches targeting sympathetic ganglia, including laparoscopy-guided alcohol-mediated celiac ganglia neurolysis – a conventional analgesic technique, have showed significant antihypertensive effects [3]. Computed tomography (CT)-guided spinal nerve root neurolysis (SNN) or lumbar ganglia neurolysis (LGN), achieved by injecting an oxygen–ozone (O2–O3) gas mixture into the thoracic intervertebral foramen or around the paravertebral lumbar ganglia, is a prevalent practice in the fields of orthopedics and pain management [4,5]. Notably, we have observed that the anatomical location associated with this technique partially overlaps with the renal sympathetic innervation. Nevertheless, the effect of this neurolysis technique on BP remains elusive.

## 方法 / Methods

Five hundred and twelve patients who underwent SNN or SGN were initially included. Within this cohort, we identified a subset of 178 HTN patients with a history of essential HTN and who were taking antihypertensive medication on admission. Patients who underwent multilevel bilateral neurolysis in one procedure, those who had more than one operation during hospitalization, and those prescribed antihypertensive medication promptly before and after the procedure were excluded. Finally, a total of 142 patients were included in this study.

The inhospital BP before and after the operation and the antihypertensive medication regimen were obtained from the Electronic Medical Order System. The office BP after discharge was sourced from the Outpatient Medical Record System and telephone follow-up.

The pain score is obtained from the Electronic Nursing Medical Records. The pain score ranking scale ranges from 0 to 10 whereby 0 indicated no pain and 10 indicating the worst pain imaginable.

Patients were placed in a prone position on a CT examination table (General Electric, Hi Speed ZX/I, USA). First, an unenhanced thin-slice (1–3 mm) scan was executed to accurately identify the target point. After that, a 22-RF cannula needle (Hakko, Japan) was then carefully inserted. Another CT scan was carried out to confirm the position of the needle. Subsequently, 6–8 ml of an O2–O3 gas mixture with an ozone concentration of 30 μg/ml was obtained from Ozone Generator (Inomed Medzintechnik GmbH, Emmendingen, Baden-Württemberg, Germany, IBM, Build 82) and was slowly injected along the puncture needle. O2–O3 gas dispersion patterns were showed by another CT scan (Fig. 1).

All adverse events during hospitalization were obtained from the Hospital Information System (HIS), and adverse events after discharge were obtained through telephone follow-up.

Statistical analysis was performed using the SPSS software (version 25, IBM) and RStudio (Build 282). The contributions of pain relief to BP reduction were assessed by Spearman's rho test. Comparisons between upper and lower locations of neurolysis were performed within the multivariable regression framework, adjusting for baseline covariates. No formal adjustment for multiple comparisons was applied.

Only one case of an adverse event was documented (0.7%, 1/142) who presented with severe upper abdominal pain next day after procedure, which was fully relieved before discharge. No adverse events related to the procedure were noted during the telephone follow-up.

## 讨论 / Discussion

During the early development of the Smithwick procedure, surgical resection of the thoracolumbar sympathetic chain was employed as a therapeutic intervention for malignant hypertension [6]. Our investigation revealed that the  ozone injection site partially overlapped with the anatomical course of preganglionic sympathetic fibers and the paravertebral ganglia associated with renal innervation. The observed correlation between BP reduction and ozone administration at the lower thoracic/upper lumbar vertebral levels (T10–L1) provides indirect evidence implicating renal sympathetic innervation in the postprocedural antihypertensive mechanism. Notably, our findings demonstrated a positive association between elevated baseline SBP and greater postoperative SBP reduction – a hemodynamic pattern consistent with outcomes from the landmark SYMPLICITY HTN-3 trial investigating catheter-based RDN [7]. These collective findings suggest that ozone -mediated neurolysis at sympathetic ganglia may produce therapeutic effects analogous to targeted renal sympathetic ablation or contemporary RDN techniques.

In our study, no relationship was observed between pain relief and BP reduction. The relationship between HTN and chronic pain is still a matter of debate. Some studies have suggested that the level of pain and resting arterial BP are positively correlated in patients with chronic pain [8]. However, recent studies have suggested that spinal cord stimulation (SCS), a surgically implanted method for alleviating pain, could result in a substantial decrease in BP that is not contingent on pain relief [9]. It was hypothesized that SCS modulates BP through body sympathetic innervation, as SCS alters the activity of neurons within the intermediolateral column, where sympathetic preganglionic neurons are located. In this study, the observed dissociation between BP reduction and pain relief following the procedure could potentially be interpreted as the O2–O3 ‘stimulating’ the sympathetic system in a manner similar to SCS.

CT-guided ozone-mediated neurolysis is a well established traditional technique in orthopedics and pain management, with its safety in intradiscal or paraganglionic local injections confirmed by long-term clinical application. Although a recent study revealed that high concentrations of ozone (18–30 μg/ml) promote an increase in the rate of apoptosis and death of neuroblastoma lineage cells [10], the specific effects of O2–O3 on the sympathetic nervous system are not yet clear. We hypothesized that a periganglion injection of ozone gas at a concentration of 30 μg/ml, as used in the present study, may reduce sympathetic nerve activity by inducing neuronal cell apoptosis or other unknown mechanisms.

In this study, no side effects of ozone-mediated neurolysis were observed. The long-term clinical use of this technique in pain and orthopedics and our previous single-arm clinical study also preliminarily confirmed the safety of this approach.

This retrospective single-center study had a small sample size and limited follow-up, especially beyond postoperative day 3, reducing statistical power. Standardized concepts such as antihypertensive medication burden (AHMB) were not used to accurately describe medication use. Baseline differences and concomitant treatments may have affected results, limiting generalizability.

In conclusion, CT-guided O2–O3 mixture gas-mediated neurolysis led to pain relief-independent BP reduction in patients with HTN. Intensive studies are necessary to explore the mechanism of BP reduction and its impact on the sympathetic nervous system.
