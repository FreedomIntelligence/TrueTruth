## English Abstract

To determine the correlation between weight‐adjusted‐waist index (WWI) with prehypertension and hypertension in the general population. This cross‐sectional study was conducted from September 13, 2023 to November 30, 2023 and included 4885 permanent residents in Zhejiang Province. The association between WWI with prehypertension and hypertension was carefully evaluated using logistic regression models and penalized spline functions. The category‐free analysis was performed to corroborate whether WWI enhanced the ability to estimate prehypertension and hypertension. Multivariable logistic regression indicated that after adjustment for conventional risk factors, each standard deviation increase in WWI levels was associated with a 29% and 50% increased risk of prehypertension (hazard ratio, 1.29; 95% confidence interval, 1.14–1.45) and hypertension (1.50; 1.34–1.68), respectively. Referring to the participants in the lowest quartile of WWI, those in the highest quartile had a significantly increased risk of prehypertension (1.64; 1.17–2.30) and hypertension (2.47; 1.80–3.39). Smoothing curve fitting and trend logistic regression suggested a linear positive correlation, and the robustness of these findings was observed in interaction tests and subgroup analyses. The category‐free analysis suggested that adding WWI levels to the traditional model ultimately improved the predictive ability of prehypertension and hypertension. The results of this study confirmed the significant correlation between WWI with prehypertension and hypertension, independent of conventional risk factors. More importantly, these findings suggested the potential usefulness of WWI levels to improve the risk stratification of hypertension and prehypertension.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension, a prevalent chronic condition characterized by sustained elevations in blood pressure, remains the leading cause of clinical adverse outcomes including myocardial infarction, stroke and chronic kidney disease in the world [1, 2]. Results from a pooled analysis of 1201 population‐representative studies with 104 million participants aged 30–79 years indicate that the global age‐standardized prevalence of hypertension was up to 34% in males (95% CI: 32–37) and 32% in females (30–34). [3] Meanwhile, according to the systematic analysis for 2019 Global Burden of Disease Study, hypertension is alone responsible for approximately 10 million deaths worldwide each year [4]. Prehypertension, a categorical term that defines a specific phenotype in the progression from a normal level of blood pressure to hypertension, is also associated with a significantly increased risk of coronary heart disease, myocardial infarction, stroke, renal disease, and hypertension [5, 6, 7, 8]. Significantly, overwhelming evidence supports intensive blood pressure interventions as an effective strategy for reducing the risk of aforesaid clinical complications, highlighting its role as a modifiable risk factor [9]. And meta‐analysis of multiple randomized controlled trials have preliminarily provided evidence on the efficacy and safety of antihypertensive drug treatment for individuals with prehypertension in prevention of cardiovascular disease [10]. Therefore, given the significant burden of health complications associated with prehypertension and hypertension and the established rewards of antihypertensive therapy, the elucidation of its determinants is of paramount importance in clinical practice.

This research highlights the practical utility of the Weight‐Adjusted‐Waist Index (WWI) as a convenient and effective tool for identifying individuals at risk for prehypertension and hypertension. Healthcare professionals can leverage WWI as an enhanced screening method, moving beyond conventional risk factors to more precisely pinpoint those who would benefit from early intervention. This simple calculation offers a clearer risk assessment, enabling more personalized and timely preventive measures, such as lifestyle modifications. Integrating WWI into routine health check‐ups or public health initiatives could significantly improve early risk stratification, helping to prevent the progression of these conditions and ultimately fostering better cardiovascular health within communities.

Obesity, defined as abnormal or excessive fat accumulation, is significantly associated with a range of clinical adverse outcomes including hypertension, diabetes and cardiovascular disease [11]. In current authoritative guidelines [12, 13], the diagnosis of overweight and obesity is still primarily recommended using traditional parameters such as body mass index (BMI) and waist circumference (WC). Nevertheless, the obesity paradox is an undeniable fact in clinical practice and epidemiological surveys, and overweight or obese patients, defined by conventional indicators such as BMI and WC, with established cardiovascular disease have improved prognosis, although being obese or overweight itself increases the risk of cardiovascular disease [14, 15, 16]. A reasonable explanation is that these traditional indicators have obvious limitations and cannot be well used to evaluate overweight or obesity. Although guidelines clearly recommend the use of dual‐energy X‐ray absorptiometry (DEXA), bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and computed tomography (CT) to quantify visceral adipose tissue [13, 17], their application in large‐scale population surveys is confined due to the complexity of the procedures, expensive expense and radiation exposure involved.

Recently, Park et al. [18] proposed a new anthropometric indicator named the weight‐adjusted‐waist index (WWI), a standardized WC for weight, and further corroborated its excellent predictive performance for cardiometabolic disease, cardiovascular and all‐cause mortality. Subsequently, a series of research results have also demonstrated its value in identifying clinical diseases and mortality, including diabetes mellitus [19], chronic kidney disease [20], depression [21, 22] and cardiovascular disease [23, 24]. Nevertheless, in terms of hypertension, few large sample investigations have evaluated the association between WWI levels and hypertension and these results were controversial. Meanwhile, robust data on its predictive performance is lacking in the same population. More urgently, the relationship between WWI and prehypertension has been largely unexplored so far. Accordingly, leveraging this large cross‐sectional investigation recruiting 4888 permanent residents, the relationship between WWI with prehypertension and hypertension was carefully evaluated to fill these gaps.

## 方法 / Methods

From September 13, 2023 to November 30, 2023, this cross‐sectional study was conducted among the general population of Dongyang City, Zhejiang Province. The research design and data collection have been described in previous studies [25]. Briefly, using a multistage, stratified, cluster randomized sampling strategy, one urban county (Jiangbei) with a total of 4 communities and two rural counties (Weishan and Nanma) with a total of 9 rural villages were randomly selected. The permanent residents at least 60 years old from the chosen villages and communities (n = 5743) were invited to participate, but subjects with mental disorder or cancer were excluded, and 4888 (85.1%) individuals completed the investigation. In the present study, we further excluded participants with missing covariates (n = 3) and analyzed data from a total of 4885 participants.

The Ethics Committee (approval number: IIT20210142B‐R3, Date: Sep 9, 2023) of the First Affiliated Hospital, Zhejiang University School of Medicine approved the study. Written consent was obtained from all participants.

A survey team of well‐trained nurses, cardiologists, and neurologists collected data using a standardized questionnaire during a face‐to‐face interview in a clinic visit. The surveyors were strictly trained before beginning the work of data collection. Furthermore, regular guidance and support were provided during the data collection process to ensure the authenticity and meticulousness of the records.

The demographic and clinical data, including age, gender, ethnic group, marital status and education level, lifestyle (smoking, drinking, dietary, sleeping habits, and physical activity), family history of specific diseases (coronary heart disease, cerebrovascular disease, coronary heart disease, and hypertension and diabetes), medical history of cardiovascular diseases (cerebrovascular disease, coronary heart disease, and heart failure), comorbidities (hypertension, diabetes, and dyslipidemia), and relative medication usage were obtained through the questionnaire. During the interview, each participant was asked to answer the question: “Have you ever been diagnosed with [specific disease] by a medical specialist?”. If he/she answered “Yes”, these related medical records were collected by trained members and were reviewed to confirm the report by clinicians ultimately. After data collection, trained staff manually abstracted relevant information to ensure data accuracy using double‐entry methods.

Physical examination results such as height, WC, and weight of participants were obtained to the nearest 0.1 cm and 0.1 kg, respectively, while they were wearing light clothing and without shoes. The BMI was calculated as weight in kilograms divided by the square of the height in meters. Using a standardized automatic electronic sphygmomanometer (J30; Omron, Kyoto, Japan), resting blood pressure was measured three times at least 1‐min intervals after rest for at least 5 min.

Blood samples were collected from subjects who had fasted for at least 8 h, through an antecubital vein using BD Vacutainer Serum Separation tubes (Becton, Dickinson and Co., Franklin Lakes, NJ, USA). Serum was subsequently isolated from the blood samples through a standard centrifuge and transported to a qualified laboratory at 20°C for testing. Using an auto‐analyzer (Abbott Diagnostics C800i, Abbott Laboratories, Abbott Park, Illinois, USA), these biochemical indexes, including fasting blood glucose (FBG), electrolyte, renal function, liver function, lipid profiles composed of total cholesterol (TC), triglyceride (TG), high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL‐C) and low‐density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL‐C) were comprehensively tested.

The WWI was calculated as WC (cm) divided by the square root of body weight (kg). Prehypertension was diagnosed as 120 ≤ a mean systolic blood pressure (SBP) < 140 mmHg and/or 80 ≤ a mean diastolic blood pressure (DBP) <90 mmHg, and hypertension was identified as use of antihypertensive drugs in the past two weeks and/or a mean SBP ≥ 140 mmHg and/or a mean DBP ≥ 90 mmHg [26]. Diabetes was diagnosed as an FPG ≥ 7.0 mmol/L, and/or self‐reported history of diabetes [27]. Dyslipidemia was diagnosed if the individuals met at least one of the following criteria: (1) serum TG level ≥ 2.26 mmol/L; (2) serum TC level ≥ 6.22 mmol/L; (3) serum HDL‐C < 1.04 mmol/L; (4) serum LDL‐C level ≥ 4.14 mmol/L; or (5) self‐reported use of lipid‐lowering drugs during the previous 4 weeks [28]. Participants who smoked at least one cigarette per day and had smoked for more than one year were defined as current smokers. Current drinking was defined as drinking ≥1 time per week [29]. Individuals who engaged in moderate to high‐intensity exercise for less than half an hour per session and fewer than three times per week were defined as lacking exercise [30]. Hemorrhagic and ischemic strokes were diagnosed by a neurologist and confirmed with CT and/or MRI, according to the World Health Organization recommendations [31]. The diagnosis of coronary heart disease was based on self‐reported history, and angina was confirmed through hospitalization treatment in secondary and above medical institutions. History of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) was defined as participants with coronary heart disease or stroke.

Continuous variables were presented as means and standard deviations, and the student's t‐test was conducted to evaluate these gender differences. Categorical variables were expressed as frequencies and percentages, and chi‐square tests were performed to demonstrate differences between groups. As a continuous variable (standardized by standard deviation), or a categorical variable (categorized by quartiles), the level of WWI was respectively incorporated into crude and multivariable logistic regression analysis to estimate the association between WWI levels with prehypertension and hypertension. The multivariable models were adjusted for established risk factors, including age, sex, marital status, education level, smoking status, alcohol consumption, physical activity, diabetes, and dyslipidemia. Subsequently, the dose–response relationship between WWI levels with prehypertension and hypertension was determined by the restricted cubic splines. Furthermore, to demonstrate the robustness of the associations between WWI levels with prehypertension and hypertension among different participants, the subgroup analyses and interaction tests stratified by conventional confounding factors, including age, sex, diabetes, dyslipidemia, and history of ASCVD, were implemented by multivariable logistic regression analysis. Ultimately, the discrimination and reclassification analyses were performed to assess the added value of WWI levels to an existing risk assessment model, including established risk factors such as age, sex, and diabetes, and the values of net reclassification improvement (NRI) and integrated discrimination improvement (IDI) were also further calculated. The SPSS version 22.0 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA) and the statistical software package R (https://www.R‐project.org, The R Foundation) were used in statistical analyses, and p‐values<0.05 were considered statistically significant.

## 结果 / Results

In the present study, a total of 4885 eligible residents remained for analysis, and these clinical characteristics of participants, including demographic and medical information by gender, are shown in Table 1. In general, the mean age of participants was 71.0 ± 6.7 years, and the proportion of male and urban residents was 44.0% and 31.7%, respectively. Compared with female inhabitants, male participants were older (0.6 ± 0.2 years older) and had significantly higher levels of education and proportion of married individuals (all p < 0.05). Meanwhile, male participants exhibited significantly higher BMI (0.5 ± 0.1 kg/m2 higher) and DBP (1.2 ± 0.2 mmHg higher), but lower WWI and lipid levels, including triglycerides (0.2 ± 0.0 mmol/L lower), TC (0.5 ± 0.0 mmol/L lower), LDL‐C (0.3 ± 0.0 mmol/L lower) and HDL‐C (0.1 ± 0.0 mmol/L lower) (all p < 0.05). Furthermore, a significant difference was observed in the proportion of current smoking, current drinking and history of ASCVD between male and female participants (all p < 0.05). There was no significant statistical difference between male and female participants for these clinical characteristics, including SBP, FBG, and the proportion of diabetes and regular exercise (all p > 0.05).

The regression analysis and restricted cubic splines were conducted to investigate the correlation between WWI with prehypertension and hypertension, as shown in Figure 1 and Table 2. As a continuous variable, a positive association between WWI with prehypertension and hypertension was demonstrated, with a per SD increase in WWI associated with a 29% increase in prehypertension prevalence (OR, 95% CI: 1.29, 1.15–1.45) and a 66% increase in hypertension prevalence (1.66, 1.48–1.85) in the crude model. The association between WWI with prehypertension and hypertension remained significant after adjusting for established covariates, including age, sex, marital status, education level, current smoking, current drinking, regular exercise, diabetes, and dyslipidemia, as appropriate [1.29 (1.14–1.45)] for prehypertension; [1.50 (1.34–1.68)] for hypertension. Moreover, the results of smoothing curve fitting and P for trend logistic regression, after adjusting for established covariates, suggested the linear positive correlation between WWI with prehypertension and hypertension (Table 2 and Figure 1).

## 讨论 / Discussion

In this large community‐based population investigation, we carefully evaluated the association between WWI levels with prehypertension and hypertension, examined its usefulness to improve the risk stratification of aforesaid adverse outcome, and identified the following findings: (1) a significant positive linear correlation was observed between WWI levels with prehypertension and hypertension, independent of a series of established covariates. (2) the aforesaid relationships were robust in both subgroup analyses and interaction tests stratified by traditional confounding factors, including age, sex, diabetes, dyslipidemia and history of ASCVD. (3) After the addition of WWI levels to the conventional model, the predictive performance for prevalent prehypertension and hypertension was significantly improved. In brief, our results demonstrated that WWI could be a potential indicator for assessing prevalent prehypertension and hypertension.

Although authoritative guidelines have recommended that obesity was a crucial independent risk factor for the occurrence and development of hypertension, the specific mechanisms by which obesity defined by WWI levels is associated with adverse outcomes remain unclear. Firstly, some studies found that WWI levels, an alternative measure of obesity, primarily represented the amount of visceral fat, and positively correlated with fat mass assessed by using BIA, DEXA, and CT [32]. The American Heart Association's scientific statement points out that excessive visceral fat can trigger systemic inflammation, activate neurohumoral pathways, and thereby increase blood pressure and lead to the incidence of hypertension [33]. Secondly, accumulating evidence indicated that insulin resistance significantly contributes to the pathogenesis and progression of hypertension, with partial studies corroborating that WWI was one of the parameters reflecting insulin resistance in the population [34]. Furthermore, there might be a reasonable explanation that WWI was a surrogate marker for established risk factors for hypertension, such as uric acid, as some studies have observed a significant positive correlation between WWI levels and uric acid [35, 36].

To our knowledge, the correlation between WWI levels and hypertension has been rarely examined in epidemiological investigations so far. Results from 9329 US adults aged ≥60 years from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey indicated that WWI levels were strongly correlated with the risk of developing hypertension [37]. Subsequently, another study based on data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey 2005–2018 provided further supplementary evidence, showing that the association between WWI levels and hypertension also applies to individuals under the age of 60 [38]. Similarly, the above correlation was also observed in the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study including 9298 middle‐aged and elderly Chinese [39]. Interestingly, another large prospective cohort consisting of 10 338 non‐hypertensive participants with a median follow‐up time of six years, the Rural Chinese Cohort Study, also demonstrated the aforementioned association, but did not indicate it in individuals aged 60 years and older [40]. There are several explanations of the differences in the above conclusions. First, Wang et al.’s study included 9329 adults aged ≥60 years (6674 hypertensive patients) across the United States and Huang et al.’s study included 4155 adults aged ≥60 years (1571 hypertensive patients) in 28 provinces across China, while Li et al.’s study only included 1768 adults aged ≥60 years (650 hypertensive patients) from rural areas of one province in China. The small sample size and geographical limitation may result in insufficient statistical power to detect a positive relationship. Second, the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey and the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study both employed a multistage sampling strategy, which is inherently designed to achieve a more robust and statistically efficient sample and provide more reliable and generalizable findings, compared with the cluster random sampling method used in the Rural Chinese Cohort Study. Notably, in the aforementioned studies, the predictive performance of WWI levels was not further evaluated, and the correlation between WWI levels and prehypertension was uncertain. In the present study, our research findings further strengthened the significant correlation between WWI levels and hypertension in individuals ≥60 years of age, more importantly, firstly corroborated the independent correlation between WWI levels and prehypertension. Meanwhile, we further observed that the aforesaid results were constant in subgroup analysis and interaction testing, independent of a series of conventional risk factors. Besides, our research results also suggested that adding WWI levels can effectively improve the risk stratification of prehypertension and hypertension.

There are some notable limitations in our study. Firstly, the cross‐sectional design of this study restricts to establishment of the exploration of the causal relationships between WWI levels with prehypertension and hypertension, as temporal associations cannot be inferred. Secondly, although the sample size of the investigation was substantial, these participants were from eastern China and over 60 years old. This may reduce the generalizability of our research findings to other populations. Thirdly, although confounding factors have been adequately adjusted in the analysis, some confounding factors demonstrated in other studies, such as circulating C‐reactive protein, highly sensitive C‐reactive protein, as well as interleukin 6, were not collected in the current investigation [41, 42]. Finally, the lack of biological measurements limited the exploration of the underlying mechanisms elucidating the association between WWI levels with prehypertension and hypertension.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In conclusion, our results underscored the strongly positive association between WWI levels and prehypertension and hypertension in the general population, independent of a range of conventional risk factors. Therefore, incorporating WWI assessment into the risk assessment of hypertension and prehypertension should be emphasized in clinical practice. In general, these findings have crucial clinical applications in enhancing early identification and intervention strategies for individuals at high risk for prehypertension and hypertension.
