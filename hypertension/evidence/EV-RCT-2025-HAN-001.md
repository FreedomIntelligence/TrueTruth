## English Abstract

Hypertensive crises require rapid blood pressure control to prevent stroke and myocardial injury. Despite Cleviprex's efficacy, its high cost limits accessibility in China. This Phase III, multicenter, randomized, double‐blind study evaluated the efficacy and safety of a generic clevidipine (China's first injectable generic clevidipine emulsion) versus the branded drug Cleviprex in managing hypertensive emergencies and urgencies among Chinese patients. A total of 377 patients of 33 medical centers from December 2021 to December 2022 were randomized, with 189 in the generic clevidipine group and 188 in the control. As a result, 95.6% of patients in the generic clevidipine group and 93.6% in the control achieved the target systolic blood pressure (SBP) reduction within 30 min (rate difference 0.020, 90% CI: −0.019 to 0.060). The mean area under the curve (AUC) of SBP outside the target range within the first hour was comparable between groups (329.8 ± 238.16 in generic clevidipine vs. 347.9 ± 302.79 in control). The median time to first achieve the target range within 30 min was 12.0 min in both groups. The proportion of patients successfully transitioning to oral therapy within 6 h was 93.4% in the generic clevidipine group and 93.6% in the control group. The incidence of drug‐related adverse events (AEs) was reported in 50 patients (27.3%) in the generic clevidipine group and in 48 (27.9%) in the control, with no unexpected safety signals. The generic clevidipine demonstrated comparable efficacy and safety to the branded drug, supporting its potential as an effective and accessible therapeutic alternative for acute hypertension management.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertensive emergencies and urgencies represent critical cardiovascular conditions characterized by abrupt, severe elevations in blood pressure, accompanied by evidence of impending or progressive end‐organ damage [1]. Globally, hypertension affects over 1.4 billion adults, with approximately 1%–2% progressing to hypertensive crises annually [2]. These conditions are common in emergency departments, accounting for over 25% of medical emergencies [3, 4]. The prevalence of hypertensive emergencies ranges from 0.3% to 20.4%, while hypertensive urgencies are more frequent at 0.9% to 84.5% [5, 6]. These conditions pose significant risks to patients, including acute cardiovascular events, stroke, renal failure, and other life‐threatening complications [7, 8, 9]. Current guidelines emphasize rapid blood pressure reduction within minutes to hours to mitigate irreversible organ injury, yet the selection of optimal therapeutic agents remains contentious due to trade‐offs between efficacy, safety, and accessibility [10].

First‐line agents such as sodium nitroprusside and nicardipine have demonstrated efficacy but are associated with significant limitations, including cyanide toxicity, reflex tachycardia, and excessive fluid administration [11]. Clevidipine injectable emulsion (Cleviprex), a third‐generation dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker approved for intravenous management of acute hypertension in multiple countries and regions such as the United States, Japan, and Australia, has emerged as a preferred option due to its rapid onset (< 2 min), short half‐life (∼1 min), and titratability, achieving blood pressure control in 88%–95% of patients within 15–30 min across clinical trials [12, 13, 14]. Its esterase‐mediated metabolism avoids renal/hepatic dependence, making it suitable for critically ill patients [15, 16, 17]. However, the original branded drug (Cleviprex) has not yet been approved for market entry in China. Its high cost and limited accessibility in resource‐constrained settings underscore the necessity for affordable therapeutic alternatives that maintain clinical efficacy without compromising therapeutic profiles. This highlights the critical balance between cost‐effectiveness and therapeutic integrity in addressing acute hypertension management under healthcare resource limitations.

Generic medications play a crucial role in healthcare systems by increasing accessibility to essential drugs [18]. However, despite their importance, there has been a notable gap in the clinical evidence for generic clevidipine injectable emulsion in China, particularly in the context of hypertensive emergencies and urgencies where precise blood pressure control is paramount. To date, no Phase III clinical trials have been published that specifically evaluate the efficacy and safety of generic clevidipine injectable emulsion in managing hypertensive crises among the Chinese population. This gap in evidence has significantly limited the comprehensive understanding of generic clevidipine injectable emulsion's therapeutic potential in this critical area, particularly given the unique clinical characteristics and treatment needs of Chinese patients with acute hypertension.

KEXIDA, a generic clevidipine injectable emulsion developed by Sichuan Kelun Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd., represents a promising candidate to address these gaps. Preclinical studies have confirmed bioequivalence to Cleviprex, with comparable pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic properties. The present Phase III, multicenter, randomized, double‐blind, positive drug parallel‐controlled study aims to comprehensively assess the efficacy and safety of generic clevidipine compared with the branded drug in patients with hypertensive emergencies and urgencies. This study is designed to provide robust evidence on whether generic clevidipine can achieve comparable BP reductions, maintain hemodynamic stability, and demonstrate a similar safety profile to the branded drug. The findings will address critical gaps in global cardiovascular care, aligning with international guidelines advocating for tailored, rapid‐acting antihypertensives to mitigate end‐organ damage and improve clinical outcomes.

## 方法 / Methods

This Phase III clinical trial was a multicenter, randomized, double‐blind, positive drug parallel‐controlled study performed at 33 medical centers between December 2021 and December 2022 to evaluate the efficacy and safety of generic clevidipine injectable emulsion in managing hypertensive non‐fulminant emergencies and urgencies.

Male or female Chinese patients aged ≥ 18 to ≤ 75 years with acute elevation of blood pressure (systolic blood pressure [SBP] ≥180 mmHg and/or diastolic blood pressure [DBP] ≥ 115 mmHg) confirmed by two consecutive measurements at least 15 min apart requiring intravenous medication for rapid blood pressure control were eligible for this study [19, 20].

The exclusion criteria were (1) inability to tolerate prolonged intravenous treatment; (2) use of other antihypertensive medications within 2 h before screening; (3) life‐threatening conditions such as malignant arrhythmias (ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation, severe bradycardia), gastrointestinal bleeding, respiratory failure, or severe impaired consciousness; (4) severe acute cardiovascular diseases (e.g., confirmed or suspected severe aortic stenosis, aortic dissection, myocardial infarction within 1 month before consent); (5) acute stroke within 1 month before consent; (6) pheochromocytoma; (7) primary aldosteronism; (8) lipid metabolism defects (e.g., hereditary hyperlipidemia, chylomicronemia); (9) cirrhosis or liver failure; (10) chronic kidney disease stage 5 requiring regular dialysis; (11) allergy to the study drug or its excipients (soy, soy products, or egg products); (12) history of alcohol or drug abuse; (13) psychiatric or psychological disorders affecting communication; (14) pregnant or nursing mothers and patients of childbearing potential (regardless of gender) who are unable to use medically accepted contraception during the trial and for 3 months after the study; or (15) participation in another interventional clinical trial within 30 days before consent.

Following screening, patients were randomized in a 1:1 ratio to double‐blind treatment with clevidipine injectable emulsion (generic clevidipine, 50 mL: 25 mg) or the control product, clevidipine injectable emulsion (brand drug, Cleviprex, 50 mL: 25 mg, manufactured by Chiesi Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd). All eligible patients were randomized using an Interactive Web Response System (IWRS).

Both the experimental and control groups followed the same medication administration protocol. Initially, during the first 30 min of intravenous infusion, an infusion pump was used to target a blood pressure reduction of 15% to 25% from baseline. The starting infusion rate was 4 mL/h, with a recommended dose doubling every 3 min. When blood pressure approached within 5 mmHg of the upper target range (15% reduction), dose increments were limited to less than 1‐fold every 5 to 10 min, with suggested increases of 2 to 4 mL/h to achieve the target range within 30 min. Once blood pressure dropped below a 15% reduction, investigators adjusted the dose in real time to maintain stable blood pressure. When blood pressure neared the lower target range (within 5 mmHg of a 25% reduction), the current infusion rate and dose were maintained without further increases. The maximum infusion rate of clevidipine injectable emulsion was capped at ≤ 64 mL/h. After the initial 30 min, the infusion rate was titrated based on blood pressure monitoring to maintain the target levels. Specifically, within the first hour of infusion, blood pressure was controlled to not exceed a 25% reduction from baseline. Between 2 and 6 h, blood pressure was managed at a safer level (around 160/100 mmHg), with adjustments according to the target levels and rate of blood pressure reduction tailored to different target organ damages. The infusion continued for at least 6 h and up to 72 h, with a maximum daily dose of 1000 mL within 24 h. If blood pressure fell below the lower target range, investigators reduced or paused the infusion based on clinical experience to prevent organ damage. If the target blood pressure range was not achieved, additional intravenous antihypertensive agents could be added as needed. Blood pressure was monitored at the following time points: immediately before infusion (within 5 min); every 3 min during the first 30 min; every 15 min (±1 min) between 30 min and 2 h; every 30 min (±3 min) between 2 and 6 h; and every 2 h (±5 min) between 6 and 72 h.

Approximately 1 h before discontinuing the clevidipine injectable emulsion, oral antihypertensive medications were initiated (selected by the investigator based on the patient's prior treatment and clinical condition). After discontinuation, patients were observed for at least 8 h to monitor for blood pressure fluctuations. Blood pressure was measured at 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 8 h after stopping the intravenous infusion (with a time window of ±3 min for 0.25 to 1 h and ±5 min for 2 to 8 h).

The study protocol was reviewed and approved by the institutional review board or independent ethics committee of each participating center. The study was conducted in accordance with the ICH Harmonized Tripartite Guidelines for Good Clinical Practice (with applicable local regulations) and the ethical principles in the Declaration of Helsinki. All patients provided written informed consent before the initiation of any study procedure.

The primary efficacy endpoint was the proportion of patients whose SBP decreased to the target range (a 15%–25% reduction from baseline) within 30 min of drug administration. Secondary efficacy endpoints included (1) the relative area under or above the blood pressure‐time curve representing the excess SBP above the upper limit and below the lower limit of the target range within the first hour of drug administration (the unit of this index is defined as the product of mmHg and minutes, with baseline SBP measured immediately before treatment and the target range as a 15%–25% reduction from baseline); (2) the time to first achievement of target SBP within 30 min; and (3) the proportion of patients successfully transitioning to oral antihypertensive therapy within 6 h after drug discontinuation. To further evaluate the efficacy, subgroup analysis was conducted on subjects in two subgroups: hypertensive emergency and hypertensive urgencies, targeting the primary endpoint of the study.

Safety evaluations included adverse events (AEs), serious adverse events (SAEs), changes in heart rate within 30 min of drug administration, laboratory tests (complete blood count, urinalysis, blood biochemistry [liver and kidney function], electrolytes, and lipid profile), vital signs, and 12‐lead electrocardiograms (ECGs). All AEs were coded using the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities (MedDRA) version 25.1 and WHODrug Global B3 format on September 1, 2022, and graded for severity according to the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE) version 5.0. Laboratory tests and ECGs were assessed for clinically significant changes from baseline to follow‐up visits.

The sample size for this study was calculated based on the primary efficacy endpoint. Assuming the treatment success rate of the test drug is similar to that of the control drug, the significance level (α) was set at 0.05, and the statistical power (1 − β) was set at a minimum of 80%. The equivalence margin for the difference between the two groups was set at ±10%. Referring to the Phase III pivotal clinical trial of the branded drug (VELOCITY), the proportion of subjects whose SBP decreased to the target range within 30 min of initiating clevidipine injectable emulsion was 88.9% [20]. Based on these assumptions, the sample size calculation indicated that at least 170 subjects were required per group. To account for a potential dropout rate of 10%, the sample size was adjusted to 189 subjects per group. Therefore, a total of 378 subjects were required for the study.

This study followed the intention‐to‐treat principle, and the data obtained from the subjects were largely divided into the safety set, the full analysis set (FAS), and the per‐protocol set (PPS). For the efficacy assessment, the primary analysis set was the PPS and the FAS. The missing data of the main efficacy indicators in FAS and PPS will be supplemented using the method of last observation data carry over (LOCF). The safety assessment results were presented for the safety set.

For continuous variables, descriptive statistics included the number of observations, number of missing cases, mean, standard deviation, median, quartiles, minimum, and maximum values. For categorical variables, frequencies and percentages were reported. The primary efficacy analysis compared the proportion of patients achieving the target SBP reduction within 30 min between the test and control groups. The difference in proportions and its 90% confidence interval were calculated using the normal approximation method. Clinical equivalence was concluded if the 90% confidence interval for the difference in proportions fell within the equivalence margin of −0.1 to 0.1. Sensitivity analysis was performed using the Newcombe method to confirm the robustness of the primary analysis results. Secondary efficacy endpoints were analyzed descriptively, with the median of intergroup differences estimated using the Hodges–Lehmann method and 95% confidence intervals calculated using the Hollander normal approximation or Wald method. Kaplan–Meier analysis was used to assess the time to first reduction of SBP to the target range within 30 min.

Safety analyses were conducted on the SS, summarizing AEs, laboratory parameters, vital signs, and ECG findings. All statistical tests were two‐sided, with a significance level of 0.05. All statistical analyses were performed using SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA).

## 结果 / Results

At 33 study sites, 414 subjects were screened for participation in this study. Among them, 37 subjects failed the screening, and 377 subjects were randomized, with 189 assigned to the generic clevidipine group and 188 to the reference drug group (Cleviprex) (Figure 1). Of these, 355 patients were included in the FAS (183 in the generic clevidipine group and 172 in the control group), and 345 patients were included in the PPS (178 in the generic clevidipine group and 167 in the control group). In the FAS, the mean age of patients in the generic clevidipine group was 50.4 ± 13.2 years, and 56.3% were male, compared to 49.8 ± 14.2 years and 61.0% male in the control group. The body mass index (BMI) was 26.7 ± 4.6 kg/m2 in the generic clevidipine group and 26.1 ± 3.7 kg/m2 in the control group, respectively. The PPS analysis showed similar trends, with no significant differences in age, gender, or BMI between the two groups (Table 1).

In terms of baseline disease characteristics, 31.7% of patients in the generic clevidipine group and 32.0% in the control group were diagnosed with hypertensive emergencies, while 68.3% and 68.0%, respectively, were diagnosed with hypertensive urgencies. The majority of patients in both groups exhibited symptoms associated with hypertensive emergencies or urgencies, such as headache and dizziness. The mean baseline SBP was 194.3 mmHg in the generic clevidipine group and 196.4 mmHg in the control group, while the mean DBP was 116.4 and 116.9 mmHg, respectively (Table 1). Overall, the study population was representative of patients with hypertensive emergencies and urgencies requiring rapid intravenous antihypertensive therapy, and the baseline characteristics were well‐balanced between the generic clevidipine and control groups, ensuring the robustness of the study design.

Regarding the primary endpoint, the results showed that in the FAS with LOCF imputation, 95.6% (175/183) of subjects in the test group and 93.6% (161/172) of subjects in the control group achieved the target range SBP within 30 min of drug administration (Figure 2). The rate difference was 0.020, with a 90% confidence interval of (−0.019, 0.060). Using the Newcombe method for sensitivity analysis, the rate difference was also 0.020, with a 90% confidence interval of (−0.020, 0.063). In the PPS with LOCF imputation, 96.6% (172/178) of subjects in the test group and 94.6% (158/167) of subjects in the control group achieved the target range. The rate difference was 0.020, with a 90% confidence interval of (−0.016, 0.057) using the normal approximation method and (−0.017, 0.060) using the Newcombe method. The unadjusted results were consistent with the LOCF imputed results, showing that the rate difference and its 90% confidence interval were within the equivalence margin of (−0.1, 0.1) (Table 2). In summary, based on the analyses of the FAS and the PPS, the rate difference of subjects whose SBP decreased to the target range between the test and control groups, calculated using the normal approximation method, had a two‐sided 90% confidence interval within (−0.1, 0.1). Additionally, the two‐sided 90% confidence interval for the rate difference calculated using the Newcombe method was also within (−0.1, 0.1), further confirming the robustness of the primary analysis results. The study achieved its primary efficacy endpoint, demonstrating that the test drug is comparable to the control drug in terms of efficacy, meeting the clinical equivalence criteria.

In the FAS, the median time to first achieve the target range within 30 min was 12.0 min in both the test group and the control group. The proportion of subjects achieving the target range at different time points (3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24, 27, and 30 min) was similar between the two groups. For example, at 12 min, 58.3% of subjects in the test group and 59.0% of subjects in the control group achieved the target range. In the PPS, the results were consistent with those in the FAS (Figure 2) (Table 3).

In the FAS with NRI (non‐resistant imputation), 93.4% (171/183) of subjects in the test group and 93.6% (161/172) of subjects in the control group successfully transitioned to oral antihypertensive therapy within 6 h after discontinuation. The rate difference was −0.2, with a 95% confidence interval of (−5.3, 5.0). In the PPS with NRI, the proportion was 93.8% (167/178) in the test group and 94.0% (157/167) in the control group, with a rate difference of −0.2 and a 95% confidence interval of (−5.2, 4.9). This indicated that the test drug and the control drug had similar efficacy in facilitating the transition from intravenous to oral antihypertensive therapy (Table 3).

Subgroup analyses were conducted based on the type of hypertensive condition (hypertensive emergency vs. hypertensive urgency). In the FAS with LOCF imputation, for hypertensive emergency patients, the proportion of subjects in the test group whose SBP decreased to the target range within 30 min was 96.6% (56/58), compared to 94.5% (52/55) in the control group. For hypertensive urgency patients, the proportions were 95.2% (119/125) in the test group and 93.2% (109/117) in the control group. In the PPS with LOCF imputation, the results were similar. The rate differences and their 90% confidence intervals were within the equivalence margin of (−0.1, 0.1) in both subgroups, indicating that the efficacy of the test drug was comparable to that of the control drug in both hypertensive emergency and hypertensive urgency patients (Table 4).

Drug‐related AEs were reported in 50 patients (27.3%) in the generic clevidipine group and in 48 patients (27.9%) in the control group. The most frequently reported AEs in the generic clevidipine group included hypokalemia (9.3%), hypertriglyceridemia (8.7%), and hyperlipidemia (6.0%), while in the control group, they were hypokalemia (6.4%), hypertriglyceridemia (5.8%), and tachycardia (4.1%). Six patients experienced SAEs: 1.1% (2/183) in the generic clevidipine group (cerebral infarction) and 2.3% (4/172) in the control group (cerebral infarction, cerebellar infarction, and heart failure), all of which were deemed unrelated to the study drug. No deaths were reported in either group during the study period.

The incidence of heart rate‐related AEs was similar between the generic clevidipine group (3.3%) and the control group (3.5%), with the most common event being tachycardia. The majority of these events were of mild to moderate severity (CTCAE Grade 1 or 2). Detailed monitoring showed that heart rate changes within 30 min of drug administration were generally stable, with no significant differences observed between the two groups. The incidence of laboratory abnormalities was low and mostly of mild severity. In the generic clevidipine group, notable laboratory AEs included hypertriglyceridemia (16 cases, 8.7%) and elevated blood glucose (2 cases, 1.1%). In the control group, hypertriglyceridemia (10 cases, 5.8%) and elevated blood glucose (1 case, 0.6%) were reported. All other laboratory parameters remained within normal ranges or were clinically insignificant. No significant changes or AEs related to vital signs were reported in either group. The incidence of ECG‐related AEs was low, with the generic clevidipine group reporting 2 cases (1.1%) of T‐wave abnormalities and 1 case (0.5%) of QRS axis deviation. The control group reported 3 cases (1.7%) of T‐wave abnormalities and 1 case (0.6%) of ST‐T segment abnormalities. All ECG abnormalities were of mild severity (CTCAE Grade 1) and were deemed related to the study drug. Overall, the safety profile of generic clevidipine was comparable to that of the control drug, with no unexpected safety signals identified.

## 讨论 / Discussion

The present Phase III, multicenter, randomized, double‐blind, positive drug parallel‐controlled study provides robust evidence on the efficacy and safety of a generic clevidipine injectable emulsion in managing hypertensive emergencies and urgencies. The findings demonstrate that generic clevidipine is comparable to the branded drug Cleviprex in terms of efficacy and safety, meeting the clinical equivalence criteria and addressing a critical gap in the treatment options for acute hypertension in China.

The primary efficacy endpoint of this study was the proportion of patients whose SBP decreased to the target range (a 15%–25% reduction from baseline) within 30 min of drug administration. In the FAS with last observation carried forward (LOCF) imputation, 95.6% (175/183) of subjects in the generic clevidipine group and 93.6% (161/172) of subjects in the control group achieved the target range SBP within 30 min. Similarly, in the PPS with LOCF imputation, 96.6% (172/178) of subjects in the generic clevidipine group and 94.6% (158/167) of subjects in the control group achieved the target range. These results are consistent with previous findings from clinical trials related to clevidipine injectable emulsion [13, 14, 15, 17]. This indicates that generic clevidipine can rapidly and effectively lower blood pressure to the target range, similar to Cleviprex.

The secondary efficacy endpoints further support the efficacy of generic clevidipine. The mean AUC of SBP outside the target range within the first hour was 329.8 ± 238.16 mmHg·min in the generic clevidipine group and 347.9 ± 302.79 mmHg·min in the control group in the FAS with LOCF imputation, suggesting that generic clevidipine can maintain blood pressure within the desired range as effectively as the branded drug. Additionally, the median time to first achieve the target range within 30 min was 12.0 min in both groups, indicating comparable onset of action. Furthermore, 93.4% (171/183) of subjects in the generic clevidipine group and 93.6% (161/172) of subjects in the control group successfully transitioned to oral antihypertensive therapy within 6 h after discontinuation in the FAS. This highlights the similar efficacy of generic clevidipine and the control drug in facilitating the transition from intravenous to oral antihypertensive therapy. These observations are in accordance with an earlier study that demonstrated benefits of clevidipine in American patients with acute and severe hypertension, with 88% of patients transitioned to oral antihypertensive therapy within 6 h of clevidipine termination [21].

Subgroup analyses based on the type of hypertensive condition (hypertensive emergency vs. hypertensive urgency) also demonstrated comparable efficacy. In hypertensive emergency patients, 96.6% (56/58) of subjects in the generic clevidipine group and 94.5% (52/55) of subjects in the control group achieved the target range within 30 min. In hypertensive urgency patients, the proportions were 95.2% (119/125) in the generic clevidipine group and 93.2% (109/117) in the control group. These results confirm that generic clevidipine is effective in both hypertensive emergency and hypertensive urgency scenarios.

The safety profile of generic clevidipine was comparable to that of the control drug. The overall incidence of AEs was 44.3% in the generic clevidipine group and 44.2% in the control group. The incidence of drug‐related AEs was 27.3% in the generic clevidipine group and 27.9% in the control group. The most frequently reported AEs in the generic clevidipine group included hypokalemia (9.3%), hypertriglyceridemia (8.7%), and hyperlipidemia (6.0%), while in the control group, they were hypokalemia (6.4%), hypertriglyceridemia (5.8%), and tachycardia (4.1%). Notably, the majority of AEs were of mild to moderate severity (CTCAE Grade 1 or 2), with only a small number of patients experiencing Grade 3 AEs in both groups. Clevidipine works by selectively blocking L‐type calcium channels in the smooth muscle cells of blood vessels, leading to vasodilation and a reduction in blood pressure. However, this mechanism can also indirectly affect electrolyte balance, particularly potassium levels [22]. Therefore, although hypokalemia has not been specifically mentioned in previous studies, this study still found that hypokalemia is the most common AE [23]. The higher incidence of drug‐related CTCAE Grade 3 AEs in the generic clevidipine group compared to the control group may be attributed to the specific formulation of generic clevidipine. This generic clevidipine injectable emulsion contains a lipid emulsion that can potentially affect lipid metabolism, leading to events such as hypertriglyceridemia [24]. Additionally, the occurrence of cerebral infarction, although rare, may be related to individual patient risk factors and the pharmacokinetic properties of the generic formulation. These findings highlight the importance of careful patient selection and monitoring, particularly in those with pre‐existing metabolic or cerebrovascular conditions, to mitigate potential risks associated with the use of generic formulations.

Six patients experienced serious SAEs: 1.1% (2/183) in the generic clevidipine group (cerebral infarction) and 2.3% (4/172) in the control group (cerebral infarction, cerebellar infarction, and heart failure). All SAEs were deemed unrelated to the study drug, and no deaths were reported in either group during the study period. The incidence of heart rate‐related AEs was similar between the generic clevidipine group (3.3%) and the control group (3.5%), with tachycardia being the most common event. Detailed monitoring showed that heart rate changes within 30 min of drug administration were generally stable, with no significant differences observed between the two groups.

Laboratory abnormalities were low in incidence and mostly of mild severity. No significant changes or AEs related to vital signs were reported in either group. The incidence of electrocardiogram (ECG)‐related AEs was also low; all ECG abnormalities were of mild severity (CTCAE Grade 1) and were deemed related to the study drug.

The findings of this study have important clinical implications, especially for the Chinese population. The generic clevidipine injectable emulsion offers a cost‐effective alternative to the branded drug Cleviprex without compromising efficacy or safety. This is particularly significant in China, where access to expensive branded medications may be limited, and the unique clinical characteristics of hypertensive emergencies and urgencies necessitate tailored therapeutic options. By providing an affordable option, generic clevidipine can potentially improve the accessibility of effective antihypertensive therapy for patients with hypertensive emergencies and urgencies, thereby enhancing overall clinical outcomes.

Moreover, the results align with international guidelines advocating for rapid‐acting antihypertensives to mitigate end‐organ damage and improve clinical outcomes in hypertensive crises. The study provides the first rigorous evaluation of a generic clevidipine injectable emulsion in a Chinese population, thereby addressing a critical gap in the clinical evidence that has long existed for this demographic. The results underscore the importance of generating localized data to better understand and manage hypertensive crises in Chinese patients. The findings support the use of generic clevidipine as a viable alternative to branded medications, ensuring that patients receive high‐quality care regardless of economic constraints. Based on the present Chinese cohort data, generic clevidipine should be initiated at 4 mL/h (equivalent to 2 mg/h) and titrated every 3 min to attain a 15%–25% SBP reduction within 30 min, with transition to oral therapy within 6 h. Blood pressure, electrolytes, and lipids should be monitored in accordance with study observations of hypokalemia and hypertriglyceridemia.

While this study provides valuable insights into the efficacy and safety of generic clevidipine, there are some limitations to consider. First, the study was conducted in a specific population, and the results may not be directly applicable to other ethnic or demographic groups. Second, patients presenting with fulminant or severe acute organ complications—such as acute stroke, myocardial infarction, aortic dissection, malignant arrhythmias, respiratory failure, or markedly impaired consciousness—were excluded because these conditions mandate urgent, protocol‐deviating interventions and would introduce uncontrolled confounding into the efficacy and safety analyses. Consequently, the study population comprised hypertensive urgencies and hypertensive emergencies that lacked these fulminant features, and the results should be interpreted within this carefully defined clinical context. Additionally, the study duration was limited to 72 h, and long‐term efficacy and safety data are not available. Future studies should explore the long‐term outcomes of generic clevidipine use and its potential benefits in diverse patient populations.

## 结论 / Conclusion

This Phase III study demonstrates that generic clevidipine is comparable to the branded drug Cleviprex in terms of efficacy and safety for managing hypertensive emergencies and urgencies. The findings support the use of generic clevidipine as a cost‐effective alternative, ensuring that patients receive effective and accessible antihypertensive therapy. Future research should focus on long‐term outcomes and the broader application of generic clevidipine in different clinical settings to further validate its therapeutic potential.
