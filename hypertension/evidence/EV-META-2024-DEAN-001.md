## English Abstract

Adherence to antihypertensives is crucial for control of blood pressure. This study analyzed factors and interventions that could affect adherence to antihypertensives in the US. PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and Embase were searched on January 21, 2022 and December 25, 2023 for studies on the adherence to antihypertensives in the US. Nineteen studies and 23 545 747 patients were included in the analysis, which showed that adherence to antihypertensives was the highest among Whites (OR: 1.47, 95% CI 1.34–1.61 compared to African Americans). Employment status and sex were associated with insignificant differences in adherence rates. In contrast, marital status yielded a significant difference where unmarried patients demonstrated low adherence rates compared to married ones (OR: 0.8, 95% CI 0.67–0.95). On analysis of comorbidities, diabetic patients reported lower adherence to antihypertensives (OR: 0.95, 95% CI 0.92–0.97); furthermore, patients who did not have Alzheimer showed higher adherence rates. Different BMIs did not significantly affect the adherence rates. Patients without insurance reported significantly lower adherence rates than insured patients (OR: 3.93, 95% CI 3.43–4.51). Polypill users had higher adherence rates compared with the free‐dose combination (OR: 1.21, 95% CI 1.2–1.21), while telepharmacy did not prove to be as effective. Lower adherence rates were seen among African Americans, uninsured, or younger patients. Accordingly, interventions such as fixed‐dose combinations should be targeted at susceptible groups. Obesity and overweight did not affect the adherence to antihypertensives.

## 背景 / Background

Antihypertensive medications play a pivotal role in the control of blood pressure, protecting the general population against detrimental outcomes such as coronary artery disease and renal failure.
1
, 
2
 Despite vast advancements in the medical management of hypertension, medication non‐adherence persists as a significant barrier to optimal blood pressure control, with a national non‐adherence rate of 31% among insured adults in the US.
3
, 
4

Low adherence to antihypertensives is common and significantly contributes to the majority of cases of uncontrolled hypertension across the country.
5
 This may be partially because many hypertensive adults experience no symptoms, decreasing their incentive to take their medications.
6
 Moreover, the general population lacks an understanding of the importance of preventing and treating the disease.
7

The causes of low adherence are multifactorial, including social determinants of health, which include socioeconomic status and education level, behavioral factors such as forgetfulness, or continued access to complex multi‐pill regimens.
8
, 
9
 Additional factors include cost, patient health beliefs, and the occurrence of side effects; overcoming these barriers requires the patient's understanding of the consequences of non‐adherence and what it means for the effectiveness in the treatment of hypertension, as well as prescriber awareness of patient circumstances.
7

To our knowledge, no meta‐analyses have been conducted on adherence to antihypertensive medications in the US. Therefore, this study aimed to analyze the patterns between adherence and various social, demographic, and behavioral factors, as well as comorbidities, interventions, and insurance utilized among adults in the US.

## 方法 / Methods

This study was conducted according to the PRISMA guidelines.

A literature search of the following databases (PubMed, Scopus, Embase, and Web of Science) on January 21, 2022 and on December 25, 2023, using key terms such as “Adherence”, “Antihypertensive”, and “United States”, was performed to identify studies of interest (view the supplementary material for the full search strategy).

We screened studies by titles and abstracts according to the following criteria:

Inclusion criteria: We included observational studies with data on the adherence to antihypertensives among adults (≥18 years) living in the United States, including cross‐sectional, case‐control, and cohort studies. Additionally, studies that investigated different interventions to improve adherence to antihypertensives were also included.

Exclusion criteria: We excluded studies that were conducted on a patient population outside the United States. Also, editorials, systematic reviews, meta‐analyses, case reports, case series, animal studies, and studies without data about adherence to antihypertensives were excluded.

In the case of duplicate studies, the most recent study with the largest study population was included.

Two independent reviewers (K.A. and W.H.) screened the studies according to our criteria. If a consensus is not achieved, a third independent reviewer was assigned to resolve the conflict.

Two independent extractors (M.A.A. and B.A.) screened the studies according to our criteria. If a consensus is not achieved, a third independent reviewer was assigned to resolve the conflict.

For the baseline and summary, the following data were extracted from the eligible studies: Author's last name, study design, sample size, state, method of measurement of adherence and race.

For the primary and secondary outcomes, the following data were extracted: age, sex, race, marital status, smoking status, level of education, BMI, alcohol intake, insurance status, type of insurance, and intervention utilized to improve adherence.

The risk of bias was assessed utilizing Newcastle‐Ottawa Scale (NOS) items,
10
 with a total score of nine points, to evaluate the quality of observational studies. We defined the observational studies with a NOS score of ≥7 stars as high quality and NOS score of <7 stars as low quality.

The included studies defined adherence using the following methods; the first was the proportion of days covered (PDC)
11
 where the cut‐off value for adherent patients was 80% or more. The second scale was the Morisky Medication Adherence Scale (MMAS 4‐ and 8‐items).
12
, 
13
, 
14
, 
15
 MMAS is a validated tool that measures adherence using patients’ Yes/No responses to a set of questions that targets potential barriers to adherence. For the 8‐item scale, the included studies defined adherence as having a score of 8, where “yes” was equivalent to 0 and “no” was equivalent to 1. On the other hand, studies that assessed adherence using the MMAS 4‐item scale defined adherence as having a score of 0. The reason behind this disparity is that the included studies reversed the scoring system on the 4‐item scale; where “yes” was equated to 1 and “no” was equated to 0. Regardless of this, both scoring systems achieved their purpose, where answering “yes” to potential barriers to adherence reflected lower rates of adherence. Data were analyzed using R software version 4.2.1 and RevMan software, version 5.4. Sensitivity analysis (leave‐one‐out test and subgroup analysis) was used. If no heterogeneity was observed, results were presented in a fixed effect model and a random effect model if significant heterogeneity was observed. Results were considered significant if the p‐value was less than .05.

## 讨论 / Discussion

Our results depict heterogeneity in the association between various sociodemographic and behavioral factors with adherence to antihypertensive medications. Specifically, our analysis yielded statistically significant racial differences in adherence to antihypertensives, with the highest adherence rates reported among Whites. Low adherence rates were seen among unmarried hypertensive patients; Smoking did not significantly affect adherence, while heavy alcohol consumption was associated with lower adherence rates. Patients who had diabetes, Alzheimer's, or depression had lower adherence rates to antihypertensives.

According to our results, higher adherence rates were observed among older patients. Some earlier studies support our findings as they linked young age with significantly lower medication adherence. This could be due to the decreased severity of the disease (or even asymptomatic hypertension) in these younger patients when compared to older individuals who are more likely to have a more severe disease and attendant complications, which translate into a better understanding of their medication needs and further incentive towards better medication adherence.
37
, 
38
, 
39
 Our review of available literature on sex disparities in antihypertensive non‐adherence returned mixed results. While some studies in Asia suggest higher adherence rates among females than males,
40
 a systematic review and meta‐analysis in Asia reported significantly lower adherence among females.
41
 In comparison to the aforementioned studies, whereas our analysis portrayed a relatively lower adherence rate among females, the difference compared to males was not statistically significant, which more closely aligns our findings with those of a broader continental systematic review and meta‐analysis where no statistically significant difference in medication adherence rate realized between males and females.
42

To some extent, racial disparities in the burden of hypertension and associated management outcomes have been established
43
; some researchers have explored possible racial differences in antihypertensive medication adherence as a potential contributor to any differences in outcomes associated with hypertension among different racial groups. In a retrospective analysis of Medicare claims data, Holmes and coworkers found that compared to Whites, the antihypertensive adherence rate was lower among African Americans, Hispanics, and other non‐white subgroups.
44
 Complementary research showed significantly lower adherence among African Americans than Whites.
45
 Our analysis further reinforced their results. We find it plausible that any existing racial and ethnic differences in adherence rates may partly be explained by underlying differences in sociocultural factors among the racial groups.
46

Some researchers believe that social support in marriage can positively influence antihypertensive medication adherence
47
; a meta‐analysis by DeMatteo and coworkers supports this position that found considerable improvements in non‐adherence rates to medical regimens for married individuals.
48
 Nevertheless, evidence from available literature suggests intermixed findings of a significant negative relationship between marital status and antihypertensive medication non‐adherence
49
 and a lack of significant association between marital status and adherence to treatment recommendations among individuals with hypertension,
50
 with our findings depicting a substantial difference in hypertensive medication adherence between married and unmarried individuals reinforcing the former.

Behavioral risk factors such as smoking have presented varying results in significance for antihypertensive medication adherence between smokers and non‐smokers. Some researchers reported significantly lower antihypertensive medication adherence among smokers compared to non‐smokers, with attendant explanations suggesting a possible relative lag in health maintenance and promotion behaviors among smokers.
51
 This notwithstanding, a cross‐sectional study combining participants from a large Health Maintenance Organization and a Veteran Affairs Medical Center did not find any statistically significant differences in antihypertensive medication adherence between smokers and non‐smokers similar to our findings.
52
 Another behavioral risk factor that could impact adherence is alcohol consumption; our results revealed that heavy alcohol consumption is associated with poor adherence; this comes in line with the findings of Bryson and coworkers,
53
 where authors have conducted a randomized controlled clinical trial in seven Veteran Affairs primary clinics and their results have shown that alcohol misuse is associated with medication non‐adherence. Another study has demonstrated similar findings among African American men suffering from hypertension.
54
 Nevertheless, a recent study conducted by Philips and coworkers.
55
 detected an insignificant association between adherence to antihypertensives and alcohol use. The different measures of adherence in that study could explain this.

Health insurance type, educational level, and employment status are sociodemographic factors widely known to contribute to disparities in health and health outcomes.
56
, 
57
 It is conceivable that individuals with higher levels of education and better employment are more likely to be insured and, by extension, have better access to hypertension treatment opportunities, which may invariably translate into relatively higher medication adherence. Our analysis revealed that employment status did not significantly affect adherence rates, contradicting the findings of a cross‐sectional study conducted by Andualem and coworkers,
58
 where employed hypertensive patients reported higher adherence rates. Another study demonstrated that unemployment was associated with higher adherence rates among hypertensive patients in Saudi Arabia.
59
 This could be attributed to sociocultural differences, as employment status is an essential determinant of health insurance status in different countries.
60

Educational attainment is a functional determinant of health literacy, which influences health behaviors, including medication adherence.
61
 Moreover, a study in New York City has shown that higher educational levels are associated with better hypertension medication adherence among Hispanics, with similar results realized by researchers in the United Arab Emirates.
62
 Such findings support our results, which showed a significant association between the level of education and higher adherence rates.

On analysis of the effect of comorbidities on adherence to antihypertensives, our findings show a significant association between the presence of various comorbidities and poor adherence to antihypertensives. Similar findings were demonstrated by Algabbani and coworkers
63
 and Saadat and coworkers
64
 in Saudi Arabia and Iran, respectively. In addition to comorbid conditions, it is crucial to assess the impact of mental health on medication adherence; our results revealed low adherence rates among hypertensive patients who suffer from depression. This comes in line with the findings of Demirtürk and coworkers,
65
 who concluded that depression is associated with poor adherence, and it was attributed to the decreased self‐efficacy of these patients.
65
, 
66
 Another study detected an insignificant relationship between depression and adherence.
67
 Notwithstanding, patients in this study had mild depressive disorder, which might not have negatively impacted their self‐efficacy.

Physicians' perception of patients' adherence to medication is impacted by obesity status; patients with higher BMI are more likely to be perceived as non‐adherent by the treating physician.
68
 However, our analysis detected an insignificant association between obesity and low adherence rates. Further studies are required to explore the impact of such perception on the management of hypertension among obese patients.

It is common knowledge that access to healthcare in the US is mainly dependent on the insurance status of the individual, with stratification in levels of access and services influenced by the generosity of different insurance types and packages.
69
 Our analysis further reinforced this by demonstrating higher adherence rates among insured patients.

Our analysis of interventions that could potentially improve adherence rates demonstrated that fixed‐dose combination (polypill) is associated with higher adherence rates. This supports the findings of multiple previous studies.
70
, 
71
, 
72

Our study has considerable strengths, including the approach to pooling multiple related studies together in this crucial review and meta‐analysis. By extension, the significant amplification in the pooled sample size of our analysis, which exceeded 23 million patients, is another noteworthy strength of our study. Furthermore, we analyzed diverse factors impacting adherence to antihypertensives, including social, demographic, and behavioral characteristics, comorbidities, and interventions.

Despite the strengths described in the preceding paragraph, we concede certain limitations in developing this paper. Of note, we find the lack of characterization of the severity and duration of hypertension across the many studies included in this review and meta‐analysis a limitation of our study. Furthermore, due to the large study sample, our analysis had significant heterogeneity, necessitating future prospective studies with a more homogeneous study population.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Racial disparity, insurance status, and comorbidities influenced the adherence rates among antihypertensive patients. Lower adherence rates were seen among African Americans, uninsured, or younger patients. Accordingly, interventions such as fixed‐dose combinations should be targeted at susceptible groups. On the other hand, obesity and overweight did not affect adherence to antihypertensives.
