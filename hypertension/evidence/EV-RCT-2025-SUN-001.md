## English Abstract

This study aimed to explore the effects of allisartan in mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension. This is a randomized, double‐blind, crossover design involving 98 patients with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension. Participants were randomized and divided into two groups: Group A (baseline—olmesartan—allisartan) and Group B (baseline—allisartan—olmesartan). Each treatment phase included 12 weeks, and participants were administered allisartan (240 mg) or olmesartan (20 mg) once daily. After treatment, both allisartan and olmesartan led to a significant decrease in systolic blood pressure (SBP) levels from baseline (Δ = 6.50 mmHg, p < 0.001 and Δ = 5.64 mmHg, p = 0.002, respectively), with no significant difference between the two drugs. Notably, allisartan led to a significant decrease in diastolic blood pressure (DBP) levels from baseline (Δ = 3.39 mmHg, p = 0.016), while olmesartan did not (Δ = 2.09 mmHg, p = 0.126), allisartan exhibited a more pronounced reduction in DBP compared to olmesartan (Δ = 3.66 mmHg, p = 0.001). Allisartan significantly dropped serum UA levels (Δ = 26.37 µmol/L, p < 0.001), whereas olmesartan did not achieve a significant reduction compared to allisartan (Δ = −7.26 µmol/L, p = 0.991). In terms of cardiac and artery stiffness, allisartan demonstrated significant reductions in left atrial volume index (LAVI; Δ = 2.86 mL/m2, p < 0.001), left ventricular mass index (Δ = 4.82 g/m2, p = 0.010) and ankle‐brachial pulse wave velocity (baPWV) (Δ = 154.49 cm/s, p < 0.001), all‐surpassing olmesartan significantly (all p < 0.01). In Conclusion, allisartan 240 mg and olmesartan 20 mg once daily achieve broadly similar reductions in blood pressure, improving left heart structure and function, mitigating arterial stiffness in individuals with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension. Compared to olmesartan, allisartan demonstrates significant reductions in serum UA.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension is the most prevalent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, contributing to chronic target damage, such as the heart, blood vessels, and kidneys, and exacerbating the overall disease burden [1, 2, 3]. By counteracting harmful effects resulting from AngII binding to Ang II type 1(AT1) receptors, Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs) not only amplify the beneficial effects of AngII binding to AT2 receptors but also convert AngII to Ang 1–7, conferring a cardiovascular protective effect. Consequently, beyond its antihypertensive efficacy, ARBs demonstrate cardiovascular and renal protective effects while enhancing glucose metabolism [4].

Notably, hypertensive patients are frequently combined with abnormal uric acid (UA) metabolism. In contrast, higher UA levels are significantly associated with heightened risks of cardiovascular or all‐cause mortality, coronary artery disease, and major adverse cardiovascular events [5]. Studies reveal that losartan, an ARB antihypertensive drug, effectively lowers UA levels in hypertensive patients, making it a priority for use in patients with hypertension and abnormal UA metabolism [6, 7]. Unlike losartan, which undergoes liver metabolism to generate various metabolites, allisartan, an AT1 receptor blocker, is an EXP3174 esterified precursor drug designed in China, and clinical trials indicated it also decreases blood UA levels in hyperuricemia patients [8]. An alisartan‐based antihypertensive regimen can effectively control blood pressure in patients with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension. A study showed the sitting blood pressure (SBP/DBP) decreased by 19.12 ± 11.71/10.84 ± 8.73 mmHg in patients with 12 weeks of allisartan isoproxil monotherapy (both p < 0.0001). 48 patients with monotherapy‐controlled BP underwent ambulatory BP monitoring, with a mean decrease in ambulatory BP of 10.04 ± 10.87/5.50 ± 8.07 mm Hg after 12 weeks of treatment, and consistent reductions between day and night [9]. and exerts a favorable effect on improving endothelial function and vascular damage in patients with mild EH [10]. A 6‐month study in patients with essential hypertension found that after taking allisartan, the left ventricular remodeling, 24‐h levels of urinary microalbumin, endothelial dysfunction, and arterial stiffness were all significantly improved compared with those of the baseline and the nifedipine group (all p < 0.05) [11]. Notably, it undergoes gastrointestinal esterase metabolism without involving the cytochrome. P450 subfamily, producing the active metabolite EXP‐3174. This distinctive metabolic process reduces the occurrence of drug interactions. Animal trials demonstrate that allisartan not only reduces blood pressure but also improves left ventricular remodeling while safeguarding the kidneys and cerebral blood vessels [12, 13]. No other studies have confirmed the benefit of allisartan in patients with hypertension. Thus, our study aims to explore the efficacy of allisartan and olmesartan on UA, systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP), left cardiac system structure and function, and vascular stiffness in patients with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension.

## 方法 / Methods

This study is a single‐center, randomized double‐blind, crossover‐design trial, conducted by trained professionals who, after obtaining consent from the subjects and blood samples. Ninety‐eight participants with essential hypertension were enrolled in the study, encompassing distinct phases: a screening phase, a run‐in phase, treatment phase 1, a washout phase, and treatment phase 2. (Figure 1). During the screening period, patients underwent assessments to ensure compliance with inclusion and exclusion criteria. Then, patients who meet the inclusion criteria enter the run‐in phase for 2 weeks and stop taking previous long‐term ARB drugs. Data on age, gender, baseline blood pressure, blood biochemical indexes, cardiac ultrasound indexes and ankle‐brachial pulse wave velocity (baPWV) measurements were collected, which are baseline indices. Subsequently, patients entered the two treatment phases (each treatment phase lasts for 12 weeks), where they were randomized (1:1) to receive either olmesartan 20 mg (Zhengda Tianqing)‐ allisartan 240 mg (Shenzhen Xinlitai Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd.) (Group A) or allisartan 240 mg (olmesartan 20 mg (Group B). The washout period is 1–2 weeks, and the median time is about 10 days (Table 1). Information on olmesartan and allisartan show in Table S1. Blood pressure, blood biochemistry, cardiac ultrasound and baPWV measurements were recorded after each drug administration phase. Patients took blood pressure measurements weekly by themselves during the 12‐week treatment period. The patient presented to the hospital for the last blood pressure measurement on the last day of week 12.

In this study, Allisartan is a complementary medication by SalubrisBio, Inc. (Shenzhen, China). The product is consistent with olmesartan (Zhengda Tianqing) in color, shape, and appearance for our clinical trial, which our Ethics Committee approved. Patients were assigned numbers based on the sequence of their consent, and only the allocation number was known at the investigator and patient level, ensuring a double‐blind approach. The drugs administered post‐patient randomization were provided by a separate team exclusively responsible for preparing and packaging the drugs. This team alone knew the randomization group and patient allocation to the medication. The research was conducted by the Helsinki Declaration guidelines and was approved by the institutional review board of the First Affiliated Hospital of Dalian Medical University (PJ‐KS‐KY‐2019‐159(X)).

Individuals aged 18–80 years with hypertension were recruited from the hypertension clinic of the First Affiliated Hospital of Dalian Medical University. The diagnosis of mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension was based on ambulatory blood pressure readings by the Chinese Guidelines for Management of Hypertension [14]. Grade 1 hypertension (mild) was defined as an SBP of 140–159 mmHg and/or DBP of 90–99 mmHg, while grade 2 hypertension (moderate) was characterized by an SBP of 160–179 mmHg, a DBP of 100–109 mmHg, or both. The exclusion criteria are as follows: (a) diagnosis of secondary hypertension, such as renal parenchymal or renal vascular hypertension; cystic kidney disease; primary aldosteronism; Cushing's syndrome; pheochromocytoma; sleep apnea syndrome; (b) diseases of the immune system; (c) drug‐induced hypertension; (d) severe hepatic and renal impairment; (e) a history of acute coronary events; (f) stroke, heart failure; (g)pulmonary embolism and malignancy within the last 3 months;(h) a history of angioedema or other contraindications to angiotensin II receptor blocker application and pregnancy.

Demographic variables, including age, gender, height, weight, body mass index (BMI) calculated as weight/height [2] (kg/m2), and body surface area (BSA), were computed using the formula:

BSA=0.0061height+0.0124weight−0.0099

Additionally, baseline medications, such as concomitant use of calcium channel antagonists (CCB), diuretics, beta‐blockers, and statins, were documented.

Blood pressure was assessed in the clinic using a self‐calibrating automated blood pressure monitor (Omron HEM‐8102A, Kyoto, Japan). Three consecutive blood pressure readings were recorded, with a 5‐min interval between measurements on the right arm. This procedure was completed after a minimum of 15 min of rest in a relaxed sitting position to mitigate potential confounding factors. Medication was then administered in each treatment period and following the two‐phase crossover treatment. All blood pressure measurements were conducted in the presence of either a nurse or a designated research staff member; they then sent the values to the research center.

Fasting venous blood was obtained from patients, and the following parameters were measured: blood urea nitrogen (Urea), blood creatinine (Cre), blood UA, total cholesterol (TC), total triglycerides (TG), high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL‐c), and low‐density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL‐c).

The GE Vivid fully digital color Doppler ultrasound electrocardiogram was employed to measure cardiac color ultrasound in the enrolled patients. Key indices collected included left atrial diameter (LAD), upper and lower LADs, anterior and posterior LADs, left ventricular end‐diastolic dimension (LVDd), interventricular septal thickness (IVST), posterior wall thickness (PWT), E/A ratio, and left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF). The left atrial volume index (LAVI), calculated as left atrial volume (LAV) divided by BSA, followed the following formula [15]:

LAV=π/6×anterior−posteriorleftatrialdiameter×superior−inferiorleftatrialdiameter×internalleftatrialdiameter

Similarly, left ventricular weight index (LVMI), determined as left ventricular weight (LVM) divided by BSA, adhered to the following formula:

LVMg=0.8×1.04×IVST+PWT+LVDd3−LVDd3]+0.6

The measurements of LVDd, IVST, and PWT were conducted continuously for three beats in centimeters to obtain the mean value, which was then calculated using Deiereux's formula [16] for correcting ventricular weights (LVMs).

The baPWV was measured using the noninvasive vascular function testing instrument BP‐203RPEIII from Omron Medical Devices. The patient assumed a supine position. Blood pressure cuffs were secured to the patient's brachial arteries of the upper limbs and the ankle arteries of the lower limbs while the patient kept their hands positioned on either side of their body. After entering general information, such as gender, age, and BMI, the measurement commenced, and the detector's processing program automatically generated the baPWV. These measurements were conducted both before baseline treatment and after completing two cross‐treatment phases, respectively.

The target sample size was calculated based on the primary efficacy variable, the change from baseline in UA, considering a standard deviation of 36 umol/L [8]. The sample size was determined to provide 90% power to detect statistical significance under the alternative hypothesis, assuming a mean arterial pressure reduction of 36 umol/L, at a two‐sided significance level of 0.05. Since this study used a crossover trial design, where each patient receives two treatments, allocating 70 patients per group was deemed necessary. Factoring in an anticipated dropout rate of approximately 20%, 90 hypertensive patients needed to be enrolled.

All data were analyzed using IBM SPSS software version 24.0. Continuous variables were presented as mean ± standard deviation or median (interquartile spacing). Normally distributed data were expressed as mean ± standard deviation, while non‐normally distributed data were presented as the median (interquartile range). Categorical variables were reported as N (%). The t‐test assessed the mean difference between groups for normally distributed data, and the Mann–Whitney U test was chosen for non‐normally distributed data. The difference before and after treatment or the difference between two drugs for the same index is represented by Δ. p < 0.05 was considered statistically different.

## 结果 / Results

Out of the 112 patients initially in the screening period, 105 met the diagnostic criteria for primary mild‐to‐moderate hypertension. Among them, 102 entered the double‐blind crossover treatment period, exclusion because some patients could not be stopped taking other ARBs in the run‐in phase, or some individual reasons, with 98 completing the study. Seven individuals discontinued medication or were lost to follow‐up for personal reasons before and after the crossover period (Figure 2). Table 1 provides an overview of the baseline characteristics. The mean age of patients with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension was 70.04 ± 5.06 years, with more females (63.3%). Baseline BMI was 26.02 ± 3.25 kg/m2, and baseline blood pressure values were 150.98 ± 17.9/89.02 ± 12.27 mmHg (SBP/DBP). Further baseline information, including blood biochemical indices, medications, cardiac ultrasound, and baPWV, is summarized in Tables 1 and 2. Some subjects were treated with CCB, and some with multiple others, such as hydrochlorothiazide, statins, and β receptor blockers, which may affect UA. These concurrent antihypertensives continued unchanged during the study, there were no discontinued before (washout phase) or during the study. They were not on any other medications. There were no significant differences in baseline indices between the two crossover treatment groups (all p > 0.05).

## 讨论 / Discussion

Our study proved that blood pressure was statistically decreased in patients with allisartan 240 mg and olmesartan 20 mg once daily, achieving broadly similar reductions in blood pressure. Regarding DBP, the initial difference between the two medications was observed to be about 2 mmHg (olmesartan vs allisartan is 89.02 vs 87.20 mmHg) at baseline, and this difference persisted at nearly 2 mmHg (86.03 vs 83.54 mmHg) post‐treatment. Consequently, while Table 3 suggests that allisartan exhibits a greater efficacy in reducing DBP, this observation could be attributed to the aforementioned factors. In a separate study comparing allisartan with a placebo in essential hypertension, patients experienced a mean reduction in SBP/DBP of 14.5/10.4 mmHg after 8 weeks of allisartan treatment [17]. In assessing the antihypertensive efficacy of the ARB class of drugs [18], olmesartan had greater antihypertensive efficacy compared to other commonly used ARBs (losartan, valsartan, irbesartan, and candesartan). Our results showed that allisartan had a similar ability to reduce blood pressure as olmesartan in patients with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension.

In this study, allisartan demonstrated a specific UA‐lowering effect, reducing serum UA by 26.37umol/L (95% CI 12.96–39.77, p < 0.001), while olmesartan showed no significant difference. UA can activate the renin‐angiotensin system (RAS), generate reactive oxygen species, and induce vascular endothelial dysfunction [19]. Common urate transporter proteins include URAT1, GLUT9, and OAT4, with URAT1 and GLUT9 being key targets for treating hyperuricemia. Losartan and its metabolite EXP‐3174 inhibit UA uptake by directly blocking URAT1 and reducing blood UA level [20, 21, 22, 23, 24]. However, losartan has been reported to have some hepatotoxicity [25, 26]. Allisartan, a precursor of losartan, generates EXP‐3174 via hydrolysis without liver enzyme metabolism, resulting in higher bioavailability and faster therapeutic response compared to losartan. Our study shows that allisartan effectively lowers UA levels, outperforming olmesartan in this regard.

Our results also revealed that allisartan significantly improved LAD, LVDd, LAVI and LVMI compared to olmesartan, suggesting a notable impact on left heart structure and function. Hypertension is often associated with cardiac structure and function damage, including left atrial enlargement, left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) and diastolic insufficiency [27]. Recommendations from the American Society of Echocardiography and the European Society of Cardiovascular Imaging for the Echocardiographic Assessment of Left Ventricular Diastolic Function show that LAVI was used to analyze the left atrial volume and can be used to estimate the cumulative effect of increasing left ventricular filling pressure over time, mainly reflecting the chronic effects of increased left ventricular filling pressure [28]. An animal study showed that allisartan reduced blood pressure and improved the pressure reflex sensitivity in spontaneously hypertensive rats and allisartan reversed LVH, prevented renal cortical atrophy, reduced aortic thickening and had a good organ‐protective effect in spontaneously hypertensive rats [29]. A study showed that LVDd, IVST, LVPWT and LVMI were significantly reduced in patients taking allisartan compared to baseline and nifedipine groups [11]. It is suggested that allisartan may improve the cardiac structure, reverse ventricular remodeling, improve LV diastolic function and delay disease progression in hypertensive patients. Our study aligns with previous research, showing allisartan's potential in improving cardiac structure, reversing ventricular remodeling and enhancing LV diastolic function.

We employed bilateral baPWV to assess arterial stiffness. Allisartan exhibited a significant decrease in baPWV, emphasizing its potential to improve vascular elasticity compared to olmesartan. BaPWV is a noninvasive and convenient way to measure arterial stiffness. Artery stiffness independently predicts cardiovascular risk and is a high‐priority therapeutic target for improving the global burden of cardiovascular disease [30]. A study showed that in the atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease population, the risk of cardiovascular events increased 1.41‐fold (1.24–1.60) for every 1SD increase in baPWV [31]. In a Japanese meta‐analysis based on a population without a history of cardiovascular disease [32], baPWV was identified as an independent predictor of the risk of developing cardiovascular disease, which could improve the validity of the Framingham Risk Score based on traditional cardiovascular risk factors. A study included 72 patients with mild hypertension who, after 30 days of allisartan treatment, experienced a decrease in baPWV of 102.8 ± 84.2 cm/s, comparable to healthy controls [10]. This finding is consistent with our findings. It suggests that allisartan may improve vascular stiffness in hypertensive patients. Animal experiments showed that the median thickness and area/diameter ratio of the arterial wall were significantly lower. The degree of mesenteric artery diastole increased in the allisartan group compared to the untreated hypertensive rats [33]. The mechanism showed that allisartan reduces blood pressure and maintains vascular remodeling through voltage‐gated potassium channels.

Our study also found both allisartan and olmesartan effectively reduced TC levels, but no significant difference between them. The cause may be related to the patient's attention to a low‐fat diet after enrolment and the administration of statins in some patients.

Some limitations are as follows. First, our study was a small sample from a single center and lacked a large‐sample and multi‐center clinical trial, there was a lack of safety evaluation of allisartan. Second, all participants were exclusively Asian patients, which precludes the generalizability of the pharmacodynamic results to other ethnic groups. The third is the weight of the two drugs. Although the products were similar in appearance, there was indeed a difference in weight. The study was randomized, and each patient was randomly assigned to receive the medications. Therefore, it was impossible to distinguish the medication from its appearance, even if the patient weighed the pill. Finally, our conclusions depend on the current dose of the two drugs; the effects of other doses require further study.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Allisartan 240 mg and olmesartan 20 mg once daily achieve broadly similar reductions in blood pressure, improving left heart structure and function, mitigating arterial stiffness in individuals with mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension. Compared to olmesartan, allisartan demonstrates significant reductions in serum UA. Our study contributes novel evidence supporting the therapeutic potential of allisartan in addressing mild‐to‐moderate essential hypertension and concurrently lowering serum UA levels within this patient population.
