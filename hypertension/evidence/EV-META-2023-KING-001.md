## English Abstract

Single-pill combination (SPC) antihypertensive products improve blood pressure (BP) control and medication adherence among patients with hypertension. It is unknown to what degree commercially available SPC products could be used to target an intensive systolic BP goal of <120 mm Hg.

This cross-sectional analysis included participants randomized to the intensive treatment arm (goal systolic BP <120 mm Hg) of the Systolic Blood Pressure Intervention Trial (SPRINT) using ≥2 antihypertensive medication classes at the 12-month post-randomization visit. Antihypertensive medication data were collected using pill bottle review by research coordinators, and regimens were categorized by the unique combinations of antihypertensive classes. We calculated the proportion of regimens used which are commercially available as one of the 7 SPC class combinations in the US as of January 2023.

Among the 3,833 SPRINT intensive arm participants included (median age 67.0 years, 35.5% female), participants were using 219 unique antihypertensive regimens. The 7 regimens for which there are class-equivalent SPC products were used by 40.3% of participants. Only 3.2% of all medication class regimens used are available as a class-equivalent SPC product (7/219). There are no SPC products available with four or more medication classes, which were used by 1,060 participants (27.7%).

Most SPRINT participants in the intensive arm used an antihypertensive medication regimen which is not commercially available as a class equivalent SPC product. To achieve the SPRINT results in real-world settings, maximize the potential benefit of SPCs, and reduce pill burden, improvements in the product landscape are needed.

## 背景 / Background

Single-pill combinations (SPCs; i.e., combining ≥2 medications in 1 tablet), also known as fixed-dose combinations, are commonly used to treat elevated blood pressure (BP) because at least 75% of patients with hypertension require multiple medications to reach goal BP levels.1–4 Compared to prescribing medications in separate pills, SPCs improve medication adherence and BP control rates, translating to fewer CVD events.5–11 Additionally, initiating hypertension treatment with dual-therapy SPC is a cost-effective strategy compared to initiating treatment with monotherapy or dual-therapy separate pill strategies.12 Yet, regardless of these benefits, prescribing SPCs is limited by the commercial availability of the specific medications and doses of SPCs that patients need.

The Systolic Blood Pressure Intervention Trial (SPRINT) showed that in adults at high risk for cardiovascular disease (CVD), targeting a systolic BP of <120 mm Hg (intensive treatment) resulted in a 25–27% reduction in major cardiovascular events and death compared to a systolic BP target of <140 mm Hg (standard treatment).1,13 Intensive treatment required, on average, 2.8 antihypertensive medication classes, one more than standard treatment. 2,13 For intensive group participants, the SPRINT investigators favored regimens with angiotensin converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs) or angiotensin-II receptor blockers (ARBs), thiazide diuretics, and calcium channel blockers (CCBs), used at an average of 2/3rds the maximum recommended doses.14 Still, 255 unique antihypertensive medication class combinations were used by SPRINT participants, and no combination was used by more than 15% of participants.14 We previously estimated that 20.7 million US adults with hypertension taking two or more medications are using a regimen which is not commercially available as a single class-equivalent SPC product;15 however, the degree to which the regimens used in the intensive treatment arm of SPRINT are available as SPC products is unknown. We describe the degree to which currently available SPC products match the antihypertensive medication class regimens used by SPRINT investigators to target intensive systolic BP goals.

## 方法 / Methods

The rationale, design, and main results of SPRINT have been published. 1,2,13,16 Data are publicly available to researchers with an institutional review board-approved project through the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute data repository (http://www.biolincc.nhlbi.nih.gov/home). Briefly, SPRINT was a multicenter, randomized trial testing two systolic BP targets among patients ≥50 years of age with hypertension at high CVD risk (history of clinical or subclinical CVD other than stroke, estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] of 20–59 mL/min/1.73 m2 using the Modification of Diet in Renal Disease equation,17 predicted 10-year CVD risk ≥15% calculated using the Framingham Risk Score,18 and/or age ≥75 years). Individuals with diabetes, previous stroke, heart failure, proteinuria ≥1 g/day, or an eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73 m2 were excluded. Participants were eligible for inclusion based on the number of antihypertensive medications being taken (zero to <5) and their systolic BP level at the pre-randomization visit (130 to 180 mm Hg). The study protocol was approved by each site’s institutional review board, and every participant provided written informed consent.

The SPRINT treatment protocol (see Supplemental Protocol)19 encouraged the use of antihypertensive medications with strong CVD outcome data from large randomized controlled trials, and investigators were given treatment algorithms adapted from BP guideline recommendations.20,21 Thiazide diuretics (preferably chlorthalidone), CCBs (preferably amlodipine), ACEIs, and ARBs were preferred. Investigators had the discretion to select medication class, dose, frequency, and combinations for each participant. There was a standard formulary with donation of two medications by manufacturers (Takeda; azilsartan and azilsartan/chlorthalidone) to provide medications free of charge to the participant; this influenced the generic medication used within antihypertensive classes, but not the antihypertensive class itself. Detailed treatment algorithms are available in Cushman et al.2

The current analysis assessed medication regimens used by the intensive group participants at the 12-month visit, by which point most medication changes had occurred.14 We included intensive group participants who were alive, attended the 12-month visit, had complete medication data at the 12-month visit, and who were taking two or more antihypertensive medications (N = 3,833, 90.2% of intensive arm participants with complete 12-month visit data; Figure 1).

Each participants’ antihypertensive medication was categorized into its respective class: renin-angiotensin-system (RAS) acting agents (ACEI, ARB, or direct renin inhibitor), CCBs, thiazide diuretics, beta-blockers, loop diuretics, potassium-sparing, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs; spironolactone or eplerenone), alpha-1 antagonists, centrally acting agents, and vasodilators. Because ACEIs and ARBs are recommended interchangeably by hypertension treatment guidelines, we combined ACEIs and ARBs into a single RAS inhibitor group. We also included direct renin inhibitors (DRIs) in the RAS inhibitor category, although use of these agents in SPRINT was rare (<10 participants at the 12-month visit). Each combination of medication classes was assessed as a unique regimen. If a participant was taking an SPC, each medication component was assessed separately. We restricted our analysis to those regimens used at the 12-month visit to allow sufficient time of the intensive systolic BP treatment goal to be targeted, and because most antihypertensive regimen changes had occurred by the 12-month visit. Details regarding how antihypertensive medication data were collected and analyzed have been previously described.14

The primary outcome was whether a participant’s entire antihypertensive medication regimen was available as a SPC product in the US according to the Food and Drug Administration’s Drugs@FDA Database and Orange Book Database as of January 2023 (Table 1). The outcome was evaluated at the class combination level. The 7 SPC regimens are: 1) RAS inhibitor/thiazide diuretic; 2) RAS inhibitor/CCB; 3) RAS inhibitor/beta-blocker; 4) Beta-blocker/thiazide diuretic; 5) Potassium-sparing diuretic/thiazide diuretic; 6) MRA/thiazide diuretic; and 7) RAS inhibitor/CCB/thiazide diuretic. For example, a participant taking losartan (an ARB) and chlorthalidone (a thiazide), and a participant taking lisinopril (an ACEI) and hydrochlorothiazide (a thiazide) would both be classified as taking the regimen of RAS inhibitor + thiazide, a class combination which is available as an SPC product.

Baseline characteristics were calculated and reported using summary statistics (i.e., means [standard deviation], medians [interquartile range], counts [percent]) overall and by number of antihypertensive medication classes in the participant’s regimen. The number and proportion of participants taking each medication regimen were calculated. The number and proportion of unique regimens used which are available as one class equivalent SPC product were calculated. Analyses were repeated among a subgroup of participants who achieved their systolic BP goal (<120 mm Hg). All analyses were performed using SAS v.9.4 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC) and Microsoft Excel, version 16.68.

## 结果 / Results

Overall, there were 4,349 participants randomized to the intensive treatment group who were alive and attended the 12-month visit. We excluded 98 participants who were missing antihypertensive medication data and 418 participants who were using less than two antihypertensive medication classes (Figure 1). Among the 3,833 remaining participants who were included in this analysis, at the pre-randomization visit, the median age was 67.0 years (25th-75th percentiles 61.0, 75.0), 35.5% were female, and the median systolic BP was 139.0 (25th-75th percentiles 130.0, 150.0) (Table 2). At the 12-month visit, the proportions using two, three, and four or more antihypertensive classes were 33.7%, 38.6%, and 27.7%, respectively. Participants who had a higher median age, body mass index (BMI), and systolic BP, or who had a lower median eGFR were more likely to use more antihypertensive medication classes; as were those who had a high school level of education or less, were retired, had Medicare or Medicaid insurance, or were current smokers.

Overall, there were 219 unique class combinations used (Table S1). The 7 class combinations for which there are available SPC products were used by 40.3% of the participants. Of the 219 medication class regimens used, only 7 are available as a class-equivalent SPC (3.2%). The most commonly used regimens available as an SPC product were 1) RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic (14.1%); 2) RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic + CCB (14.0%); and 3) RAS inhibitor + CCB (6.2%) (Table 3, Figure 2). The most used regimens which do not have a corresponding SPC product were 1) RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic + CCB + beta-blocker (8.3%); 2) RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic + beta-blocker (7.3%); and 3) RAS inhibitor + beta-blocker + CCB (4.5%). The proportion of participants using a regimen which is available as a single class-equivalent SPC product was 78.0% among participants taking 2 medication classes, 36.3% among participants taking 3 medication classes, and 0.0% among participants taking ≥4 medication classes.

There were 2,103 intensive arm participants taking two or more antihypertensive medication classes who achieved their assigned systolic BP target <120 mm Hg at 12 months (54.9%) who were using 153 unique antihypertensive medication class combinations (Table S2). No participants in this subgroup were using an MRA + thiazide diuretic, for which there is a class-equivalent SPC available. The remaining 6 class combinations for which there are class-equivalent SPC products available were used by 44.1% of this subgroup, including the three most common regimens: 1) RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic (16.8%); 2) RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic + CCB (14.5%); and 3) RAS inhibitor + CCB (6.6%). Commonly used regimens which are not entirely available as a class-equivalent SPC include: 1) RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic + beta-blocker (8.3%); 2) RAS inhibitor + CCB + beta-blocker (3.9%); and 3) thiazide diuretic + CCB + beta-blocker (3.2%).

## 讨论 / Discussion

In this analysis of SPRINT, participants randomized to intensive systolic BP treatment (goal <120 mm Hg) were using 219 unique antihypertensive class regimens at the 12-month visit, and 40% of intensive arm participants were using a medication regimen for which there is a class-equivalent SPC product. A further 20% of participants were using one of the 3 most common regimens which do not have an available class-equivalent SPC product. The final 40% of participants were using 209 unique regimens, all of which are also not available as a class-equivalent SPC. The largest gap in SPC availability is for those taking four or more antihypertensive medication classes where there are currently no SPC products available. Further, only two triple-therapy SPC products exist, and both are the same class combination of RAS inhibitor + thiazide + CCB. Results were similar among the subgroup of participants who achieved their systolic BP goal at 12 months. These findings imply that, to target and achieve intensive systolic BP goals in real-world clinical practice using the same antihypertensive regimens used by SPRINT participants, SPC products could be used for less than half of SPRINT-eligible patients.

Successful implementation of intensive systolic BP goals in real-world hypertension management requires overcoming several logistical and behavioral challenges. For example, clinical inertia, or the failure to escalate therapy despite BP being above target, is a well-established problem in hypertension management, as is poor patient adherence to medications.22 Using more medications at higher doses, as was required to target intensive systolic BP in SPRINT, is likely to magnify these challenges. However, SPCs offer a therapeutic option that can overcome these barriers to intensive systolic BP goals. SPCs reduce the number of prescriptions, improve patient adherence by reducing pill-burden, and even overcome physiologic barriers to BP lowering, as synergistic medications at low doses are more efficacious and safer than monotherapy at standard doses.5–10 A recent meta-analysis of 44 studies found that compared to free-equivalent combinations (i.e., separate pills for each medication prescribed), patients who are prescribed SPC products have significantly better medication adherence, longer medication persistence, and reduced time to BP control at several systolic/diastolic BP thresholds.11 An important caveat is that the reduction in CV events with SPCs relative to individually-prescribed medications has been observed in retrospective cohorts5,6 but not in clinical trials. A randomized clinical trial comparing these strategies is needed to determine the causal relationship between SPC use relative to individually-prescribed medications on CV outcomes. Currently, SPC products are endorsed by the 2017 American College of Cardiology/American Heart Association BP guidelines to improve medication adherence and reduce time to BP control.22,23 Yet, less than one-third of patients with hypertension are using SPC products.24,25 Applying a similar methodology to the National Health and Nutrition Examination Surveys, we estimated that 20.7 million US adults with hypertension taking two or more medications are using a regimen which is not commercially available as a single class-equivalent SPC product.15 Assuming that 12.3% of the 20.7 million are eligible for the SPRINT trial,26 then over 2.5 million SPRINT-eligible US adults do not have access to a class-equivalent SPC product. An important consideration for antihypertensive pharmacoequity27,28 (i.e., the equitable provision of medication for all) is that, should new SPC products be developed and marketed to US patients, these products would likely be brand-name only and be associated with higher cost burdens to the patient (especially disadvantaged persons) and the health system, limiting their utility until they become generic. Although new SPC options may be beneficial, the utilization of currently-available SPCs could also be substantially improved.

To complicate matters further, SPC products may not be appropriate for all patients or may not be available with specific doses of medications that are needed. Of the 23 SPC products containing a thiazide diuretic, 21 (91%) contain hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), often in a dosage form supplying 12.5 mg per pill, which is thought to be suboptimal to provide adequate CVD risk reduction. There are pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic properties of specific medications within each class which may also hinder use of class equivalent SPC products. For example, the only available SPC containing a beta-blocker and RAS inhibitor is nebivolol/valsartan (VYDUO®; containing a cardioselective beta-blocker with vasodilatory activity), which may not be an appropriate class equivalent switch for use of any beta-blocker, the collection of which crosses a wide range of activity on beta receptors and alpha receptors. Similarly, a patient’s prior side effect history may also influence the appropriateness of switching to a class equivalent SPC product. For example, a patient taking eplerenone and a thiazide diuretic who had a side effect on spironolactone therapy may not be willing to switch to the only class equivalent SPC of spironolactone/HCTZ. While a class equivalent SPC product may exist for a patient’s regimen, it may not be clinically appropriate to use it. Similarly, in order to truly replicate the regimens employed by SPRINT investigators,14 SPC products would be needed that align with the SPRINT protocol. For example, chlorthalidone was the recommended thiazide diuretic of choice (and was used by 88% of SPRINT participants taking a thiazide diuretic2), but the current SPC market in the US only hosts two products with chlorthalidone: azilsartan/chlorthalidone and atenolol/chlorthalidone.

Approximately 18 million US adults meet SPRINT eligibility criteria and could potentially benefit from the regimens described herein being available as an SPC.26 Some have expressed concerns about whether the SPRINT population is generalizable to the broader US population.29–31 Although SPRINT excluded key populations of patients who would potentially benefit from more intensive systolic BP lowering based on predicted CVD risk alone (e.g., patients with diabetes, prior stroke, or heart failure), these populations could still benefit from widespread SPC availability of the most common regimens used in SPRINT. Specifically, there are 5 antihypertensive medication class regimens— one dual-therapy (thiazide diuretic + CCB), three triple-therapy (RAS inhibitor + beta-blocker + CCB, thiazide diuretic + CCB + beta-blocker, RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic + beta-blocker), and one quadruple-therapy (RAS inhibitor + thiazide diuretic + CCB + beta-blocker)—which were each used by at least 2% of the intensive arm population and which are not currently available as a class-equivalent SPC product. Together, these 5 regimens were used by 25.7% of the intensive arm population and represent the largest gap in SPC product availability. A key similarity among most of these regimens is the presence of a beta-blocker, which the 2017 ACC/AHA blood pressure treatment guidelines no longer recommend first-line for blood pressure-lowering without the presence of a compelling indication (e.g., history of myocardial infarction).23 The greater availability of SPCs containing beta-blockers would assist with reducing pill burden and improving adherence in persons with indications for beta-blockers, which are associated with significant morbidity and mortality.

The findings of the current study should be interpreted within the context of the following known strengths and limitations. SPRINT included a robust medication data collection process, including pill-bottle validation and confirmation with electronic medical record lists if available. Nonetheless, medication regimens were constructed based on free-text data collection fields, and although we do not anticipate that the data management included significant errors, they are still possible. We also did not include medication dose in this analysis because class availability is the first barrier to potential SPC use. The medication use patterns observed in the current study reflect SPRINT investigator preferences based on a standard formulary, treatment algorithm, and evidence-based guidelines at the time of the trial (2010–2015). However, since the time from when SPRINT was conducted and the current analysis, there have not been significant changes to guideline recommended first- or second-line antihypertensive medication classes. It is possible that the donation of azilsartan/chlorthalidone product by the manufacturer may have influenced our results; however, because this SPC product was classified as a RAS inhibitor/thiazide diuretic in the current analysis, and there are 13 other such products which are generic and low-cost, we do not expect any meaningful impact on our results due to the donation of this product to study participants. SPRINT investigators were given latitude to prescribe combinations of their choosing for each participant based on a standard formulary, potentially limiting the generic medications that were used. Though, this is similar to real-world clinical care where providers are often limited by a patient’s insurance or health system formulary. We did not report the SPC products used by SPRINT participants, as fewer than 5% of participants in each treatment group were taking an SPC product. The current study’s estimates use commercial product availability as of June 2022, and results may change in the future if more SPC products become available. Finally, our results apply only to SPRINT-eligible patients who need ≥2 antihypertensive medication classes to target intensive systolic BP goals; based on our prior work,14 approximately 11.5% of SPRINT-eligible patients may be able to use <2 antihypertensive medication classes to target intensive systolic BP goals.

Although this study demonstrated that only 3.2% of the medication regimens used in SPRINT intensive arm participants are available as SPC products, the proportion of intensive arm participants using an entirely SPC-available regimen was higher at 40.3%. SPC products combine medications with synergistic mechanisms of action while fulfilling guideline-directed treatment. Interventions are needed to increase the use of patients taking multidrug antihypertensive regimens, which could be achieved by increasing the proportion of patients taking SPC products.
