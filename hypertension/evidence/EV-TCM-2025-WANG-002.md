## English Abstract

The management of cardiovascular (CV) diseases is considerably hindered by the presence of anxiety disorders, which act as a significant barrier to medication adherence. This systematic review and meta-analysis aim to synthesize existing evidence on the relationship between anxiety and medication adherence in individuals with CV diseases.

A systematic search was conducted across five major databases from inception to 2025. The methodological quality of the included studies was assessed using the National Institutes of Health (NIH) quality assessment tool. A random-effects meta-analysis was conducted utilizing Comprehensive Meta-Analysis software (version 3), with with p < 0.05 indicating significance.

A total of 23 studies were selected, including 18 with cross-sectional and five with a longitudinal study design. The combined sample size was 72,815 participants, with an average age of 60.7 years and 51.7% were male participants. Majority of studies (n = 15) reported a negative association between anxiety and cardiovascular (CV) medication adherence. Meta-analysis (n = 20) further supported this overall negative association (SMD = -1.087, 95% CI [-1.469, -0.705], p < 0.001) which was also evident across clinical subgroups: hypertension (SMD = -0.648, 95% CI [-1.293, -0.004], p = 0.049), congestive heart failure (SMD = -0.51, 95% CI [-0.92, -0.10], p = 0.02), and ischemic heart disease (SMD = -2.322, 95% CI [-3.498, -1.146], p < 0.001).

This review primarily contributes to the understanding of negative association between anxiety and medication adherence in the context of CV diseases. However, the predominance of cross-sectional designs, and substantial heterogeneity necessitate cautious interpretation of these findings.

## 背景 / Background

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), cardiovascular (CV) diseases are the leading cause of death worldwide [58]. In 2021 alone, CV diseases contributed to an estimated 20.5 million deaths, accounting for approximately one-third of the total global mortality [40]. Due to their widespread prevalence, CV diseases place a substantial burden on society, leading to disability, diminished functioning, and increased healthcare costs, particularly among the elderly population [4]. In the European Union (EU), CV disease incurs an estimated annual economic cost of €282 billion, translating to approximately €630 per capita [41]. Likewise, in low- and middle-income countries, the financial impact of CV diseases between 2011 and 2015 was projected at $3.7 trillion [5].

In the management of CV disorders, pharmacological treatment is a central component. Evidence has consistently demonstrated its effectiveness in reducing mortality and extending life expectancy across a range of CV conditions [9, 33]. However, the effectiveness of CV medications largely depends on patients’ adherence to prescribed treatment regimens [9]. Studies have confirmed that poor medication adherence is associated with increased mortality rates among individuals with CV diseases [30, 42, 46]. A recent comprehensive analysis of real-world evidence by Chen et al., which included 18 studies, identified a consistent and significant inverse linear relationship between adherence to CV medications and the occurrence of CV events or mortality. The exposure–effect analysis revealed that a 20% improvement in medication adherence was associated with an 8% reduction in the risk of CV events and a 12% reduction in the risk of mortality [9]. Similarly, suboptimal adherence to CV medications has also been associated with increased healthcare costs [59].

Various factors contribute to medication non-adherence that can be divided into multiples groups [31, 38]. Among these factors, psychological well-being has also been identified as a significant contributor to medication non-adherence [32]. Mental disorders constitute 14% of the global disease burden and contribute to 30% of the burden from non-fatal conditions. This burden is further exacerbated by the prevalence of medication non-adherence among individuals affected by these disorders [51]. Among psychological disorders, anxiety disorders are particularly prevalent and are often associated with significant functional impairment [54]. The presence of anxiety has been associated with increased morbidity and mortality rates, with poor medication adherence identified as a contributing factor to these adverse outcomes [15].

Anxiety disorders are commonly observed in patients with CV disease and can have a significant impact on cardiac health [7, 11]. These disorders have been linked to both the onset and progression of CV conditions, and in many cases, are associated with adverse CV outcomes, including increased mortality [7, 11]. Given that anxiety can impair cognitive focus, reduce energy levels, and diminish motivation, they may negatively affect a patient's ability and willingness to adhere to prescribed treatment regimens [15]. For these reasons i.e., the potential association between anxiety and medication non-adherence, combined with the frequent co-occurrence of CV and anxiety disorders, there is a plausible risk of increased rates of non-adherence to CV medications among individuals experiencing both conditions.

While the existing literature has examined the relationship between psychological factors and cardiovascular medication adherence, the focus has primarily been on depression. For instance, Poletti et al. reviewed thirty-one studies examining depression as a barrier to medication adherence in patients with cardiovascular diseases. However, their review was restricted to individuals with chronic heart failure (CHF) and exclusively addressed depression. Additionally, no meta-analysis was conducted, limiting the ability to quantify the overall effect size. As a result, a comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis examining the relationship between anxiety disorders and CV medication adherence remains an important gap in the literature. Therefore, the objective of this systematic review and meta-analysis is to evaluate the association between anxiety disorders, identified through either clinical diagnosis or validated questionnaire-based assessments, and medication adherence in adults with cardiovascular disease. Additionally, the study aims to investigate potential variations in this association across subgroups defined by type of CV disease, the tools used to assess anxiety and medication adherence, and geographic regions.

## 方法 / Methods

The protocol of this systematic review and meta-analysis was not registered in a prospective registry, however, the protocol was predefined prior to the initiation of the study.

In accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA) guidelines [44], a systematic and comprehensive search strategy was employed across five databases, including PubMed, Cochrane Library, ScienceDirect, Google Scholar, and the British Medical Journals covering the period from inception to March 2025. Additionally, articles were also retrieved by examining the reference lists of selected studies, as well as related systematic reviews and meta-analysis.

To ensure precision and relevance, specific key terms were developed and used as search criteria in the databases. The search terms comprised the following: adherence OR compliance OR non-adherence OR non-compliance AND anxiety OR anxiety disorders OR generalized anxiety disorder OR GAD OR panic disorder OR social anxiety disorder OR specific phobias AND cardiovascular diseases OR coronary artery disease OR CAD OR myocardial infarction OR heart attack OR stroke OR hypertension OR atherosclerosis OR heart failure OR arrhythmia OR peripheral artery disease OR PAD AND cohort OR cohort study. To further refine the search and obtain more accurate results, multiple keywords were combined using Boolean operators such as AND/OR. The search strategy employed for the PubMed database is shown in supplementary data 01.

To improve the clarity of the study selection process, the inclusion criteria were structured according to the Population, Intervention, Comparator, Outcome, and Study Design (PICOS) framework. The population included adults aged 18 years and older diagnosed with anxiety disorders. We considered anxiety to be present if indicated by standardized questionnaire scores or confirmed through clinical diagnosis. The intervention focused on the presence of anxiety and its potential role to medication adherence in individuals with CV disease. While a specific comparator group was not mandatory, studies that included comparisons between individuals with and without anxiety were considered. The outcome of interest was quantitative data related to medication adherence. Finally, the study design was limited to observational studies published in English language only.

Studies were excluded if they did not clearly define adherence or failed to specify how adherence and/or anxiety were measured. In addition, review articles, methodological or protocol papers, conference proceedings, case reports, editorials, letters, and other types of correspondence were not included.

Two reviewers independently screened all retrieved titles and abstracts using the predefined search strategies. Subsequently, the same reviewers conducted full-text screening of abstracts that met the inclusion criteria, and final articles were selected for further evaluation. Any disagreements or discrepancies between the two reviewers were resolved through consensus.

The quality assessment of the included studies was carried out using the National Institutes of Health (NIH) quality assessment tool designed for observational cohort and cross-sectional studies. This tool provided a structured and transparent approach to assessing the internal validity of the included studies. It consists of 14 specific criteria aimed at evaluating methodological rigor and risk of bias. Each criterion was rated as "yes," "no," or "cannot determine/not reported/not applicable." Two independent reviewers conducted the assessments for each study in accordance with these criteria, with any discrepancies resolved through consensus [43].

To collect data relevant to the objectives of this review, a structured data extraction form was developed. Two authors independently extracted information from the included studies and subsequently cross-checked their results to ensure accuracy and consistency. The data extraction form captured key elements such as study design, characteristics of the study population, general study details, information related to anxiety including the measurement tools used, the specific CV medication examined, method used to evaluate medication adherence, primary findings, and the authors' conclusions.

Meta-analyses were performed using Comprehensive Meta-Analysis (CMA) software version 3. The standardized mean difference (SMD) was selected as the primary effect size to standardize outcome metrics across studies. For studies where higher scores represented greater adherence, effect sizes were directionally aligned to ensure that negative SMD values consistently indicated lower adherence associated with higher anxiety levels.

A random-effects model was applied using the DerSimonian–Laird method [14] to account for anticipated heterogeneity across study populations and methodologies. Subgroup analyses were conducted based on pre-specified variables, including CV disease type, the tools used to assess both anxiety and medication adherence, and geographic regions.

To evaluate the robustness of the findings, a leave-one-out sensitivity analysis was conducted. Studies presenting extreme effect sizes [2, 19] were excluded to reduce bias in pooled estimates. Heterogeneity was quantified using the Higgins I2 statistic, with values of ≤ 25% interpreted as low, 25–50% as moderate, and ≥ 50% as high heterogeneity [29]. Publication bias was assessed through visual inspection of funnel plots and using Egger’s regression, applied to analyses including ten or more studies. Statistical significance was defined as a p-value less than 0.05.

## 结果 / Results

The initial search across five major databases resulted in 2,485 research articles. After eliminating 578 duplicate entries identified across the databases, a total of 1,907 records were retained for further screening. A screening of titles and abstracts led to the selection of 108 articles for full-text review. Of these, 87 were excluded for various reasons: 22 were excluded due to inappropriate study design, 19 focused on psychiatric disorders other than anxiety, 13 examined non-cardiovascular medications, and 12 lacked a clear definition of medication adherence. An additional 11 studies focused on adherence unrelated to medication, seven were not published in English, two involved paediatric populations, and one was an intervention-based study. Ultimately, 23 studies met all eligibility criteria and were included in the final analysis (Fig. 1). The included studies revealed three overarching patterns concerning the association between anxiety disorders and adherence to CV medications.

Majority of studies (n = 15), comprising 11 cross-sectional (n = 9,103) [1, 2, 8, 13, 17–19, 25, 28, 35, 56] and four longitudinal investigations (n = 3,283) [3, 20, 23, 36], reported a negative relationship, whereby elevated anxiety levels were associated with reduced medication adherence. While cross-sectional studies identified this relationship at a single time point, the longitudinal studies offered more robust evidence, demonstrating that both baseline anxiety and increases in anxiety symptoms over time were predictive of poorer adherence outcomes. This negative association was consistently observed across clinical subgroups, with supporting findings reported in 5 of 8 hypertension studies, 2 of 5 studies on CHF, 4 of 5 studies on IHD, and 4 of 5 studies addressing other CV conditions. There were three studies (n = 6,029) [8, 17, 23] reported their findings based on a combined assessment of anxiety and depression. As a result, the observed associations in these studies may be confounded by the overlapping effects of both these conditions.

Among the 15 studies, three studies examined gender as a moderator of the negative anxiety–adherence relationship. In Korhonen et al., women with frequent anxiety symptoms demonstrated a higher relative risk of nonadherence (RR = 1.38) compared to men (RR = 1.24); however, this interaction was not statistically significant (p = 0.38) [36]. In contrast, Gentil et al. found a significant gender interaction, where men with anxiety or depressive disorders had significantly higher odds of nonadherence (AOR = 2.39) then women (AOR = 0.92) [23]. Supporting this gender-related trend, Eisele et al. also reported that male sex, along with higher educational level, was independently associated with reduced adherence (odds ratio < 1) [17].

A subset of five studies (n = 2,289)—four cross-sectional and one longitudinal—found no significant association between anxiety and adherence. Among these, two studies were focused on CHF [45, 50], and one each on hypertension [37], IHD (Sudana & Asih, 2019), and mixed CV conditions [55]. Notably, the longitudinal study by Rasmussen et al., which followed 1,464 CHF patients over a three-year period, concluded that anxiety was not a significant predictor of non-adherence. Similar conclusions were reported in the four cross-sectional studies, which also found no independent link between anxiety and adherence. However, similar to the three studies reporting a negative association, one study (n = 255) [55] also presented its findings based on a combined measure of anxiety and depression.

There were three cross-sectional studies, encompassing 58,140 participants, which reported that individuals with anxiety were more likely to adhere to their CV medications. In particular, Kim et al. using data from a large Korean cohort of 57,105 individuals with hypertension, observed slightly higher adherence rates among those with anxiety disorders (Ha Kyoung [34]). This finding was echoed in Deek and Massouh, who reported a similar association in hypertensive patients [12], and in Dyussenova et al., who found that CHF patients with elevated anxiety levels demonstrated greater adherence [16].

A total of 20 studies were included in the meta-analysis investigating the relationship between anxiety and medication adherence among patients with CV diseases. The pooled SMD = −1.087, 95% CI [−1.469, −0.705], p < 0.001, indicating a statistically significant negative association, whereby higher anxiety levels were associated with lower medication adherence (Fig. 2). The analysis revealed substantial heterogeneity across studies (I2 = 99.38%), suggesting considerable variability in effect estimates. The sensitivity analysis (n = 18) demonstrated that the exclusion of two studies with extreme effect sizes [2, 19] had a modest influence on the pooled effect. Nonetheless, the overall effect remained statistically significant (SMD = −0.561, 95% CI [−0.9, −0.237], p = 0.001).

In the subgroup analysis based on CV type, three categories were assessed. The HTN subgroup, which included seven studies yielded a pooled SMD = −0.648, 95% CI [−1.293, −0.004], p = 0.049, indicating a statistically negative significant association between anxiety and medication adherence in this population. The studies exhibited a high level of heterogeneity (I2 = 99.46%), showing considerable variation in the reported outcomes. In case of CHF subgroup (n = 4), the pooled SMD = −0.51, 95% CI [−0.92, −0.10], p = 0.02, indicating a statistically significant negative association between anxiety and medication adherence. Nonetheless, heterogeneity among the included studies was high (I2 = 97.99%). Similarly, for the IHD subgroup (n = 4), the pooled SMD = −2.322, 95% CI [−3.498, −1.146], p < 0.001), reflecting a strong and statistically significant inverse relationship. This analysis also revealed high heterogeneity among studies (I2 = 99.18%).

In the second category of subgroup analysis, stratification was performed based on the tools used to assess anxiety and medication adherence. Among studies employing the HADS-A scale, the pooled SMD = −2.008, 95% CI [−2.752, −1.265], p < 0.001), indicating a statistically significant negative association, although heterogeneity was high (I2 = 99.18%). With regard to adherence assessment tools, two subgroup analyses were conducted. For studies utilizing the MMAS, the pooled effect size also suggested a negative association, though the result was not statistically significant (SMD = −0.412, 95% CI [−0.933, 0.109], p = 0.121), with high heterogeneity (I2 = 98.75%). Similarly, studies defining adherence using the 80% days covered threshold yielded a negative but non-significant association (SMD = −0.539, 95% CI [−1.148, 0.069], p = 0.083), also marked by high heterogeneity (I2 = 98.75%).

A subgroup analysis was performed according to geographic region, separating studies conducted in Asia and Europe. Among the studies from Asian countries, the pooled results indicated a statistically significant inverse relationship between anxiety and medication adherence (SMD = −2.090, 95% CI [−3.463, −0.717], p = 0.003), though heterogeneity was high (I2 = 99.51%). In contrast, studies conducted in European countries also showed a negative association, but it did not reach statistical significance (SMD = −0.396, 95% CI [−0.817, 0.026], p = 0.066), and heterogeneity remained substantial (I2 = 99.20%). ## 讨论 / Discussion

To our knowledge, this is the first systematic review and meta-analysis focused on the link between anxiety and CV medication adherence. Majority of studies included (n = 15) found an inverse association between anxiety and CV medication adherence. This inverse relationship was consistently observed across all clinical subgroups, including hypertension, CHF, and IHD. The meta-analysis supported these findings, yielding a pooled SMD = −1.087, 95% CI [−1.469, −0.705], p < 0.001).

There were two studies [2, 19] which reported large effect sizes, likely attributable to methodological features such as the use of self-reported adherence measures, cross-sectional study designs, and relatively small sample sizes. Sensitivity analysis (n = 18) indicated that, although the overall association between anxiety and medication adherence remained statistically significant following their exclusion (p = 0.001), the pooled SMD was notably reduced from −1.087 to −0.561. This reduction highlights the substantial influence of these studies on the overall meta-analytic estimate. Their exclusion also corresponds with the non-significant results observed in subgroup analyses based on European countries, and medication adherence tools.

In the subgroup analysis based on the tools used to assess anxiety and medication adherence, the subgroup utilizing the HADS-A showed a statistically significant negative association between anxiety and medication adherence (p < 0.001). However, this significance did not persist for two medication adherence measurement tools. When comparing effect sizes, a stronger negative association was observed with the objective measure of adherence, defined as ≥ 80% days covered (SMD = −0.539), compared to the self-reported MMAS (SMD = −0.412). This discrepancy may suggest that anxiety has a greater impact on patients’ perceived adherence behaviors than on their actual medication-taking patterns.

Current research presents mixed findings on the relationship between anxiety and medication adherence across various disease groups. Two systematic reviews have assessed this relationship in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). In the case of COPD, a review of 34 studies found mixed outcomes, indicating ongoing uncertainty [57]. Likewise, evidence from studies on individuals with IBD showed varied results, with no definitive conclusion [47]. In contrast, a meta-analysis by Wykowski et al., which included 11 studies found that people living with HIV (PLHIV) who reported anxiety had significantly higher odds of poor adherence to antiretroviral therapy (ART) compared to those without anxiety (pOR = 1.59, 95% CI: 1.29–1.96, p < 0.001) [60].

Of the studies included, three reported a positive association between anxiety and CV medication adherence. Interestingly, these studies have emerged from resource-limited or culturally specific contexts, including Lebanon [12], Pakistan [1], and South Korea [34]. These findings suggest that, within certain cultural frameworks, anxiety may be associated with increased health vigilance and, consequently, higher medication adherence. However, despite all three studies originating from Asian countries, this pattern should not be overgeneralized. Subgroup analyses revealed a significant negative association between anxiety and adherence in Asian countries (p = 0.003), while findings from European cohorts were non-significant (p = 0.066). Among these three studies, the study by Kim et al. is particularly noteworthy. Their analysis, based on a nationally representative sample (n = 57,105), demonstrated a statistically significant positive association (AOR = 1.08, 95% CI: 1.06–1.10). This association remained after adjusting for sociodemographic and clinical variables, though slightly attenuated (AOR = 1.03, 95% CI: 1.02–1.04) (H. K. [34]). However, the use of administrative international classification of diseases (ICD) codes for diagnosing anxiety and pharmacy refill data as a proxy for adherence limits diagnostic accuracy and overlooks the complexity of adherence behavior. This study may also be influenced by reverse causality, whereby individuals who adhere well to treatment may have more frequent interactions with the healthcare system, increasing the likelihood of an anxiety diagnosis. In such cases, it is possible that adherence precedes the identification of anxiety, rather than anxiety influencing adherence. Additionally, the observed effect size was modest, indicating that the statistical significance may be largely driven by the study’s large sample size rather than reflecting a clinically meaningful association.

There are numerous factors that could contribute to the inverse relationship between anxiety and medication adherence. Individuals with anxiety often report heightened concerns about adverse drug effects [53] and experience cognitive impairments—such as reduced attention, memory, and executive function—that can disrupt routine adherence behaviors [15, 48]. These mechanisms align with constructs of the Health Belief Model, which suggests that health behaviors are influenced by perceived susceptibility, perceived severity, perceived benefits, and perceived barriers [26]. In anxious individuals, elevated perceived barriers and reduced self-efficacy may undermine motivation to adhere to treatment. Additionally, an important consideration is the high comorbidity between anxiety and depression. Research indicates that 45–67% of individuals diagnosed with depression also meet criteria for at least one anxiety disorder, while 30–63% of those with anxiety meet criteria for concurrent depression [6, 21, 39]. Given the well-documented impact of depression on adherence—where depressed individuals have 1.76 times higher odds of non-adherence compared to non-depressed individuals [27]—the frequent co-occurrence of anxiety and depression may compound their negative influence on adherence.

The association between anxiety and CV medication adherence has important implications for clinical care and the management of CV diseases. Given the high prevalence of anxiety disorders among individuals with CV conditions [7, 11], it is critical to routinely identify anxiety as a potential risk factor for non-adherence, particularly to CV medications. However, the relationship between anxiety and medication adherence is also shaped by other multiple factors, including co-occurring psychological conditions, sociocultural context, gender, and individual characteristics such as self-efficacy and illness perception. Moreover, anxiety is only one among several well-established barriers that have been consistently associated with lower adherence. Other challenges include adverse drug effects [22], treatment complexity, financial burdens [10, 24, 49], negative beliefs about medications, inadequate social support, and issues related to healthcare providers [22]. Therefore, effective adherence interventions must take a comprehensive, patient-centered approach that is responsive to the multifaceted needs of the target population. Healthcare systems should consider implementing focused strategies, such as incorporating routine anxiety screening into CV clinics. For those identified with elevated anxiety symptoms, structured referral mechanisms to integrated behavioral health services should be established. These interventions may enhance medication adherence and improve patient outcomes.

It is important to acknowledge several limitations inherent in this review. Most notably, 18 out of the 24 included studies employed cross-sectional designs, which restrict the ability to infer causal relationships or to capture the evolving nature of the relationship between anxiety and CV medication adherence over time. Additionally, the review revealed a high degree of methodological heterogeneity, with meta-analytic heterogeneity estimates ranging from I2 = 97.99% to 99.51%. Another noteworthy limitation in four studies [8, 17, 23, 55] is the concurrent assessment of anxiety and depression using a combined measure. This methodological approach introduces potential confounding, as it limits the ability to distinguish the independent effect of anxiety on CV medication adherence from that of depression.

To address these limitations, future research should prioritize prospective longitudinal designs that focus specifically on anxiety, without the confounding influence of other psychological disorders. Additionally, while a few studies [17, 23, 36] explored the moderating role of gender in the anxiety–adherence relationship, further targeted research is needed to clarify gender-specific patterns and their implications. It is also important to note that certain subpopulations—such as individuals with cerebrovascular disease or congenital heart disease—were underrepresented in the meta-analysis, with only one study included for each. This limited representation underscores the need for more focused research in these groups to enhance the strength and generalizability of future findings.

## 结论 / Conclusion

The findings primarily support a negative association between anxiety and CV medication adherence, though some studies reported positive or non-significant relationships. However, several limitations such as the predominance of cross-sectional study designs, and notable heterogeneity warrant cautious interpretation of these results. Future research should prioritize prospective longitudinal designs to better elucidate the temporal and causal nature of the association between anxiety and medication adherence in cardiovascular populations.
