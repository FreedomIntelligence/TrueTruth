## English Abstract

Ambulatory blood pressure measurement (ABPM) data from the PRECISION study have shown that in patients with confirmed resistant hypertension (RHT), aprocitentan leads to substantial lowering of BP, particularly at night-time. Here, the ABPM data were used to define subgroups with substantial prognostic value (dipping pattern, BP load, heart rate, arterial stiffness, and salt sensitivity) and to evaluate the BP-lowering effect of aprocitentan on these subgroups.

The effects of aprocitentan on ABPM derivatives including dipping pattern, BP load, and heart rate were evaluated. The BP lowering effect of aprocitentan was assessed based on the subject's baseline arterial stiffness and salt sensitivity.

At week 4, the placebo-corrected and dose-dependent decrease of ambulatory systolic BP was more marked for baseline non-dippers than dippers. The proportion of non-dippers at baseline whose dipping pattern normalized at week 4 was higher for the aprocitentan group compared to placebo. The decrease in BP load during daytime/nighttime was also more pronounced with aprocitentan compared to placebo. Despite the BP lowering effect of aprocitentan, heart rate remained unchanged at week 36. The BP lowering effect of aprocitentan was not diminished by increased arterial stiffness index or salt sensitivity risk at baseline.

In addition to a significant and sustained day and night-time ambulatory BP reduction, aprocitentan was associated with a higher proportion of patients experiencing dipping pattern normalization and more pronounced BP load reduction, indicating prognostic relevance. Increased arterial stiffness or salt sensitivity did not alter the BP response to aprocitentan, suggesting additional clinical benefit.

## 背景 / Background

Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM) is increasingly recognized for its enhanced ability to diagnose hypertension and monitor treatment compared to conventional office blood pressure (BP) measurement. Advantages of ABPM include reduced white-coat effect caused by the medical environment, improved reproducibility from numerous readings, less influence of potential placebo effects, and ability to discriminate between apparent and true resistant hypertension (RHT) [1–3]. ABPM is more closely associated with hypertension-mediated organ damage than office BP measurements [4] and is a better predictor of cardiovascular events and mortality [5,6]. It allows for the quantification of night-time BP with identification of abnormal nocturnal dipping patterns, which have superior predictive value compared to daytime BP [6–8]. BP load can be established from ABPM to document the duration of lack of BP control and the effect of antihypertensive therapy [9,10]. Additional prognostically relevant information can be derived from ABPM that cannot be obtained from other BP measurement procedures like the ambulatory arterial stiffness index (AASI) [11], and the salt sensitivity risk (SSR) [12]. Their relevance and association with BP phenotypes and changes with various antihypertensive therapies have recently been confirmed [13–15].

PRECISION demonstrated that, in patients with RHT, aprocitentan lowered automated office and 24-h ambulatory BP after 4 weeks of treatment, maintained over 48 weeks, and the ABPM results indicated a particularly pronounced reduction of nocturnal BP [16]. Aprocitentan is a dual endothelin ETA/ETB receptor antagonist, administered orally once daily with a half-life of 44 h [17]. It is the first endothelin receptor antagonist (ERA) approved for the treatment of difficult-to-control hypertension in the United States and Europe providing a new, complementary therapeutic option through specific targeting of the endothelin pathway critically implicated in the hypertension pathogenesis [18]. As endothelin may contribute to arterial stiffening and salt sensitivity [18,19], aprocitentan could impact AASI and SSR derived from ABPM.

Given the apparent unique BP lowering profile of aprocitentan in high-risk patients with confirmed RHT and multiple additional comorbidities, we analyzed the impact of aprocitentan on various prognostically relevant indices derived from ABPM, including dipping pattern, BP load, and heart rate to substantiate potential benefits beyond pure BP lowering. The BP lowering effect of aprocitentan was evaluated based on subject's baseline AASI (below or above the median) and SSR.

## 方法 / Methods

The data supporting these study findings are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.

PRECISION was a multicenter, blinded, randomized, parallel-group, phase 3 study (NCT03541174). The study design [20] and primary results [16] have been described previously. Patients with RHT were treated with aprocitentan 12.5 and 25 mg in a three-part study that included a double-blind placebo-controlled treatment part (4 weeks), a single-blind active treatment part (32 weeks), and a double-blind placebo-controlled withdrawal part (12 weeks). The present exploratory post hoc analyses investigate the impact of treatment with aprocitentan on established and prognostically relevant ABPM-derived indices.

The patients described in these analyses were PRECISION participants with a valid 24-h ABPM at baseline, a history of uncontrolled office BP despite taking at least three antihypertensives within 1 year before screening and sitting SBP at least 140 mmHg as assessed by unattended automated office BP measurement [20].

During screening (4–12 weeks), all patients switched from individual antihypertensive therapies (except beta-blockers) to at least 4 weeks of standardized background therapy, consisting of a single-pill triple-combination of a calcium channel blocker (amlodipine), an angiotensin receptor blocker (valsartan), and a diuretic (hydrochlorothiazide), at fixed doses of either 5/160/25 or 10/160/25 mg, as tolerated. Placebo was subsequently added to patient treatment in a single (patient)-blind run-in period (4 weeks). Patients who remained hypertensive despite at least 8 weeks of treatment with standardized background therapy were randomized to aprocitentan 12.5 or 25 mg, or placebo in a 1 : 1 : 1 ratio once-daily in the 4-week part 1; during the 32-week part 2, all patients received aprocitentan 25 mg; in the 12-week part 3, patients were re-randomized to aprocitentan 25 mg or placebo in a 1 : 1 ratio [20]. Compliance, measured by valsartan urinary concentrations, was around 98% during the entire study. Direct observed treatment was performed at the four planned ABPM assessments.

ABPM was performed over a 24-h period at baseline, and weeks 4, 36, and 40. Ambulatory SBP and DBP were measured every 20 min from 06 : 00 to 23 : 00 and every 30 min from 23 : 00 to 06 : 00. Mean daytime ABPM was calculated from 09 : 00 to 21 : 00 and mean night-time ABPM from 01 : 00 to 6 : 00. Valid 24-h ABPM data required a total duration of at least 21 h and at least 70% valid readings over the period.

Dippers had a change from daytime to night-time SBP at least 10%; nondippers had a change from daytime to night-time SBP less than 10%. Dipping status was assessed at randomization.

Daytime BP load was the proportion of daytime BP measurements in which SBP was greater than 135 mmHg and DBP greater than 85 mmHg. Night-time BP load was the proportion of night-time BP measurements in which SBP was greater than 120 mmHg and DBP greater than 70 mmHg.

The AASI was calculated from ABPM data as 1 minus the regression slope of DBP to SBP. The patient population was dichotomized into two groups, above and below the baseline median AASI, and BP responses to aprocitentan were evaluated in both groups.

SSR was derived from ABPM data as per Castiglioni et al. [12], using the above definition of dippers/non-dippers: high SSR was attributed to non-dippers with a 24-h heart rate of greater than 70 bpm, low SSR to dippers with a 24-h heart rate 70 bpm or less, and intermediate SSR to the remaining combinations (dippers with heart rate >70 bpm or non-dippers with heart rate ≤70 bpm).

The statistical analyses of PRECISION have been described previously [20]. Changes from baseline in ABPM were analyzed using an analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) with a factor for treatment group and a covariate for baseline (or withdrawal-baseline) 24 h mean BP.

Least-squares mean (LSM) differences from placebo in BP changes, and 95% confidence intervals (CI) were reported.

Missing data was not imputed, and no multiplicity adjustments have been applied, as these subgroup analyses are exploratory in nature.

Patients were analyzed according to their baseline AASI (below or above the median value reported for the overall population, i.e. 0.54). Patients with high baseline AASI (≥ 0.54) were older (63 ± 11 versus 60 ± 10 years) more frequently female (46 versus 33%; Table S6), more likely to be obese or severely obese (75 versus 65%) and to have diabetes (63 versus 44%) or chronic kidney disease stage 3–4 corresponding to an estimated glomerular filtration rate in the 15–60 ml/min/1.73 m2 range (26 versus 18%). Patients with high SSR were younger than patients with low SSR (59 ± 11 versus 65 ± 10 years), more frequently Black or African-American (14 versus 5%; Table S9), and more likely to be obese or severely obese (77 versus 56%) and to have diabetes (59 versus 47%). ## 讨论 / Discussion

In PRECISION, ABPM reduction was particularly pronounced during night-time, which, given the prognostic superiority of nocturnal versus diurnal BP [6–8], might translate into additional reduction of cardiovascular risk. These exploratory post- hoc analyses investigated the impact of baseline dipping pattern on treatment response. As expected, 60% were non-dippers at baseline. Compared with dippers, non-dippers were more likely to be obese and have diabetes or macro-albuminuria. These known risk factors for RHT have been associated with increased ET-1 production [18], suggesting that these high-risk patients could particularly benefit from treatment with an ERA. The BP lowering effect of aprocitentan at week 4 was more pronounced for non-dippers at baseline, an intermediate BP phenotype associated with a significant risk of poor outcome [6–8]. The night-time response to aprocitentan was more pronounced for non-dippers, and this effect persisted after an additional 32 weeks of single-blind treatment with aprocitentan 25 mg. BP lowering was achieved with no heart rate increases. Treatment with 12.5 and 25 mg of aprocitentan resulted in normalization of the dipping pattern at week 4 in 44 and 40% of patients with a non-dipping pattern at baseline, respectively, versus 31% with placebo. Restoration of normal nocturnal BP drop is relevant for patients at high risk of cardiovascular events, who usually maintain their baseline non-dipping status [8]. The substantial proportion of patients whose dipping pattern normalized at 4 weeks and sustained at 36 weeks is remarkable and has to our knowledge not been demonstrated with other antihypertensive drug classes. This may reflect the relevance of the endothelin pathway in the pathophysiology of RHT and underlying mechanisms including nocturnal hypertension and increased salt sensitivity, potentially rendering therapeutic targeting of endothelin receptors a suitable approach in this high-risk cohort.

ABPM-derived BP load is another useful metric of BP control. By measuring the proportion of BP readings that exceed a predetermined threshold, BP load has the advantage over standard BP of incorporating the duration of lack of BP control thereby providing an integrated measure of the overall burden hypertension poses on the system [9]. After 4 weeks of treatment, the daytime and night-time BP load decrease with aprocitentan was more than double that with placebo, which was maintained until week 36. This effect, in line with the BP lowering efficacy of aprocitentan is relevant given the well established association between BP load and target organ damage [9]. Although not directly investigated here, consistent reduction in BP load is expected to result in regression of hypertension-mediated organ damage, and therefore improved cardiovascular outcomes [1,2].

Arterial stiffness is associated with RHT [21] potentially leading to a cycle of accelerated stiffening and hypertension [19]. Increased arterial stiffness is coupled with reduced efficacy of BP lowering, a poor BP response to antihypertensive treatments and drug dosage increase [22,23]. Furthermore, not all antihypertensive agents are effective in reducing arterial stiffness [22]. Here, AASI was derived from ABPM, the BP lowering efficacy of aprocitentan was evident for all patients, including those at high-risk with increased AASI at baseline. Endothelin, through its impact on vascular remodeling and fibrosis [24], could play a role in the mechanobiology of arterial stiffening [19].

Salt sensitivity, a critical factor in hypertension pathogenesis requiring an increase in antihypertensive medication [25], has been associated with overstimulated endothelin system in RHT patients [18]. We, therefore, investigated the association of ABPM-derived SSR [12], an established surrogate marker of salt sensitivity, with BP response to aprocitentan. Patients with an intermediate to high SSR at baseline had a stronger (12.5 mg dose) or similar (25 mg dose) response to aprocitentan. A marginal decrease in the percentage of patients with high SSR was observed with aprocitentan, most likely because of the effect of aprocitentan on nocturnal BP, as aprocitentan has no relevant effect on heart rate. BP lowering in patients with high baseline SSR with a dual ERA demonstrates the association between ET-1 and salt-sensitive hypertension [18].

The rigorous design of PRECISION used to confirm the diagnosis of RHT and eliminate pseudo-hypertension is its major strength [20]. One limitation is the lack of reproducibility of the dipping phenotypes identified by ABPM given the limited number of hypertensive patients maintaining the same dipping pattern over the years independently of the type of treatment [26]. However, night-time hypertension is predictive of poor cardiovascular outcome [6,7] and reproducibility is considered highest in the majority of patients who have a non-dipping pattern of nocturnal BP [8]. The ABPM-derived salt sensitivity measure is indirect and may be influenced by other factors [12]. Here, the analyses were exploratory, which can be limited by increased risk of bias, reduced statistical rigor, and should be interpreted cautiously until confirmed by prospective studies [27].

## 结论 / Conclusion

The present investigation establishes concomitant relevant changes in indices derived from ABPM that are likely to translate into improved outcomes for this patient cohort at high risk for future cardiovascular events. The observed substantial and sustained reduction in BP load (about 20%) as well as restoration of a normal dipping pattern further substantiate the efficacy of aprocitentan in patients with RHT and may indicate a specific role for aprocitentan in this patient cohort. Typical features of RHT including higher BMI, older age, chronic kidney disease, stiffer arteries, and increased salt sensitivity tend to make it more difficult to achieve blood pressure control in these individuals. Remarkably, based on the current analysis and previously published data [16], it appears that aprocitentan performs particularly well in lowering BP in patients with these characteristics (older age, chronic kidney disease) and as demonstrated here, with no reduction in BP lowering efficacy even in the presence of arterial stiffness or higher salt sensitivity. Although the latter features were not changed by treatment with aprocitentan in the observed time frame, it is well established that such changes may require years to become evident.

The availability of aprocitentan in routine clinical practice is a welcomed and much needed addition to our armamentarium to effectively treat patients with RHT with the added benefit of targeting an otherwise unopposed yet highly relevant mechanism in the pathogenesis of hypertension, likely yielding therapeutic benefit beyond BP lowering per se.

The European Medicines Agency (EMA) and the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved aprocitentan in 2024 and the UK Medicines and Healthcare Regulatory Agency (MHRA) in January 2025. The EMA and MHRA approved the 12.5 and 25 mg doses for the treatment of RHT and the FDA approved the 12.5 mg dose for the treatment of hypertension in combination with other antihypertensive drugs when BP is not adequately controlled. The mode of action of aprocitentan involves the blockade of endothelin receptors (ETA and ETB), a target which so far has not been tackled by any existing antihypertensive drug. Several prognostic markers in resistant hypertension, such as dipping pattern, arterial stiffness, or salt sensitivity may be associated with an upregulated endothelin system supporting an important role for endothelin receptor antagonists.

The present post hoc exploratory analyses confirm the efficacy of aprocitentan beyond conventional office BP lowering. Aprocitentan lowers non-dipping rates and BP load and is also notably efficacious in patients with non-dipping pattern of nocturnal BP, elevated AASI or increased SSR, all of whom have a high risk of cardiovascular events and usually a poor response to treatment. Achieving high rates of BP control with aprocitentan in these high-risk patients should reduce the risk of adverse hypertension-related outcomes.
