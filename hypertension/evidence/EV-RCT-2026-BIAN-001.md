## English Abstract

Dietary sodium and potassium intake are key determinants of blood pressure (BP) control in hypertensive patients. However, their associations with longitudinal home BP control in patients with apparent treatment‐resistant hypertension (aTRH) remain unclear. In this prospective cohort study, baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium and potassium excretion, objective biomarkers of dietary sodium and potassium intake, as well as urinary sodium‐to‐potassium (Na/K) ratio were evaluated as exposure variables. The primary outcome was longitudinal home BP control during follow‐up. Mixed‐effects models were used to assess these associations. A total of 172 patients were followed for a median of 39.3 months, contributing 7547 morning and 6640 evening home BP measurements. After multivariable adjustment, each 1‐standard deviation (SD) increase in urinary sodium excretion (99.49 mmol) was associated with poorer home systolic BP (SBP) control (morning: odds ratio [OR] 0.59, 95% confidence interval [CI] 0.38–0.89; evening: OR 0.65, 95% CI 0.44–0.96). Each 1‐SD increase in the urinary Na/K ratio (1.98 units) was associated with poorer morning (OR 0.68, 95% CI 0.48–0.97) and evening home SBP control (OR 0.69, 95% CI 0.51–0.94). A higher Na/K ratio was also associated with poorer overall evening home BP control (OR 0.73, 95% CI 0.54–0.98). Consistent associations were observed when BP was analyzed as a continuous outcome. Urinary potassium excretion alone was not significantly associated with home BP control. Dietary sodium restriction and maintenance of sodium‐potassium balance may represent important strategies for improving home BP control in patients with aTRH.

Trial Registration: Not applicable. This study was a prospective observational cohort study with no assignment of participants to any intervention; thereby clinical trial registration was not required.

## 背景 / Background

Resistant hypertension (RH) is defined as blood pressure (BP) in a patient that remains uncontrolled despite the concurrent use of 3 antihypertensive drug classes, typically including a long‐acting calcium channel blocker (CCB), a renin‐angiotensin system inhibitor (RASi), and a diuretic, or as a patient whose BP requires the use of 4 or more antihypertensive medications [1]. When factors such as medication dose, adherence, out‐of‐office BP, or pseudoresistance cannot be adequately excluded, the term apparent treatment‐resistant hypertension (aTRH) is commonly used. Population‐based and clinical studies have reported that the prevalence of aTRH among treated hypertensive adults ranges from 12% to 19.7% and from 15% to 18%, respectively [1, 2]. Compared with patients with nonresistant hypertension, aTRH represents a high‐risk phenotype and is associated with an increased risk of adverse cardiorenal outcomes, cardiovascular mortality, and all‐cause mortality [3, 4, 5]. Nevertheless, despite intensified pharmacological treatment, a substantial proportion of patients with aTRH continue to exhibit uncontrolled BP, particularly in out‐of‐office settings.

Recent guidelines have emphasized the importance of out‐of‐office BP measurements in the diagnosis and evaluation of RH [6, 7, 8]. Compared with office blood pressure measurement (OBPM), home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM) offers greater accessibility and long‐term repeatability. It allows assessment of diurnal BP variability and evaluation of the sustained antihypertensive effects of therapy [6, 9]. HBPM more accurately reflects BP burden under real‐life conditions and has demonstrated superior predictive value for target organ damage and adverse cardiovascular events compared with OBPM [10, 11]. Hence, HBPM is strongly recommended to guide antihypertensive therapy in patients with primary hypertension and plays a crucial role in the diagnosis and management of aTRH [7, 12, 13]. Importantly, masked uncontrolled aTRH is not rare [14]; even when office BP appears to be controlled, patients with aTRH may repeatedly exhibit poor home BP control during follow‐up. Compared to controlled home BP, uncontrolled home BP, especially elevated morning home BP, has been identified as an independent predictor of major adverse cardiovascular events, cardiovascular mortality, and all‐cause mortality [11].

Dietary sodium and potassium play a central role in BP regulation [15]. 24‐hour urinary sodium and potassium excretion are widely used as objective biomarkers of sodium and potassium intake in clinical studies. Extensive epidemiological evidence has shown that higher sodium intake is associated with elevated BP levels and increased cardiovascular risk [16, 17], a relationship that is particularly pronounced in Chinese populations, where daily sodium intake often exceeds 5 g/day [18, 19]. Meanwhile, baseline sodium intake has also been identified as an independent predictor of incident hypertension in normotensive individuals [20]. In contrast, 24‐hour urinary potassium excretion has been inversely associated with BP levels and the risk of composite cardiovascular outcomes [19, 21]. A moderate sodium intake (3–5 g/day) combined with higher potassium intake has been associated with lower mortality and reduced cardiovascular event risk [22]. More importantly, sodium restriction has been consistently shown to be an effective strategy for BP control [23]. Dose‐response meta‐analyses have revealed an approximately linear relationship between sodium reduction and decreases in both systolic and diastolic BP, which persists across the full range of dietary sodium exposure [24]. Similarly, in patients with RH, several small‐scale interventional studies demonstrated that substantial reductions in dietary sodium intake contribute to marked short‐term BP lowering [25]. Nevertheless, most existing studies primarily focused on cross‐sectional BP parameters, whereas BP control in daily life is inherently dynamic. In patients with aTRH, poor BP control typically does not present as persistently uncontrolled BP but rather as recurrent episodes of BP dysregulation over time. Additionally, pervious evidence has largely emphasized office BP outcomes, while the importance of home BP management has been relatively ignored. Therefore, in this prospective cohort study of patients with aTRH, we aimed to investigate the associations of baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium, potassium excretion and sodium‐to‐potassium (Na/K) ratio with home BP control, assessed through repeated morning and evening home BP measurements during longitudinal follow‐up.

## 方法 / Methods

This was a prospective cohort study designed to evaluate the association of dietary sodium and potassium intake with home BP control among patients with aTRH. In the present study, aTRH was defined as uncontrolled office BP despite the concurrent use of ≥3 antihypertensive drug classes at maximally tolerated doses, typically including a diuretic; or as controlled office BP requiring the use of ≥4 antihypertensive medications, without formal exclusion of medication non‐adherence or white‐coat effect [1]. Participants were consecutively recruited from outpatients attending the Department of Cardiology at Peking Union Medical College Hospital between August 2017 and December 2025. The inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) Adult patients who were diagnosed with aTRH; (2) availability of complete baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium and potassium excretion data; (3) non‐dialysis status. The exclusion criteria included: (1) secondary hypertension (common causes including endocrine hypertension and renal artery stenosis); (2) a 24‐hour urine volume <1000 mL; (3) absence of regular HBPM. This study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Peking Union Medical College Hospital (No. ZS‐1377) and was conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants.

Baseline data of eligible participants were collected at enrollment through structured questionnaires and review of previous medical records. These data included demographic characteristics (sex and age), anthropometric measurements (height, weight, and body mass index [BMI]), cardiovascular risk factors (smoking status, diabetes mellitus, and dyslipidemia), clinical comorbidities (atrial fibrillation, congestive heart failure, coronary artery disease, cerebrovascular disease, peripheral vascular disease, chronic kidney disease [CKD], and albuminuria), and clinical management information (office BP, number and classes of antihypertensive medications, and the use of antiplatelet agents and statins). BMI was calculated as weight (kg) divided by height squared (m2). According to BMI cutoffs for the Chinese population, individuals with a BMI ≥28 kg/m2 were classified as obese. At enrollment, fasting blood samples were obtained for the measurement of fasting plasma glucose, glycated hemoglobin, serum creatinine, total cholesterol, low‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, and triglycerides. Urinary albumin‐to‐creatinine ratio (ACR) was also assessed. Renal function was evaluated using serum creatinine and urinary albumin excretion. The glomerular filtration rate (GFR) for each participant was estimated using the 2021 Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration equation (CKD‐EPI). Albuminuria was defined as a urinary ACR ≥30 mg/g.

Office BP was measured during routine outpatient visits for all participants according to standardized procedures recommended by the Chinese Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Hypertension (2024 revision) [26]. Specifically, after resting for at least 5 min in a seated position, BP was measured on the upper arm using a validated automated oscillometric device (HEM‐7130; Omron Healthcare Co., Ltd, Kyoto, Japan) with an appropriately sized cuff [27]. At the initial visit, BP was measured in both arms, and the arm with the higher BP reading was used for subsequent measurements. Two consecutive measurements were obtained at 30–60s intervals, and the average of the two readings was recorded. If the difference between the two systolic or diastolic BP readings exceeded 10 mmHg, a third measurement was performed, and the average of the three readings was used as the office BP value.

All participants received standardized training on home BP measurement. For HBPM, the same device (Omron HEM‐7130) was recommended to all participants. The majority of patients used this recommended device; however, a small number of participants used alternative upper‐arm cuff‐oscillometric devices of different brands. For these participants, the measurement accuracy of their devices was verified by research staff at enrollment by comparing readings with those obtained using the study‐recommended device, and only devices demonstrating acceptable agreement were permitted for use in the study. Morning BP measurements were performed in a seated position after awakening with an empty bladder and before taking morning antihypertensive medications and breakfast. Evening BP measurements were performed in a seated position after dinner and before bedtime. At each time point, BP was measured twice, and the average of the two readings was recorded. If the difference between the two systolic or diastolic BP readings exceeded 10 mmHg, a third measurement was obtained, and the average of the three readings was used as the home BP value. Home BP data were collected as part of routine clinical care, and all home BP records obtained after baseline assessment were included in the analysis.

Home BP control was the primary outcome of this study and was defined separately for morning and evening measurements, including systolic BP (SBP) control, diastolic BP (DBP) control, and overall BP control (both SBP and DBP controlled). Secondary outcomes included home BP levels (SBP and DBP analyzed as continuous variables), office BP control, and office BP levels. Each participant contributed multiple BP measurements during follow‐up.

Given the potential benefits of intensive BP lowering [28, 29, 30], more stringent BP targets recommended by the Chinese Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Hypertension (2024 revision) were adopted in this study [26]. For participants aged <80 years, home SBP <130 mmHg and home DBP <80 mmHg were defined as home SBP control and home DBP control, respectively, and home BP <130/80 mmHg was defined as overall home BP control. Correspondingly, office SBP <130 mmHg and office DBP <80 mmHg were defined as office SBP control and office DBP control, respectively, and office BP <130/80 mmHg was defined as overall office BP control. For participants aged ≥80 years, home SBP <135 mmHg and home DBP <85 mmHg were defined as home SBP control and home DBP control, respectively, and home BP <135/85 mmHg was defined as overall home BP control; office SBP <140 mmHg and office DBP <90 mmHg were defined as office SBP control and office DBP control, respectively, and office BP <140/90 mmHg was defined as overall office BP control. For participants with diabetes mellitus, CKD, atrial fibrillation, congestive heart failure, coronary artery disease, or cerebrovascular disease, BP targets for both home and office BP were <130/80 mmHg.

In this study, baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium, potassium excretion, and urinary Na/K ratio were the primary exposure variables. All participants received both oral and written instructions for 24‐hour urine collection. Specifically, participants were instructed to void and discard the first urine sample at 7:00 a.m. on the first day of collection, then collect all urine passed over the subsequent 24 h into a designated container. Boric acid was added as a preservative to the collection container at the time of the first void and mixed thoroughly. The final urine sample was collected at 7:00 a.m. on the following day and added to the container. Participants were repeatedly instructed to avoid missing any urine samples during the collection period. Female participants were advised to avoid urine collection during menstruation. Total urine volume was measured or urine weight was recorded, with urine weight (g) considered equivalent to urine volume (mL). All participants entered their total urine volume into the Peking Union Medical College Hospital mobile application and submitted a 2‐mL urine aliquot for laboratory analysis. Urinary sodium and potassium concentrations (mmol/L) were measured using the ion‐selective electrode method. 24‐hour urinary sodium or potassium excretion (mmol) was calculated as 24‐hour urine volume (L) multiplied by the corresponding urinary sodium or potassium concentration (mmol/L). The urinary Na/K ratio was calculated as urinary sodium concentration (mmol/L) divided by urinary potassium concentration (mmol/L). Measurements of urinary sodium and potassium concentrations, as well as calculations of 24‐hour urinary sodium and potassium excretion and the Na/K ratio, were performed by the Department of Laboratory Medicine at Peking Union Medical College Hospital.

Categorical variables are presented as numbers (percentages), and continuous variables are expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD) or median (interquartile range [IQR]), as appropriate, according to their distribution. Multivariable generalized linear mixed‐effects models (GLMMs) were used to examine the associations between baseline 24‐hour urinary electrolyte excretion and longitudinal home BP control. A random intercept for each participant was included in all models, and time since baseline was modeled using restricted cubic splines with three degrees of freedom. All models were adjusted for sex, age, BMI, smoking status (current smoker or non‐smoker), diabetes mellitus, dyslipidemia, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), baseline office BP (SBP and/or DBP, depending on the outcome of interest), and classes of antihypertensive medications, including RASi, β‐blockers, CCB, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRA), and thiazide diuretics. Antihypertensive medication classes were included as time‐varying covariates, defined according to the most recent outpatient prescription prior to each home BP measurement. All exposure variables were analyzed as continuous variables and were standardized. Continuous covariates were also standardized, and effect estimates are presented per 1‐SD increase. In models evaluating urinary sodium excretion, urinary potassium excretion was additionally adjusted for, and vice versa. In contrast, models evaluating the urinary Na/K ratio did not include urinary sodium or potassium excretion. Missing covariate data were handled using multiple imputation. No imputation was performed for outcome variables, exposure variables, time variables, or antihypertensive medication classes. The proportion of missing data for each covariate prior to imputation was reported in Tables S1 and S2. Models were fitted separately within each imputed dataset, and results were pooled using Rubin's rules.

For analyses of secondary outcomes, multivariable linear mixed‐effects models (LMMs) with the same covariate adjustment strategy were used to evaluate the associations between urinary electrolyte excretion and home BP levels and office BP levels. Multivariable GLMMs were applied to examine the associations between baseline urinary electrolyte excretion and office BP control. Interaction and subgroup analyses were conducted according to demographic characteristics (sex and age), clinical comorbidities (obesity, diabetes mellitus, and CKD), and baseline use of antihypertensive medications (MRA and thiazide diuretics).

Several sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the robustness of the primary findings. First, the primary analyses were repeated using complete‐case data. Second, the first home BP measurement for each participant was excluded from the outcome to minimize potential learning effects associated with initial self‐measurement. Third, to evaluate whether the choice of baseline BP adjustment influenced the results, an additional analysis was performed in which the first home BP measurement was used as a covariate to adjust for baseline home BP, with all subsequent home BP measurements serving as outcomes. Fourth, truncated follow‐up windows were applied to evaluate whether heterogeneity in follow‐up duration influenced the observed associations. Specifically, home BP outcomes were restricted to measurements obtained within 6, 12, 24, and 36 months after baseline, and the same generalized linear mixed‐effects modeling strategy was applied to each truncated dataset.

All analyses were performed using R version 4.4.1 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). All statistical tests were two‐sided, and a p value <0.05 was considered statistically significant. Interactions were considered statistically significant if the p value for interaction was <0.05.

## 结果 / Results

Of the 199 patients initially assessed for eligibility, 27 were excluded: 13 due to secondary hypertension, 6 due to a 24‐hour urine volume <1000 mL, and 8 due to the absence of regular HBPM. Consequently, 172 patients were included in the final analysis, contributing 7547 morning and 6640 evening home BP records (Figure S1). The median follow‐up duration was 39.3 (18.8, 63.7) months, and the median number of follow‐up visits was 7 (4, 11). The characteristics of the patients are summarized in Table 1. The mean age of the patients was 55.28 ± 15.54 years, and 55.81% were male. At baseline, 38.95%, 49.42%, and 11.63% of patients were receiving three, four, and five or more antihypertensive medications, respectively. The median baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium, potassium excretion, and urinary Na/K ratio were 175.50 (129.00, 237.25) mmol, 53.25 (41.40, 69.88) mmol, and 3.30 (2.29, 4.48), respectively.

In multivariable GLMMs adjusted for sex, age, BMI, smoking status, diabetes mellitus, dyslipidemia, eGFR, baseline office BP, classes of antihypertensive medications, and baseline 24‐hour urinary potassium excretion, baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium excretion was significantly and inversely associated with home SBP control during follow‐up. Specifically, for each 1‐SD increase in urinary sodium excretion (99.49 mmol), the odds of achieving controlled morning home SBP decreased by 41% (OR: 0.59, 95% CI: 0.38–0.89; p = 0.013), and the odds of achieving controlled evening home SBP decreased by 35% (OR: 0.65, 95% CI: 0.44–0.96; p = 0.030). No statistically significant associations were observed between urinary sodium excretion and morning or evening home DBP control or overall home BP control (Figure 1). Baseline 24‐hour urinary potassium excretion was not significantly associated with home BP control during follow‐up (Figure 1).

After adjustment for potential confounders, baseline 24‐hour urinary Na/K ratio was inversely associated with home BP control during follow‐up. For each 1‐SD increase in urinary Na/K ratio (1.98 units), the odds of achieving controlled morning home SBP decreased by 32% (OR: 0.68, 95% CI: 0.48–0.97; p = 0.035), the odds of achieving controlled evening home SBP decreased by 31% (OR: 0.69, 95% CI: 0.51–0.94; p = 0.018), and the odds of achieving overall evening home BP control decreased by 27% (OR: 0.73, 95% CI: 0.54–0.98; p = 0.039). No statistically significant associations were observed between urinary Na/K ratio and morning or evening home DBP control or overall morning home BP control (Figure 1).

In multivariable LMMs accounting for repeated home BP measurements and individual‐level random effects, baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium excretion and urinary Na/K ratio were significantly associated with home SBP levels during follow‐up (Figure 2). Higher baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium excretion was associated with higher morning and evening home SBP levels. Specifically, for each 1‐SD increase in urinary sodium excretion (99.49 mmol), morning home SBP increased by 2.57 mmHg (95% CI: 0.74–4.39; p = 0.006), and evening home SBP increased by 2.11 mmHg (95% CI: 0.26–3.95; p = 0.025). Higher baseline urinary Na/K ratio was also associated with higher home SBP levels; for each 1‐SD increase in urinary Na/K ratio (1.98 units), morning home SBP increased by 1.53 mmHg (95% CI: 0.00–3.06; p = 0.049), and evening home SBP increased by 1.50 mmHg (95% CI: 0.04–2.96; p = 0.044). Baseline 24‐hour urinary potassium excretion was not significantly associated with home SBP levels. No statistically significant associations were observed between baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium, potassium excretion, or urinary Na/K ratio and morning or evening home DBP levels.

A total of 1340 office BP records were included in multivariable GLMMs. As shown in Figure 3, for each 1‐SD increase in baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium excretion (101.87 mmol), the odds of achieving controlled office SBP decreased by 25% (OR: 0.75, 95% CI: 0.56–1.00; p = 0.049). No significant associations were observed between urinary sodium excretion and office DBP control or overall office BP control. In contrast, higher baseline 24‐hour urinary potassium excretion was positively associated with office BP control. For each 1‐SD increase in urinary potassium excretion (21.91 mmol), the odds of achieving controlled office SBP (OR: 1.33, 95% CI: 1.05–1.69; p = 0.020) and overall office BP control (OR: 1.47, 95% CI: 1.14–1.88; p = 0.003) were significantly higher, whereas the association with office DBP control did not reach statistical significance. A higher baseline urinary Na/K ratio (per 1‐SD increase of 2.05 units) was associated with a lower odd of achieving overall office BP control (OR: 0.76, 95% CI: 0.59–0.99; p = 0.043). However, the associations between urinary Na/K ratio and office SBP control or office DBP control did not reach statistical significance.

Consistent associations were observed when office BP parameters were analyzed as a continuous outcome (Figure S2). Baseline 24‐hour urinary sodium excretion was associated with higher office SBP levels during follow‐up. Specifically, for each 1‐SD increase in urinary sodium excretion (101.87 mmol), office SBP increased by 1.80 mmHg (95% CI: 0.01–3.60; p = 0.049). No statistically significant association was observed between urinary sodium excretion and office DBP levels. Similarly, no statistically significant associations were observed between baseline 24‐hour urinary potassium excretion or the urinary Na/K ratio and office BP levels.

Figure S3 and S4 present the subgroup analyses results for morning and evening home BP control, respectively. Overall, no statistically significant interactions were observed between baseline 24‐hour urinary electrolyte excretion and most subgroup variables (all p values for interaction > 0.05). The inverse associations of urinary sodium excretion and urinary Na/K ratio with home SBP control were generally consistent across subgroups. Likewise, the association between a higher urinary Na/K ratio and poorer overall evening home BP control remained consistent across all subgroups.

Results from all sensitivity analyses were consistent with the primary findings. The inverse associations of urinary sodium excretion and urinary Na/K ratio with home SBP control, as well as the association between a higher Na/K ratio and poorer overall evening home BP control, persisted in complete‐case analyses (Table S3), after exclusion of the first home BP measurement (Table S4), and after replacing baseline office BP with the first home BP measurement as a covariate (Table S5). Analyses using truncated follow‐up windows of 6, 12, 24, and 36 months (Tables S6–S9) also yielded consistent results.

## 讨论 / Discussion

The present study suggests that dietary sodium intake and sodium‐potassium balance play an important role in the BP management among patients with aTRH. A small difference in dietary pattern may contribute to critical differences in home BP control during longitudinal follow‐up.

Although evidence regarding the impact of dietary sodium intake on BP in patients with aTRH is limited [31], our findings are consistent with existing evidence derived from the general hypertensive population [16, 23, 32], demonstrating an inverse association between higher sodium intake and home BP control and BP levels. Mechanistically, excessive dietary sodium intake represents a key contributor to treatment resistance in patients with aTRH, who are known to exhibit pronounced salt sensitivity [33, 34, 35, 36]. Several short‐term interventional studies have highlighted the importance of sodium restriction for office BP management in patients with RH [25, 37]. Accordingly, dietary sodium restriction has been recommended as an essential therapeutic strategy for patients with RH [1, 38, 39]. However, most previous studies have relied on single office BP measurements or short‐term ambulatory BP monitoring (e.g., 24 h) [40, 41]. In contrast, our study used repeatedly measured home BP during longitudinal follow‐up as the outcome and applied mixed‐effects models to account for within‐individual changes over time. This approach allowed us to demonstrate that excessive sodium intake may be an important contributor to recurrent fluctuations and poor home BP control in patients with aTRH. By addressing these methodological limitations of prior studies, our findings provide real‐world evidence supporting sodium restriction for out‐of‐office BP management in this high‐risk population. Moreover, compared with office BP, home BP showed stronger associations with baseline urinary electrolyte excretion, which may reflect the higher reliability, lower measurement variability, and closer relationship with habitual sodium intake achieved through standardized HBPM.

Notably, the urinary Na/K ratio showed a more consistent association with home BP control across multiple BP parameters (including overall evening home BP control) compared with urinary sodium or potassium excretion alone. This finding is consistent with existing evidence [42]. From a pathophysiological insight, sodium and potassium exert opposing effects on peripheral vascular resistance, fluid balance, and BP regulation. Sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion are closely linked, and high sodium intake increases potassium excretion, particularly when sodium reabsorption is enhanced in the cortical collecting duct, as commonly observed in primary hypertension [15]. Conversely, increased potassium intake promotes urinary sodium excretion. Short‐term potassium depletion induces intracellular acidosis, which enhances the expression and activity of sodium‐hydrogen exchanger type 3 (NHE‐3) in the proximal tubule, leading to increased renal sodium reabsorption, whereas long‐term potassium depletion augments renal sodium pump activity, again resulting in abnormal sodium retention. In addition, sodium‐potassium homeostasis plays an important role in endothelium‐dependent vasodilation [15, 43, 44]. Therefore, the urinary Na/K ratio may more directly reflect the integrated effects of dietary patterns on BP regulation. In two cohorts of patients with CKD [45, 46], urinary Na/K ratio was significantly associated with aTRH, whereas urinary sodium or potassium excretion alone was not. The urinary Na/K ratio has also been shown to provide additional predictive value for identifying individuals at high risk of developing aTRH [46]. It should be noted that both of these studies were based on OBPM. Our study extends these findings by demonstrating an association between 24‐hour urinary Na/K ratio and home BP control in patients with aTRH, using out‐of‐office BP measurements. Collectively, these findings suggest that the balance between sodium and potassium intake may be more effective for cardiovascular risk stratification than consideration of either electrolyte alone. By comparison, urinary potassium excretion alone did not show a consistent association with home BP control or BP levels, which may be influenced by antihypertensive treatment, particularly the effects of RASi, MRA and diuretics. This finding may also reflect residual confounding related to extrarenal potassium excretion pathways, such as gastrointestinal elimination [16]. Furthermore, no significant associations were observed between urinary sodium, potassium excretion, or the Na/K ratio and home DBP control or levels. This finding may reflect differences in the pathophysiological sensitivity of SBP and DBP to sodium‐related fluid retention and plasma volume expansion [47, 48].

This study has several strengths. First, the present investigation specifically targeted patients with aTRH, a high‐risk subgroup that has been underrepresented in previous researches. By leveraging longitudinal follow‐up data, we extend predominantly cross‐sectional evidence and, to our knowledge, are the first to link baseline urinary electrolyte excretion to subsequent home BP control in this population. Second, home BP parameters were defined as the primary outcome, which more directly reflects BP levels under real‐life conditions and reduces the influence of the white‐coat effect. Third, 24‐hour urinary electrolyte measurement, the most accurate method available, was employed to assess daily dietary sodium and potassium intake. This approach provides a more precise estimation than methods based on spot urine samples [49], thereby maximizing the reliability of our findings. Fourth, rather than adjusting only for the baseline number of antihypertensive medications as commonly done in prior studies, we modeled time‐varying antihypertensive medication classes based on the most recent prescription before each home BP measurement, better reflecting dynamic regimen changes. This approach also highlighted the significant associations of thiazide diuretics and MRA with home BP control (data not shown). These agents exert important antihypertensive effects by inhibiting sodium reabsorption and promoting urinary sodium excretion [36, 50], mechanisms that are highly consistent with the findings of the present study and further highlight the central role of sodium restriction in BP management of aTRH.

The interpretation of the present findings should be considered in light of several limitations. First, although 24‐hour urinary sodium excretion is regarded as the gold standard for assessing dietary sodium intake, incomplete urine collection remains a major challenge and may lead to underestimation of sodium intake. In this study, several measures were implemented to minimize incomplete collection: all participants received both oral and written instructions detailing the standardized 24‐hour urine collection procedure, and participants with a 24‐hour urine volume <1000 mL were excluded from the study to reduce the likelihood of including substantially incomplete specimens. However, despite these measures, urinary creatinine excretion was not measured to formally assess the completeness of the 24‐hour collection, which represents a limitation. Without creatinine‐based verification, some degree of collection error cannot be entirely excluded, and this may have introduced non‐differential measurement error in the exposure variables, potentially attenuating the observed associations. Second, urinary electrolyte excretion was assessed only at baseline and therefore could not capture dynamic changes in dietary sodium and potassium intake during follow‐up. Third, although we adjusted for a wide range of potential confounders, residual confounding cannot be entirely excluded. Fourth, given the observational nature of the study, no causal relationship can be inferred. Finally, the effects of RASi, diuretics, and MRA on renal sodium and potassium excretion cannot be completely excluded.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Our findings suggest that dietary sodium restriction and maintenance of a balanced sodium‐to‐potassium intake may represent important complementary strategies, besides pharmacologic therapy, in achieving target home BP control among patients with aTRH. Given the high prevalence of high sodium‐low potassium dietary patterns among hypertensive patients, modification of habitual dietary practices may represent an important component of BP management in aTRH. Large‐scale randomized controlled trials are warranted to further clarify the effects of this specific dietary intervention pattern on BP parameters in this population.
