## English Abstract

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP) contribute significantly to maternal and neonatal morbidity. Precision medicine emphasizes individualized risk prediction, while nursing interventions (NIs) address behavioral and psychosocial care. This study evaluated the clinical benefit of a structured NI compared to routine prenatal care in reducing complications among women diagnosed with HDP. This retrospective quasi-experimental study was conducted at Peking University Shenzhen Hospital. Participants were primigravida Chinese women (median age 25 years) with a confirmed diagnosis of HDP. Of 3260 screened women, 265 met the inclusion criteria: gestational hypertension (48%), preeclampsia (31%), or superimposed preeclampsia (21%). Risk was initially assessed via self-reported questionnaires starting at 15 gestational weeks until 7 days postpartum. Participants were allocated to either an NI (Women with hypertensive disorders of pregnancy received prenatal care and NIs sessions until 7 days postpartum [NI cohort], n = 122), receiving structured education and behavioral sessions, or a routine prenatal care (PR cohort, n = 143). This investigation identifies a significant association between a protocolized NI and improved obstetric trajectories, particularly in younger women and those with a higher body mass index (BMI). While these associative findings are encouraging, the non-randomized, retrospective nature of the study necessitates caution, and the results should be viewed as hypothesis-generating rather than evidence of a definitive causal effect. The study population consisted of primigravida women with a median prepregnancy BMI of 23.8 kg/m2. The NI was independently associated with significantly higher odds of spontaneous vaginal delivery (adjusted odds ratios: 2.15; 95% confidence interval: 1.84–2.51; P < .001). The intervention was independently associated with a significant reduction in the risk of neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) admissions (adjusted odds ratios: 0.28; 95% confidence interval: 0.11–0.72; P = .008). Raw delivery outcomes also favored the NI cohort regarding normal delivery rates (45% vs 17%, P < .0001) and labor duration (82% vs 49%, P < .0001). Maternal complications were comparable, and nausea was significantly lower in the NI group (18% vs 29%, P = .032). Secondary neonatal outcomes demonstrated significant reductions in low birth weight (9% vs 15%, P = .048) and respiratory distress syndrome (3% vs 9%, P = .021). A NI was most effective for women aged ≤25 years and those with a BMI>24 kg/m2.

## 背景 / Background

Precision medicine aims to utilize quantitative models to assess disease risk, facilitating proactive clinical prevention strategies.[1,2] The integration of large-scale electronic health records (EHRs) has enhanced the scalability of predictive algorithms, allowing for the extraction of research-quality phenotypes that capture clinical variability.[3,4] Large cohort studies, such as the Tohoku Medical Megabank Project, have successfully demonstrated the utility of these records in estimating risks for noncommunicable diseases by integrating environmental and clinical factors.[5,6]

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP), encompassing ICD-10 codes O13–O16, remain a leading global cause of maternal morbidity and mortality.[7,8] In Asian populations, established risk factors include nulliparity, obesity (body mass index [BMI] > 24 kg/m2), and psychosocial stress.[9,10] Despite progress in early identification through maternal and paternal polygenic risk scores or self-reported questionnaires,[2,9] a significant implementation gap remains in translating these risk signals into effective clinical management. While pharmacological prevention is well-documented, the association between protocolized, non-pharmacological NIs and obstetric outcomes remains under-explored.[10]

Recent cross-sectional and cohort analyses have linked HDP to long-term hypertension and significant pregnancy complications.[9,11] Disparities in prenatal care adherence further complicate the management of high-risk populations.[12] However, nursing-focused investigations suggest that non-treatment interventions, including prenatal consultation, maternal case management, and home visits, improve self-efficacy and maternal outcomes in women with preeclampsia.[10,13–15] Furthermore, digital-based NIs have enhanced health behaviors in chronic conditions, suggesting potential applicability to HDP management.[16,17] Collectively, these findings establish a dual foundation: predictive modeling for risk identification and structured nursing care for improved outcomes.[18,19]

Current precision medicine initiatives emphasize “closed-loop” systems where risk stratification is immediately followed by tailored clinical actions.[1,20] This study addresses the implementation gap by evaluating a clinical protocol that integrates early-gestation modeling with a structured nursing framework. The scientific rationale rests on the convergence of nursing science and predictive modeling to address the multifactorial behavioral and psychosocial dimensions of HDP.[1,8,21]

The primary objective of this investigation was to quantify the association between a structured NI protocol and maternal/neonatal outcomes. While risk variables were collected via questionnaire, they served to characterize the cohort profile rather than to establish a new diagnostic tool. We hypothesized that the women with hypertensive disorders of pregnancy who received prenatal care and NIs sessions until 7 days postpartum (NI cohort) would demonstrate a significant association with superior delivery outcomes and reduced neonatal morbidity compared to routine prenatal care (PR).

## 方法 / Methods

The authors designed the study protocol, which was approved by the Peking University Shenzhen Medical Ethics Review Board (Approval No. 035, dated April 25, 2023). The study adhered to the laws of China and the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki (2008 revision). As this was a retrospective quasi-experimental study, trial registration, informed consent, and consent to publication were waived by the Ethics Review Board.

This investigation utilized a retrospective quasi-experimental design to compare outcomes between patients receiving protocolized NIs and those receiving routine care, conducted at Peking University Shenzhen Hospital, Futian District, Shenzhen, Guangdong, China, between April 28, 2023, and August 1, 2024. Retrospective quasi-experimental designs using medical records have been widely employed in precision medicine and predictive modeling studies to evaluate disease risks and intervention outcomes.[3–5] Due to the retrospective nature of the data extraction from EHRs, allocation concealment and participant blinding were not applicable. However, to ensure objective outcome assessment, investigators responsible for data analysis remained independent of the clinical intervention team. The study utilized a retrospective quasi-experimental design with time-based allocation. This approach, while pragmatic in a clinical setting, introduces potential temporal confounding, as changes in hospital protocols over time may influence maternal outcomes independently of the specific NI.

Eligible participants were women with completed self-reported questionnaires documented in medical records. Exclusion criteria included incomplete questionnaire data, absence of delivery records, preexisting hypertension at pregnancy onset, and chronic hypertension with superimposed preeclampsia. These criteria align with prior studies on HDP, which emphasize the importance of excluding chronic hypertensive cases to ensure accurate classification of gestational hypertension and preeclampsia.[6,22,23] The study population included women identified with HDP or high-risk hypertensive traits according to the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) 2019 guidelines. All interventions were initiated at a median gestational age of 18.4 ± 2.1 weeks. The quantitative model (clinical benefit score (CBS)) was utilized as an evaluative tool to compare the NI and PR cohorts, not as a prospective identification algorithm.

Early risk identification was conducted using a structured self-reported questionnaire administered at the first prenatal visit (approx. 15 weeks). The tool consists of 10 clinical and demographic variables: maternal age, prepregnancy BMI, parity, history of hypertension, family history of preeclampsia, smoking status, physical activity level, and dietary habits. Each item is weighted based on a predetermined algorithm to generate an initial risk profile.

Diagnostic criteria for HDP were based on the ACOG, 2019 guidelines.[8]

Gestational hypertension: Systolic blood pressure (BP)≥140 mm Hg or diastolic BP ≥90 mm Hg after 20 weeks of gestation in the absence of proteinuria.[8]

Preeclampsia: Systolic BP ≥140 mm Hg or diastolic BP≥90 mm Hg after 20 weeks of gestation with proteinuria (≥1+) and absence of nephritis.[8]

Superimposed preeclampsia: Hypertension present before 20 weeks of gestation with proteinuria (≥1+) and absence of nephritis.[8]

For prevalence estimation of HDP, we assumed an expected proportion p=0.10</mathgraphic> based on recent Chinese epidemiology and cross-population data, targeted absolute precision d=0.05, and set two-sided α=0.05, yielding the single-proportion sample size formula n=Z1−α/22p(1−p)d2, with an inflation of 10–15% to accommodate anticipated missingness in retrospective records[22,23]; for between-cohort comparisons (NI cohort vs routine prenatal care), we powered two-sided tests at α=0.05 and 1−β=0.80 to detect conservative differences in binary outcomes (composite neonatal complications, hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, and low platelet count, abruption) guided by recent quasi-experimental and meta-analytic evidence, using the two-proportion framework $n=(Z1−α/22p(1−p)+Z1−βp1(1−p1)+p2(1−p2))2(p1−p2)2$ and applying exact tests in sensitivity analyses for rare events[10,14]; for early-pregnancy predictive modeling (logistic regression and machine learning classifiers), adequacy was determined by ensuring sufficient outcome events for discrimination (area under curve), calibration (slope, intercept, curves), overall accuracy (Brier score), and clinical utility (decision-curve analysis) with internal validation via bootstrap or nested cross-validation in line with contemporary EHRs/phenotype-based risk modeling standards,[2–4] and model parsimony was prioritized given HDP’s multifactorial etiology and moderate event rates.[2,23]

Women identified with HDP were allocated to either:

NI cohort: The NI followed a standardized Four-Pillar Protocol initiated at gestational week 15 until 7 days postpartum.

Education & Counseling: Bi-weekly 30-minute sessions covering HDP pathophysiology and BP self-management.

Behavioral Modification: Daily monitoring of sodium intake (<6g/day) and prescribed light physical activity (30 minutes/day), recorded in a digital log.

Psychosocial Support: Targeted stress-reduction techniques and anxiety screenings conducted during each session.

Symptom Vigilance: Training on the recognition of “red-flag” symptoms (e.g., scotoma, epigastric pain) to prompt immediate clinical escalation.

To ensure standardization, all NI staff utilized a unified manual. Intervention fidelity was evaluated via the CBS; participants in the NI cohort were required to complete >80% of scheduled sessions to be included in the high-fidelity analysis group.

Routine prenatal care cohort: Received standard prenatal care, initiated at gestational week 15 until 7 days postpartum. This design parallels quasi-experimental and cohort studies evaluating NIs in HDP.[10,15,17]

This investigation follows a retrospective quasi-experimental design. The study evaluates a sequential protocol where a quantitative predictive model was first utilized for risk stratification, followed by the implementation of structured NIs (NI) for identified high-risk patients.

Predictive Model versus Intervention: The quantitative mathematical model, based on early-gestation self-reported questionnaires, was used solely to identify the high-risk cohort. Once identified, the NI cohort (n = 122) received the non-pharmacological therapeutic intervention. The control group (PR) followed standard prenatal care without the integrated predictive-intervention protocol.

Participants were allocated to cohorts based on a time-sequenced quasi-experimental design. The Routine Prenatal Care (PR) cohort (n = 143) consisted of eligible women who received care between April 2023 and November 2023. Following a one-month protocol implementation phase, the NI cohort (n = 122) was enrolled between December 2023 and August 2024. This time-based allocation was utilized to prevent “treatment leakage” between groups within the same clinical setting.

Routine prenatal care included regular checkups by obstetricians, nurses, or midwives, with recommendations for a healthy lifestyle, medical information, and prenatal nutrition. Such care is consistent with international guidelines for maternal health management.[16]

Women in the NI cohort received additional NIs, including home-based comprehensive nursing care, structured training programs, vascular symptom management, case management, music therapy, and clinical nursing pathways, administered for a minimum of 30 minutes daily. Evidence from recent systematic reviews and quasi-experimental studies supports the efficacy of these interventions in improving maternal outcomes in HDP.[10,15,17]

The non‑therapeutic nursing sessions employed in the study consisted of structured, protocolized interventions focused on education, monitoring, psychosocial support, and behavioral reinforcement rather than pharmacologic or invasive treatment. Nurses provided health education on HDP, reinforced adherence to prenatal visits, monitored BP and symptoms, and offered counseling to reduce anxiety and strengthen self‑efficacy, while case management ensured timely escalation if abnormal findings emerged. Obstetricians remained the primary clinical supervisors and furnished medical care whenever diagnostic thresholds were met or complications developed, including initiation of antihypertensive therapy, magnesium sulfate, or delivery interventions as required. Care was contained within the same institution, with no external medical support reported, and therapeutic interventions were indeed administered if preeclampsia manifested later, even in women without initial risk indications, ensuring that preventive nursing care was complemented by physician‑directed treatment when clinically necessary. This integrated approach reflects established nursing care plans for HDP, which emphasize patient education, monitoring, psychosocial support, and collaboration with physicians to optimize maternal and neonatal outcomes.[10]

Primary Outcome: The primary endpoint of this study was the mode of delivery, specifically comparing the incidence of spontaneous vaginal delivery between the NI and PR cohorts.

Secondary Outcomes: Maternal secondary outcomes included labor duration and the presence of pregnancy-induced symptoms (nausea, headache, etc). Neonatal secondary outcomes included neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) admission rates, birth weight, and neonatal complications (respiratory distress syndrome (RDS), jaundice).

Subgroup Analyses: post hoc exploratory analyses were conducted to evaluate the consistency of the primary outcome across maternal age and BMI strata.

These outcomes align with prior studies evaluating maternal and neonatal health in HDP.[11,23]

Inclusion Criteria: Primigravida women, confirmed HDP diagnosis (gestational hypertension, preeclampsia), and completion of the baseline 15-week questionnaire.

Outcome Validity: Primary outcomes (delivery mode, labor duration) were extracted from standardized EHRs with 100% verification by a secondary researcher to ensure data reliability.

Potential selection bias associated with this non-randomized approach was addressed by performing a baseline comparability analysis and utilizing multivariate logistic regression to adjust for temporal and demographic confounders.

Categorical variables are presented as frequencies with percentages. Continuous variables are expressed as mean ± standard deviation if normally distributed, or as median with interquartile range (first quartile value–first quartile value) if non-normally distributed. Normality was assessed using the Kolmogorov–Smirnov method, and the Bartlett test was used to verify the homogeneity of variances for normally distributed data.

Inter-group comparisons for categorical variables were performed using the Chi-square (χ2) test or Fisher’s exact test (including the Fisher-Freeman–Halton test for larger contingency tables). The Mann–Whitney U test was applied for non-normally distributed continuous variables. To identify independent predictors of delivery outcomes and neonatal health, we conducted a multivariate logistic regression analysis.[8,9] The models adjusted for potential confounders, including maternal age, prepregnancy BMI, and parity. Results are presented as adjusted odds ratios (aOR) with 95% Confidence Intervals (CI).[20]

The internal validity of the predictive model and the CBS was assessed through 2 primary metrics. Calibration was evaluated using the Hosmer-Lemeshow goodness-of-fit test, which yielded a nonsignificant result (χ2 = 5.12, P = .745), suggesting no significant deviation between observed and predicted outcomes. Discrimination was assessed via the C-statistic (area under curve), which was 0.82 (95% CI: 0.76–0.88), indicating a high level of discriminative ability within this study population.

Missing data (<5% for primary variables) were addressed using multiple imputation. To evaluate the study’s precision, a power calculation was performed based on the primary outcome (mode of delivery); for an alpha of 0.05, the study sample size provided 87% power to detect the observed difference between the NI and PR groups.

Primary computations were performed using R, Version 4.3.x (R Foundation for Statistical Computing), for resampling validation and regression modeling (packages: pROC, RMS, RMDA), and IBM SPSS Statistics, Version 29, for descriptive and exact tests. Machine learning pipelines for robustness checks were implemented via Python, version 3.11. All results were considered statistically significant at a P-value < .05. Flowcharts were generated using standardized AI-assisted logic tools (https://grabstract.io/graphical-abstracts) and validated against Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology guidelines.

## 结果 / Results

Between April 28, 2023, and August 1, 2024, a total of 3260 pregnant women were screened at Peking University Shenzhen Hospital, Shenzhen, Guangdong, China. After excluding 260 women due to incomplete questionnaires, missing delivery records, or preexisting hypertension, data from 3000 women were included in the final analysis. Of these, 265 women were enrolled in the comparative analysis of non-treatment interventions, with 122 in the NI cohort and 143 in the prenatal care only (PR) cohort (Fig. 1).

The 265 participants were primigravida with a mean age of 24.8 ± 3.2 years. The average prepregnancy BMI was 23.5 kg/m2 for the NI group and 24.2 kg/m2 for the PR group. Baseline characteristics, including gestational age at diagnosis and lifestyle habits, were balanced between the NI and PR cohorts (Table 1). The median gestational age at diagnosis was 18.4 weeks, and no significant differences were observed in prepregnancy BMI or HDP subtypes between the groups (P > .05), ensuring the comparability of the cohorts for the subsequent intervention analysis. The majority of women (96%) were primiparas. HDP was distributed as gestational hypertension (48%), preeclampsia (31%), and superimposed preeclampsia (21%). Other comorbidities included anemia (42%) and diabetes (16%). No statistically significant differences were observed between cohorts across these baseline parameters.

Maternal complications after delivery were largely comparable. Post-obstetric pain (VAS ≥3) occurred in 66% of NI and 73% of PR women (P = .165). Vomiting rates were similar (10% vs 12%, P = .594), while nausea was significantly higher in the PR cohort (29% vs 18%, P = .032).

Neonatal outcomes revealed important differences (Table 2). Stillbirth (1%–2%), neonatal death (1%–2%), and low Apgar scores (2%–3%) were comparable between cohorts. However, NICU admissions were significantly higher in the PR cohort (7% vs 2%, P = .041). Composite adverse neonatal events were also more frequent in the PR cohort (0.14 vs 0.04 events per newborn, P = .004).

To identify independent predictors of delivery success and neonatal safety, a multivariate logistic regression analysis was performed. The model adjusted for potential confounding factors, including maternal age, prepregnancy BMI, and parity, which are established risk determinants for HDP complications.

The results indicated that the NI was the strongest independent factor associated with a normal delivery (aOR: 2.15; 95% CI: 1.84–2.51; P < .001). Conversely, the NI cohort was significantly associated with a reduced risk of NICU admission (aOR: 0.28; 95% CI: 0.11–0.72; P = .008). Notably, a prepregnancy BMI > 24 kg/m2 remained a significant independent risk factor for neonatal complications requiring intensive care (aOR: 1.45; 95% CI: 1.12–1.88; P = .015), validating the use of localized BMI thresholds for this cohort (Table 3).

## 讨论 / Discussion

This investigation demonstrates that a protocolized nursing framework is independently associated with significantly improved obstetric and neonatal outcomes (primary outcome) in a Chinese primigravida cohort. These findings align with the precision medicine paradigm, which advocates for individualized risk stratification using scalable data and clinical analytics.[1,2] By leveraging early-gestation risk profiles (secondary outcome), our approach translates predictive signals into tailored care pathways, paralleling large-scale cohort infrastructures like the Tohoku Medical Megabank Project that integrate clinical records to guide maternal health management.[3–6]

The observed HDP prevalence of 9.0% in our cohort is consistent with recent systematic reviews and meta-analyses of Chinese populations, suggesting that our screening captured clinically relevant risk without inflating false-positive rates.[7,8,22,23] While higher prevalence rates have been reported in heterogeneous global populations, those often reflect broader variability in age, BMI, and parity.[10,11,24] Our findings highlight that once high-risk status is identified, the implementation of structured nursing, including education, counseling, and symptom surveillance, is linked to a 2.15-fold increase in the odds of spontaneous vaginal delivery and a significant reduction in labor duration.[9,10,12]

The superiority of the NI group in maternal outcomes echoes prior research documenting the benefits of structured nursing on maternal self-efficacy and obstetric trajectories.[10,13–15] Mechanistically, these interventions likely modulate psychosocial constructs, such as health locus of control and stress resilience, which influence physiological labor dynamics.[14,16] Furthermore, neonatal benefits: specifically reduced NICU admissions (2% in NI vs 7% in PR) and lower rates of RDS, reinforce the value of this integrated care model.[11,14,23] The CBS introduced here serves as an exploratory retrospective metric for measuring intervention fidelity rather than a prospective diagnostic tool.[3,4]

Subgroup analyses revealed that younger mothers (≤25 years) and overweight women (>24 kg/m2) derived the most pronounced associative benefits from the NI protocol. This trend aligns with evidence linking BMI and adherence behaviors to HDP trajectories.[13,20,24] The reduction in neonatal morbidity among these subgroups likely reflects a higher susceptibility to educational reinforcement and behavioral modification, which attenuates fetal stress and placental insufficiency.[15,21] These patterns suggest that tailoring nursing intensity to specific demographic and anthropometric risk strata may maximize clinical gains.[9,18]

This study has several limitations that necessitate a cautious interpretation of the results. First, the retrospective, non-randomized, quasi-experimental design precludes the establishment of definitive causality; our findings represent strong clinical associations rather than direct effects. Although multivariate models adjusted for age and BMI, the possibility of residual confounding from unmeasured factors, such as socioeconomic status or baseline stress, remains.[3,4] Second, the time-based allocation introduces potential temporal confounding, as broader institutional improvements in obstetric technology over the study period may have influenced outcomes.[12,14]

Additionally, the CBS and associated modeling are exploratory and lack external validation in independent cohorts; thus, they should not be used to guide real-time clinical decision-making at this stage.[1,3,4] Finally, the modest sample size and focus on Han Chinese participants may limit the generalizability of the findings to diverse populations or different healthcare infrastructures.[5,6,19] Future research should employ a multi-center prospective three-arm design (standard care, intervention-only, and model-guided intervention) to precisely delineate the added clinical value of the quantitative modeling phase.[9,17,19]

## 结论 / Conclusion

In synthesis, this investigation identifies a significant association between a protocolized framework of structured NI and improved obstetric outcomes. By utilizing self-reported data for initial risk stratification, the study established that non-pharmacological NIs significantly optimize obstetric trajectories. Specifically, the NI cohort achieved a markedly higher rate of normal delivery (45.1% vs 17.5%, P < .001) and a substantial reduction in the risk of NICU admission (aOR: 0.28; 95% CI: 0.11–0.72; P = .008).

These findings highlight that risk stratification is most clinically impactful when paired with structured psychosocial and behavioral support. The implementation of NI was also associated with a 2.15-fold increase in the odds of spontaneous vaginal delivery (95% CI: 1.84–2.51) after adjusting for confounders. While the retrospective quasi-experimental design and demographic homogeneity limit causal inference, the consistency of results, particularly among women aged ≤25 years and those with a BMI>24 kg/m2, suggests that these interventions are robust across different high-risk strata. Future inquiry should prioritize prospective, multi-center trials to evaluate the scalability of these nursing protocols, potentially through digital platforms, to refine intervention strategies and further reduce neonatal morbidity (e.g., RDS reduction from 9.1%–3.3%) across diverse populations.
