## English Abstract

Postoperative cough after bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) is common and can delay recovery. We evaluated whether acupressure at Tiantu (CV22), Chize (LU5), and Taiyuan (LU9), quantified by a flexible pressure sensor, improves anesthesia recovery.

This single-center randomized controlled trial enrolled adults undergoing BAL under general anesthesia with a laryngeal mask airway. Patients were randomized 1:1 to receive acupressure (Group A) or routine PACU care without acupressure (Group C). In Group A, each acupoint was pressed sequentially for 3 min (CV22 → LU5 → LU9) with pressure recorded by a flexible sensor. Primary outcomes were cough severity and frequency; secondary outcomes included PACU time, heart rate, and complications such as tachycardia, hypertension, and stress urinary incontinence.

A total of 120 patients were analyzed (60 in each group). Peak acupressure pressures were 30.2 N for CV22, 35.1 N for LU5, and 25.3 N for LU9, each peaking at 2.0 s. Compared with controls, patients receiving acupressure had a significantly shorter PACU stay (mean difference − 16.0 min, 95% CI − 18.9 to − 13.2), lower cough frequency (− 1.06 times/min, 95% CI − 1.83 to − 0.30), and reduced heart rate (− 9.5 beats/min, 95% CI − 13.7 to − 5.4). The incidence of moderate cough (10.0% vs. 48.3%, RR 0.21, 95% CI 0.09–0.46), severe cough (35.0% vs. 60.0%, RR 0.58, 95% CI 0.39–0.87), tachycardia (8.3% vs. 26.7%, RR 0.31, 95% CI 0.12–0.80), and stress urinary incontinence (6.7% vs. 23.3%, RR 0.29, 95% CI 0.10–0.82) was also significantly lower in the acupressure group. The frequency of sore throat and hemoptysis at six hours did not differ between groups. No adverse events related to acupressure were observed.

Acupressure at CV22, LU5, and LU9, applied with quantified pressure, significantly reduced postoperative cough and related complications and shortened PACU recovery time after BAL.

## 背景 / Background

Bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) is an essential diagnostic and therapeutic tool for respiratory diseases [1, 2]. However, 40%−60% of patients undergoing BAL experience postoperative cough, often accompanied by discomfort such as sputum production and wheezing. These symptoms can lead to complications such as postoperative bleeding, arrhythmia, and stress urinary incontinence [3, 4]. While ultrasound nebulization is commonly used to treat chronic cough, its effectiveness in alleviating acute postoperative cough remains limited [5]. Therefore, managing postoperative cough continues to present a challenge in modern medical care.

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) acupressure points, including Tiantu (CV22), Taiyuan (LU9), Zusanli (ST36), and Chize (LU5), have been reported to relieve cough in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) [6–8]. However, research on the use of acupressure for acute cough following BAL under general anesthesia is sparse. During the anesthesia recovery period, the patient’s sensory system is not fully restored, making it difficult to assess the immediate effectiveness of acupressure. Additionally, quantifying the optimal pressure and duration for acupressure remains an unresolved challenge in TCM research.

The quality of anesthesia recovery is a key indicator of patient outcomes, and postoperative complications are a significant factor influencing recovery [9, 10]. Given the potential benefits of TCM therapies in alleviating postoperative symptoms, this study had two main objectives: (1) to evaluate the effectiveness of acupressure at Tiantu (CV22), Chize (LU5), and Taiyuan (LU9) points in improving the quality of anesthesia recovery by reducing cough and related complications, and (2) to explore the underlying principles of acupressure by quantifying the applied pressure and duration with flexible pressure sensor technology, thereby providing a more objective and standardized approach to this intervention.

## 方法 / Methods

This study was conducted at a tertiary hospital, following the CONSORT guidelines (Fig. 1).The sample size was calculated using G*Power 3.1 software, based on preliminary pilot data comparing postoperative cough frequency between the two groups. Assuming a two-sided α of 0.05, a power (1 − β) of 0.80, an expected mean difference of approximately 1 cough per minute with a standard deviation of 2, the required sample size was 63 patients per group. To allow for an anticipated dropout rate of 20%, we planned to enroll 76 patients per group (152 in total). In practice, 163 patients were screened and 132 patients were randomized. Because very few prior studies have applied flexible pressure sensors in conjunction with acupressure, no external reference was used for the calculation; the estimate relied on our pilot data.

Flow diagram depicting the progression through the phases of a 2-group parallel randomized trial

A total of 163 patients scheduled to undergo flexible bronchoscopic lavage under general anesthesia were assessed for eligibility. Twenty patients did not meet the inclusion criteria, ten declined participation, and two were excluded due to unexpected cancellation of surgery. Finally, 132 patients were randomized in a 1:1 ratio to the acupressure group (Group A) or the control group (Group C). The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the First Affiliated Hospital of Zhengzhou University (Approval No.: 2023-KY-0912-003).This randomized controlled trial was registered with the ISRCTN Registry (ISRCTN18030444).Written informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to their inclusion in the study.

Inclusion criteria: (1) Age between 18 and 64 years old, (2) ASA Class I or II, (3) No cough or mild cough, (4) scheduled to undergo bronchoalveolar lavage under general anesthesia with a laryngeal mask airway. Patients undergoing the procedure without general anesthesia or without a laryngeal mask were not included.

The exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) skin lesions around Tiantu (CV22), Chize (LU5), and Taiyuan (LU9) points, (2) recent massive hemoptysis, (3) diffuse alveolar hemorrhage, (4) untreated active tuberculosis, (5) obvious electrolyte imbalance, (6) Inability to understand communication-related trials or informed consent.

All patients were instructed to fast for 8 h before the procedure. Upon arrival in the operating room, the following parameters were continuously monitored: ECG, blood pressure (BP), SpO2, PETCO2, and bispectral index (BIS). Oxygen was administered via a mask at a flow rate of 6–8 L/min for at least 3 min before the procedure.

“Remifentanil (0.5–1.0 µg/kg), etomidate (0.15–0.2 mg/kg), and rocuronium bromide (0.1 mg/kg) were administered intravenously by the same anesthesiologist, with BIS values maintained between 40 and 60. After induction, a laryngeal mask airway was inserted, and patients were mechanically ventilated with the anesthesia machine. Tidal volume was set to 6–8 ml/kg, and the ventilation frequency was adjusted to 12–20 breaths per minute, with PETCO2 maintained at 35–45 mmHg (1 mmHg = 0.133 kPa).”

During the maintenance phase, propofol was administered intravenously at a rate of 94 − 7 mg·kg-1·h-1. Flexible bronchoscopy lavage was performed by the same respiratory endoscopist, using sterilized normal saline at room temperature. A total of 60–120 ml of saline was injected in stages (20–50 ml per stage), aiming for a total recovery rate of ≥ 30%. After the procedure, the propofol infusion was discontinued, and the patient was transferred to the Post Anesthesia Care Unit (PACU). Upon awakening, PETCO2 was maintained below 45 mmHg, and SpO2 was ≥ 95% on room air. The laryngeal mask was removed, and oxygen was administered via mask at a flow rate of 6–8 L/min.

After disinfecting the acupressure sites, a flexible pressure sensor system (ESPB-01, Zhongke Renhe Technology Co., Ltd., China) was used. The system includes a disposable flexible pressure electrode (50 × 50 mm) and a Bluetooth transmitter (measurement range: 0–3500 pF; baud rate: 9600; transmission frequency: 10 Hz). The device does not provide electrical stimulation but records and transmits real-time pressure data during acupressure (Fig. 2).

Patients were instructed to inhale deeply while the acupressure was applied. The thumb was used to apply pressure to each acupoint, with the pressure increasing to a peak over 2.0 s. Acupressure was applied sequentially in the following order: Tiantu (CV22), Chize (LU5), and Taiyuan (LU9). Each acupoint was pressed for 3 min using the same method, for a total intervention time of 30 min.

Pressure at the acupoints (measured in Newtons, N) and the duration of pressure application (measured in seconds) were recorded. The pressure curve for each acupoint is shown in Fig. 3.

Tiantu (CV22) reached a peak pressure of 30.2 N at 2.0 s.

Chize (LU5) reached a peak pressure of 35.1 N at 2.0 s.

Taiyuan (LU9) reached a peak pressure of 25.3 N at 2.0 s.

Patients in the control group received routine PACU monitoring but no acupressure intervention. If coughing occurred, no specific treatment was given. Patients were observed until cough was relieved to a mild degree or resolved completely, at which point they were transferred from the PACU. All other aspects of PACU care were the same as in Group A.

Complications and adverse events during anesthesia recovery: All patients were closely monitored in the PACU. The anesthesiologist recorded adverse events, including cough severity, dizziness, nausea, vomiting, hypoxemia, throat soreness, hemoptysis, and stress urinary incontinence. Any adverse event was promptly treated according to standard PACU protocols (e.g., oxygen supplementation, antiemetics, supportive care). If stress urinary incontinence occurred, patients were assisted with changing clothes. All AEs were documented for analysis.

Both groups were evaluated with the modified Aldrete score to determine readiness for discharge. Patients were eligible to leave the PACU once their score reached ≥ 9 and their cough severity was classified as none or mild. Discharge was completed in the presence of family members. Before leaving the PACU, all patients completed the Visual Analogue Scale for Anxiety and the General Comfort Questionnaire to assess perioperative anxiety and comfort.

At 6 h after surgery, patients were contacted by telephone to assess the presence of cough, sputum production, or blood-streaked sputum.

The intensity and duration of coughing was recorded. According to the outbreak phase of cough, a series of coughs in each time course is recorded as 1 cough, and the intensity of cough is divided into three levels: mild (1 ~ 2 times/min), moderate (3 ~ 4 times/min), and severe (> 5 times/min). The evaluation was done immediately after extubation, 30 min after removal of the laryngeal mask, and on the transferring out of the PACU.

Data analysis was performed using SPSS 25.0 software. Normally distributed continuous variables were expressed as mean ± standard deviation (± s), with comparisons between groups made using the independent samples t-test. For non-normally distributed data, the rank sum test for two independent samples was applied. Count data were expressed as the number of cases (percentage), with the chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test used for between-group comparisons. Multiple linear regression analysis was performed to identify factors influencing the Visual Analog Scale (VAS) score. Statistical significance was defined as a p-value < 0.05.

## 讨论 / Discussion

Currently, pharmacological treatments for postoperative cough have been shown to improve the quality of anesthesia recovery in patients [11]. Compared to conventional cough and asthma medications, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) acupressure therapy specifically targeting points such as Tiantu (CV22), Chize (LU5), and Taiyuan (LU9) offers a faster onset of action with fewer adverse reactions [12]. Furthermore, acupressure helps reduce pressure on the peripheral skin of the trachea, enhancing patient comfort during recovery [13]. TCM acupressure therapy is also highly versatile, making it suitable for use in patients with liver or kidney insufficiency, with no need for adjustments in the applied pressure [14].

This study evaluated the efficacy of TCM acupressure therapy combined with flexible pressure sensor technology [15], which allowed us to quantify peak therapeutic pressures—30.2 N at Tiantu (CV22), 35.1 N at Chize (LU5), and 25.3 N at Taiyuan (LU9)—measured at the 1.5-second time point. By objectively measuring both the pressure and duration of acupressure, this approach supplements the traditional reliance on patients’ subjective sensations of soreness, numbness, distension, or pain [16], and makes acupressure application more precise and standardized. This contributes to a better understanding of its potential as a complementary and alternative therapy.

The results of this study demonstrate that acupressure effectively reduced the incidence of moderate and severe cough in patients recovering from anesthesia, which is consistent with earlier reports showing that stimulation of Tiantu (CV22), Chize (LU5), and Taiyuan (LU9) can relieve post-infectious cough [17]. Similar benefits of acupressure or related techniques have been reported in patients undergoing thoracic surgery and endoscopic procedures, where stimulation of respiratory-related acupoints was associated with reduced airway complications and improved recovery quality [21–23]. These findings reinforce the potential of acupressure as a non-pharmacological strategy to support anesthesia recovery. Mechanistically, in TCM theory, Tiantu (CV22) is classified as a Ren Mai acupoint [18], while Chize (LU5) and Taiyuan (LU9) belong to the hand Taiyin meridian, associated with lung function. These acupoints are believed to regulate breathing, promote lung function, ease qi flow, and relieve symptoms such as cough and asthma [19]. Additionally, acupressure may exert anti-inflammatory effects through activation of the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway, in which efferent vagus nerve fibers inhibit the release of inflammatory mediators [20]. A recent study further demonstrated that acupoint application at Tiantu (CV22) and related points significantly reduced the incidence of postoperative cough, sputum production, and hoarseness, while also improving patient satisfaction, findings that are consistent with the results of our study [21].

Patients receiving acupressure had significantly shorter dwell times in the PACU, lower incidences of stress urinary incontinence and arrhythmias, and higher comfort scores. These findings suggest that TCM acupressure therapy not only mitigates cough-related complications but also improves overall anesthesia recovery quality. This supports earlier studies suggesting that alleviating postoperative complications like cough can reduce patient anxiety, enhance comfort, and improve recovery outcomes [22]. Previous studies have also reported that weak or low-intensity acupressure and transcutaneous electrical acupoint stimulation can improve postoperative symptoms and patient satisfaction, thereby enhancing recovery quality, which is consistent with our findings [23].

However, this study has some limitations. First, it was a single-center trial conducted at one comprehensive hospital in China, which may limit the generalizability of the findings. Second, future research should examine the combined use of acupressure with pharmacologic cough suppressants to compare their relative and synergistic effects. Such studies could provide more comprehensive evidence on the role of acupressure as part of an integrated approach to managing postoperative cough.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In conclusion, acupressure is an effective adjunctive method for relieving cough during anesthesia recovery in patients undergoing BAL. It reduces the need for medication to manage cough, enhances patient comfort, and is safe, with no associated risks. The therapy also shortens the duration of recovery in the PACU and lowers the incidence of common postoperative complications such as stress urinary incontinence and tachycardia. As a non-invasive and low-risk intervention, acupressure offers a promising alternative to traditional treatments for managing postoperative cough.
