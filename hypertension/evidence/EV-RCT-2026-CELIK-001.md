## English Abstract

Ramadan fasting involves prolonged daily abstinence from food and water and may affect blood pressure (BP) regulation, particularly in individuals with chronic conditions. Evidence focusing specifically on ambulatory BP characteristics during Ramadan remains limited. This study evaluated 24‐hour ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM) parameters obtained during Ramadan in fasting and non‐fasting individuals. In this prospective cross‐sectional study, 231 adults underwent 24‐hour ABPM during Ramadan between 2021 and 2023. Participants were classified as fasting or non‐fasting and as hypertensive or non‐hypertensive. Demographic characteristics, comorbidities, laboratory findings, and echocardiographic parameters were recorded. Ambulatory BP values were analyzed for 24‐hour, daytime, and nighttime periods. Of the participants, 117 were fasting and 114 were non‐fasting. Baseline demographic characteristics and cardiovascular comorbidities were similar between groups, although diabetes mellitus (DM) and chronic kidney disease (CKD) were more frequent in non‐fasting individuals. No significant differences were observed in 24‐hour or daytime systolic and diastolic BP values. Mean nighttime BP was lower in fasting participants (p = 0.032). Echocardiographic parameters were comparable. Fasting individuals had higher serum albumin levels (p = 0.027) and lower neutrophil counts (p = 0.030), while other biochemical markers did not differ significantly. Ramadan fasting was not associated with adverse ambulatory BP changes in hypertensive or normotensive individuals and may be considered safe in appropriately selected patients with well‐controlled hypertension (HT).

## 背景 / Background

Fasting during Ramadan is one of the five core pillars of Islam and is mandatory for all healthy adult Muslims. During this sacred month, individuals refrain from food, drink, smoking, and sexual activity from sunrise (suhoor) to sunset (iftar). This daily fasting routine involves abstaining from both food and water during daylight hours, then eating freely at night for one month [1, 2].

The fasting period can last up to 18 h during the summer, and in hot or humid conditions, individuals may face dehydration and overheating [3, 4]. Later in life, as chronic diseases become more common, fasting during Ramadan can pose certain health risks. Although Islamic teachings exempt those whose health might be seriously harmed by fasting, especially frail older adults and patients with severe acute or chronic illnesses such as recent heart attacks, advanced heart failure, poorly controlled arrhythmias, severe valvular heart disease, or severe pulmonary hypertension (HT), many Muslim patients remain highly motivated to continue this lifelong spiritual practice [5, 6]. For patients who wish to fast, suitable medical adjustments might include personalized reviews of cardiovascular medications, scheduling drug doses for suhoor and iftar, opting for once‐daily or longer‐acting formulations when possible, and carefully adjusting diuretic therapy based on clinical condition and tolerance [7].

Alterations in sleep patterns, medication timing and dosage, physical activity, and the repeated fasting–refeeding cycles may affect blood pressure (BP) regulation in hypertensive patients during Ramadan [8]. Several studies suggest that fasting during Ramadan does not harm overall health, even in patients on dialysis, and that BP levels tend to remain stable [9, 10]. Each year, physicians must assess the ability of high cardiovascular risk patients to fast, determine appropriate therapeutic adjustments, and plan suitable monitoring strategies during the fasting period [11].

Instead of assessing the physiological effects of fasting itself, this study aimed to analyze 24‐hour ambulatory BP profiles collected during Ramadan among individuals who were fasting and those who were not fasting. The primary objective was to compare 24‐hour ambulatory BP patterns, including daytime, nighttime, and 24‐hour average BP values, between fasting and non‐fasting participants during Ramadan. The secondary objectives were to compare biochemical parameters and echocardiographic findings between the two groups and to examine these differences based on HT status. Most previous studies compared the same patients before and during Ramadan; in contrast, our three‐year cross‐sectional design focused solely on measurements taken during Ramadan, enabling an assessment of BP characteristics across different subgroups under real‐life fasting conditions.

## 方法 / Methods

This prospective, observational, cross‐sectional study was conducted at Necmettin Erbakan University Medical Hospital during the Ramadan periods of 2021–2023. A total of 231 participants underwent 24‐hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM).

Eligible participants were adults over 18 years old who were either fasting or not fasting during the 24‐hour ABPM period. Both individuals with primary HT and normotensive participants were included. Patients younger than 18, those with secondary HT, a history of cerebrovascular events, malignancy, or decompensated heart failure, as well as those with incomplete clinical data or without written informed consent, were excluded.

Participants were first classified into fasting and non‐fasting groups based on their Ramadan fasting status. Subgroup analyses were then conducted according to HT status. Each participant underwent a personal interview and clinical examination, during which demographic and clinical data—including age, sex, sleep duration, comorbidities, treatment, and lifestyle factors—were recorded.

Twenty‐four‐hour ABPM was conducted using an automated device set to take measurements every 15 min during the day (06:00–22:00) and every 30 min at night (22:00–06:00). These measurements were analyzed for 24‐hour, daytime, and nighttime periods, including systolic, diastolic, and mean BP values. According to current HT guidelines, elevated ambulatory BP was defined as a mean 24‐hour BP ≥130/80 mmHg, a mean daytime BP ≥135/85 mmHg, and a mean nighttime BP ≥120/70 mmHg [12, 13].

Laboratory tests included blood glucose, glycosylated hemoglobin (HbA1c), serum electrolytes, creatinine, hemoglobin, total cholesterol, low‐density lipoprotein (LDL), high‐density lipoprotein (HDL), and triglycerides (TG). All participants also underwent transthoracic echocardiography, including assessment of left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) and ventricular wall thickness, to evaluate cardiac systolic function and possible target‐organ involvement.

All participants provided written informed consent. The study protocol received approval from the Ethics Committee of Necmettin Erbakan University, Meram Faculty of Medicine (Approval No: 2025/6131). No funding supported this research.

## 讨论 / Discussion

In this study, no significant difference was found between fasting and non‐fasting groups among hypertensive patients regarding BP control. However, patients with DM and CKD were more likely to avoid fasting. Although fasting during Ramadan is a key religious duty for Muslims, Islamic teachings allow exemptions for individuals with acute or chronic illnesses that could endanger their health. Our findings indicate that well‐controlled HT is not a major obstacle to fasting, and many Muslim patients with this condition continue to fast safely during Ramadan.

Unlike most studies in the literature, which commonly compare BP profiles of the same individuals before and during Ramadan, our study evaluated 24‐hour ABPM conducted exclusively during Ramadan over a three‐year period. This design allowed us to assess BP characteristics in real‐world conditions and among different subgroups during the fasting month. Although the lack of pre‐Ramadan measurements limits direct intraindividual comparison, this approach offers a unique perspective by focusing solely on the Ramadan period and reducing seasonal variations that could affect BP.

Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics, including age, sex, HT, coronary artery disease, and congestive heart failure, did not differ significantly between fasting and non‐fasting groups. Notably, patients with DM and CKD were significantly more likely to abstain from fasting. Although the International Diabetes Federation–Diabetes and Ramadan International Alliance (IDF–DAR) guidelines recommend risk stratification for diabetic individuals who intend to fast, many patients with DM in our cohort ultimately chose not to fast [14]. A similar trend was observed among CKD patients, despite literature suggesting no significant differences in adverse outcomes—such as acute kidney injury, hypoglycemia, or cardiovascular events—between fasting and non‐fasting CKD populations [15].

A meta‐analysis of 33 studies involving 3213 participants reported that Ramadan fasting was linked to reductions in systolic and diastolic BP among hypertensive individuals [16]. In our study, the mean and daytime 24‐hour systolic, diastolic, and average BP values did not significantly differ between fasting and non‐fasting participants, either in the overall cohort or within hypertensive and non‐hypertensive subgroups. However, all values tended to be lower in the fasting group. Notably, only the mean nighttime average BP was significantly lower among fasting participants.

Erdem et al. compared hypertensive individuals before and during Ramadan and similarly reported no significant differences in mean and daytime 24‐hour systolic, diastolic, or average BP. They also observed a significant reduction in mean nighttime BP during the fasting period, which is consistent with our findings [17]. The lower nighttime average BP observed among fasting participants may be partly related to Ramadan‐associated changes in antihypertensive medication timing, with treatment often shifted to suhoor and iftar, as well as to concurrent changes in sleep–wake patterns, nocturnal food and fluid intake, and overall circadian behavior during the fasting month.

Other studies have also shown that Ramadan fasting does not lead to a statistically significant overall reduction in BP [18, 19, 20, 21]. Tezcan and Büyükterzi assessed newly diagnosed hypertensive patients before and after Ramadan and found no significant differences between fasting and non‐fasting groups, further supporting the idea that fasting does not significantly change BP levels [22]. Additionally, Fakhrzadeh et al. reported no significant changes in the BP of healthy volunteers before and during Ramadan, confirming our observations among non‐hypertensive participants [23].

Beyond BP parameters, our study also uncovered differences in inflammation‐related biomarkers. Contrary to earlier reports, serum albumin levels were significantly higher in the fasting group [24, 25]. Similarly, consistent with our findings, Yousef et al. reported lower neutrophil counts among fasting individuals [26]. A recent meta‐analysis showed that Ramadan intermittent fasting may decrease inflammatory and oxidative stress markers [27]. Since albumin is a negative acute‐phase reactant and neutrophils are positive acute‐phase reactants, the higher albumin and lower neutrophil levels seen in our fasting participants may indicate a reduction in systemic inflammation.

In this three‐year analysis of 24‐hour ABPM profiles during Ramadan, we found no significant differences in BP control between fasting and non‐fasting individuals, including those with HT. Although patients with DM and CKD were less likely to fast, fasting appeared safe for individuals with well‐controlled HT. The significantly higher albumin levels and lower neutrophil counts observed in fasting participants suggest a potential reduction in systemic inflammation associated with Ramadan fasting. These findings support the safety of fasting in appropriately selected patients and add to the growing evidence that Ramadan fasting does not adversely affect BP regulation.

This study has several limitations. First, 24‐hour ABPM measurements were taken only during Ramadan, and the lack of pre‐Ramadan data prevented direct within‐subject comparisons. Second, although data were collected over three consecutive years, the study was conducted at a single center, which may limit how broadly the findings apply. Additionally, detailed and standardized information on daily antihypertensive medication load was not available for all participants, restricting further analysis of their potential influence on ABPM results. Lastly, lifestyle factors such as dietary habits were not formally evaluated, which may have influenced BP and inflammatory markers.
