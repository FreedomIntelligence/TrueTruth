## English Abstract

Arterial hypertension is a global issue and achieving the blood pressure control is a challenge. It has been suggested that telemonitoring benefits these patients but there is a lack of research evaluating tools to assist doctors in caring for patients with arterial hypertension. Herein, we detail a protocol for a single-center, randomized, open-label study with a control group to develop a blood pressure monitoring system with a built-in algorithm supporting the physician’s decisions.

Two hundred patients aged 18–80 with a confirmed diagnosis of arterial hypertension and uncontrolled blood pressure during the screening visit will be recruited for the study. Patients with suspected or diagnosed secondary hypertension and pregnant/breast-feeding women will be excluded. In the initial study phase, a system will be developed for remote blood pressure monitoring, recording measurements, and detecting situations requiring medical assessment and potential intervention to identify patients needing urgent medical attention through innovative analysis of blood pressure measurements and co-morbidity data. Next, telemetric and algorithm-supported care will be compared to the standard care of hypertensive patients. Patients will be randomized 1:1 to each group and be observed for 6 months. Pharmacotherapy will be modified, office blood pressure, home blood pressure, and 24-h ambulatory blood pressure will be measured, and the presence of organ complications will be checked during visits.

The primary endpoints include the time from study enrollment to the day of achieving blood pressure control and achieving blood pressure control within 6 months. Secondary endpoints involve some other parameters of blood pressure control and the costs analyses. The results are expected after 2026 and are anticipated to increase patient-to-physician ratios, allow the remote identification of urgent cases, reduce outpatient appointments, and achieve faster blood pressure control through telemetric observation with the algorithm compared to standard care.

## 背景 / Background

Arterial hypertension (AH) is a significant global epidemiological and social problem, typically involving a persistent elevation of blood pressure due to homeostatic dysregulation. AH affects 20% to 50% of the adult population in developed countries [1] and is a recognized risk factor for the development of cardiovascular diseases such as coronary heart disease, heart failure, stroke, and kidney failure [2–4] with associated complications causing approximately 7% of deaths. In Poland, about 9 million people (31% of adults) suffer from AH, with its prevalence increasing to over 75% in those aged 65 and older. Moreover, one in three adults is unaware of their hypertension with only one in eight patients achieving blood pressure control [5]. Furthermore, most Poles are unaware of the optimal blood pressure targets and possible complications of hypertension, influencing patient compliance during treatment [6].

The recent development of telemedicine, an approach to patient care that involves transmitting data measured in the patient’s home to a database accessible to their doctor, has reduced in-person visits for AH management, with the 3-month telemetric monitoring of patients with AH resulting in more patients achieving blood pressure control (56% vs. 40%) [7]. In another larger study, patients with telemetrically observed blood pressure achieved a decrease in systolic blood pressure of 22 mmHg compared to 7 mmHg in the control group [8]. However, the widely used “smartwatch” devices are unvalidated methods of measuring blood pressure and such measurements may differ significantly from those obtained with traditional devices; hence, they should not be considered when making therapeutic decisions [9]. Currently, no studies have summarized expert knowledge in the field of AH or evaluated heterogeneous sources and data structures, statistical analysis, and the selection and integration of appropriate prognostic techniques to recommend diagnostic or therapeutic decisions to physicians. Our telemetry and algorithm-assisted care approach can benefit by reducing the number of visits and also by expediting them when needed. Additionally, therapeutic recommendations can reduce the time required for physician decisions.

The SOT-ART-NT study is a single-center, randomized, open trial that will compare standard medical care for patients with AH, based on regular visits to the hypertension clinic (control group), and remote patient care supported by a designed teleinformatic system to assist and support the physician (study group). This is a non-inferiority trial designed to assess whether the algorithm-supported telemedical care is not clinically worse than standard care in achieving blood pressure control. ## 讨论 / Discussion

This study aims to develop a blood pressure monitoring system with a built-in algorithm supporting the physician’s decisions. Market analysis indicates that some solutions support remote blood pressure monitoring but there is a lack of a system that autonomously supports the doctor in decision-making based on arterial pressure measurements and other patient medical information [7–9].

Standard specialized care for patients with AH, according to the guidelines of the Polish Society of Hypertension, requires at least four in-person visits per year [10]. The number of visits could be reduced to the necessary minimum by introducing a telemedical care system, with the eventual goal of reducing to one in-person visit per year while maintaining at least a comparable level of blood pressure control in patients [7–9]. The number of patients with AH is constantly increasing, and as studies show, the percentage of proper blood pressure control remains low [1–6]; therefore, the healthcare system for patients with AH needs to be reorganized to ensure good quality care for all patients.

The process of diagnosing and treating hypertension is usually initiated by the patient, who, after noticing disturbing symptoms, schedules a visit to a specialist doctor. A significant weakness of this process is that the symptoms observed by the patient are associated with the consequences of hypertension, so a consultation with a specialist is often delayed from the perspective of full treatment effectiveness.

Telemedical care can improve very poor blood pressure control [7–9]. Additionally, algorithms will be developed based on multiple data such as current blood pressure measurements, cardiovascular risk, and coexisting diseases to support physicians’ diagnostic and therapeutic decisions regarding patients under their care. The developed system will help physicians to react quickly and make optimal decisions, as well as allow for the supervision of a relatively large population of patients, which should significantly impact cost reduction.

The envisioned monitoring model is based on informing the patient about the need to consult a physician even before visible/perceptible symptoms of the disease occur. By acquiring data through telemedicine, the healthcare professional plays a proactive role in identifying anomalies and potential cases of diseases in practice. In an organizational sense, the project’s implementation will change the relationship between the patient and the medical facility. The introduction of intelligent solutions into diagnostic and treatment processes using simple and widely used devices (blood pressure monitors, mobile phones, databases) will allow timely decisions and actions based solely on their knowledge and experience. Depending on the measurement analysis, the system will recommend appropriate diagnostic/therapeutic decisions, thereby minimizing the consequences of delayed medical actions and allowing the medical facility staff to focus on providing better patient service quality.
