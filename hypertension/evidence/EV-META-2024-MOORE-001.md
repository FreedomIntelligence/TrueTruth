## English Abstract

To estimate the association between mean arterial pressure (MAP) during pregnancy and neonatal outcomes in participants with chronic hypertension (cHTN) using data from the Chronic Hypertension and Pregnancy (CHAP) Trial.

A secondary analysis of the CHAP Trial, an open-label, multicenter, randomized trial of antihypertensive treatment in pregnancy was conducted. The CHAP trial enrolled participants with mild chronic hypertension [blood pressure (BP) 140–159/90–104mmHg] and singleton pregnancies <23 weeks’ gestation, randomizing them to active treatment (maintained on anti-hypertensive therapy with a goal BP of <140/90mmHg) or standard treatment (Control) (antihypertensives withheld unless BP reached ≥160mmHg systolic or ≥105mmHg diastolic). We employed logistic regression to measure the strength of association between MAP (average and highest across study visits) and select neonatal outcomes. Unadjusted and adjusted odds ratios (per 1 unit increase in mmHg) of primary neonatal composite outcome [bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD), retinopathy of prematurity (ROP), necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC), or intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) 3 or 4] and individual secondary outcomes [neonatal intensive care unit admission (NICU), low birth weight (LBW) <2500g, and small for gestational age (SGA)] were calculated.

A total of 2,284 participants were included: 1155 Active and 1129 Control. Adjusted models controlling for randomization group demonstrated that increasing average MAP per mmHg was associated with an increase in each neonatal outcome examined except NEC, specifically: neonatal composite [aOR=1.12 (95% CI 1.09–1.16)], NICU admission [aOR=1.07 (1.06–1.08)], LBW [(aOR=1.12 (1.11–1.14)], SGA<5th%ile [aOR=1.03 (1.01–1.06)] and SGA<10th%ile [aOR=1.02 (1.01–1.04)]. Models using highest MAP, as opposed to average MAP, also demonstrated consistent associations.

Increasing MAP was positively associated with most adverse neonatal outcomes, except for NEC. Given that the relationship between MAP and adverse pregnancy outcomes may not be consistent at all MAP levels, future work should attempt to further elucidate if there is an absolute threshold or relative change in MAP where fetal benefits are optimized along with maternal benefits.

ClinicalTrials.gov, NCT02299414.

Lower mean arterial pressure was associated with improved neonatal outcomes among individuals with chronic hypertension.

## 背景 / Background

Two percent of US pregnancies are complicated by chronic hypertension (cHTN), leading to adverse maternal and infant outcomes.1,2 Hypertensive conditions during pregnancy are directly responsible for over 10% of maternal deaths and additionally contribute to hypertensive morbidities such as stroke, cardiomyopathy, and other cardiovascular-related events, which are increasing in the U.S.3–5 Furthermore, neonates of mothers with cHTN are at increased risk of adverse outcomes including low birth weight (LBW), preterm birth (PTB), and perinatal death.6

The Chronic Hypertension and Pregnancy (CHAP) Trial was designed to assess the maternal and fetal benefits of using antihypertensive medications to achieve tight blood pressure control (<140/90) versus more lenient criteria (160/105). Among participants with mild chronic hypertension treated to a BP below 140/90mmHg, the CHAP Trial demonstrated a reduction in mean blood pressures as well as adverse pregnancy outcomes including severe hypertension, preeclampsia, preterm birth, and low birth weight but not for some neonatal outcomes such as SGA, neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) admission, and severe neonatal complications.7 While BP measurements are used in clinical practice, mean arterial pressure (MAP) has demonstrated improved accuracy (i.e., greater area under the curve) over systolic or diastolic blood pressures in predicting adverse pregnancy outcomes.8 In high-risk individuals in the same meta-analysis, diastolic blood pressure was most predictive of preeclampsia.8 It is hypothesized that MAP may be superior than traditional BP measures as elevated MAP may induce nitric oxide synthesis resulting in a cascade of adverse physiologic events.9 Therefore, in light of the conflicting data in the literature, the objective of our study was to estimate the association between MAP during pregnancy and neonatal outcomes in participants with cHTN using data from the CHAP trial. We hypothesized that higher MAP would be associated with adverse neonatal outcomes.

## 方法 / Methods

This is a secondary analysis of the CHAP Trial, an open-label, multicenter, randomized treatment trial of cHTN in pregnancy. Eligible participants had a known or new diagnosis of chronic hypertension, viable singleton fetus, and were randomized at less than 23 weeks’ gestational age. Participants in the active treatment group (Active) were randomized to antihypertensive medication (nifedipine or labetalol) to maintain a BP of <140/90mmHg; participants in the standard treatment group (Control) were not treated with antihypertensive treatment unless systolic BP (SBP) reached ≥ 160mmHg or diastolic (DBP) ≥ 105mmHg. In the original trial, the goal BP of <140/90mmHg for participants in the treatment arm corresponds to a MAP of <107mmHg; however, it is worth noting that a MAP value of 107mmHg reflects a range of SBP and DBP measurements. Further details regarding trial methodology were previously published.7

The primary exposure for this secondary analysis was MAP. MAP was defined as DBP + 1/3 (SBP – DBP) and was calculated as a single value for each documented study clinic visit, which occurred at routine frequencies for prenatal care.9 For each participant both the average MAP value (i.e., the mean of calculated MAPs from each study visit after enrollment) and highest MAP value (i.e., the single highest calculated MAP among all study visits after enrollment) were determined. The primary study outcome was a neonatal composite outcome including bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD), retinopathy of prematurity (ROP), necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC; Bell stages 1–3), or intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) 3 or 4. Individual secondary outcomes included neonatal intensive care unit admission (NICU), low birth weight (LBW) <2500g, and small for gestational age (SGA) less than the 5th and 10th percentiles according to Duryea’s curve.10 All neonatal outcomes were based on clinical diagnoses supported by relevant tests/imaging as documented in NICU records.

Baseline characteristics were reexamined given the selected study subset differed slightly from the original trial cohort due to incomplete data for some participants. While race/ethnicity is not a biological driver of adverse outcomes, it was included among these characteristics because pregnancy related morbidity and mortality is over-represented among individuals comprising some racial/ethnic groups.3,5 MAP quartiles were calculated for the study population and plotted visually to represent the relationship between MAP and select neonatal outcomes. Separate logistic regression models assessed associations between average MAP and highest MAP on the above neonatal outcomes, with associations quantified with odds ratios and 95% confidence intervals. Odds ratios can be interpreted as the increase in odds of the select outcome per increasing 1mmHg MAP. Models were adjusted for randomization group to ensure isolation of MAP effects, as treatment status was related to both achieved MAP values and neonatal outcomes potentially influencing this relationship.

Because gestational age at delivery could influence the relationship between treatment of cHTN and adverse pregnancy outcomes (e.g. the neonate affected by early or prolonged MAP elevations or acute severe MAP elevations near delivery), stratified subgroup analyses were also conducted for regression models to examine differences by gestational age at delivery (preterm subgroups: 22–28 weeks, 29–32 weeks, 33–36 weeks; term subgroup: ≥ 37 weeks) for select outcomes as the intervention reduced preterm birth <35 weeks in the parent trial. Subgroups with outcome incidence <5 were not computed due to unstable estimates. Analyses were conducted using SAS software, v9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC) and statistical significance was assessed at a 0.05 level without adjustment for multiple comparisons. This secondary analysis was deemed exempt by the institutional review board.

## 结果 / Results

Of the original 2,408 participants from the original trial, a total of 2,284 participants were included in this analysis (1,155 from the Active group and 1,129 from the Control group) and 124 were excluded due to missing data for either recorded MAP values or neonatal outcomes. The study population was diverse in terms of race/ethnicity, insurance status, type of cHTN, and BMI at enrollment. Other demographic and clinical characteristics are presented in Table 1.

Crude and adjusted models (controlling for randomization group) showed consistent results across outcomes examined for both average and highest MAP (Table 2). Adjusted models demonstrated that increasing MAP was associated with increasing odds of the primary neonatal composite outcome. Specifically, the adjusted odds of the primary neonatal composite were 12% higher for each 1 mmHg incremental increase in average MAP [aOR 1.12, (1.09–1.16)], and 7% higher for each 1 mmHg incremental increase in highest MAP [aOR=1.07, (1.04–1.09)]. (Table 2). Each neonatal outcome comprising the composite was also associated with higher average MAP except for NEC. For secondary outcomes, NICU admission [aOR 1.07 (1.06–1.08)], LBW [(aOR 1.12 (1.11–1.14)], SGA<5th% [aOR 1.03 (1.01–1.06)] and SGA<10th% [aOR 1.02 (1.01–1.04)] were each associated with higher average MAP. Models using highest MAP demonstrated consistent associations for primary and secondary outcomes except IVH grade 3/4.

Stratified subgroup analyses by gestational age demonstrated similar findings across GA subgroups for both average [aOR=1.08 (1.06–1.10) and aOR=1.07 (1.03–1.11), respectively] and highest MAP [aOR=1.05 (1.03–1.07) and aOR=1.06 (1.03–1.09), respectively] when assessing LBW as the outcome, though the 22–28w and 29–32w subgroups were too small to assess. However, the neonatal composite outcome was entirely determined by the preterm subgroup as there were zero observations among the term subgroup for the composite outcome (Table 3).

Lastly, Figure 1 presents the frequency of both the neonatal composite and LBW outcomes by MAP quartiles using average MAP values per participant. When comparing quartiles (Quartile 1: < 92.2 mmHg, Quartile 2: 92.2–97.0 mmHg, Quartile 3: 97.1–101.6 mmHg, Quartile 4: >101.6 mmHg), the frequencies of both the composite and LBW increase with increasing MAP quartile. This was especially marked in Quartile 4 with frequencies of the composite (1.2%) and LBW (10.5%) over twice that of Quartile 3 composite (0.4%) and LBW (4.8%).

## 讨论 / Discussion

After employing regression analysis, we demonstrated that higher MAP was associated with an increase in adverse neonatal outcomes except NEC. Put another way, a lower MAP appeared to have a protective association with neonate outcomes. This association was also demonstrated for LBW after stratifying by gestational age at birth. This finding is in contrast to associations in prior literature reporting higher MAPs are beneficial to the neonate.11,12

The original CHAP trial reported a significant treatment effect for PTB and LBW but not for the composite outcome or NICU admissions for neonates born to mothers with cHTN in the Active treatment group compared with the Control group.7 Using average and highest MAP as continuous exposures, this analysis demonstrated a positive relationship between increasing MAP and most adverse neonatal outcomes, not including NEC, independent of gestational age at delivery, though the majority of neonatal morbidity was represented among earlier gestational age groups. This suggests a potential dose-response relationship between MAP and adverse neonatal outcomes among women enrolled in the trial, likely related to physiological changes to blood flow in the fetal compartment that occur because of lower MAP during pregnancy.13

Studies investigating the use of MAP in pregnancy, as opposed to systolic or diastolic measurements, are limited. A 2008 systematic review of over 60,000 pregnant individuals concluded that MAP was a better predictor of preeclampsia than systolic or diastolic blood pressure or absolute increase in blood pressure [receiver operator characteristic curve 0.76 (95% CI 0.70 – 0.82) for MAP versus 0.68 (95% CI 0.64 – 0.72) for systolic blood pressure].8 A meta-regression analysis of RCTs and follow-up meta-analysis among mild-to-moderate pregnancy hypertension found that treatment-induced decreases in MAP resulted in lower mean birthweight, increasing the risk of small-for-gestational-age neonates.11,12 However, more recent studies have demonstrated that lower MAP is not associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes, especially low birth weight.14–16 Retrospective data suggest that MAP may be a better predictor of adverse pregnancy outcomes than traditional markers of BP.17

CHAP demonstrated that treatment of mild chronic hypertension improves maternal and perinatal outcomes. The present study demonstrates that higher MAP is associated with an increase in adverse neonatal outcomes. It is therefore possible that the mechanism by which treatment improves outcomes is via a reduction in MAP. Our findings that neonatal risks increase with every 1 mmHg increase in MAP lend themselves to future questions with respect to treatment. It is worth emphasizing that the ORs and aORs are indexed per 1 mmHg unit increase in MAP. The ORs act multiplicatively, so a change in e.g., 5 mmHg MAP would result in more impressive increases in odds. Given that CHAP led to national changes in clinical guidelines for the management of CHTN, treatment to lower MAPs should be considered investigational and reserved for rigorous research protocols. It should also be noted that the authors used average MAP, which is sensitive to the number of BP measurements, in addition to highest MAP for each participant over the study period.

Compared with previous studies, this analysis has notable strengths. First, this secondary analysis was conducted on a cohort of participants from a large, open-label, multi-center, randomized controlled trial. Previous analyses incorporating a similar number of participants were observational in nature or only constructed via meta-analysis and therefore subject to study heterogeneity. Second, this study examined several neonatal outcomes that could result from maternal blood pressure derangements including both fetal growth parameters and clinical markers of neonatal wellbeing (e.g. NICU admission, BPD, ROP, NEC, and IVH). There are also notable limitations. This study did not quantify outcomes of fetal growth (e.g., in grams) as previous studies have shown but only included measures of association. Future work could use alternative methods (e.g., a linear mixed model) to examine the relationship between treatment group and longitudinal MAP measures. Still, the odds ratios presented indicate a consistent association between increasing maternal MAP and adverse neonatal outcomes. Also, while we did not adjust for multiple comparisons, researchers may impose a more restrictive level of significance and reevaluate statistical significance. Moreover, the direction of association was consistent across comparisons for adjusted and unadjusted analyses for various outcomes that are linked with neonatal health and development. Last, we acknowledge that the neonate may be more affected by early or prolonged MAP elevations, or acute severe MAP elevations near delivery, which this study was not designed to assess.

Increasing MAP was positively associated with most adverse neonatal outcomes, except for NEC. Given that the relationship between MAP and adverse pregnancy outcomes may not be consistent at all MAP levels, future work should attempt to further elucidate if there is an absolute threshold or relative change in MAP where fetal benefits are optimized along with maternal benefits. Additionally, the effect of MAP changes in pregnancy on long-term childhood outcomes is important to consider in ongoing CHAP child follow-up studies.
