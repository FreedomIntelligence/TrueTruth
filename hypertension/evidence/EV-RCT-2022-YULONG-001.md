## English Abstract

The aim of this study was to investigate the association between baseline resting heart rate (RHR) and all‐cause death in the China Stroke Primary Prevention Trial (CSPPT). A post hoc analysis was conducted using data from 20,648 hypertensive adults without cardiovascular disease in the CSPPT and with baseline RHR data available. Over a median follow‐up duration of 4.5 years, the baseline RHR and risk for all‐cause death had a nonlinear relationship. The risk of all‐cause death was higher in participants with an RHR ≥85 bpm (hazard ratio 1.42; 95% confidence interval 1.03–1.96, p = .031) than in those with a baseline RHR of 75–80 bpm. The effect of RHR on all‐cause death during the treatment period was modified by the folate level (p = .020) and systolic blood pressure (SBP) during treatment(p = .056). The effect of RHR on the risk of all‐cause death was stronger when the folate level was low than when it was high; the risk was higher when the RHR was < 75 bpm or ≥80 bpm than when it was 75–80 bpm. RHR had a greater effect on the risk of all‐cause death when SBP during treatment was well controlled than when it was not; again, the risk was higher when the RHR was < 75 bpm or ≥80 bpm than when it was 75–80 bpm. A higher baseline RHR resulted in an increased risk of all‐cause mortality in Chinese adults with hypertension but no history of stroke or myocardial infarction.

## 方法 / Methods

The CSPPT was a multicenter, randomized controlled trial performed between May 19, 2008, and August 24, 2013, in 32 communities in the Jiangsu and Anhui provinces in China. The CSPPT tested the hypothesis that treatment with enalapril and folic acid would be more effective than enalapril alone for reduction of the risk of first stroke in Chinese adults with hypertension. Each study participant underwent a physical examination and completed questionnaires on lifestyle and history of disease and medication use at the time of screening. Eligible participants stratified by methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR) C677T genotype (CC, CT, or TT) were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to receive a single daily oral tablet containing 10 mg of enalapril and 0.8 mg of folic acid or a daily oral tablet containing 10 mg of enalapril only. All study investigators and participants were blinded to the randomization procedure and treatment assignment. Participants were scheduled for follow‐up every 3 months. At each follow‐up visit, vital signs, compliance with trial medication, concomitant medication use, adverse events, and possible endpoint events were documented by trained research staff and physicians.
16
 The CSPPT was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Institute of Biomedicine, Anhui Medical University, Hefei, China (Federal Wide Assurance number FWA00001263). All study participants provided written informed consent before data collection.

Of 29,190 candidates screened, 20,702 were enrolled in the CSPPT and randomized between May 2008 and August 2009. All participants in the CSPPT were included in the present study except for those without baseline RHR data.

Seated blood pressure (BP) measurements were obtained at baseline according to the standard method by trained research staff using a mercury sphygmomanometer and an appropriately sized cuff. After BP was recorded, RHR measurements were obtained. The patients were required to refrain from intensive exercise for half an hour before measurement of BP and RHR. Smoking and intake of caffeine were prohibited on the day of measurement. The resting state was achieved by sitting for 10 min with the legs straight and relaxed and the right arm exposed and placed at the level of the heart. For BP, triplicate measurements were obtained for the same arm with at least 2 min between readings. The mean of three independent measurements of SBP and diastolic BP (DBP) were used in the analyses. A pulse measurement of at least 30 or 60 s was considered to be RHR.

In the CSPPT, all‐cause death was defined as death from any cause. Evidence for death included death certificates from hospitals or reports of a home visit by the investigators. All‐cause death was reviewed and adjudicated according to the standard criteria by an independent endpoint adjudication committee.

Body mass index was calculated as weight divided by the square of height (kg/m2). Time‐averaged on‐treatment SBP and DBP and time‐averaged RHR were calculated for each participant using all of the post‐baseline BP and RHR results up to the last visit before the date of the primary outcome or death or the end of follow‐up in the patients without events.

Baseline characteristics are presented as the mean (for normally distributed continuous variables), median (for non‐normally distributed continuous variables), or percentage (categorical variables) as appropriate and stratified by baseline RHR category (< 65, 65–70, 70–75, 75–80, 80–85, and ≥85 bpm). Differences in baseline characteristics were classified on the basis of baseline RHR category and compared using one‐way analysis of variance for normally distributed continuous variables or the chi‐square test for categorical variables. Non‐normally distributed data were compared using the Kruskal–Wallis rank test. Outcomes in various groups of baseline RHR were compared using the Kaplan–Meier method and log‐rank test. For the functional form of RHR, we used restricted cubic spline regression for modeling of RHR to allow for flexibility. Multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression models were used to evaluate the effect of baseline RHR on the risk of all‐cause death, with estimation of adjusted hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). Results in various baseline RHR subgroups on the risk of all‐cause death were also explored using Cox proportional hazards regression models. For analysis of subgroups, we listed all the risk factors for hypertension mentioned in the 2018 Chinese Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Hypertension
5
 (except for family history of premature cardiovascular disease) and divided the study participants on the basis of whether or not they had three or more risk factors. Potential confounders were identified to be age, sex, research center, body mass index, smoking status, alcohol consumption, SBP and DBP at baseline, fasting blood glucose, total cholesterol, triglycerides, high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol, total homocysteine, folate, vitamin B12, MTHFR C677T genotype, creatinine, time‐averaged SBP and DBP over the treatment period, time‐averaged RHR, and antihypertensive, glucose‐lowering, lipoprotein‐lowering, and antiplatelet agents.

To rule out the possibility of reverse causality, we also conducted an analysis after exclusion of participants who died during the first half or one year of follow‐up.

A sensitivity analysis was performed to explore the relationship between RHR and all‐cause death in participants who did not take beta‐blockers.

Empower (www.empowerstats.com; X&Y Solutions, Inc., Boston MA) and R software version 3.6.3(http://www.r‐project.org) were used for all statistical analyses. A two‐tailed alpha value of 0.05 was considered to be statistically significant in all analyses.

## 讨论 / Discussion

In this post hoc analysis of the CSPPT data, we found that the relationship between baseline RHR and risk for all‐cause death was not linear, with an increased risk at a baseline RHR of ≥85 bpm in comparison with a reference range of 75–80 bpm. Furthermore, the effect of RHR on all‐cause death was affected by the folate level and SBP during treatment; this effect was stronger in the group with a low folate level and the group with well‐controlled SBP during treatment when the RHR was < 75  or ≥80 bpm than when it was 75–80 bpm. Therefore, an increase in RHR is a risk factor for all‐cause mortality in hypertensive Chinese patients without CVD.

Previous studies have demonstrated that an increase in RHR affects the prognosis of patients with hypertension. Post hoc analysis of the ONTARGET/TRANSCEND study showed that RHR, in particular in‐trial average heart rate, was independently associated with significant increases in cardiovascular events and all‐cause death.
8
 Moreover, an analysis of the SPRINT study found that an elevated baseline RHR contributes to an increased risk of cardiovascular events regardless of BP control and that the increase in cardiovascular risk associated with a higher RHR was more marked in the intensive treatment group than in the standard treatment group.
9
 In a cohort study performed at the Cooper Clinic (Dallas, TX, USA), the investigators found that hypertensive individuals with a high RHR (> 80 bpm) were at greater risk of cardiovascular disease and all‐cause mortality than their counterparts with a lower RHR (< 60 bpm).
7
 Cohort studies from China have also found that all‐cause and cardiovascular mortality rates are higher when the RHR is increased in both hypertensive and normotensive individuals.
13
, 
14
 However, these observational cohort studies did not exclude patients with a history of cardiovascular disease, such as myocardial infarction, stroke, or heart failure. In our present analysis of hypertensive persons without a history of stroke or myocardial infarction in China, the risk of all‐cause mortality was 42% higher in the group with an RHR ≥85 bpm than in the group with an RHR of 75–80 bpm (HR 1.42, 95% CI 1.03–1.96, p = .031).

Our study is a post hoc analysis of an RCT and reports an association of increased RHR with higher all‐cause mortality in hypertensive Chinese participants without cardiovascular disease. The most recent Chinese Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Hypertension published in 20185 do not mention elevated RHR as a cardiovascular risk factor in patients with hypertension or recommend a threshold RHR value for intervention. However, the 2017 Chinese expert consensus on management of heart rate in patients with hypertension proposes an RHR of > 80 bpm and a 24‐h Holter average heart rate > 75 bpm as intervention targets in patients with hypertension.
17
 Our post hoc analysis adds to the evidence for RHR as a cardiovascular risk factor in patients with hypertension in China. However, we did not find a direct causal relationship between an increased RHR and cardiovascular mortality.

In the present study, patients with the lowest risk of all‐cause mortality were those in the group with an RHR of 75–80 bpm, which is at odds with the findings of previous studies.
18
, 
19
 We analyzed the data after exclusion of persons who died during the first half‐ or one year of follow‐up to rule out the possibility of reverse causality and found no change in our results. A recent study of hypertensive Chinese patients by Sun and associates
15
 found that the risk of major adverse cardiac events after multivariate adjustment was 0.45‐fold higher in the group with an RHR of < 65 bpm and 0.391‐fold higher in the group with an RHR of ≥80 bpm than in the group with an RHR of 70–74 bpm. However, a Western study found the lowest risk to be associated with an RHR range of 70–79 bpm for all‐cause mortality and 60–69 bpm for cardiovascular mortality in hypertensive persons.
7
 Although numerous studies have demonstrated that a higher RHR increases these risks in hypertensive patients,

7
 and even in China.

 Further studies are needed to identify the target RHR for intervention in hypertensive patients in China.

23
 indicate that sustained elevation of heart rate contributes to the pathogenesis of vascular disease. In animal studies, an accelerated heart rate is associated with cell signaling events that lead to vascular oxidative stress, endothelial dysfunction, and acceleration of atherogenesis. Clinical studies have found a positive correlation between increased RHR and circulating markers of inflammation. This may be the pathophysiological mechanism via which RHR is independently associated with an increased risk of hypertension.

We also found that a low folate level (p = .020) and controlled SBP(p = .056) may modify the association between RHR and mortality. In the group with low folate, the risk of all‐cause death was higher if RHR was < 75  or ≥80 bpm than if it was 75–80 bpm. Moreover, a subgroup analysis based on treatment found no interaction between RHR and risk of all‐cause death in the group with a low folate level in the CSPPT. A previous study found that the risk of cardiovascular death was higher in hypertensive adults with a low or high folate level than in those with a serum folate level in the middle range.
24
 However, the mechanism for the association of a low folate level with RHR and its impact on the risk of death remains unclear, and further studies are needed to explain this observation.

In the group with well‐controlled SBP, the risk of all‐cause death was higher when RHR was < 75  or ≥80 bpm than when it was 75–80 bpm. A previous post hoc analysis of the CSPPT data showed that uncontrolled SBP (≥140 mmHg) during treatment increased the risk of stroke and the composite endpoint of cardiovascular events,
25
 which suggests that an increased RHR has less of an effect in patients with uncontrolled SBP. Moreover, several studies have shown that an increase in RHR increases the risks of all‐cause and cardiovascular mortality in the normotensive/general population
13
, 
20
 and pre‐hypertensive patients.
21
 Our findings indicate that even when BP is well controlled, an increase in RHR is still associated with increased mortality. Despite the steady improvement in control of hypertension over the years, there has been no reduction in cardiovascular mortality,
1
 which highlights the importance of management of other risk factors, in particular heart rate, in Chinese patients with hypertension even when SBP is adequately controlled. Our present findings add to the evidence for management of RHR in patients with well‐controlled SBP.

As shown in Table 1, our RHR groups were not well balanced for sex. Supplementary Tables 1 and 2 show the baseline characteristics of our study population stratified by sex. The increase in RHR was smaller in women than in men during follow‐up. We then reanalyzed the data in Table 2 when stratified by sex to compare the association between RHR and risk of all‐cause mortality in men with that in women, as shown in Supplementary Tables 3 and 4. Although sex had no significant effect on the results of the subgroup analyses, there was a trend of all‐cause mortality being higher when the baseline RHR was ≥85 bpm than when it was 75–80 bpm in women but not in men. This sex‐related difference in the effect of RHR on all‐cause mortality is not understood. The existing literature suggests that increased CVD mortality is the likely driver of the association of RHR with mortality in men.
26
, 
27
 However, reports in women are not consistent.
28
, 
29
 The present study was performed in a Chinese population with baseline characteristics that were different from those in previous studies, which may partly explain the lack of consistent findings. Future studies are needed to examine the apparent sex difference in the relationship between RHR and outcomes.

This study was a post hoc analysis of data from the CSPPT, which was an RCT in which adjudication of endpoints was standardized. Furthermore, the CSPPT included a large sample size, data of good quality, and a relatively long follow‐up duration, which contributed to the accuracy of the results. Our study investigated hypertensive persons in the CSPPT who did not have cardiovascular disease. Trained research staff obtained the RHR by palpating the pulse for at least 30 or 60 s after the patient had been seated for 10 min following each BP reading, as recommended by the Chinese expert consensus on management of heart rate in hypertensive patients.
17
 To our knowledge, this post hoc analysis of an randomized controlled trial is the first to identify an association of increased RHR with higher all‐cause mortality in hypertensive Chinese persons without cardiovascular disease. During the study period, although beta‐blockers were not prohibited, the proportion of patients who used these agents during follow‐up was very small. Our data were not changed by a sensitivity analysis of participants who did not take beta‐blockers, which suggests that our results are robust.

Nevertheless, this study had several limitations. First, it was performed in rural areas in China, which means that its findings cannot be extrapolated to all populations. Second, atrial fibrillation could affect the accuracy of RHR measurements obtained by pulse palpation. However, a previous study found that atrial fibrillation was uncommon in hypertensive patients and that its occurrence was decreased by use of angiotensin‐converting enzyme inhibitors.
30
 Third, our use of baseline RHR, which had fewer missing data, instead of time‐averaged RHR might have introduced a degree of selection bias. Finally, although this study found an association between higher RHR and increased all‐cause mortality in hypertensive persons, it could not establish a causal relationship. Further studies are still needed to determine if there is a need to intervene on RHR in hypertensive persons.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In China, a higher baseline RHR resulted in an increased risk of all‐cause mortality in adults with hypertension and no history of stroke or myocardial infarction.
