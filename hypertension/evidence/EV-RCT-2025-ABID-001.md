## English Abstract

Hypertension is one of the leading causes of mortality related to cardiovascular disease. Since the advent of modern medicine, a multitude of efforts have been made to achieve better BP control. However, despite a wide range of monitoring protocols and medication therapies, only 23% of patients with hypertension achieve BP control. Renal denervation (RDN) has emerged as an adjunctive therapy to medications and lifestyle modification for BP control. Two methods of RDN were approved by the US Department of Food and Drug Administration in 2023: Radiofrequency ablation and an ultrasound-based method. Radiofrequency ablation uses heat-generating electrodes placed on the renal artery using a catheter and delivers heat through alternating current. Ultrasound-based technique uses ultrasonic heating to create a ring of ablative energy that interrupts renal nerve signaling. Over the past two decades, well-conducted randomized sham control trials and registry analysis have proven RDN to be an effective and safe treatment option for resistant hypertension with promising results. RDN also becomes appealing in patients with CKD who have an underlying sympathetic overactivity. This article aims to provide a comprehensive review of RDN, including current evidence and its implications in routine clinician practice.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension, defined as BP >130/80 mm Hg by the American Heart Association (AHA),1–4 affected around 650 million people worldwide in 1990 and that number reached 1.28 billion in 2021, nearly doubling within just three decades (World Health Organization). Long-standing and uncontrolled hypertension increases risks of cardiovascular disease (CVD), CKD, stroke, and premature death.5 Hypertension at the age 50 years is shown to be associated with a shorter life expectancy by nearly 5.1 years in men and 4.9 years in women.6

In addition to the disease burden, it also causes a major financial burden. Hypertension control and its related comorbidities demand a great amount of global expenditure, which was around 370 billion US dollars back in 2001 and is estimated by the AHA to be around 20 billion dollars for direct and 40 billion dollars for indirect costs by 2030.5 These costs are a product of BP medications, laboratory tests, clinic visits, frequent hospitalizations, and loss of income due to morbidity/mortality.5

Patients' nonadherence to diet and medications contributes to inadequate control of hypertension.2 In addition, the Western diet, obesity, smoking, stress, and alcohol consumption aggravate hypertension.6 There is growing evidence to support that adequate control of hypertension would dramatically decrease the rate of cardiovascular and kidney-related comorbidities.5 The Third National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey linked mortality study conducted on 13,000 adults elaborated on all causes as well as CVD-specific mortality related to treated but poorly controlled hypertension versus well-controlled hypertension versus untreated hypertension. The results showed that hypertensive individuals with uncontrolled BP despite treatment were at elevated risk of all-cause and CVD mortality when compared with individuals with well-controlled BP.7

In the realm of challenges facing hypertension control, renal denervation (RDN) has recently emerged as a reliable adjunctive measure to achieve better BP control. Currently, three methods of device-based RDN are available: Radiofrequency energy (radiofrequency ablation [RFA]), ultrasonic denervation, and denervation through the injection of neurolytic agents.2 Current evidence shows that RDN effectively reduces BP with minimal risk of structural or functional kidney damage. European guidelines and US Food and Drug Administration approved two systems of RDN at the end of 2023 as an important adjunctive therapy for resistant hypertension. In this review, we aim to consolidate data available on RDN and both approved systems to enable practitioners to use RDN as an adjunctive therapy to improve BP control.

The concept of RDN dates back to the early part of the 20th century, as sympathectomy from surgical resection of the splanchnic nerves was performed at various institutions across the country. However, surgery was met with a host of complications, and as antihypertensive medical therapy evolved, surgical RDN was largely abandoned. Fast forward to contemporary practice and endovascular catheter-based RDN for resistant hypertension is now a reasonable option for select patient groups. The renal nerves are located within the adventitia of the renal artery at a depth of approximately 3–7 mm. The renal nerves, being the target of RDN, are situated in a desirable location for the two US Food and Drug Administration approved RDN catheters, the Symplicity Spyral RDN system by Medtronic, Inc. and the Paradise Ultrasound RDN System by ReCor, Inc. (1).

## 方法 / Methods

RDN is a device-based method that interrupts the renal sympathetic nerves, thus diminishing the hyperactive signaling between the kidney and the central nervous system responsible for hypertension.3 Several methods of RDN have been discovered and evaluated, including denervation by RFA, ultrasound energy, or injection of neurotoxin into perivascular space.

In RFA, heat-generating electrodes are placed on an arterial wall using a catheter. Heat produced because of medium frequency alternating current using electrodes gets delivered to nerves within the adventitial layer of renal artery. The resultant heat damages nerves within the arterial wall while sparing the renal arterial wall, causing denervation by diminishing the sympathetic signals.3,8 The most current reiteration of the Medtronic RDN device is the Symplicity Spyral system. The Symplicity Spyral system has a helical design with four radiofrequency electrodes to enhance the efficacy of ablation.9 The Spyral system focuses on the distal renal bed where the renal nerves are near the renal artery. Histologic renal nerve evidence supports sustained external distraction and reduction in renal NE through 180 days post-RDN in animal models.10 Retrospective analysis of the Renal Denervation in Patients With Uncontrolled Hypertension (SYMPLICITY HTN-3) trial using radiofrequency RDN demonstrates that both total number of ablations were positively associated with greater BP reductions.11

The second system of RDN, Ultrasonic Denervation, uses a catheter to achieve denervation by establishing a circumferential thermal ablation pattern through acoustic energy. This catheter comes with an inflatable balloon, irrigated to maintain a cooler temperature in the arterial wall than in the perivascular space. It allows for penetration and sympathetic nerve damage of up to 6–7 mm from the arterial lumen, thus achieving improved control of the sympathetic activity responsible for hypertension. One such system currently being used in clinical practice is the ReCor Paradise Catheter.12 This system uses a noncompliant balloon with a built-in cooling system and delivers 360° of sonification. In contrast to the Symplicity device, The Paradise device focuses on the more proximal renal nerve bed, as most of the renal nerves are in that anatomical distribution, albeit further from the renal artery.13 The Paradise system has an ultrasound energy delivery targeted at depth that can affecct deeper circumferential distribution and affect the nerve endings that lie downstream. The safety profile of both the Spyral and Paradise system is excellent as evident in their pivotal trials.14,15

## 结论 / Conclusion

Hypertension is a prevailing health issue with significant comorbidities. However, despite the grand scale of the problem, control over hypertension is not optimal due to many factors, such as a lack of patient adherence to medication and a healthy lifestyle. The increasing disease load of hypertension directly increases the financial burden it has on the economy worldwide. Scientific evidence has proven RDN to be a safe and highly effective method of BP reduction. However, despite a range of promising and satisfactory data, RDN remains an underutilized modality, primarily limited to a few advanced tertiary care research-based institutes nationwide. A variety of resources must be used to educate physicians in community-based practices to increase their awareness and confidence to use RDN as a potential treatment option.
