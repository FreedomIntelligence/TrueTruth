## English Abstract

To evaluate maternal and neonatal outcomes by type of antihypertensive used in participants of the Chronic Hypertension in Pregnancy trial (CHAP).

We conducted a planned secondary analysis of CHAP, an open-label, multicenter, randomized trial of antihypertensive treatment vs. standard care (no treatment unless severe hypertension developed) in pregnant patients with mild chronic hypertension (blood pressure 140–159/90–104 mmHg prior to 20 weeks gestation) and singleton pregnancies. We performed 3 comparisons based on medications prescribed at enrollment: labetalol vs. standard care, nifedipine vs. standard care, and labetalol vs. nifedipine. While active vs. standard care groups were randomized, medication assignment within the active treatment group were not randomly assigned but based on clinician or patient preference. The primary outcome was the occurrence of superimposed preeclampsia with severe features, preterm birth<35 weeks, placental abruption, and/or fetal/neonatal death. The key secondary outcome was small for gestational age (SGA) neonates. We also compared medication adverse effects between groups. Relative risks (RR) and 95% confidence intervals were estimated using log binomial regression to adjust for confounding.

Of 2,292 participants analyzed, 720 (31.4%) received labetalol, 417 (18.2%) nifedipine and 1,155 (50.4%) no treatment. The mean gestational age at enrollment was 10.5 ± 3.7 weeks, nearly half (47.5%) identified as Non-Hispanic Black and 44.5% used aspirin. The primary outcome occurred in 217 (30.1%), 130 (31.2%), 427 (37.0%) in the labetalol, nifedipine, and standard care groups, respectively. Risk of the primary outcome was lower among those receiving treatment (labetalol use [vs. standard] aRR 0.82, 95% CI 0.72–0.94; nifedipine use [vs. standard] aRR 0.84, 95% CI 0.71–0.99), but there was no significant difference in risk when comparing labetalol to nifedipine (aRR 0.98, 95% CI 0.82–1.18). There were no significant differences in SGA or serious adverse events between participants receiving labetalol and nifedipine.

No significant differences in predetermined maternal or neonatal outcomes were detected based on the use of labetalol or nifedipine for treatment of chronic hypertension in pregnancy.

ClinicalTrials.gov, NCT02299414.

Either labetalol or nifedipine reduces pregnancy risks with no increased risk of SGA in patients with mild chronic hypertension.

## 背景 / Background

The Chronic Hypertension and Pregnancy (CHAP) randomized controlled trial found that active treatment of mild chronic hypertension (systolic/diastolic BP 140–159/90–105 mm Hg) in pregnancy to blood pressure <140/90 mmHg, compared with treatment of only severe hypertension (BP≥160/105 mmHg), reduced the risk of the composite primary outcome of superimposed preeclampsia with severe features, indicated preterm delivery <35 weeks, placental abruption, and fetal or neonatal death.1

Labetalol and nifedipine are first line antihypertensive medications recommended by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) for treatment of chronic hypertension in pregnancy. Although one prior randomized trial of 112 patients comparing labetalol to nifedipine reported similar reductions in systolic and diastolic blood pressures with no difference in maternal and fetal outcomes2, data to support which drug should be the first line antihypertensive agent in pregnancy are lacking.2,3

Thus, our primary objectives were to compare the effectiveness of oral labetalol and nifedipine in reducing adverse pregnancy outcomes, and to evaluate their safety for treatment of mild chronic hypertension in pregnancy. We hypothesized that there would be no significant differences between labetalol and nifedipine in reducing adverse pregnancy outcomes, and in their safety in terms of SGA and adverse effects, when used for the treatment of mild chronic hypertension to BP<140/90 mmHg in pregnancy.

## 方法 / Methods

We performed a planned secondary analysis of a United States open-label, multicenter randomized controlled trial of treatment of mild chronic hypertension during pregnancy (CHAP trial [Chronic Hypertension and Pregnancy], NCT 02299414). Institutional review boards reviewed and approved the parent trial at each participating center. The inclusion and exclusion criteria for the trial have been previously described.1 Briefly, pregnant patients at <23 weeks’ gestation with singleton pregnancies and a new or prior diagnosis of mild chronic hypertension based on clinic blood pressures (BP), systolic 140–159 mmHg and/or diastolic 90–104 mmHg prior to 20 weeks’ gestation were included. Participants with severe hypertension (BP≥ 160/105mmHg), those who required more than one BP medication at randomization, those using other antihypertensive medication other than labetalol or nifedipine as first line treatment, or those with comorbidities requiring BP goals < 140/90 mm Hg were excluded. Participants who after randomization, later in pregnancy, required more than one antihypertensive medication were included. For this analysis, only participants with outcome data available were included.

The primary exposure was the antihypertensive medication following randomization to active treatment or standard care groups. The treatment groups were defined by the antihypertensive medication an individual was prescribed at enrollment, specifically labetalol or nifedipine for the active treatment group, or none for the standard care group. We performed pairwise comparisons of the three treatment groups- labetalol versus standard care, nifedipine versus standard care, and labetalol versus nifedipine.

The choice of antihypertensive regimen for those in the active treatment group was based on the patient’s medical history, prior experience with antihypertensive medication or patient, clinician or institutional preference. Participants randomized to active treatment in the parent study received one of two regimens: [1] Initiation of labetalol 200 mg twice daily or maintenance of their established dose prior to randomization, with up titration in 200 mg increments as needed, to a maximum of 2400 mg/day divided to two or three times daily, or [2] Initiation of nifedipine 30 mg extended release once daily, or maintenance of their dose established prior to randomization, with 30 mg increments as needed to a maximum of 120 mg/day, to attain blood pressures < 140/90 mmHg. Nifedipine could be divided into twice daily dosing if necessary to optimize BP control or in response to symptoms. If the maximum dose of either antihypertensive agent was reached, the second preferred antihypertensive (labetalol or nifedipine) was then added unless contraindicated. Participants in the standard care (no treatment) group on antihypertensive medications discontinued treatment after randomization if blood pressures were <160/105mmHg and were only started on antihypertensive medication for BP≥160/105 mm Hg. For this analysis, participants assigned to no therapy at randomization who later required antihypertensive medication in pregnancy remained in the standard care group. Also, participants who were assigned to one antihypertensive and later discontinued or switched to the second antihypertensive medication were classified by their initial antihypertensive medication assignment.

The primary outcome used was the same for the parent trial, which was a composite of superimposed preeclampsia with severe features occurring before or up to two weeks after delivery, medically indicated preterm birth <35 weeks, placental abruption, and/or fetal or neonatal death <28 days. Superimposed preeclampsia was defined per ACOG criteria.4 Notably, blood pressures ≥160/110 mmHg without clinical symptoms and signs of preeclampsia, proteinuria, or laboratory abnormalities were not sufficient to diagnose preeclampsia with severe features. Superimposed preeclampsia was defined as worsening hypertension >160/100 mmHg after 20 weeks’ gestation and proteinuria, or in the absence of proteinuria, worsening hypertension above prior baseline ≥140/90 mmHg and cerebral or persistent visual symptoms, or thrombocytopenia <100,000, or serum creatinine ≥1.2mg/dl or a doubling from baseline, or two-fold elevation in liver enzymes, or HELLP syndrome or persistent right upper quadrant pain or pulmonary edema. The primary outcome was ascertained through central adjudication by investigators or clinicians unaware of treatment assignment.

The key secondary safety outcome was small for gestational age (SGA) <10th percentile (according to the Duryea birth weight population standard)5; we also examined SGA<5th percentile. Additional secondary outcomes included individual components of the primary composite, any preeclampsia, preterm birth <37 weeks’ gestation, neonatal intensive care unit admission, neonatal seizures, any neonatal respiratory support and a neonatal composite of bronchopulmonary dysplasia, retinopathy of prematurity, necrotizing enterocolitis and grade 3 or 4 intraventricular hemorrhage. Individual components of the neonatal composite were also reported as secondary outcomes. Neonatal composites were based on clinical diagnoses supported by relevant tests/imaging, as documented in the NICU records. We also reported adverse events, per MedDRA classification (any, serious, non-death and death) by group.

Participants were followed until 6 weeks after birth. Neonatal death was evaluated until 28 days postpartum and superimposed preeclampsia until 2 weeks postpartum. Placental abruption was defined as greater than usual bleeding in the absence of placental previa or trauma, associated with contractions, non-reassuring fetal heart tracing and/or clinical diagnosis of abruption leading to delivery. Indicated preterm birth was defined as medically-indicated preterm birth <35 weeks’ gestation due to a maternal or fetal reason, excluding spontaneous preterm labor or membrane rupture.1 Race and ethnicity were self-reported by participants and were not included as covariates in adjusted analyses (given their status as social constructs that may not reflect biologic differences), although some prior studies show differences in outcomes by race/ethnicity.6–8

Differences in continuous baseline characteristics were assessed by ANOVA for comparisons across all three groups and by the two-sample t-test for pair-wise comparisons between any two treatment groups. Differences in categorical baseline characteristics were assessed by the Chi-squaretest. Unadjusted and adjusted (for baseline characteristics that were significantly different between each comparison group) log binomial multivariable regression models were used to estimate risk ratios and 95% confidence intervals for the association between the exposure and outcomes. All analyses were performed in R version 4.1.3.9–11

## 结果 / Results

Among 2,408 trial participants, 34 participants who used antihypertensives other than labetalol or nifedipine at randomization and 82 without ascertained outcomes were excluded. A total of 2,292 participants were included in this secondary analysis; 720 (31.4%) initially received labetalol, 417 (18.2%) initially received nifedipine and 1,155 (50.4%) were allocated to standard care (reserving medication use until systolic BP ≥160 mmHg or diastolic BP ≥105 mmHg occurred) [Appendix 1, available online at http://links.lww.com/xxx]. Characteristics of participants are shown in Table 1. Among all included participants, the mean age was 32.3 ± 5.7 years and the mean gestational age at enrollment was 10.5 ± 3.7 weeks’ gestation. The mean systolic and diastolic blood pressures were 137.2 ± 14.3 and 84.1 ± 10.7 mmHg respectively. A majority of the participants had known chronic hypertension, of whom 55.8% were on antihypertensive medications prior to pregnancy; 21.8% were newly-diagnosed with chronic hypertension during pregnancy. Approximately half (47.5%) reported being Black, 75.6% were obese, 15.5% had preexisting diabetes and 44.5% reported aspirin use. Participants who received labetalol were enrolled at an earlier gestational age compared to those receiving standard care. Participants assigned to nifedipine use compared to those receiving standard care were more likely to use aspirin use and have a higher body mass index. Compared to participants in the labetalol group, those who initially used nifedipine were more likely to be non-Hispanic Black, use aspirin and have newly diagnosed, or hypertension not on antihypertensive medication (Appendix 2, available online at http://links.lww.com/xxx).

Among participants initially treated with labetalol after study randomization, 567 (78.8%) took only labetalol during the pregnancy and 153 (21.3%) added nifedipine use. In comparison, among participants initially started on nifedipine at baseline, 302 (72.4%) took only nifedipine and 115 (27.6%) added labetalol use. In the control group, 545 (47.2%) participants never took any medication, 247 (21.4%) added labetalol only, 215 (18.6%) added nifedipine only, and 148 (12.8%) eventually added both nifedipine and labetalol (Appendix 1, http://links.lww.com/xxx). Overall, 80.6% reported taking either labetalol or nifedipine at >80% of visits- 83.0% in the labetalol group and 77.9% in the nifedipine group. Among participants who were initially started on labetalol, only 20 (2.8%) attained maximum daily dose of medication. In contrast, 53 (12.7%) participants initially started on nifedipine attained the maximum daily dose. Among the whole study population, 74 (3.2%) participants reported at least one switch from labetalol to nifedipine and 26 (1.1%) reported at least one switch from nifedipine to labetalol.

The incidence of study outcomes by group are shown in Table 2. Among all participants, 774 (33.8%) developed the primary composite outcome, 602 (26.3%) developed superimposed preeclampsia with severe features, 334 (14.6%) were delivered prior to 35 weeks’ gestation, 42 (1.8%) had placental abruption and 91 (4.0%) participants had a fetal or neonatal death. SGA <10th percentile was found in 242 (10.6%) participants. The primary composite outcome occurred in 217 (30.1%), 130 (31.2%), 427 (37.0%) in the labetalol, nifedipine and standard care groups respectively. The incidence of SGA< 10th in the labetalol, nifedipine and standard care groups were 85 (12.0%), 41 (10.1%), 116 (10.4%) respectively.

After adjustments for baseline differences, active treatment with either labetalol or nifedipine, compared with no treatment at baseline was associated with a reduced risk of the primary composite outcome (labetalol vs. standard care aRR 0.82, 95% CI 0.72–0.94; nifedipine vs. standard care aRR 0.84, 95% CI 0.71–0.99; Table 3). There was no significant difference in the primary composite outcome between participants who received labetalol compared with nifedipine (aRR 0.98, 95% CI 0.82–1.18). Similar comparisons did not show any associations between treatment and SGA; respectively (labetalol vs. standard care, aRR 1.18, 95% CI 0.90–1.53; nifedipine vs standard care, aRR 0.98, 95% CI 0.69–1.35; labetalol vs nifedipine, aRR 1.23, 95% CI 0.87–1.77 for SGA <10th, and aRR 0.98, 95% CI 0.65–1.45; aRR 0.94, 95% CI 0.57–1.50; aRR 1.04, 95% CI 0.62–1.79 for SGA <5th percentile). Among components of the primary outcome, labetalol compared with standard care was associated with reduced risks of superimposed preeclampsia with severe features (aRR 0.78, 95% CI 0.66–0.91), while nifedipine was associated with reduced risks of preterm birth <35 weeks’ gestation (aRR 0.67, 95% CI 0.49–0.89).

Regarding other secondary outcomes (Table 3), labetalol was associated with a significantly reduced risk of any preeclampsia, NICU admission and any respiratory support compared with standard care. Nifedipine was also associated with a significant reduction of any preeclampsia and a lower risk of low birth weight when compared to standard care. There were no significant differences in the risk of the primary composite outcome and its components, or other secondary outcomes between participants who received labetalol compared with those receiving nifedipine (Table 2). Labetalol was superior in achieving lower BP compared with nifedipine (mean differences of mean prenatal blood pressure: SBP −3.2, 95% CI −4.4- −2.0; DBP −1.3, 95% CI −2.2- −0.4; Table 2).

Overall, 657 (28.7%) participants reported any adverse event- 204 (28.3%) in the labetalol group, 149 (35.7%) in the nifedipine group and 304 (26.3%) among participants not receiving any antihypertensive medication at baseline. In pairwise comparisons, any adverse events were significantly higher with nifedipine compared with labetalol (35.7% vs. 28.3%; p = 0.009), and with nifedipine compared with standard care (35.7% vs. 26.3%; p = 0.0003). Adverse events were not significantly higher with labetalol compared with standard care (28.3% vs 26.3%; p = 0.34). Headache, medication intolerance, dizziness, nausea, dyspepsia, neonatal jaundice and vomiting were the most frequent reported adverse events. Thus, labetalol compared to nifedipine appeared to have fewer adverse events and be better tolerated. In contrast, serious adverse events were similar across all 3 groups (Appendix 3, available online at http://links.lww.com/xxx). There were no significant differences in maternal, fetal or neonatal deaths across all groups (Appendix 3, http://links.lww.com/xxx).

## 讨论 / Discussion

The CHAP trial demonstrated that treating mild chronic hypertension was superior to deferring treatment until severe hypertension developed, but questions remained about whether, or to what extent, the choice of first line treatment affected outcomes. We provide evidence that initial treatment with either labetalol or nifedipine was associated with improved outcomes without an increase in SGA infants compared with standard care. Furthermore, there did not appear to be a significant difference between labetalol and nifedipine in reducing the risk of adverse pregnancy outcomes.. Therefore, either medication appears to be an appropriate first line for the treatment of mild chronic hypertension in pregnancy. Labetalol appeared to be associated with fewer adverse events and may be better tolerated in pregnancy.

A Cochrane review of 11 randomized trials (638 participants ) of treatment of mild to moderate chronic hypertension in pregnancy that compared beta-blockers and calcium channel blockers, found that both classes of medications reduced severe hypertension (RR 0.70, 95% CI 0.56–0.88) but there was no difference when calcium channel blockers were compared with beta blockers (including labetalol).12 Similarly, no differences in risks of preeclampsia, fetal or neonatal death, SGA or preterm delivery <37 weeks’ gestation were found, when other antihypertensives were compared with calcium channel blockers or betablockers.12 In contrast, a recent meta-analysis of 61 trials (6,923 participants) found that labetalol compared with calcium channel blockers as a group, reduced the risks of proteinuria or preeclampsia (OR 0.63, 95% CI 0.41–0.96) when used for treatment of non-severe hypertension in pregnancy.13 However, their results were not disaggregated by specific calcium channel blocker medications, and also found no differences in other outcomes including perinatal death, SGA, preterm birth or NICU admission.13 The point estimates for our results were in the direction of more modest potential benefits for preeclampsia, and preeclampsia with severe features but were not statistically significant. Importantly, acknowledging power limitations, there were no increased risks of SGA, and other neonatal outcomes with either labetalol or nifedipine use in pregnancy.. Thus, patients and providers can reassuringly select either medication for the management of mild chronic hypertension to BP goals < 140/90mmHg in pregnancy. Overall, our results aligned with prior studies3,12,13, that have not found differences between labetalol and nifedipine.

Labetalol, a third-generation mixed alpha- and beta-adrenergic antagonist, lowers peripheral vascular resistance with minimal effects on cardiac output and uteroplacental blood flow. Labetalol is contraindicated in people with obstructive pulmonary disease.14 Nifedipine, a dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker, also reduces systemic vascular resistance but is contraindicated in people with tachycardia.14 Although well tolerated by most individuals, labetalol and nifedipine manifest distinct side effects.

There remain limited data on guidelines for addition of a second antihypertensive medication or switching to an alternate first line medication for control of mild chronic hypertension in pregnancy. Understanding these factors may contribute to improved patient satisfaction and adherence to antihypertensive treatment in pregnancy. Notably, our study compared two first line antihypertensive medications for treatment of mild chronic hypertension to BP goal < 140/90mmHg in pregnancy. Whether patients with hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (preeclampsia or gestational hypertension) should be treated to similar goals remains an active area of investigation. 15 Our results are not directly applicable to patients with hypertensive disorders of pregnancy.

Our study’s strengths include the large number of participants enrolled in the parent trial, rigorous ascertainment of study exposure and outcomes, and central and blinded adjudication of study outcomes. Our study also had several limitations. Treatment group membership for participants on medications was defined as the treatment prescribed at enrollment after randomization to active treatment or standard care groups and did not account for crossover or temporal treatment patterns. Moreover, randomization was to a treatment strategy, not to a specific medication, i.e., participants randomized to active treatment were then prescribed specific medication(s) in a nonrandom manner that could depend on health system or patient preferences, which introduces the possibility of bias. Our sample size was limited for some outcomes of interest. Notably, the number of participants prescribed nifedipine was just over half of those prescribed labetalol.. The adjusted risk ratio estimate for the primary composite outcome was 0.98, consistent with an interpretation of no clinically relevant difference between labetalol and nifedipine for this composite outcome. However, the low frequency of events for some of the secondary outcomes including SGA leaves the possibility that small clinically meaningful differences may have been missed. Of note, the 124 individuals missing data regarding SGA were mainly those with miscarriages for whom the measurements were not possible. It remains unknown whether there are differences in pharmacogenomic, or long term maternal and infant cardiovascular outcomes between labetalol and nifedipine when used in pregnancy... Finally, we did not adjust for multiple comparisons, which increases the probability of at least one false positive result, but our study question was a preplanned secondary analysis focused on individual testing, in which case the per-comparison error is a valid error rate to control and is controlled for each test.10,11 Moreover, our findings are consistent with those in the original report and the direction of association across a range of outcomes. In summary, our findings support the use of either labetalol or nifedipine as initial first line agents for the management of mild chronic hypertension in pregnancy to reduce the risk of adverse maternal and other perinatal outcomes with no increased risk of fetal harm.
