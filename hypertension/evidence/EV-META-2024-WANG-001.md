## English Abstract

In China, the prevalence of hypertension is high and the use of combination antihypertensive therapy is low, which contributes to inadequate blood pressure (BP) control. The availability of simplified treatments combining complementary BP-lowering agents may help more patients achieve their goals.

This Phase III, multicenter, randomized, double-blind, noninferiority study included Chinese adults with mild-to-moderate hypertension. Following a 1-month run-in on perindopril/indapamide bi-therapy, patients with uncontrolled systolic/diastolic BP (≥140/90 mmHg) were randomized to perindopril 5 mg/indapamide 1.25 mg/amlodipine 5 mg (Per/Ind/Aml) single-pill combination (SPC) or perindopril 4 mg/indapamide 1.25 mg plus amlodipine 5 mg (Per/Ind + Aml) for 6 months. Uptitration was permitted from month 2 onwards. The primary efficacy objective was the noninferiority of Per/Ind/Aml in lowering office systolic BP at 2 months. The secondary objectives included the effectiveness of SPC on diastolic BP, uptitration efficacy, and office BP control (systolic/diastolic <140/90 mmHg). A subgroup of patients participated in 24-h ambulatory BP monitoring (ABPM).

A total of 532 patients were randomized: Per/Ind/Aml (n = 262) and Per/Ind + Aml (n = 269). Overall, the mean (±SD) age was 55.7 ± 8.8 years, 60.7% were male, and the mean office systolic/diastolic BP at baseline on Per/Ind was 150.4/97.2 mmHg. Systolic BP decreased in both groups at 2 months from baseline: −14.99 ± 14.46 mmHg Per/Ind/Aml versus −14.49 ± 12.87 mmHg Per/Ind +Aml. A predefined noninferiority margin of 4 mmHg was observed (P < 0.001). The effectiveness of the Per/Ind/Aml SPC was also demonstrated for all secondary endpoints. ABPM demonstrated sustained BP control over 24 h. Both treatments were well tolerated.

Per/Ind/Aml is an effective substitute for Per/Ind + Aml, providing at least equivalent BP control over 24 h in a single pill, with comparable safety.

## 背景 / Background

Current guidelines for the management of arterial hypertension recommend the use of initial (dual) combination therapy in most patients for immediate blood pressure response, better short- and long-term blood pressure control, and continued/improved patient adherence [1]. Single-pill combination (SPC) therapy is now the preferred strategy for both initial two-drug combination treatment and three-drug combination therapy [1], as research has shown a direct correlation between the number of pills and poor treatment adherence [2,3]. It is estimated that three drugs are required to achieve blood pressure control in a quarter to a third of patients [4]. For those requiring three drugs, a combination of an angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor or angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB) with a calcium channel blocker and a thiazide/thiazide-like diuretic is recommended [1]. Randomized controlled trials have shown that three different blood pressure-lowering mechanisms of action combined in a triple therapy achieve greater blood pressure reductions and allow greater proportions of patients to achieve blood pressure control compared with dual therapies [5]. Triple therapies are generally well tolerated with adverse event profiles similar to dual regimens [6], and when prescribed as an SPC, simplify the management of hypertension through fewer drug combinations and fewer medication changes. Triple SPCs of different classes of drugs with complementary mechanisms of action may be particularly beneficial in countries with a high prevalence of hypertension and poor rate of blood pressure control, such as China.

The efficacy and safety of triple SPC of perindopril/indapamide/amlodipine versus individual free components have previously been studied in Caucasian [7] and Indian patients [8]. Nevertheless, it is important to determine the efficacy and safety of new therapies in different populations, as the characteristics may differ with regard to ambulatory blood pressure and others [9]. The aim of the present study was to evaluate the clinical efficacy and safety of a triple perindopril 5 mg/indapamide 1.25 mg/amlodipine 5 mg SPC versus a dual-pill combination of perindopril 4 mg/indapamide 1.25 mg plus amlodipine 5 mg over 6 months, in treated Chinese patients with uncontrolled hypertension. Perindopril tert-butylamine (erbumine) 4 mg (the salt formulation currently marketed in China) is bioequivalent to 5 mg of perindopril arginine.

## 方法 / Methods

Eligible patients were men or women of Asian origin aged ≥18 years with mild-to-moderate essential hypertension (office mean sitting systolic blood pressure [SBP] ≥140 to <180 mmHg and diastolic blood pressure [DBP] ≥90 to <110 mmHg) despite treatment with maximal dose antihypertensive monotherapy or starting dose dual therapy for at least 1 month prior to screening.

The main exclusion criteria were a body mass index (BMI) of >30 kg/m2; significant cerebrovascular, cardiovascular, renal, or hepatic disorders; diabetes; secondary hypertension; and any contraindications to treatment with perindopril, indapamide, and/or amlodipine.

This Phase III, national, multicenter, randomized, double-blind, noninferiority study was conducted in Chinese patients with mild-to-moderate hypertension between May 2019 and February 2022. The participants were representative of the general population and were recruited from both urban and rural areas.

The study was divided into a 1-month run-in period and 6-month double-blind treatment period.

After the initial screening, all eligible patients entered the active run-in phase, during which they received perindopril 4 mg/indapamide 1.25 mg SPC and a placebo capsule, both taken once daily in the morning before breakfast; all other antihypertensive medications were discontinued. Patients who completed the run-in period and met the entry criteria were randomized to an SPC of perindopril 5 mg/indapamide 1.25 mg/amlodipine 5 mg (Per/Ind/Aml) or a free combination comprising an SPC of perindopril 4 mg/indapamide 1.25 mg plus separate amlodipine 5 mg (Per/Ind + Aml), administered concomitantly for 6 months (Fig. 1). Randomization was performed using fixed, centralized randomization by an interactive response system (IRS), stratified according to the center. To maintain blind treatment allocation, patients randomized to the test drug arm also took one capsule of placebo each morning during the double-blind period.

Study design including uptitration of daily dose at 2 and 4 months of follow-up (M2, M4) for patients with uncontrolled office systolic/diastolic blood pressure at randomization (≥140/90 mmHg).

During the study, follow-up visits were conducted at months (M) 1, 2, 4, and 6. At M1, no titration was permitted, and patients with SBP/DBP ≥180/110 mmHg were withdrawn from the study. At M2, patients with SBP/DBP ≥180/110 mmHg were withdrawn from the study; those with SBP/DBP <140/90 mmHg continued at the same dose, and those with SBP/DBP ≥140/90 mmHg were uptitrated to the next dose of the treatment strategy (Per 10 mg/Ind 2.5 mg/Aml 5 mg or two pills of Per 4 mg/Ind 1.25 mg plus one pill of Aml 5 mg). At M4, patients with SBP/DBP <140/90 mmHg continued at the same dose, and those with SBP/DBP ≥140/90 mmHg were up-titrated to the next dose of the treatment strategy (Per 10 mg/Ind 2.5 mg/Aml 10 mg or two pills of Per 4 mg/Ind 1.25 mg plus two pills of Aml 5 mg).

All patients provided written informed consent prior to screening. The clinical study protocol was reviewed and approved by the Independent Ethics Committee or Institutional Review Board of each center. This study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT05820880).

The primary efficacy objective was to assess the noninferiority of Per/Ind/Aml in lowering office sitting SBP after 2 months of treatment compared to the free combination components (Per/Ind + Aml) in patients with essential hypertension, uncontrolled after 1 month of Per 4 mg/Ind 1.25 mg dual therapy.

The secondary objectives were to assess the effects of 2 months of treatment with SPC versus free combination on sitting DBP, uptitration efficacy, and office blood pressure control (SBP/DBP <140/90 mmHg). The effect of SPC and free combination on ambulatory blood pressure after 2 months of treatment was assessed in a subgroup of patients participating in 24-h ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM). These participants agreed to perform 24-h ABPM at baseline and at M2, and had the option of performing a 48-h ABPM at M6. In this group, baseline and M2 ABPM recordings started just before drug intake in the morning and ended after 24 h, shortly before taking the next drug dose. The purpose of the optional 48-h ABPM exploratory analysis at M6 was to mimic the effects of a missed dose in real life. The recording started just before drug intake in the morning, which corresponded to the last drug intake of the study. The recording ended after 48 h, and no study drug or other treatment for hypertension was taken the following morning (1 day with treatment omitted) and until ABPM removal.

For safety/tolerability evaluation, patients were examined for orthostatic hypotension, vital signs, and adverse events at all study visits. Complete clinical laboratory examinations (biochemical and hematological blood measurements) and 12-lead electrocardiography were performed at inclusion (M0) and at 6 months (M6). Simplified laboratory tests were performed during visits M1, M2, and M4. The number of events and the number and percentage of patients reporting at least one event, presented by primary system organ class and/or preferred term, were provided for adverse events and serious adverse events over the M0–M6 period.

At each visit, the patients were required to return to the entire treatment box along with the used and unused blisters. Treatment adherence was assessed by comparing the total number of capsules dispensed with the number of capsules returned at each visit.

The office blood pressure was measured using a validated automatic device, and each center used the same device at each visit. With the patient in a sitting position, three measurements were taken at 1–3 min intervals after at least 5 min of rest. Blood pressure values were calculated as the mean of the last two readings.

ABPM was performed for a minimum of 24 h before office visits M0 and M2 using a SpaceLabs Healthcare ambulatory blood pressure monitoring device (model 90207). Daytime measurements were defined as 6 am-10 pm and nighttime as 10 pm-6 am for data analyses. Moreover, to calculate the blood pressure values for real daytime and night-time periods, participants in the ABPM assessments were provided with a diary and requested to record information on daily activities such as getting up, taking study treatment, and going to bed.

The sample size was calculated for the primary endpoint of the study using a two-sided Student's t test for independent samples with a type 1 error of 5% and to achieve a statistical power of 90% for noninferiority of Per/Ind/Aml compared with Per/Ind + Aml. Assuming an expected difference between the groups at M2 of 0 mmHg, a standard deviation of 14 mmHg, and a noninferiority margin of 4 mmHg, it was estimated that 259 patients per group should be included in the study. After adjusting for an exclusion rate of 5% in randomized patients, the final sample size was fixed at 272 patients per treatment group.

Reductions in office sitting SBP from baseline were analyzed using analysis of covariance (ANCOVA), including the fixed categorical effects of treatment and center and baseline value as a covariate. Noninferiority was considered to be achieved at a one-sided significance level of 0.025 if the upper limit of the 95% confidence interval (CI) for this difference was below the noninferiority margin of 4 mmHg. The estimate of the difference, standard error of the estimate, two-sided 95% CI of the estimate, and the associated P-value of the noninferiority test were calculated.

All efficacy analyses on office blood pressure were performed in both the full analysis (all patients in the randomized set having taken at least one dose of randomized treatment and having a value at baseline and at least one postbaseline value of SBP over the M0–M2 period) and per protocol set (all patients in the full analysis set without relevant deviation(s) that could affect the evaluation of randomized treatment on the primary efficacy endpoint). Missing data were imputed using the last observation carried forward (LOCF) method. The ABPM exploratory analysis included all patients in the randomized set who had taken at least one dose of medication and had a valid mean 24-h SBP both at baseline and M2. Up-titration efficacy was assessed within groups using a paired Student's t-test on the change from the previous visit for quantitative endpoints and a McNemar test for quantitative endpoints in up-titrated patients.

All safety analyses (including frequency of adverse events, serious adverse events, and notable laboratory abnormalities) were performed in the safety set, which comprised all patients who received at least one dose of the double-blind study medication.

All statistical analyses were performed under the supervision of the Global Biometric Department of the Institut de Recherches Internationales Servier, using SAS/PC Software version 9.2 (SAS Institute, Cary, North Carolina, USA).

## 结果 / Results

A total of 1053 patients were selected to enter the run-in phase of the study during which they received the Per 4 mg/Ind 1.25 mg SPC. After 1 month of treatment, 532 patients were considered eligible for inclusion in the trial. The main reason for noninclusion was that blood pressure was controlled with run-in dual therapy. The patients were randomized: 262 to Per/Ind/Aml and 269 to Per/Ind + Aml. Of the randomized patients, 18.6% were withdrawn during the study (47 [17.9%] in the Per/Ind/Aml group versus 52 [19.3%] in the Per/Ind + Aml group), mostly for nonmedical reasons, including those related to the national lockdowns set up during the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic (Table 1). The full analysis set (FAS) comprised 520 patients, as 12 patients from the randomized set were excluded because of missing values for the primary efficacy endpoint at baseline and at M1 or M2. The per-protocol set comprised 433 patients (81.4% of the randomized patients) who completed the study (216 [82.1%] and 217 [80.7%] patients, respectively). No relevant between-group differences were detected in any of the patient characteristics. The ABPM dataset included 74 patients.

Disposition of study patients by treatment group

ABPM indicates ambulatory blood pressure monitoring.

Patient demographics and baseline characteristics were comparable between the two treatment groups (Table 2). Overall, the mean age of the patients was 55.7 ± 8.8 years, 60.7% were male, and the mean sitting SBP/DBP at baseline was 150.4/97.2 mmHg (Table 2). The mean duration of hypertension was slightly longer in the Per/Ind/Aml than in the Per/Ind + Aml group (9.8 ± 7.5 years versus 7.8 ± 6.6 years). The majority (96.8%) of patients had previously been treated for hypertension. Prior antihypertensive treatments comprised mainly calcium channel blockers (60.8% Per/Ind/Aml versus 61.7% Per/Ind + Aml), agents acting on the renin-angiotensin system (47.5% versus 45.7%, respectively), and diuretics (14.1% versus 11.2%, respectively).

Baseline and demographic characteristics of the study participants

The titration profiles showed no significant differences between the two randomization groups. At the M2 visit, 122 (51.3%) patients in the Per/Ind/Aml group and 117 (47.6%) in the Per/Ind + Aml were uptitrated to a higher dose. At the M4 visit, the corresponding numbers were 45 (20.5%) and 49 (22.1%), respectively.

The addition of Aml to the previous Per/Ind regimen led to a similar decrease in office sitting SBP in both groups after 2 months compared with baseline: −14.99 ± 14.46 mmHg with Per/Ind/Aml versus −14.49 ± 12.87 mmHg with Per/Ind + Aml. The predefined noninferiority margin of 4 mmHg was statistically demonstrated (one sided P value < 0.001), with an estimate (SE) of the adjusted between-group difference of 0.23 (1.08 mmHg, 95% CI −1.90 to 2.36).

Both treatment strategies were effective in reducing office DBP during the M0–M2 period. The decrease was similar in both treatment groups with a mean change of -8.56 ± 8.22 mmHg for Per/Ind/Aml versus −8.74 ± 7.74 mmHg for Per/Ind + Aml, with an estimate (SE) of the adjusted between-group difference of 0.32 (0.67 mmHg, 95% CI −1.00 to 1.64).

Uptitration to higher dosages of the triple-drug regimen provided additional benefits on blood pressure lowering and control, without relevant differences between the two treatment groups. In the 233 patients not controlled at M2 and uptitrated to the next Per/Ind/Aml (n = 118) or Per/Ind + Aml (n = 115) dose of the treatment strategy, decreases in office sitting SBP and DBP were observed in both groups over the M2–M4 period. For SBP, the estimate (SE) was −9.03 (1.10) mmHg for Per/Ind/Aml and −7.21 (1.17) mmHg for Per/Ind + Aml. The reductions for DBP were −5.93 (0.71) for Per/Ind/Aml and -4.14 (0.70) mmHg for Per/Ind + Aml.

Similarly, in the 94 patients (n = 45 and 49, respectively) not controlled at M4 and uptitrated to the next dose in the treatment strategy, decreases in office sitting SBP over the M4–M6 period were −6.08 (1.55) mmHg and −4.93 (1.72) mmHg, respectively, and decreases in office sitting DBP were −3.61 (0.90) mmHg and −4.51 (1.02) mmHg, respectively.

At M2, the proportion of patients achieving overall blood pressure control (SBP/DBP <140/90 mmHg) was similar between the two groups: 49.0% in the Per/Ind/Aml group and 52.4% in the Per/Ind + Aml group (estimate [SE] −0.14 (0.18); 95% CI −0.48 to 0.20). The proportion of patients with blood pressure control continued to increase at M4, 65.7%, and 65.1% of patients, respectively, a level that was maintained at M6 (Fig. 2).

Proportion of patients with office blood pressure control (systolic/diastolic blood pressure < 140/90 mmHg) at each time point in the two treatment arms.

Treatment adherence was high in both groups, at 96.5% and 98.9% in the Per/Ind/Aml and Per/Ind + Aml groups, respectively, for the entire duration of the study.

A total of 74 patients took part in the ABPM analysis: 33 Per/Ind/Aml and 41 Per/Ind + Aml. Both groups showed a reduction in mean ambulatory SBP and DBP over 24 h at M2 compared with M0 (Fig. 3). For SBP, the reductions were −12.85 ± 15.41 mmHg and −11.36 ± 10.61 mmHg, respectively. For DBP, the reductions were −7.69 ± 9.12 mmHg and −6.92 ± 6.35 mmHg, respectively. In both groups, blood pressure reductions were maintained over both the daytime and nighttime periods, providing 24-h blood pressure control (Table 3). The ABPM results confirmed the robust decreases observed in office blood pressure measurements and that these were a result of marked reductions in both daytime and nighttime values.

A total of 30 patients participated in the exploratory 48-h ABPM analysis at M6 : 13 with Per/Ind/Aml and 17 with Per/Ind + Aml. In both groups, the mean ambulatory SBP and DBP over the 24−48 h period during which no study drug was administered were similar to those observed over the first 24 h (Table 4). Thus, despite 1-day of treatment omission, the effect of both treatments on ambulatory blood pressure was maintained, with no rebound effect.

Exploratory 48 h ambulatory blood pressure analysis at six months of follow-up (M6) illustrating systolic (SBP) and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) values with one dose intake at the start and dose omission on day 2

Both treatments were well tolerated with similar adverse event profiles. No patient reported COVID-19 infection as an adverse event during this study. During the M0–M6 period, treatment emergent adverse events (TEAEs) were reported with a similar frequency in both groups: 67.2% of patients in the Per/Ind/Aml group versus 65.8% in the Per/Ind + Aml group. The proportion of patients reporting treatment-related TEAEs was also similar in both groups: 42.0% in the Per/Ind/Aml group versus 40.1% in the Per/Ind + Aml group. The most frequently reported treatment-related TEAEs had a similar incidence in both groups and were in line with those described in the Summary of Product Characteristics for the individual drug components: hyperuricemia and hypokalemia with indapamide and cough with perindopril. Potentially clinically significant abnormal (PCSA) values of triglycerides (15 patients [5.9%] and 11 patients [4.2%] in the Per/Ind/Aml and Per/Ind + Aml groups, respectively) and gamma glutamyl transferase (4 patients [1.6%] and 7 patients [2.7%], respectively) were observed without significant differences between the groups. Emergent PCSA hematological values were not detected in the Per/Ind/Aml group and were sparse in the Per/Ind + Aml group.

It should be noted that peripheral edema (expected with amlodipine and known to be dose-dependent) and hypotension appeared in only a few cases. Two cases of orthostatic hypotension were reported, one in each group.

Thirteen serious TEAEs were reported in 12 (4.6%) patients in the Per/Ind/Aml group and 13 serious TEAEs were reported in 9 (3.3%) patients in the Per/Ind + Aml group. Three serious TEAEs in two patients were considered treatment-related (all in the Per/Ind + Aml group): cerebral infarction, demyelination, and cough-variant asthma. TEAEs leading to treatment withdrawal were reported in eight (3.1%) patients in the Per/Ind/Aml group and in 15 (5.6%) patients in the Per/Ind + Aml group. There were no clinically relevant changes or differences between groups in vital signs (weight, BMI, and heart rate) during the M0-M6 period.

## 讨论 / Discussion

This Phase III study was conducted to assess whether a triple SPC of Per/Ind/Aml was as effective as the same components prescribed as two pills (Per/Ind + Aml) in Chinese patients with blood pressure not controlled with Per/Ind SPC dual therapy. The results showed that the Per/Ind/Aml SPC led to a significant decrease in office sitting SBP at 2 months. Similar results were obtained with the two-pill combination of Per/Ind + Aml. Noninferiority of the primary endpoint was demonstrated. The importance of this SBP reduction is highlighted by a recent report from the Blood Pressure–Lowering Treatment Trialists’ Collaboration (BPLTTC), which showed that rates of major cardiovascular events were reduced by 10% per 5 mmHg reduction in SBP [10]. In the present study, each dose uptitration resulted in rapid and clinically significant decreases in office blood pressure. The efficacy results for Per/Ind/Aml and Per/Ind + Aml on the secondary endpoints including changes in office sitting DBP and blood pressure control, were in line with those observed for the primary endpoint while demonstrating a similar safety and tolerability profile. These results confirm that findings with the Per/Ind/Aml SPC in other populations [7,8] are also applicable to Chinese patients with hypertension.

SPC therapy is the preferred strategy for initial two-drug combination treatment of hypertension and for three-drug combination therapy because the reduction in pill burden has been shown to enhance adherence to multiple-agent antihypertensive therapy [2,11,12]. Data from real-world studies provide the best signal of whether a combination therapy given as a single pill will have advantages compared with the same drugs given as individual pills, because unlike in clinical trials, patients are not closely monitored and are not reminded to take their medication. A recent real-world analysis from Italy compared medication adherence and the incidence of cardiovascular outcomes between two cohorts of patients treated with Per/Ind/Aml as a multiple-pill or single-pill combination in over 40 000 patients between 2010 and 2020 [13]. Adherence to medication (defined as proportion of days covered ≥80%) was significantly better in the SPC cohort (59.9% versus 26.9%, P < 0.001). This translated into a lower mortality rate (29.9 versus 33.7 per 1000-person years, P < 0.05) and reduction in the composite endpoint of cardiovascular events and mortality (105.8 versus 139.0 per 1000-person years, P < 0.001).

The current study included an ABPM subgroup, as this is the most accurate means of characterizing blood pressure variability and assessing the effectiveness of antihypertensive therapy throughout the 24-h period [14]. For patients requiring a triple combination, European guidelines recommend an ACE inhibitor or ARB with a calcium channel blocker and a thiazide or thiazide-like diuretic [1]. Each component of SPC should have a sufficient duration of action to provide 24-h blood-pressure lowering activity to ensure that once-daily dosing is effective. In the current study, the reduction in blood pressure with both treatments was sustained over the full 24 h. This confirms that these drugs have a long half-life and are suitable for once-daily dosing [15]. Although only 30 patients participated in the exploratory 48-h ABPM analysis, the mean ambulatory blood pressure over the 24–48 h period (following drug omission) was similar to that observed over the first 24 h in both treatment groups. To our knowledge, data on maintained blood pressure lowering over 48 h after intake do not exist for other SPC. Omission of an occasional dose of antihypertensive therapy is common in real life [16]. These results confirmed the sustained blood pressure-lowering effects of a perindopril, indapamide, and amlodipine combination, and the absence of a withdrawal/blood pressure overshoot effect after a missed dose. Per/Ind/Aml is currently the only triple-therapy SPC that includes an ACE inhibitor and thiazide-like diuretic. In this regard, Per/Ind/Aml offers advantages over triple SPCs containing hydrochlorothiazide as the diuretic component, the latter having a half-life of 6–15 h compared to 14–24 h for indapamide [17].

A combination of antihypertensive agents is generally required to reach the target blood pressure, regardless of whether it is defined as 140/90 or 130/80 mmHg. However, a recent retrospective analysis of the prescribing practices for Chinese patients with a diagnosis of hypertension who received at least one prescription for antihypertensive therapy between January 2021 and December 2021 revealed that only approximately 19% of the population were receiving a dual combination and 3% a three-drug combination [18]. Nationwide data from the May Measurement Month (MMM) China project have confirmed the underuse of combination therapies, which was reported by only a quarter (26.7%) of the 19 523 treated hypertensive patients who volunteered detailed information [19]. The use of SPC was reported to be only 8.5%.

In the current trial, both treatment regimens were well tolerated, with similar adverse event profiles even at the highest doses. Previous comparisons of Per/Ind/Aml SPC with its free components have also not raised any concerns regarding an increased rate of adverse events [7,20]. This included no increase in the incidence of dry cough, which is common in Asian patients [21]. Tolerability concerns can lead to a tendency among physicians to prescribe lower than the recommended doses of antihypertensive drugs [22]. The results of this study demonstrated the uptitration efficacy of the triple combination with no increased risk of adverse effects. Combining drugs with synergistic physiological and pharmacological actions can counterbalance any adverse effects of the individual components [3,23]. In addition, the use of combination antihypertensive therapy leads to the attainment of blood pressure control with lower doses of each medication, with a consequent reduction in the risk of dose-related adverse effects.

This study is the first to demonstrate the noninferiority of a Per/Ind/Aml SPC compared to an equivalent two-pill strategy in Chinese patients with mild-to-moderate hypertension for achieving a blood pressure target of <140/90 mmHg. As the primary goal of therapy is now to lower office blood pressure to <130/80 mmHg in most patients, future research should also aim to demonstrate noninferiority of the two treatment strategies for achieving this stricter blood pressure target. From January 2020, the study was affected by the COVID-19 pandemic, which had an impact on the recruitment of patients and resulted in a number of protocol deviations (missing blood sample results and blood pressure readings), albeit with a similar frequency between treatment groups and without a major impact on the outcome of the study.

ABPM provided data on several important parameters that cannot be obtained with other measures including 24-h blood pressure, blood pressure variability, and sustained antihypertensive effect. Despite the small number of patients in the ABPM substudy, the results confirmed the blood pressure reduction observed with office blood pressure measurement in both groups showing a sustained decrease throughout the 24-h period and no rebound effect in the event of missed medication. Of course, this is a sub-study involving a small number of patients, and it would be desirable to confirm its results in specific studies.

## 结论 / Conclusion

Adequate treatment of hypertension in primary care plays a key role in addressing the burden of hypertension in China. The Per/Ind/Aml triple SPC provided significant reductions in office blood pressure, demonstrating noninferiority to the same components administered as two pills. Both treatments were associated with significant improvements in blood pressure control, sustained 24 h blood-pressure lowering and good tolerability.
