## English Abstract

Sickle cell disease (SCD) is a genetic blood disorder associated with chronic cardiovascular and pulmonary complications, including pulmonary hypertension (PHT), venous thromboembolism (VTE), and cardiac involvement (CI), all of which contribute significantly to premature mortality. Despite their clinical importance, the burden of these conditions in adolescents and adults with SCD remains poorly characterized.

We conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis to evaluate the prevalence and impact of these complications in SCD patients aged ≥ 10 years. Following PRISMA guidelines, we searched MEDLINE/PubMed, Web of Science, and Scopus for observational studies reporting prevalence or incidence. We did not apply any date or time restrictions to the literature search. Pooled estimates were calculated using random-effects meta-analysis.

The studies yielded from the search were published between January 1, 1974, and October 12, 2023. Seventy-nine studies met the inclusion criteria, of which fifty-nine were included in the meta-analysis. The pooled prevalence estimates were as follows: PHT 30% (95% CI 26–34), VTE 13% (9–18), and CI 24% (17–33), with substantial heterogeneity (I2 > 90%) among the studies. PHT was primarily diagnosed via Doppler echocardiography, while VTE was identified through ultrasonography or CT angiography. CI manifestations included diastolic dysfunction and structural abnormalities. These complications were strongly associated with increased mortality and functional impairment.

Chronic cardiovascular and pulmonary complications are highly prevalent in adolescents and adults with SCD, leading to significant morbidity and mortality. Routine screening and early intervention are essential to mitigate disease progression and improve outcomes. Future research should focus on standardized diagnostic criteria and targeted therapies to reduce the burden of these conditions in SCD.

## 背景 / Background

Sickle cell disease (SCD) is a genetic blood disorder characterized by misshapen, crescent-shaped red blood cells and is associated with a range of complications that vary in both frequency and severity [1, 2]. These complications, which include anemia, chronic pain, organ damage, and susceptibility to infections, significantly impact the lives of individuals with SCD [2, 3]. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), an estimated 300,000 children are born with SCD each year, with about 75% of cases occurring in sub-Saharan Africa [4]. The Global Burden of Diseases, Injuries, and Risk Factors Study (GBD) indicates that between 2000 and 2021, the number of babies born with SCD globally increased by 13.7% (95% uncertainty interval: 11.1–16.5) to 515,000 (ranging from 425,000 to 614,000) births [5]. The WHO emphasizes that effective management strategies and preventive programs can potentially reduce the disease burden [5]. Andong (2017) reported that late diagnosis and chronic multisystem complications contributed to the low quality of life of patients living with SCD [2]. As a chronic illness with multiple comorbidities, SCD is recognized as both a non-communicable disease (NCD) and a significant contributor to NCD-related mortality [6, 7].

Cardiovascular diseases, including heart disease, stroke, arterial and venous thrombosis, as well as chronic lung diseases such as pulmonary hypertension (PHT), kidney disease, and anemia, are among the leading causes of mortality in individuals with SCD. These conditions are also leading contributors to the global burden of NCDs, which in turn account for significant morbidity and premature deaths globally [8]. As the number of individuals living with SCD continues to increase, particularly in the Caribbean and sub-Saharan Africa, due to population growth and improved survival rates resulting from advancements in medical care, the burden of chronic diseases is also expected to increase [9].

Kanter and Kruse-Jarres (2013) reported that children and adults with SCD commonly experience comparable symptoms and challenges. However, with advancing age, the risk of chronic complications and disease progression increases, potentially contributing to early mortality [10]. These complications encompass a wide array of conditions, including avascular necrosis, sickle retinopathy, leg ulcers, pulmonary hypertension, nephropathy, thromboembolic complications, cardiac failure, transfusional iron overload, and decreased bone density [10–12]. The transformation of SCD from a severe life-threatening disease of childhood into a chronic, lifelong condition through the use of disease-modifying treatments imposes increasingly new challenges to healthcare providers and patients [13]. Among these challenges are physical, psychosocial, and emotional difficulties encountered by children with SCD. The need for chronic continuous treatments and regular multidisciplinary monitoring increases the social and emotional burden on patients and an increasing population of children with SCD who require long-term care [13].

This systematic review evaluates the burden and outcomes of chronic cardiovascular and pulmonary complications affecting adolescents (aged 10 to 19, as defined by the WHO) [14] and adults with SCD. Through a comprehensive search strategy and analysis of existing literature, we aimed to identify the prevalence and/or incidence of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications and demonstrate their impact on health outcomes among adolescents and adults with SCD. Mortality and low functional capacity rates arising from cardiovascular and pulmonary complications remain high in individuals with SCD [15]. Therefore, understanding the prevalence and clinical patterns of chronic cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in this population is crucial for guiding clinical practice and informing policy, facilitating the development of appropriate strategies and programs to alleviate disease burden and improve outcomes.

## 方法 / Methods

This review was registered with the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO), registration number CRD42023474623 (Additional file 1).

The Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-analysis (PRISMA) served as a template for reporting the present review. The template is displayed in Additional file 2 [16].

Two reviewers (AN and NMK) developed a comprehensive literature search strategy. Major databases searched included MEDLINE/PubMed, Web of Science, and Scopus. No date or time restrictions were applied to the literature search. The search syntax for the PubMed database is provided in Additional file 3. This syntax combines MeSH subheadings and free-text keywords for the database search. AN searched for studies using keywords including sickle cell, burden, outcome, prevalence, incidence, and mortality. Additionally, chronic diseases were further delineated to include common cardiovascular and pulmonary complications, such as PHT, left ventricular diastolic dysfunction (LVDD), dysrhythmias, cardiomyopathy, myocardial infarction, venous thromboembolism (VTE), airway hyperreactivity, and sleep-disordered breathing.

Boolean operators ‘AND’ and ‘OR’ were employed to incorporate search results for different concepts and synonyms within each concept. All identified articles were imported into the Rayyan software for further review [17].

The inclusion criteria for our study were as follows:i.Sickle cell disease patients (adolescents and adults).ii.Participants aged 10 years and above (adolescents 10–19 years, adults 20 + years).iii.Observational studies, including cross-sectional, cohort, and case-control designs.iv.Studies investigating cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in SCD.v.Availability of data on the prevalence and/or incidence of chronic cardiovascular or pulmonary complications.vi.Diagnosis of chronic diseases reported by standard methods.

Eligible studies were included irrespective of language, year, or setting provided a full-text article was available in English. However, one Russian article was excluded due to the absence of a full-text English version for data extraction despite its abstract being published in English.

Title and abstract screening were conducted independently by two authors (NMK and SMNB) using Rayyan [17], with any conflicts resolved by AN. All studies that met the inclusion criteria underwent full-text review by two reviewers (NMK and SMNB), with discrepancies resolved through consensus. Overall agreement between the reviewers ranged from 70 to 80%. The studies selected for inclusion in the narrative synthesis were imported into Zotero for reference management.

The Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) critical appraisal checklist for cohort, cross-sectional, and case-control studies was utilized to assess the quality of included observational studies [18]. This checklist assesses 11 critical aspects for cohort studies, 8 for cross-sectional studies, and 10 for case-control studies. Two reviewers, NMK and SMNB, made the final decisions regarding study quality. Since there was no validation study to provide a cutoff score for rating study quality, a scoring system was devised for each tool. A well-conducted, high-quality study achieved scores of 11, 8, and 10 for cohort, cross-sectional, and case-control studies.

Data extraction was conducted in alignment with the study objectives and inclusion criteria. Relevant data was extracted concurrently by two reviewers, NMK and SMNB, using a standardized data extraction sheet in both Microsoft Excel and Word. The data extracted included the citation, study design, study location and setting, sample size, participant demographic information, chronic disease identified, outcome and measure of association, estimates of prevalence/incidence and associated factors, and other major findings. Specific data extracted included age and gender for demography and SCD subgroups such as hemoglobin (Hb) SS, HbSC disease, and sickle beta zero thalassemia (HbS β0-thal).

We performed a descriptive synthesis of all the studies included in the review, including synthesis of (a) PRISMA Flow, (b) a quality assessment using JBI tools, (c) a description of the study characteristics and findings, and (d) a narrative synthesis. Studies contributing to the Summary of Findings Table are detailed in Additional file 4, along with their references. Statistical analysis was conducted using R software version 4.4.1. The Microsoft Excel sheet containing data items was imported into R for analysis [19]. Studies were categorized into four groups: PHT, VTE, pulmonary diseases, and cardiac involvement (CI). Pooled prevalence for PHT, VTE, CI, and pulmonary disease was calculated by performing a meta-analysis of proportions using the DerSimonian–Laird random-effects model, allowing for more generalizable conclusions. Logit transformation was employed to transform the observed proportions and bring skewed data closer to a normal distribution. PHT, VTE, and CI prevalence were illustrated using a forest plot.

Study heterogeneity was examined using the Cochrane Q test and quantified using I2 values. Heterogeneity thresholds of 50% and 75% were used to indicate moderate and high levels, respectively [19]. The Baujat plot identified studies contributing to heterogeneity or outliers [20], while the leave-one-out analysis assessed each study’s influence on the overall summary proportion. A funnel plot and Egger’s regression test were used to assess for publication bias. A symmetry funnel plot suggested no publication bias, whereas an asymmetrical one indicated the presence of publication bias. If publication bias was confirmed, a trim-and-fill analysis was performed.

## 结果 / Results

The database search yielded 1174 records: 256 were obtained from PubMed, 504 from Web of Science, and 414 from Scopus. Additionally, one record was identified through a manual screening of citations. The studies yielded from the search were published between January 1, 1974, and October 12, 2023. A total of 541 duplicate records were removed, leaving 634 records for screening. After thoroughly screening titles and abstracts, we found that 517 records did not meet the criteria, and these were excluded. During this process, we identified one additional duplicate article, which may have been overlooked due to variations in some fields that the Rayyan software could not detect. Among 117 records identified for further review, 115 full articles were retrieved. Two articles were not retrieved and were excluded due to the unavailability of full text, despite efforts to retrieve them through database searches and direct communication with the authors, who did not respond. After assessing the full texts of the remaining 115 articles for eligibility, 36 were excluded. The 36 studies were excluded due to wrong study design, absence of relevant outcomes, wrong study populations, and age groups, small sample sizes, exclusive biomarker measurement (e.g., NT-proBNP), or potential bias from questionnaire-based assessments. We defined a “wrong study population” and “age group” as studies focused on chronic diseases in individuals with sickle cell trait or in children below age 10. Additionally, we categorized studies with fewer than 30 participants in each cohort as having a “small sample size.”

Seventy-nine studies were included in the qualitative synthesis, of which 59 were eligible and included in the meta-analysis. The remaining 20 studies were excluded from the meta-analysis for various reasons, including reporting VTE in sickle cell trait with unspecified genotypes, unclear genotype subgroups, TRV measurement during an acute episode, use of large population datasets, and unrelated biomarker assessments. Cardiac complications were diagnosed using Doppler echocardiography (ECHO). While right heart catheterization is considered the gold standard for diagnosing PHT, most studies did not perform this procedure. We postulated that this was due to the expense and unavailability of the procedure in many settings or anticipation of post-procedure SCD-adverse events. According to Maggio RD (2018), the rate of post-cardiac catheterization SCD-adverse events was reported to be 10% [22]. Therefore, noninvasive Doppler ECHO was recommended as the screening tool to suggest a diagnosis of PHT and establish risk categories based on peak TRV.

In studies investigating VTE, including isolated deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or pulmonary embolism (PE), the standard diagnostic tests used to confirm a suspected VTE included magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), compression ultrasonography for DVT or computed tomography pulmonary angiography (CTPA) for PE. Additionally, other pulmonary complications, including asthma and pulmonary dysfunction, were assessed using spirometry.

In most studies, mortality or increased risk of death was the main outcome associated with chronic diseases. However, not all studies included in the analysis reported specific outcomes related to the chronic diseases they investigated. In addition to mortality, reduced exercise capacity was a significant outcome of PHT. Studies evaluating VTE reported recurrent VTE, increased healthcare utilization and hospital stay, and maternal morbidity, among other outcomes. Additional cardiovascular and pulmonary outcomes noted included increased overall mortality, maternal mortality, and the development of cor pulmonale.

A meta-analysis focusing on specific pulmonary diseases, including pulmonary dysfunction and restrictive and obstructive lung disease, yielded only three studies after excluding one potential outlier. Our study highlights a high prevalence and burden of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications, including PHT, VTE, and CI in adolescents and adults with SCD. The frequent overlap of these conditions, such as arrhythmias emerging in SCD patients with pre-existing cardiac disease or PHT, underscores the importance of prioritizing routine screening and proactive monitoring to mitigate progression, reduce hospitalization, and ultimately improve patient outcomes.

## 讨论 / Discussion

This systematic review evaluated the prevalence of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in adolescents and adults with SCD and their impact on health outcomes. Our review included 79 observational studies in narrative synthesis and 59 in quantitative analysis. Among the 79 studies, 74 were classified as low-risk for bias, while five were deemed moderate-risk. Because studies were characterized according to various chronic diseases and geographical regions, significant heterogeneity was observed in all estimates (I2 > 65%). This heterogeneity likely resulted from differences in clinical settings, sample characteristics, and outcome measures used. As a result, we employed a random effects model to obtain more conservative pooled estimates.

PHT, VTE, and CI are common complications that affect patients with SCD [23]. The pathophysiology of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in SCD is multifactorial. Intravascular hemolysis releases free hemoglobin, which depletes endothelial nitric oxide, a potent vasodilator, reducing its bioavailability and promoting vasculopathy and PHT [24]. Elevated soluble adhesion molecules (ICAM-1, VCAM-1, selectins) and pro-inflammatory cytokines (tissue necrosis factor, endothelin-1, interleukin-10) promote the adhesion of red blood cells, leucocytes, and platelets to the vascular endothelium, contributing to microvascular occlusion [25]. Activated platelets and endothelial tissue necrosis factor expression enhance the hypercoagulable stage [24].

In addition, ischemia–reperfusion injury, endothelial dysfunction, chronic inflammation, and oxidative stress contribute to myocardial injury through activation of both myocytes and non-myocytes [26]. Dysregulation of the renin–angiotensin system further exacerbates cardiac complications [24].

PHT was assessed using an elevated TRV measured by Doppler ECHO. ECHO remains the primary noninvasive screening tool for diagnosing PHT in SCD. However, its dependence on TRV and susceptibility to operator expertise, patient-related variability, and coexisting cardiac conditions limit its diagnostic accuracy, particularly in distinguishing pre- and post-capillary PHT. Because our prevalence estimates of PHT in adolescents and adults with SCD were based mainly on reported ECHO findings, variability in measurements can lead to under- and overdiagnosis of PHT. We recommend RHC to confirm PHT and standardized research protocols to reduce variability in assessing this patient population.

The final pooled prevalence of PHT was 30% (95% CI 26–34), aligning with findings from previous observational studies on PHT in SCD [27, 28]. Notably, Elfaki YA and colleagues recently reported a similar prevalence of 29% among a cohort of Sudanese patients with SCD [29], with even higher PHT rates documented by Klings ES and Fonseca GH [30, 31]. According to a review by Ford and Gillard, the reported prevalence of PHT in adults with SCD ranges from 25 to 40% [32]. In adult patients, the prevalence of PHT is significant and increases the risk of death [27, 33]. Additionally, PHT is associated with severe limitations in exercise capacity [34].

Studies evaluating PHT in children, including adolescents, reported a high incidence of PHT, although it was comparatively lower than that observed in adults. Sahoo et al. reported a PHT prevalence of 20.4% in children aged 5 to 14 years [35], similar to the 22.9% reported by Sokunbi et al. in children aged 5 to 18 years in Nigeria [36]. Comparably, a systematic review by Hariharan VS evaluating PHT among children and adolescents with SCA reported a prevalence of 22% [37]. In children receiving hydroxyurea, the prevalence of PHT was low [38]. Khargekar and colleagues demonstrated that a hydroxyurea dose above 20 mg/kg/day, with a mean rise in HbF of 18.46% post-hydroxyurea therapy, was beneficial in reducing TRV [39]. Klings ES et al. highlighted that in children ≥ 8 years, the TRV determined morbidity risk rather than mortality risk, providing a baseline for future comparisons [40].

In leave-one-out sensitivity analysis, excluding any individual studies did not substantially change the pooled prevalence of PHT, indicating robust findings. However, studies by Oni OO et al. [41], Musa BM et al. [42], and Mehari A et al. [43] produced the largest, although still modest, changes in the pooled estimate. These studies were influential primarily because they reported relatively extreme PHT prevalence estimates, were conducted in high-burden settings, and/or had smaller sample sizes, which increased their effect on both the pooled prevalence and the observed heterogeneity.

We also employed a logistic regression model that included gender and region to examine sources of heterogeneity in reported PHT prevalence. The higher prevalence observed in high-income settings (the USA, the UK, parts of North Africa) may reflect greater diagnostic intensity, routine ECHO screening, and more standardized definitions of PHT. In contrast, lower estimates from West Africa likely reflect underdiagnosis, limited access to ECHO, survivorship bias, and shorter life expectancy in this setting.

Furthermore, studies with a higher proportion of female participants generally reported lower PHT prevalence, suggesting a possible male predominance; however, this association did not reach statistical significance. Overall, substantial residual heterogeneity remained, indicating that other unmeasured factors, such as diagnostic criteria, disease severity, and healthcare access, likely contribute to the observed variability.

VTE is a frequent complication of SCD. Several studies have highlighted the substantial prevalence of VTE in patients with SCD, with rates fluctuating between 8 and 25% [44–46]. In a longitudinal chart review of a cohort of patients, Lawrence and colleagues suggested that patients with VTE had greater SCD severity, evidenced by increased frequency of vaso-occlusive events and healthcare utilization [47]. Our study reported a pooled prevalence of 13% (95% CI 9–18), similar to the rate of 12.5% (95% CI 11.5–13.6) reported by Brunson and colleagues among patients with SCD by age 40 [46].

In the Cooperative Study of Sickle Cell Disease (CSSCD), the incidence of first VTE in patients aged ≥ 15 years was 11.3% (95% CI 8.3–15.3), with a higher risk of death reported among SCD patients with a history of VTE than those without [48]. It is worth noting that while the prevalence of VTE is reportedly high in this patient population, other studies have demonstrated low numbers of VTE events. Ajayi and colleagues reported a lower prevalence of 8.2% based on a medical record review of 783 individuals with SCD [45]. At an academic center in Saudi Arabia, VTE events complicated 3% of hospitalizations involving patients with SCD [49]. A substantially lower VTE prevalence of 2% was reported among adult patients with SCD in Nigeria [50]. Light J et al. investigated the impact of hormonal contraception (HC) on the risk of VTE in women with SCD. Among 219 women who used HC, 38 out of 184 (20.6%) experienced a VTE while actively using HC, whereas 20 out of 151 (13.2%) women who were never exposed to HC experienced a VTE. The study hypothesized that increased thrombotic risk was more likely influenced by disease severity than HC exposure [51].

In the leave-one-out sensitivity analysis for VTE, omitting either Okoye et al. (2022) [50] or Alkindi et al. (2021) [44] changed the pooled VTE prevalence. The influence of the two studies in the leave-one-out analysis likely reflects differences in study methods and patient populations. Okoye et al. examined a younger cohort with shorter follow-up and reported an unusually low VTE prevalence, while Alkindi et al. used a case-control design enriched for VTE cases, thereby increasing heterogeneity (I2).

CI is a recognized complication in patients with SCD. The prevalence and types of cardiac manifestations vary based on the specific condition and population studied. While systolic heart dysfunction is relatively rare in SCD patients, diastolic dysfunction is more prevalent and serves as an indicator of severe disease and a high risk of mortality [52]. A study by Lopes et al. demonstrated that ventricular hypertrophy and diastolic dysfunction were among the main cardiac alterations [53]. Our analysis yielded a CI prevalence of 24% (95% CI 17–33), which was notably high. An autopsy study of 306 patients revealed that CI was common, with cardiomegaly, myocardial micro-infarcts, and heart failure seen in 58%, 20%, and 10% of cases, respectively. Clinical manifestations were reported in 25% of patients, although autopsy findings suggested a high prevalence of 75%, raising concerns about an underestimated degree of CI [54].

Damy and colleagues evaluated ECHO abnormalities in adult patients with SCD. They reported a high prevalence of CI with left ventricular dilation in 35%, left atrium dilation in 78%, and cardiac output elevation in 23% [55]. However, at a single tertiary center for SCD in the UK, Natarajan and colleagues found that among patients who underwent a transthoracic ECHO, 17.5% had left atrium dilation, 11.3% had valvular heart disease, and 5.34% had left ventricular cavity dilation [56]. These values were lower than those reported by Damy and colleagues.

The studies compiled herein showed a substantial frequency of cardiac abnormalities in patients with SCD. The development of a cardiac abnormality was closely associated with a pre-existing cardiac condition [52, 57]. Bahashwan et al. reported a relationship between PHT and arrhythmias, highlighting that although arrhythmias are relatively uncommon in patients with SCD, they were observed in those at high risk of PHT. This finding emphasizes the importance of screening for arrhythmias in this patient subgroup, as early detection may help reduce mortality [58].

Given these findings, a CI prevalence of 24% in patients with SCD was within the range reported in previous studies, underscoring the importance of regular cardiovascular assessment in this population. Regular cardiovascular assessment can help detect and identify PHT and arrhythmias early, monitor left ventricular dysfunction, evaluate exercise capacity, prevent strokes, and inform treatment strategies.

In our study, the most common cardiac and pulmonary complications were left heart disease, diastolic dysfunction, cardiac dilatation, heart failure, and PHT. Less frequent but clinically relevant complications included myocardial infarction, mitral valve regurgitation, coronary artery ectasia, non-rheumatic valvular heart disease, restrictive and obstructive lung disease, and PE.

The prevalence of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in SCD varies with age, sex, and disease severity. Pulmonary abnormalities may appear in early childhood but become more pronounced in adolescents and adults, who are at greater risk for chronic lung disease and PHT [59]. Although SCD has no predilection for either sex regarding its inheritance, studies suggest males are more vulnerable to complications, including skin ulcers, respiratory, musculoskeletal, and cardiovascular complications. Females more commonly experience anxiety and depression, as well as autoimmune conditions [60, 61]. Individuals with severe genotypes, such as HbSS, are at the highest risk for these complications [62].

Risk factors and lifestyle aspects that contribute to the development or worsening of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in SCD are closely linked to the underlying disease pathology. These factors include chronic hemolytic anemia, recurrent vaso-occlusive episodes, hypoxia, infections, and pre-existing chronic conditions [52]. Lifestyle and health-related factors, such as smoking, poor nutrition, dehydration, underuse of hydroxyurea, limited access to routine screening, and delayed diagnosis, play a significant role in exacerbating these complications [63].

Predictive indicators of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in SCD include clinical symptoms and diagnostic test findings. Pulmonary complications often present with recurrent respiratory symptoms such as wheezing, exertional dyspnea, hypoxemia, exercise intolerance, and a personal history of asthma, allergies, or atopy [64]. Laboratory indicators include abnormal pulmonary function tests and elevated hemolysis markers such as LDH [65]. PHT may be suggested clinically by dyspnea on exertion and signs of heart failure, including elevated jugular venous pressure, a loud P2, and peripheral edema. Doppler ECHO, the 6-min walk test, and NT-proBNP are valuable in screening PHT in patients with SCD. Additionally, elevated serum creatinine, proteinuria, and chronic kidney disease are associated with increased cardiovascular risk [52]. More recently, biomarkers and imaging modalities such as serum ferritin, cardiac MRI with gadolinium enhancement, and T1 mapping are being incorporated into the assessment of sickle cell cardiomyopathy [66].

Current treatments help reduce the hemolytic and vaso-occlusive burden in SCD, but their direct impact on cardiovascular and pulmonary complications remains limited, particularly once organ damage has occurred. Hydroxyurea, a well-studied cytostatic agent, increases HbF levels and has demonstrated clinical benefit in reducing complications [67]. A 2024 meta-analysis showed that a daily dose of 20mg/kg significantly increased HbF and conferred protective effects when initiated early, helping to prevent cerebral, renal, and splenic injury [39]. Additional pharmacological treatments such as Voxelotor, Crizanlizumab, and L-glutamine offer complementary mechanisms by inhibiting hemoglobin polymerization, reducing vaso-occlusive episodes, and decreasing oxidative stress [9].

Significant global disparities hinder the management of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in SCD. In low-resource settings, limited access to routine screening and inconsistent interpretation of findings delay timely diagnosis and intervention. While the NIH and American Society of Hematology guidelines advise against routine pulmonary function testing, screening is recommended as part of diagnostic evaluation when respiratory symptoms are present [68]. Similarly, the limited availability of ECHO, RHC, and longitudinal data impedes early detection of PHT and cardiomyopathy [64]. Access to advanced therapies, such as hydroxyurea, stem cell transplantation, and gene therapy, is limited by high cost [69]. Adjuvant options like chronic red cell exchange are frequently unavailable, and poor adherence to care and follow-up further delays diagnosis and long-term care, worsening outcomes in high-burden countries [70].

Reducing the burden of cardiovascular and pulmonary complications requires a comprehensive approach. Preventive measures include prophylactic antibiotics, vaccinations, regular health follow-ups, and lifestyle changes such as staying hydrated, managing exercise intensity, and avoiding extreme temperatures. Effective pain management and regular blood transfusions are crucial for managing SCD crises and preventing severe complications [71].

Our study has several limitations. The included studies varied in clinical settings, sample characteristics, and outcome measures, resulting in significant heterogeneity. This variability limited the analysis of outcomes and meaningful subgroup comparisons. Although meta-regression was conducted to explore sources of heterogeneity in PHT prevalence, analyses were limited to region and gender because of inconsistent reporting of age and genotype across studies. As with all study-level meta-regression, findings are subject to ecological bias, and residual heterogeneity remained substantial, limiting causal inference.

In addition to limitations affecting prevalence estimates, a quantitative synthesis of morbidity and mortality data was not possible. Although some studies included reported associations using hazard ratios, risk ratios, or odds ratios, these estimates were based on heterogeneous outcome definitions, varying follow-up durations, and inconsistent adjustment of confounders. Furthermore, the attribution of mortality was uncertain, as some reported deaths may not have been directly related to the chronic complication under evaluation but to intercurrent factors, such as maternal mortality in severe SCD. Consequently, meaningful pooling of effect estimates was not possible, and morbidity and mortality outcomes were summarized narratively.

The studies included in the review assessed the prevalence of chronic complications in adolescents and adults with SCD. However, most did not explicitly differentiate findings between the two groups, limiting the ability to draw definitive conclusions about age-related differences in prevalence. Notably, a study by Suell MN (2005) focused on patients aged 12 to 22.8 years reported a prevalence of PHT of 11% (9 of 80) among adolescents [72]. Additionally, the meta-analysis of VTE and CI complications included only eight studies for the pooled prevalence estimate, which may introduce bias. To address this, we performed sensitivity analyses, which included leave-one-out analysis, influential analysis, and Baujat plots, and assessed heterogeneity to minimize potential biases.

## 结论 / Conclusion

SCD is associated with a range of chronic cardiovascular and pulmonary complications that vary in severity and contribute to morbidity and mortality. Our meta-analysis reveals a significantly increased prevalence of specific cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in adolescents and adults living with SCD. Most studies in this review reported multiple outcomes or chronic conditions, emphasizing the need for careful monitoring to detect additional cardiac and pulmonary manifestations in individuals with pre-existing disease.

We recommend routine screening and monitoring from age 5 to enable early detection and management of emerging cardiovascular and pulmonary complications in this at-risk population.
