## English Abstract

There are no studies that quantitatively synthesize epidemiological data on hypertension (HT) in Venezuela. To systematically review and conduct a meta‐analysis of population‐based studies on the prevalence of HT in Venezuela. For this, a literature search was conducted across the following databases: PubMed, EMBASE, Scopus, SciELO, LILACS, and Google Scholar, from January 2004 to December 2024, in both English and Spanish. A meta‐analysis was performed using the DerSimonian and Laird random‐effects model with inverse variance weighting. Publication bias was analyzed using a funnel plot and Egger's regression test. A subgroup analysis was conducted to explore potential sources of heterogeneity. Our systematic review followed the PRISMA guidelines, and the qualitative methodological evaluation of each study was performed using the Joanna Briggs Institute checklist. Of 13 potentially relevant studies, after sensitivity analysis regarding heterogeneity, 7 studies were retained for the final pooled prevalence estimate and quantitative synthesis. The pooled prevalence of HT was 42% (95% CI: 39%–46%, I
2 = 98.6%), based on a global sample of 83 369 individuals. This prevalence was higher in men 41% (95% CI: 33%–49%; I
2 = 97.6%), individuals of ≥50 years 45% (95% CI: 42%–48%; I
2 = 98.6%), and studies with a national scope 49% (95% CI: 48%–50%; I
2 = 62.6%). Our findings showed that 4 out of 10 Venezuelan adults suffer from HT, a rate exceeding that reported in most regional and global studies.

## 背景 / Background

Hypertension (HT) is a significant global public health concern, being the leading contributor to preventable morbidity and mortality in the adult population. According to the World Health Organization, approximately 1.28 billion people between 30 and 79 years old suffer from HT, with up to two‐thirds of them residing in low‐ or middle‐income countries. In addition, a substantial proportion of these individuals are unaware of their condition, have not been diagnosed or treated, or are under treatment but are not monitored [1, 2].

In Venezuela, several reports over the past three decades have shown an increasing prevalence of HT in our population. This rise is probably associated with a higher mean age among participants and with methodological differences, particularly because the latter reports were national in scope, whereas earlier ones were regional. Hernández et al. discussed this trend in a critical review of the evolution of HT prevalence in Venezuela [3] and a narrative review of data on cardiometabolic risk factors in the last 20 years [4]. Previously, Mijares et al. conducted a systematic review of 20 prevalence studies of HT and its determinants in Venezuela, concluding that insufficient data prevented estimating the true prevalence and that considerable variability in reported percentages highlighted important methodological considerations regarding the study's selection and comparability [5].

In light of the lack of quantitative syntheses of epidemiological data on HT in Venezuela and the importance of determining its global prevalence to establish preventive and early intervention strategies that favor cardiometabolic health and impact national public health in terms of morbidity and mortality, we decided to conduct this study. The aim was to systematically review and perform a meta‐analysis of population‐based studies on HT prevalence in Venezuela.

## 方法 / Methods

To conduct the study, we followed the steps proposed in the PRISMA guidelines [6]. The study protocol was registered in the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO), with registration number CRD42025640289.

A systematic literature review was conducted on studies reporting the prevalence of HT in Venezuelan populations aged ≥18 years of both sexes. The search was conducted through December 2024 using the PubMed, EMBASE, Scopus, SciELO, LILACS, and Google Scholar databases. Studies published from January 2004 to December 2024 were included; the search languages were English and Spanish.

In searches across PubMed, EMBASE, and Scopus, the terms used were: “Hypertension” or “High Blood Pressure” and “Prevalence” and “Venezuela.” The Emtree search included all relevant MeSH terms. Conversely, the terms used for Scielo and Lilacs were “Hipertensión,” “Presión arterial elevada,” “Prevalencia,” and “Venezuela.” In Google Scholar, 16 results were screened using the term “Hypertension in Venezuela;” additionally, articles identified manually from the reference lists were evaluated. Filters for date, age group, and study type were applied; subsequently, duplicate records were excluded using the Mendeley citation manager, and potentially relevant titles and abstracts were reviewed (Table S1).

Finally, the full‐text review identified those studies as excluded for various reasons (Figure 1).

Flow diagram of the study selection process.

Of these, one study was excluded because it was designed to evaluate patients strictly within a hospital setting, another focused on hypertensive patients in very specific population groups (i.e., solely in diabetic subjects), and two studies lacked a well‐defined cut‐off point for HT or a sufficient sample size.

Screening and data extraction were performed independently by two investigators (JS and RA). Any conflicts were resolved by agreement of both investigators or by the intervention of another investigator, who was also the author of this study (VB). We included cross‐sectional studies of Venezuelan adult populations conducted to determine HT prevalence. HT was defined as systolic blood pressure ≥140 mmHg, diastolic blood pressure ≥90 mmHg, or taking antihypertensive drugs, based on the European Society of HT cut‐off points [7].

Relevant information was extracted using a special form prepared by the authors. The following information was extracted from the included studies: first author, year of publication, study period, sample size, city/municipality, study design, scope of the study (local or national), mean age, distribution by sex, instrument used to measure blood pressure, mean systolic and diastolic blood pressure, frequency of being aware of one's HT, and the prevalence of HT in general and by sex. Data quality was assessed using the Joanna Briggs Institute checklist, with each study's risk of bias evaluated (Figure 2).

Assessment of risk of bias in included studies using the Joanna Briggs Institute questionnaire.

In the systematic review, a random‐effects meta‐analysis was performed to estimate prevalence and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) using the DerSimonian and Laird method [8]. Prevalence variance was stabilized using the Freeman–Tukey arcsine transformation [9], which was applied to estimate HT prevalence (overall and by sex) and to conduct the sensitivity analysis (according to scope, sample size, age group, and the percentage of women ≥60%). The I
2 statistic and Q test were used to quantify heterogeneity. A sensitivity analysis was performed to identify outliers using the leave‐one‐out diagnostic test. Publication bias was assessed visually using a funnel plot, and the effect estimate for small‐sample‐size studies was assessed using Egger's test. The analysis was processed in the statistical package JAMOVI version 2.6.19.0 and R Project version 4.4.2, and the significance level was 5%.

## 结果 / Results

A total of 13 references were identified through the search and screening strategy. After excluding duplicate articles and papers unrelated to HT prevalence, nine full‐text papers were considered the total eligible studies for review and prevalence calculation (n = 130 949) [10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18].

Five of the seven studies selected had a national scope, specifically the EVESCAM study, and the reports from the May Measurement Month program, which covered the largest proportion of participants evaluated, ranged from 362 to 46 732 individuals. The remaining studies had a subnational scope and were conducted in various regions of Venezuela (Barquisimeto, Maracaibo, San Cristóbal, Palanvecino, Ejido, Rangel, Catia, and Sucre). In four studies, women accounted for >60% of participants. In all studies included, two or more blood pressure measurements were obtained, and the mean of at least two tests was used to define HT cases.

Among the nationwide studies, the reports from the May Measurement Month program lacked clearly defined inclusion criteria for the sample and did not establish confounding factors; consequently, they exhibited a higher risk of bias. The remaining studies complied with most of the items in the Joanna Briggs Institute checklist.

The 9 eligible studies were initially included in the meta‐analysis; the prevalence of HT in the total eligible studies (n = 130 949) was 42% (95% CI 36%–48%; I2 = 99.75%, p < 0.01), with a range of 25%–60% (Figure 3). However, the statistical analysis revealed a high probability of outlying studies. Therefore, a sensitivity analysis was performed using the leave‐one‐out diagnostic test, showing that the first and ninth studies [10, 18] included in the meta‐analysis met the criteria for outliers (influential studies) (Figure 4).

In light of these results, study number one [10] and study number nine [18] were excluded as they were considered outlying studies. A new analysis was conducted after excluding these studies, yielding an HT prevalence of 42% (95% CI 39%–46%; I
2 = 98.6%, p < 0.01) across 7 studies (n = 83 369) (Figure 5).

Global prevalence of hypertension in Venezuela after sensitivity analysis.

Regarding sex, prevalence ranged between 35% and 51% in men, with a global HT prevalence of 41% (95% CI 33%–49%; I
2 = 97.6%, p < 0.01), and between 28% and 48% in women, with a global HT prevalence of 34% (95% CI 25%–44%; I
2 = 98.9%, p < 0.01). The aggregate prevalence of HT was higher in studies with individuals with a mean age of ≥50 years: 45% (95% CI: 42%–48%, I
2 = 98.6%) as opposed to studies conducted with individuals with a mean age of <50 years: 33% (95% CI: 28%–39%, I
2 = 57.9%). Likewise, the pooled prevalence was higher in studies with a higher proportion of women ≥60%: 45% (95% CI: 43%–48%, I
2 = 97.9%). Blood pressure patterns were similar across studies, with higher pooled prevalence in national studies and those with sample sizes ≥3000 individuals, reaching 49% (95% CI: 48%–50%; I2 = 62.6%). Similarly, in studies showing a higher risk of bias, prevalence was 49% (95.0% CI: 45%–52%; I2 = 56.1%) (Table 2).

## 讨论 / Discussion

According to the studies included in this systematic review, global HT prevalence in the Venezuelan adult population was approximately 42%, with percentages ranging from 31% to 49% over the last 20 years, based on 7 population‐based cross‐sectional studies covering 83 369 individuals, suggesting that four out of every ten adults in Venezuela are affected by this condition. Additionally, an increase in prevalence was observed in recent years, as reported in previous studies [3, 4].

These figures are higher than those shown in most of the meta‐analyses carried out in our region and even in some global reports, such as that shown by Ruiz et al., who observed a prevalence of 22% in their systematic review and meta‐analysis conducted between 1998 and 2018 in the Peruvian population (mean age: 35.9–74.8 years) [19]. In addition, Zurique et al. reported an aggregate prevalence of 24% in a systematic review of 23 studies in Colombian individuals between 2002 and 2016 (mean age: 15–92 years) [20]. Ranzani et al. conducted a global systematic review that included 299 studies involving more than 19 million individuals from low‐ and middle‐income countries and reported a prevalence of approximately 30% in urban and 28% in rural populations [21], evidencing the high frequency of this disease in our population.

The subgroup analysis showed a higher aggregate prevalence in men, in individuals aged ≥50 years, and in reports with a national scope, which included a larger proportion of patients. These results show the epidemiological impact of the latest studies on HT in Venezuela and the need to intensify preventive measures and disease control in these population groups. Comparatively, these findings vary worldwide, given the social, cultural, clinical, and methodological differences across participant groups. Nevertheless, most reports, both globally and nationally, show higher HT rates in men, older adults, urban areas, and individuals with more comorbidities [22, 23, 24].

It is important to note that the pooled prevalence should be interpreted cautiously, given the observed heterogeneity. The high heterogeneity observed may be due to population differences across the reports included in the meta‐analysis, despite adjustment for outliers in a sensitivity analysis and a qualitative assessment of bias (the majority had low risk), since this behavior was evident across all subgroups. This behavior appears to be common across most epidemiological meta‐analyses, regardless of the number of included studies [19, 20, 25]. Additionally, by including studies from the last 20 years, differences in blood pressure measurement devices and protocols became evident; this may constitute another important source of heterogeneity, as technological advances can introduce greater precision in newer devices, leading to greater heterogeneity in historical data alongside manufacturer‐ and calibration‐related variance. From a methodological standpoint, objective measures from automated methods are likely to be more precise than subjective manual measurements; these operational conditions may also introduce heterogeneity into the analysis [26].

Likewise, although our search included grey literature and data from unindexed bibliographies, by filtering after applying the selection criteria, we avoided selecting studies with a high risk of bias, thereby minimizing their influence on the results. It is important to note that the studies showing a higher risk of bias in the qualitative analysis were the reports from the May Measurement Month program, which, in turn, showed the highest prevalence levels. Therefore, given the high heterogeneity observed, this variable was included in the subgroup analysis, which showed that studies with “lower bias” were those exhibiting greater heterogeneity. However, neither the study weighting nor the sensitivity analysis was influenced by the risk of bias. In any case, the need to promote new research on this problem should be highlighted despite the several social, economic, and health difficulties faced by Venezuela [27].

Similarly, small‐study effects were observed, suggesting possible publication bias, likely due to the contrast between small regional studies and larger screening campaigns, another potential contributor to the high heterogeneity. In this sense, the May Measurement Month campaigns are valuable for public health awareness but methodologically unsuitable for estimating national prevalence because they rely on voluntary participation and thus constitute convenience samples rather than probability samples.

Another important aspect to consider is that the overall prevalence was calculated using the European Society of HT cut‐off points (140/90 mmHg or taking antihypertensive drugs), which are the values most commonly used over the last 20 years. However, the current trend is to reduce these criteria to 130/80 mmHg, as recommended by various societies [28, 29]. This fact would not change the interpretation of our analysis; on the contrary, it would increase the prevalence figures in each study and, consequently, the global prevalence, as has been suggested in various reports, including within our population [30]. It should be highlighted that the percentage of awareness of HT ranged from 19.4% to 86%, and the percentage of HT control ranged from 17.7% to 72.7%, showing a variability even greater than that of global prevalence. In addition, it underlines the need to propose preventive intervention policies to ensure early diagnosis of HT and to promote educational measures that raise awareness about the disease, the importance of antihypertensive therapy, and the need to maintain blood pressure within the currently suggested goals. The varying proportions of individuals with HT awareness and control have been less evident across reports conducted in Africa, the Middle East, and Oceania [31, 32, 33] and require future analyses focused on these characteristics, which are not addressed by the limited data in this regard.

Among the limitations of our study are the low number of reports included in the quantitative synthesis and the high heterogeneity observed. Therefore, we emphasize that the results of this meta‐analysis should be interpreted with caution, especially given that the studies with the greatest weight and highest prevalence rates are those derived from the May Measurement Month campaigns, which, due to their methodological characteristics, are unlikely to be representative of the Venezuelan population. Thus, it is necessary to highlight the scarcity of national health surveys in Venezuela, not only for HT but also for various non‐communicable chronic diseases, as well as the lack of data from remote regions with significant economic and socio‐political barriers to data collection and making difficult to pinpoint the prevalence of these conditions, not only generally but also within different minority groups at the national level.

Similarly, the lack of accurate data in the original studies did not allow for the estimation of the global prevalence of awareness, treatment, and control of the disease, which should be addressed in future reports. Therefore, this research evidences the need not only for urgent actions and measures to improve the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of HT in Venezuela but also for investments in the creation of national databases to generate vital statistics on the main health problems affecting the adult population, given the scarce availability of quality data for public health evaluations.

## 结论 / Conclusion

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first systematic review with meta‐analysis estimating the global prevalence of HT in the Venezuelan population in the last 20 years, which is around 42%, higher than that reported in most regional and global reports. Higher percentages were observed in men, individuals aged ≥50 years, and in studies with a national scope. However, future analyses should include a greater number of studies to obtain more robust data with a lower risk of bias.
