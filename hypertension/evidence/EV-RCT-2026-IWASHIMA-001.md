## English Abstract

This study assessed the efficacy and safety of sacubitril/valsartan in 27 essential hypertensive patients (mean age 74 years; male 48.1%) after switching from azilsartan, an angiotensin receptor blocker. At baseline and 3 and 6 months after the start of sacubitril/valsartan treatment, home blood pressure (HBP) and N‐terminal pro‐brain natriuretic peptide (NT‐proBNP) levels were measured. The mean dosage of azilsartan was 27±10 mg/day at baseline and that of sacubitril/valsartan after 6 months of treatment was 170±47 mg/day. After 6 months, significant reductions in mean morning HBP (144 ± 13/77 ± 13 to 128 ± 10/71 ± 9 mmHg) and median (IQR) NT‐proBNP level (188.2 [85.3–887.3] to 124.0 [83.6–696.2] pg/mL) were observed. Twenty‐one out of 27 patients accomplished recording of their nighttime HBP, and the morning‐nighttime difference in systolic HBP was decreased (8.8 ± 13.5 to 3.9 ± 8.5 mmHg)(all p < 0.05 by repeated‐measures ANOVA). In hypertensive patients, sacubitril/valsartan was generally well tolerated, except for causing hypotension, effectively controlled out‐of‐office BP, and decreased NT‐proBNP.

## 背景 / Background

Treatment of hypertension reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease outcomes, but blood pressure (BP) control rates remain suboptimal [1]. Sacubitril/valsartan, a dual angiotensin receptor‐neprilysin inhibitor, was first approved for the treatment of heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, and seems to be useful for treating resistant hypertension [2]. In several clinical trials, sacubitril/valsartan has shown a superior BP‐lowering effect compared with an angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB) such as valsartan or olmesartan [3, 4]. There have been several studies in which the BP‐lowering effect of sacubitril/valsartan was evaluated using ambulatory blood pressure monitoring [5, 6]; however, limited evidence is available for that evaluated using home blood pressure (HBP) monitoring [7]. HBP measurements are more reproducible and are particularly suited to long‐term follow‐up of treated patients because it is cheap and may improve treatment compliance.

The aim of this study was to assess the efficacy and safety of sacubitril/valsartan in comparison with azilsartan, an ARB, a widely used antihypertensive agent, in essential hypertensive patients.

## 方法 / Methods

This study consecutively enrolled 58 hypertensive patients who were admitted and underwent general checkup including BP evaluation at our institution (Kansai Medical University Kori Hospital) between January 2022 and December 2024, and had an office systolic BP (SBP) of at least 140 mm Hg or diastolic BP of at least 90 mm Hg on at least two separate office visits despite receiving azilsartan treatment. We excluded 31 patients from analysis because they met the exclusion criteria (n = 23), or had inadequate morning home BP recordings (n = 5), or developed symptomatic hypotension and could not continue (n = 3). Thus, a total of 27 patients were statistically analyzed. Exclusion criteria were age <18 years, life expectancy of less than one year, apparent infection, myocardial infarction and/or stroke within 30 days, hemodialysis or erythropoietin therapy, current pregnancy, and malignancy. All patients were switched from azilsartan (20 or 40 mg daily) to sacubitril/valsartan. Patients were initially instructed to take sacubitril/valsartan 100 mg once daily, and the dosage was gradually increased up to 200 mg once daily, but it was decreased or remained at 100 mg once daily if the patient had symptomatic hypotension and/or did not agree to increase the dosage. No patient had a change in other medication throughout the study period. Diabetes mellitus was defined according to the American Diabetes Association criteria [8]. Previous cardiovascular disease was defined as a history of myocardial infarction or stroke.

Physicians and hospital nurses instructed patients how to measure their home BP, using the Omron HCR‐7608T2, HCR‐7204T, or HCR‐7201 digital upper arm‐cuff sphygmomanometer (Omron Healthcare, Kyoto, Japan), which use the same algorithm as the HEM‐7130, HEM‐7320F, and HEM‐7500F [9]. A standard arm cuff was used for home BP measurement; all patients had an arm circumference between 22 and 34 cm. Patients were asked to record their home BP in the non‐dominant arm after at least 2 min of rest in the morning within 1 h after awaking and before taking their BP‐lowering medication, and in the evening just before going to bed [10]. All patients were asked to record their home BP for at least seven consecutive days at baseline and 3 and 6 months after the start of sacubitril/valsartan treatment, and home BP was determined as the mean of seven daily measurements.

At baseline and 3 and 6 months after the start of sacubitril/valsartan treatment, after overnight fasting, venous blood sampling as well as first‐morning urine sampling from all of the subjects were performed, and plasma N‐terminal pro‐brain natriuretic peptide (NT‐proBNP) level and urinary albumin excretion or protein excretion, as the average of two values, expressed as the albumin/creatinine ratio or protein/creatinine ratio, were evaluated.

The clinical study protocol was reviewed and approved by the independent ethics committee of Kansai Medical University Kori Hospital (2025082) and The University of Osaka Graduate School of Medicine (25278), and all enrolled patients in this study gave informed consent to participate in this study. This study was registered in the UMIN Clinical Trials Registry (UMIN R000065227).

Summary statistics are presented as mean±standard deviation for continuous variables and percentage for categorical variables unless otherwise specified. Log transformation was performed before analysis for variables with a skewed distribution. Differences between before and after sacubitril/valsartan treatment were evaluated using repeated‐measures ANOVA with post hoc Bonferroni test for continuous variables, or χ2 test for categorical variables. The correlations between baseline variables and their changes by sacubitril/valsartan treatment were assessed by linear regression analysis. All p values were two sided, and those < 0.05 were considered statistically significant. All of the calculations were performed using a standard statistical package (SPSS, version 24.0; SPSS Inc., Chicago, Illinois, USA).

## 讨论 / Discussion

This study included essential hypertensive patients who did not achieve adequate BP control despite taking azilsartan, and found that switching to sacubitril/valsartan achieved significant overall decreases in home BP as well as in NT‐proBNP level from baseline to 6 months after the start of sacubitril/valsartan treatment.

Our results showed that the mean dosage of 170 mg daily of sacubitril/valsartan was superior to the mean dosage of 27 mg daily of azilsartan in lowering BP after 6 months of treatment. Previous network meta‐analysis showed that a dosage of 80 mg azilsartan was superior to 200 mg sacubitril/valsartan in lowering BP over a treatment period of up to 12 weeks. The mean difference in office SBP after treatment favored 80 mg azilsartan compared with 200 mg sacubitril/valsartan (−1.01 mmHg), and favored 40 mg azilsartan compared with 100 mg sacubitril/valsartan (−2.81 mmHg) [11]. In consideration of this previous result, our results obtained seem to be reasonable. In Japan, the approved maximum dosage of azilsartan and sacubitril/valsartan is 40 mg and 400 mg daily, respectively. Therefore, especially in Japan, sacubitril/valsartan may be useful in the management of treatment‐resistant hypertension.

Increased morning‐nighttime difference in home SBP was reported to be a predictor of worse outcomes [12, 13]. Even though preliminary data, in this study, sacubitril/valsartan treatment decreased the morning‐nighttime difference in home SBP. Further, the prevalence of achieved home SBP <135 mmHg after treatment was almost the same between morning and nighttime. Most patients included in this study were elderly and/or had reduced eGFR; and impaired sodium excretion and overactivation of the sympathetic nervous system, which are thought to be important contributing factors to morning hypertension [14], are common in these patients [15]. Neprilysin inhibition enhances the activity of the natriuretic peptide system, leading to natriuresis, diuresis and inhibition of the renin‐angiotensin system (RAS), which act as a potentially beneficial counter‐regulatory system in states of RAS activation such as seen in hypertension. Sacubitril/valsartan's actions are composed of neprilysin inhibition and angiotensin receptor blockade, representing stronger RAS inhibition which may therefore reflect higher antihypertensive intensity [16]. Sacubitril/valsartan may have a consistent BP‐lowering effect throughout 24 h, with good antihypertensive coverage overnight and in the early morning predosing period.

Although volume status is a significant contributor to both BP control and NT‐proBNP level, in this study, correlations between change in body weight and change in morning home SBP or change in NT‐proBNP level were not significant. Further, NT‐proBNP reduction was found in subjects without BP reduction, suggesting that, independent of body weight change, sacubitril/valsartan may potentially have BP‐ and NT‐proBNP‐decreasing effects.

In this study, 3 out of 30 patients developed symptomatic hypotension and discontinued sacubitril/valsartan, and 8 out of 27 patients could not be titrated up to 200 mg/day. Although sacubitril/valsartan treatment was effective for lowering BP, it should be performed with attention to hypotension. On the other hand, sacubitril/valsartan treatment did not worsen eGFR or cause severe hyperkalemia. Increasing the ARB dose can induce hyperkalemia, and switching from an ARB to sacubitril/valsartan may be a safe option to intensify antihypertensive treatment.

This study was an uncontrolled, single‐arm, pre–post study, which was not randomized or blinded; however, the outcome was objective and was assessed in a blinded manner. The absence of a comparator group introduces risks of regression to the mean, Hawthorne effect, selection bias, and confounding by treatment intensification. This study included a limited number of patients and was conducted over a relatively short time. The obtained results might be framed as exploratory. Further randomized controlled trials in a larger population with long‐term outcomes are needed to confirm our results.

In conclusion, sacubitril/valsartan was well‐tolerated, effectively controlled out‐of‐office BP, and improved NT‐proBNP level, and may offer an effective and safe approach to controlling resistant hypertension.
