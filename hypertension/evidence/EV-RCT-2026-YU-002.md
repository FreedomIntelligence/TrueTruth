## English Abstract

This study aimed to describe joint longitudinal patterns of SBP and modified Life's Essential 8 (LE8) and to further investigate their associations with risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality in the Kailuan Study. Since systolic blood pressure (SBP) was used to assess the dual‐trajectories, modified LE8 score was calculated as the average of 7 component score after excluding blood pressure. A group‐based dual‐trajectories model was used to characterize joint longitudinal patterns of SBP and modified LE8 using data from 2006/2007 to 2014/2015 survey. Multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression models were used to assess associations of dual‐trajectories patterns with risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality (hazard ratio, HR and 95% confidence interval, 95% CI). A total of 48,596 participants were included in the current analysis, and five dual‐trajectories groups were identified. Compared with group 3, group 2 had the highest risk of gastrointestinal cancer (HR, 1.64; 95% CI, 1.17–2.29), followed by group 4 and group 5, whereas group 1 had the lowest risk of gastrointestinal cancer (HR, 1.09; 95% CI, 0.78–1.51). Similar results were observed for associations between dual‐trajectories of SBP and modified LE8 and all‐cause mortality. This study found joint longitudinal patterns of SBP and modified LE8 and revealed their associations with the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality. The findings suggest that individuals with lower modified LE8 scores had higher risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality even at similar SBP levels, underscoring the importance of comprehensive cardiovascular health for risk stratification and prevention.

## 背景 / Background

With population aging, widespread unhealthy lifestyles, and the insidious onset of disease, the burden of gastrointestinal cancers has been increasing [1]. As a major public health challenge, effective cancer prevention is essential for reducing mortality and prolonging life expectancy. Emerging evidence suggests that elevated systolic blood pressure (SBP) or hypertension may be associated with an increased risk of gastrointestinal cancer, whereas the association between diastolic blood pressure (DBP) and gastrointestinal cancer has generally been nonsignificant [2, 3, 4, 5]. SBP has long been regarded as a more direct and clinically relevant determinant of adverse health outcomes and remains the primary target in blood pressure management [6, 7, 8]. However, most previous studies have investigated the associations of SBP and other risk factors with risk of gastrointestinal cancers separately, thereby overlooking the intrinsic interrelationships between SBP levels and other established risk factors associated with cancer. Dietary habits, diabetes, body mass index (BMI), dyslipidemia, smoking, and physical inactivity are well‐established factors contributing to cancer risk [9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16]. To achieve the strategic goals of improving cardiovascular health (CVH) by 20% and reducing cardiovascular disease (CVD) mortality by 20%, the American Heart Association (AHA) introduced “Life's Simple 7” (LS7) in 2010 [17]. However, with accumulating evidence and clinical experience, limitations in its content and scoring framework have become increasingly evident, as LS7 no longer adequately reflects the evolving needs of CVH assessment. To further promote CVH at both individual and population levels, the AHA expanded the original seven metrics by adding sleep health and introduced a new 0–100 scoring system for each component, collectively termed “Life's Essential 8” (LE8), thereby updating and strengthening the conceptual framework of cardiovascular health [18]. Although LE8 was introduced to improve CVH assessment, its components have also been associated with the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and mortality. Previous studies have found that sustained high LE8 scores are inversely associated with cancer and mortality. However, these studies had a short exposure period and therefore failed to capture long‐term change patterns of LE8 [19]. Furthermore, although the independent trajectories of SBP and LE8 have been evaluated, their joint longitudinal patterns have not been thoroughly examined. Consequently, the combined long‐term trajectories of SBP and modified LE8 score excluding the blood pressure component (hereafter referred to as modified LE8), as well as their associations with the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality, remain largely unknown.

Therefore, to address this gap, we utilized data from the Kailuan Study to develop a group‐based dual‐trajectories model, characterizing the joint longitudinal patterns of SBP levels and modified LE8 scores from 2006 to 2015. Subsequently, we further investigated the associations of the dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 scores with the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality.

## 方法 / Methods

The Kailuan Study is a large‐scale, ongoing prospective cohort study conducted in Tangshan, China, enrolling employees and retirees of the Kailuan Group [20, 21]. A total of 101 510 participants underwent the first health examination at Kailuan General Hospital and its 10 affiliated hospitals between 2006 and 2007 (abbreviated 2006/2007 survey), with subsequent surveys conducted biennially. The surveys primarily included a standardized questionnaire, laboratory tests, and clinical measurements. In addition, the incidence of CVD events, cancers, and death was collected annually by professionally trained medial staff. This study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and was approved by the Ethics Committee of Kailuan General Hospital. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants.

We defined the time from the 2006/2007 survey to the 2014/2015 survey as the exposure period to identify the joint longitudinal patterns of SBP levels and modified LE8 scores, we used the time of 2014/2015 survey as the starting point (baseline) for the follow‐up and further observed the occurrence of all‐cause mortality and gastrointestinal cancer during the period of 2015–2022. Initially, a total of 101 510 individuals participated in the 2006/2007 survey. Participants were excluded if they had a history of cancer at baseline or had missing data on SBP, DBP, and components of the modified LE8 scores. Next, for participants enrolled in the 2006/2007 survey, we further excluded 37 386 participants without participating in the 2014/2015 survey, 947 participants with a history of cancer between the 2006/2007 survey and 2014/2015 survey, 1241 participants without participating in the 2008/2009 survey, 2010/2011 survey, and 2012/2013 survey, and 5147 participants who participated in the surveys during 2008–2015 years but had missing data on SBP, DBP, and modified LE8 components. Finally, a total of 48 596 participants were included in the analysis of gastrointestinal cancer risk. A total of 49 524 participants were included in the analysis of all‐cause mortality according to inclusion and exclusion criteria described above. Flowchart of the participants in the current analysis LE8, Life's essential 8.

Information on all participants was collected by trained interviewers through face‐to‐face interviews using a standardized questionnaire, including demographic data (age, sex, educational status, type of work, and marital status), a history of disease (CVD and cancers), family history of cancer, medication use (antihypertensive drugs, hypoglycemic drugs, and lipid‐lowering drugs), and lifestyle factors (sleep duration, smoking status, drinking status, physical activity, salt intake, tea intake, and high‐fat food intake). Participants were asked whether they had ever been diagnosed with hypertension, diabetes mellitus, dyslipidemia, CVD, or cancer. For those who responded affirmatively, information on medication use was further collected. Participants were asked whether their parents or grandparents had ever had cancer, it was defined as “a family history of cancer” if a participant answered “yes” to the question. Educational status was categorized as below high school and high school or above. The type of work was categorized as mental work and physical work. Drinking status was classified as current or noncurrent, and current drinkers were defined as a current consumption of average of at least 100 mL (alcohol content ≥ 50%) of alcohol daily for at least one year. The participants were asked whether they currently smoked and were categorized as never smokers, former smokers, and current smokers, current smokers were further asked about frequency and amount of smoking. Interviewers asked participants about the weekly frequency of physical activities, the duration of each physical activity was at least 20 min. Participants were further asked how many hours do you sleep on an average night. Participants were also asked about their taste preferences, frequency of high‐fat food intake, and frequency of tea intake.

Blood pressure was measured by trained medical staff using a standard method and a validated sphygmomanometer between 7:00 and 9:00 am on the day of the survey. Blood pressure was measured from right brachial artery using a calibrated mercury sphygmomanometer between the first and fourth surveys, and was measured using a calibrated automatic electronic sphygmomanometer (Omron HEM‐8102A, Dalian, China) since 2014 [22]. Participants were advised to avoid tea, coffee, and smoking at least 30 min and take rest for at least five minutes before blood pressure measurement. In the same environment, blood pressure was measured in duplicate at 1–2 min intervals, and again if the difference between the two measurements was higher than 5 mmHg. The average of the two measurements was taken as the final results. On the day of the health examination, height and weight were measured by trained medical staff using standardized methods (without shoes and in light clothing), the values were accurate to 0.1 cm and 0.1 kg, respectively. BMI was calculated as weight in kilograms divided by the square of height in meters (kg/m2).

After an overnight fast of at least 8 h, cubital venous blood samples were drawn from each participant by trained medical staff in the early morning of the survey day and stored at ‐80°C [23]. Subsequently, all blood samples were transported to the central laboratory at Kailuan General Hospital. And then automatic biochemical analyzer (Hitachi 747;Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan) was performed to determine serum creatinine, total cholesterol (TC), triglycerides (TG), fasting blood glucose (FBG), low‐density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL‐C), high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL‐C), and high‐sensitivity C‐reactive protein (hs‐CRP). Non‐high‐density lipoprotein cholesterol (non‐HDL‐C) was calculated as TC minus HDL‐C. The estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) was calculated using the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration formula based on serum creatinine level [24].

LE8 comprises four behavior factors and four metabolic factors [18], behavior factors included diet, physical activity, smoking status, and sleep duration, four metabolic factors included BMI, blood lipids, blood glucose, and blood pressure. Since we aimed to assess the dual‐trajectories of SBP and LE8 scores, a modified LE8 scores excluding the blood pressure component was constructed based on the remaining seven metrics. Diet health was assessed by self‐reported taste preferences, frequency of high‐fat food intake, and frequency of tea intake. Each component of LE8 was scored on a scale from 0 to 100, with higher scores representing better CVH status. The overall modified LE8 scores was calculated as the average of the 7 scores for the 7 components, definitions and scoring approach for each component are provided in Table S1.

In this study, the 2014/2015 survey was used as the starting point (baseline) for follow‐up, the endpoint was the first incident of gastrointestinal cancer, all‐cause mortality, or December 31, 2022, whichever came first. In addition to self‐reported physician diagnosis of CVD and cancer collected through biennial standardized questionnaires, incident disease of the Kailuan Group was collected annually through the Tangshan medical insurance system and the Kailuan social security system, and then three trained medical staff reviewed inpatient cases and coded diseases according to the International Classification of Disease, 10th version (ICD‐10) [25]. Cancers diagnosis were confirmed based on pathology and imaging. Gastrointestinal cancers include cancers of esophagus (C15), stomach (C16), small intestine (C17), colorectum (C18–C21), and liver (C22). CVD events, including coronary heart disease (I20–I22 and I24–I25), stroke (I60, I61, and I63), heart failure (I50), and atrial fibrillation (I48), were defined based on the ICD‐10 coding criteria [20]. Data on deaths were collected via death certificates obtained from provincial vital statistics office and the Kailuan social security system.

Liver cirrhosis was diagnosed according to the Guideline for the Prevention and Treatment of Viral Hepatitis. Ultrasonographic findings included heterogeneous, increased hepatic echogenicity with a nodular appearance; dilatation of the portal and splenic veins; thinning, tortuosity, and irregular caliber of the hepatic veins; and the presence of ascites. Hypertension was defined as SBP ≥ 140 mmHg and/or DBP ≥ 90 mmHg (1 mmHg = 0.133 kPa), use of antihypertensive drugs, or a self‐reported history.

Based on data distribution, continuous variables were expressed as the mean ± standard deviation (SD) or median (interquartile range) and were compared using analysis of variance or the Kruskal–Wallis test. Categorical variables were expressed as case number and percentages [N (%)] and were compared using the Chi‐square test. Because the proportion of missing data for covariates was less than 5%, we used multiple imputation by chained equations to impute the missing values.

Kaplan–Meier curves were used to estimate the cumulative incidence of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality among different groups, and differences were assessed using the Log‐rank test. Incident rates of all‐cause mortality and gastrointestinal cancer were calculated as the total number of events per 1000 person‐years. A group‐based dual‐trajectories model was used to characterize the joint longitudinal patterns of SBP and modified LE8 scores. The model was implemented using the PROC TRAJ procedure in SAS software [26].

The proportional hazards assumption was satisfied using Schoenfeld residuals analyses prior to the analyses. First, multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression models were used to assess the associations of dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 with the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality (hazard ratio, HR and 95% confidence interval, 95% CI). Model 1 was unadjusted. Model 2 was adjusted for age and sex. Model 3 was adjusted for covariates in model 2 plus hs‐CRP categories, educational status, type of work, marital status, drinking status, liver cirrhosis, and family history of cancer. In addition, we assessed the associations between LE8 score with risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality. Given the potential influence of age, sex, and educational status on the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality, we conducted several analyses by age (< 60 years and ≥ 60 years), sex (females and males), and educational status (below the high school and high school or above). Multiplicative interactions were assessed by including cross‐product terms between subgroup variables and exposure variable in multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression models. In addition, we conducted several sensitivity analyses to assess the robustness of the results by excluding participants with eGFR lower than 60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 (sensitivity analysis 1); excluding those with a history of CVD (sensitivity analysis 2); excluding those with antihypertensive drugs, hypoglycemic drugs, or lipid‐lowering drugs (sensitivity analysis 3); excluding those with hs‐CRP ≥ 10 mg/dL (sensitivity analysis 4); adjusting for modified LE8 scores and hypertension at the 2006/2007 survey (sensitivity analysis 5); adjusting for modified LE8 scores and hypertension at the 2014/2015 survey (sensitivity analysis 6). Considering the competing relationship between CVD and death, the association between dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 and the risk of gastrointestinal cancer were assessed using a competing risk model to remove estimation biases from competing events (sensitivity analysis 7). For analyses with all‐cause mortality, we conducted sensitivity analysis 7 after excluding participants with a history of cancer at the 2014/2015 survey.

All statistical analyses were conducted using SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC), two‐sided p values < 0.05 were considered statistically significant.

## 结果 / Results

A total of 48 596 participants were included in the analysis investigating the association of dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 scores with the risk of gastrointestinal cancer. Of these, 77.31% were men, with a mean age of 56.50 years (Figure 1). We fitted all trajectory models with the number of trajectory groups ranging from 1 to 6. The optimal dual‐trajectories model was selected based on an average posterior probability (AvePP) > 0.70, the lowest Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), and a minimum group membership proportion > 5% (Table S2). Ultimately, a five‐group dual‐trajectories model of SBP and modified LE8 scores was identified (Figure 2 and Figure S1): group 1 was characterized by low SBP and low modified LE8 (23.94%); group 2 was characterized by high SBP and very low modified LE8 (13.51%); group 3 was characterized by very low SBP and very high modified LE8 (21.64%); group 4 was characterized by very high SBP and moderate modified LE8 (12.16%); group 5 was characterized by high SBP and high modified LE8 (28.76%). Baseline characteristics of participants according to dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 are presented in Table 1. Compared with participants in the other groups, those in group 4 were older, had a higher proportion of males, the highest SBP (164.69 mmHg), the lowest proportion of participants with a high school or above (13.20%), and moderate modified LE8 scores.

Based on the trajectory fitting criteria, a five‐group trajectories model of LE8 scores was identified (Figure S2): low‐stable group (persistently low LE8 scores throughout the exposure period), moderate‐increasing group (moderate LE8 scores at baseline with an increased trend), moderate‐decreasing group (moderate LE8 scores at baseline with an decreased trend), moderate‐stable group (persistently moderate LE8 scores), and high‐stable group (persistently high scores). Compared with the low‐stable group, the risks of gastrointestinal cancer were decreased by 33% and 45% in the moderate‐stable and high‐stable groups; the HRs (95% CIs) of all‐cause mortality for the moderate‐increasing, moderate‐decreasing, moderate‐stable, and high‐stable groups were 0.73 (0.63, 0.86), 0.70 (0.63, 0.77), 0.56 (0.50, 0.62), and 0.44 (0.37, 0.53), respectively (Table S3).

During a mean follow‐up of 7.45 years, a total of 480 gastrointestinal cancers and 3389 cases of all‐cause mortality occurred. Incidence rates per 1000 person‐years for gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality were highest in group 4 (2.10 and 23.36) and lowest in group 3 (0.75 and 3.29). The results from Kaplan–Meier curves showed that participants in group 4 had significantly higher risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality than those in other groups, and the Log‐rank tests indicated significant differences among groups (P<0.05; Figure 3 and S3). In model 1, participants in group 4 had the highest risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality, followed by group 2 and group 5, whereas group 1 had the lowest risks (Table 2). After adjusting for all potential covariates, group 2, characterized by high SBP and very low modified LE8, had the highest risk of gastrointestinal cancer (HR, 1.64; 95% CI, 1.17–2.29), followed by group 4 (HR, 1.53; 95% CI, 1.09–2.16) and group 5 (HR, 1.26; 95% CI, 0.92–1.71), whereas group 1, characterized by low SBP and low modified LE8, had the lowest risk of gastrointestinal cancer (HR, 1.09; 95% CI, 0.78–1.51), although this association was not statistically significant. Similar results were observed for the associations between dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 and all‐cause mortality. ## 讨论 / Discussion

In this prospective cohort study, we identified five distinct longitudinal dual‐trajectories of SBP levels and modified LE8 scores. Our findings indicated that participants in group 2, characterized by high SBP and very low modified LE8 scores, had the highest risk of gastrointestinal cancer, followed by group 4 (very high SBP and moderate modified LE8 score), whereas group 1 (low SBP and low modified LE8 score) had the lowest risk. At comparable SBP level, participants with higher modified LE8 scores exhibited a lower risk of gastrointestinal cancer than those with lower modified LE8 scores. Similar findings were observed in the analysis of all‐cause mortality. Improving overall CVH through optimal health behaviors and factors, as reflected by higher modified LE8 scores, was associated with lower risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality, and this protective association persisted even among individuals with elevated SBP.

Existing evidence suggests that higher LE8 scores are associated with increased longevity and a reduced risk of chronic noncommunicable diseases, including diabetic nephropathy, stroke, myocardial infarction, and heart failure [27, 28, 29, 30, 31]. However, evidence regarding the association between LE8 scores and risk of cancer remains limited, particularly for site‐specific cancers. To our knowledge, most previous studies have primarily focused on the association between LS7 and risk of cancer, consistently showing that a greater number of ideal health metrics is associated with a lower risk of cancer [28, 31]. Compared with LE8, the LS7 scoring system is relatively crude and may be insufficient to capture individual CVH. In contrast, LE8 comprises 8 metrics, each quantitatively scored on a standardized scale scale from 0 to 100, and further incorporates sleep duration as a novel dimension of CVH assessment [18]. Accumulating evidence suggests that suboptimal sleep duration is associated with an increased risk of cancer [32]. Using data from three survey waves (2006/2007, 2008/2009, and 2010/2011) of the Kailuan Study, Wu et al. found that higher LE8 scores were significantly associated with a reduced risk of digestive system cancers [19]. Although that study described trajectory patterns of LE8 scores, the relatively short 4‐year exposure duration may have limited the detection of meaningful changes, resulting in relatively stable trajectories. However, cardiovascular function gradually deteriorates with age. Therefore, the exposure duration in the present study was extended to 8 years, enabling the identification of distinct trajectory patterns. Our findings suggest sustained high LE8 scores were associated with lower risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality. Notably, the protective effects associated with higher LE8 scores should not be overlooked, highlighting the public health importance of improving CVH.

The pathogenesis of gastrointestinal cancers is highly complex. Previous studies suggest that hypertension is a risk factor for gastrointestinal cancers, whereas favorable CVH behaviors and factors may mitigate this risk. Accumulating evidence further suggests that hypertension and its accompanying risk factors may act synergistically in cancer development [33, 34, 35]. Therefore, investigating the joint trajectories in SBP and CVH scores is crucial for developing targeted prevention strategies and personalized treatment approaches. To our knowledge, no studies have specifically examined the association between joint trajectories of SBP and modified LE8 scores and the risks of gastrointestinal cancers and all‐cause mortality. The present study integrated the joint effects of SBP and modified LE8 scores, identifying distinct trajectory patterns and providing new evidence for characterizing and monitoring their co‐evolution over time. This study employed a group‐based dual‐trajectory model to construct trajectory groups of SBP and modified LE8 scores, systematically describing their interrelationship and visualizing of joint longitudinal patterns. The study also showed that the dual‐trajectories of SBP and modified LE8 scores were closely associated with an increased risk of gastrointestinal cancer. Notably, compared with group 3, characterized by very low SBP and very high modified LE8 scores, groups 2 (high SBP and very low modified LE8 scores) and group 4 (very high SBP and moderate modified LE8 scores) exhibited significantly increased risks of gastrointestinal cancers. Although SBP levels in group 2 were lower than those in group 4, group 2 which had extremely low modified LE8 scores seemed to have a greater impact on the risk of gastrointestinal cancers. In addition, although comparable SBP trajectories in group 2 and group 5, group 5 was not associated with an increased risk of gastrointestinal cancers due to its higher modified LE8 scores. Overall, this finding highlight the importance of maintaining favorable CVH in populations at high risk for gastrointestinal cancer. These findings suggest that lifestyle modification and improvements in LE8 scores may help attenuate the risk of gastrointestinal cancer, even among individuals with elevated blood pressure.

This study demonstrated that even among individuals with comparable SBP levels, higher modified LE8 scores were associated with a significantly lower risk of all‐cause mortality. Notably, the dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 scores were more strongly associated with all‐cause mortality, particularly among middle‐aged and younger individuals and males. This age difference may be attributable to the greater reversibility of cardiovascular risk factors and the increased effectiveness of health behavior interventions during middle adulthood [36, 37]. Because atherosclerosis and organ dysfunction are not yet fully established in middle adulthood, changes in LE8 scores may serve as a more sensitive indicator of CVH, despite the accumulation of unhealthy lifestyle behaviors. Middle‐aged and younger individuals who maintained higher LE8 scores may have a lower risk of all‐cause mortality, potentially through improvements in metabolic status, reduced inflammation, and delayed progression of vascular injury [38, 39, 40, 41]. In addition, stronger associations were observed among males, which may be explained by sex differences in lifestyle behaviors, metabolic characteristics, and hormonal profiles. Males are more likely to smoke, drink, and engage in insufficient physical activity [42], which may contribute to lower modified LE8 scores and greater susceptibility to adverse health outcomes associated with joint changes in SBP and modified LE8 scores. Taken together, middle‐aged and younger individuals, as well as males, may have greater potential for intervention through blood pressure management and CVH promotion, and strengthened health management strategies may further reduce the risk of all‐cause mortality.

This study highlights the critical role of SBP levels in assessing the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality, while accounting for their joint longitudinal patterns with CVH scores. These findings not only elucidate the complex interplay between blood pressure and CVH but also provide novel evidence to support refined risk stratification and the development of individualized prevention strategies for high‐risk populations. Early control of SBP should be encouraged to reduce its potential impact on chronic diseases. At the same time, maintaining favorable CVH is equally important, as their synergistic improvement may yield sustained and cumulative benefits for both cardiovascular and overall systemic health over the long term. Collectively, these findings underscore the importance of comprehensive management of blood pressure and CVH from early adulthood onward, providing feasible public health strategies for reducing the risks of cancer and all‐cause mortality.

Using data from a large Chinese prospective cohort, this study is the first to investigate the associations of dual‐trajectories patterns of SBP and modified LE8 scores with the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality. By applying an extended group‐based trajectory model over an 8‐year exposure period, we identified distinct joint trajectory patterns that capturing the dynamic interplay between SBP and modified LE8 scores and underscoring the importance of dual‐trajectories analysis. Nevertheless, several limitations should be acknowledged. First, due to the occupational nature of the Kailuan Group, the study population was predominantly males; nonetheless, the sample size of females remained sufficient for the analyses. Second, dietary information was not included in the original questionnaire, resulting in a dietary component of modified LE8 that differed from the AHA definition. However, the LE8 assessment from the Kailuan Study has been widely recognized, and related findings have been published in high‐impact international journals. Third, Helicobacter pylori infection has been reported to be closely associated with gastric cancer and peptic ulcer disease; however, data on H. pylori infection were not available, which may have led to residual confounding. Fourth, endoscopic examinations were not routinely performed due to their invasive nature and high cost, limiting their feasibility in large‐scale epidemiological studies. In addition, pathological information on gastrointestinal cancers was incomplete during follow‐up, precluding detailed classification of cancer subtypes based on histopathology.

## 结论 / Conclusion

In conclusion, over an 8‐year follow‐up period, we identified five distinct joint trajectories of SBP and modified LE8 scores. The findings suggest that, even at similar SBP levels, individuals with lower modified LE8 scores have higher risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality. These findings highlight the potential of SBP as a predictor for identifying high‐risk population and underscore the importance of early and sustained interventions to maintain optimal CVH and SBP levels, thereby reducing the risks of gastrointestinal cancer and all‐cause mortality.
